Life Insurance Present Values

The expected present value of a benefit, decoded — and the small algebraic bridges that link insurance to annuities.

Every life insurance benefit reduces to one idea: discount the payment back to today and weight it by the probability it is ever made. From that single random variable Z, everything else follows — its mean, its variance, and its quiet relationship to the annuity ä that pays the survivor.

Commit the three identities below to memory. On exam day they convert a problem you cannot solve into one you can, often in a single line.

The Three Identities
Aₓ = 1 − d·äₓ
Var(Z) = ²Aₓ − (Aₓ)²
äₓ = (1 − Aₓ) / d

Read them as a triangle. Know any two corners — the insurance Aₓ, the annuity äₓ, or the discount d — and the third is fixed. Most “hard” questions are simply asking you to walk one edge of that triangle.

The variance formula hides a trap of its own: the ²A term uses the squared discount factor v², which is the same as valuing the benefit at the higher rate (1+i)² − 1. Compute it as a separate insurance, not by squaring Aₓ.

Know any two corners of the triangle, and the third is no longer a question.
Common Trap

Under UDD, multiply only the term / death piece by i ⁄ δ — the pure endowment is never rescaled.
