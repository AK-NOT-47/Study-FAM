Key Concepts

1. Derivatives, underlying assets, and short-selling

A derivative is a financial instrument whose value is derived from the price of something else — its underlying asset. On FAM the underlying is a tradable security: a stock (common stock — ownership, dividends, voting), an index (e.g., Dow Jones, S&P 500), or a mutual fund. Mathematically these behave identically; we write the price as $S_t$ ($t$ in years).

Known vs. random prices (notation that matters)
  • $S_0$ (or $S_t$, "now") — the current price, which we can look up, so it is treated as a known constant.
  • $S_T$ — a future price of a risky asset, which is a random variable (nobody knows tomorrow's price).
  • Underlying assets are assumed to pay no dividends (or to reinvest them) — mathematically the same.

Long vs. short. A long position is a positive holding — you own the asset; the payoff of a long stock is $+S_T$. A short position is a negative holding — you owe the asset; the payoff of a short stock is $-S_T$ (the opposite of long).

Short-selling lets you profit from a price decline on a stock you do not own, in three steps:

  1. Borrow shares now from a lender (promise to return them later).
  2. Sell the borrowed shares immediately and invest the proceeds at the risk-free rate.
  3. Buy back the shares later and return them to the lender.

A payoff is the amount you would have if you completely cashed out at a given time, ignoring cash flows on other dates. A risk-free zero-coupon bond has payoff equal to its maturity value $B$ regardless of anything else. We combine long/short positions in stocks and bonds into a portfolio; in particular, long stock + short bond reappears in put–call parity below.

2. What is an option?

Motivating scenario: an insurer will receive $\$1{,}000$ of premium in one year and wants to buy a stock index then for today's price of $\$1{,}000$ — but the index might rise. It buys, for an upfront fee, the right (not the obligation) to pay $\$1{,}000$ for the index in one year. This caps its purchase price at $\$1{,}000$: it exercises only if the index rises above $\$1{,}000$. That contract is a call option.

An option gives its owner the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at an agreed price.

  • Call option — the right to buy the asset for the strike price $K$. Mnemonic: COB — Call is Option to Buy.
  • Put option — the right to sell the asset for $K$. Mnemonic: POS — Put is Option to Sell.
  • Expiration (expiry / maturity date) — when the contract ends; strike price $K$ — the exchange price; exercising — invoking your right.
  • European — exercisable only at expiry $T$. American — exercisable any time up to $T$. FAM's pricing formulas are European; American options appear in the binomial early-exercise context.

The buyer (holder) pays a premium up front and is long; the seller (writer) receives the premium, is short, and takes on the obligation. We write the time-$t$ premiums as $c(t)$ and $p(t)$, with $T-t$ years to expiry — e.g. $c(0)$ is today's call price.

Intuition: an option is asymmetric. The long side caps its loss at the premium but keeps unlimited (call) or large (put) upside; the short side keeps a fixed premium but bears open-ended risk — exactly insurance's risk-transfer logic, which is why this lives on FAM.

3. To exercise or not? — and Payoff vs. Profit

A rational holder exercises only when it pays. A call is exercised iff $S_T>K$ (buy cheap at $K$, worth more); a put iff $S_T\lt K$ (sell dear at $K$, cheaper in market). Otherwise the holder walks away and the payoff is floored at $0$.

  • Payoff = value of the option at expiry, ignoring what you paid for it. Always $\ge 0$ for the long holder.
  • Profit = payoff minus the accumulated cost of the position (premium grown at the risk-free rate to time $T$).
PositionPayoff at $T$Profit at $T$
Long stock$S_T$—
Short stock$-S_T$—
Long call$(S_T-K)_+=\max(S_T-K,\,0)$$\max(S_T-K,0) - C e^{rT}$
Short call$-\max(S_T-K,\,0)$$C e^{rT} - \max(S_T-K,0)$
Long put$(K-S_T)_+=\max(K-S_T,\,0)$$\max(K-S_T,0) - P e^{rT}$
Short put$-\max(K-S_T,\,0)$$P e^{rT} - \max(K-S_T,0)$

Here $C, P$ are the time-0 premiums and $S_T$ is the asset price at expiry. A short position's payoff is always the opposite of the matching long position — its diagram is the reflection of the long diagram about the horizontal axis.

Diagram shapes (commit these to memory)
  • Long call: flat at the loss line for $S_T\lt K$, then rises at 45° (slope $+1$) past $K$. Breakeven at $S_T = K + Ce^{rT}$.
  • Long put: falls at 45° (slope $-1$) up to $K$, then flat. Breakeven at $S_T = K - Pe^{rT}$.
  • Short positions are the mirror image across the horizontal axis of the corresponding long position.

4. Option moneyness

Moneyness describes what the option's payoff would be if exercised right now — ignoring its exercise style entirely (we pretend it could be exercised immediately, even if the payoff is negative).

MoneynessIf exercised now, payoff is…CallPut
In-the-money (ITM)positive$S>K$$S\lt K$
At-the-money (ATM)exactly $0$$S=K$$S=K$
Out-of-the-money (OTM)negative$S\lt K$$S>K$

Moneyness is a snapshot for classification only; the actual payoff is still floored at $0$ because no rational holder exercises into a loss.

5. Options as insurance & embedded options

A call is insurance against a price increase (it caps what you'll pay for the asset at $K$); a put is insurance against a price decrease (it guarantees you can sell at $K$). Insurers package such protection into guarantees, and many guarantees can be rewritten as options — these are embedded options.

The classic decomposition uses $\max(S_0,S_T)=\max(S_0-S_T,0)+S_T$: a "get at least your money back" guarantee equals a share of the asset plus a long put struck at the guaranteed floor. A variable-annuity guaranteed minimum maturity benefit (GMMB) is a put on the policyholder's fund; an equity-indexed annuity's participation feature is effectively a call. Once the insurer recognizes the embedded option, it can hedge the guarantee by buying that option — exactly the machinery in the rest of this topic.

6. Put–Call Parity and the law of one price (the single most testable formula)

A portfolio of long $K$-call + short $K$-put has payoff $\max(S_T-K,0)-\max(K-S_T,0)=S_T-K$ — identical to long stock + short bond maturing for $K$ (a forward to buy the stock at $K$). The law of one price (the no-arbitrage assumption) says two positions with identical payoffs must cost the same, else an arbitrage ("free money": buy low, sell high, no risk, no net investment) exists. Equating costs gives, for European options on the same underlying, strike $K$, and expiry $T$:

$$ C - P = S_0 - K e^{-rT} \quad(\text{non-dividend stock}) $$

Parity holds only for European options and only when call and put share the same underlying, strike, and time to expiry. The dividend versions below adjust $S_0$ downward; you are not separately tested on the dividend forms, but they are good to know.

7. The Binomial Model — two equivalent pricers

Assumptions: a risky stock and a risk-free bond, discrete trading times, no transaction costs, short-selling allowed, and no arbitrage. Over one period the stock either goes up to $S_0 u$ or down to $S_0 d$ (with $u>d$; note $d$ need not be below $1$).

(a) Replicating portfolio. Buy $\Delta$ shares and lend (or borrow) $B$ in cash so the portfolio exactly reproduces the option's two possible payoffs. By the law of one price the option must cost the same: price $= \Delta S_0 + B$. Sign reading is crucial — see the table below.

(b) Risk-neutral valuation. Invent a probability under which the stock earns the risk-free rate on average; price the option as its expected payoff discounted at the risk-free rate. Same answer, faster.

Replicating-portfolio sign table
CallPut
$\Delta$ (shares)$+$ → buy stock$-$ → short-sell stock
$B$ (cash)$-$ → borrow$+$ → lend

So: replicate a call by buying stock and borrowing; replicate a put by short-selling stock and lending.

The risk-neutral measure ($Q$) vs. the real world ($P$)

The risk-neutral probability is a pricing tool, not the true probability. The $Q$-measure (risk-neutral measure) is what we price with; the $P$-measure is the physical / real-world distribution. Risk-neutral pricing says we may discount every asset at the risk-free rate as if investors demanded no risk premium. As a sanity check, discounting the stock's own risk-neutral expected price returns $S_0$ exactly. The real-world up-probability never enters the option price (a result that surprises everyone the first time).

Delta $\Delta$ — the number of shares in the replicating portfolio — is the option's price sensitivity to the stock and the foundation of delta-hedging: a writer who holds $\Delta$ shares per option written is (instantaneously) immune to small stock moves.

8. Multi-period binomial: backward induction, the binomial shortcut, and self-financing

Keeping $u,d$ constant makes the tree recombine ($S_0ud=S_0du$). Two ways to price:

  • Node-by-node (backward). Apply the one-period formula at every node from expiry back to time 0. Required when a question asks for a replicating-portfolio component ($\Delta$ or $B$) at an interior node, or for an American option.
  • Binomial-probability shortcut (European only). Compute each terminal payoff, weight by the binomial probability of reaching it, and discount once back to time 0.

The hedge is self-financing: the value of the portfolio held into a node exactly equals the cost of the portfolio you need at that node, so you only invest cash at time 0 and merely rebalance thereafter — this continual rebalancing is dynamic hedging.

For an American option, at each node take the larger of the continuation value and the immediate-exercise value.

9. The lognormal stock model

Let the binomial period length shrink to zero: the tree becomes a continuous-time lognormal stochastic process (geometric Brownian motion), $\{S_t\}_{t\ge0}$. Define the accumulation factor $S_{t+\tau}/S_t$; these factors are independent over non-overlapping intervals and lognormally distributed. The log-return $\ln(S_{t+\tau}/S_t)$ is normal with mean $\mu\tau$ and variance $\sigma^2\tau$, where (with $\tau$ in years) $\mu$ is the annual expected return and $\sigma$ the annual volatility. This lets us compute real-world probabilities of future prices — see the Formulas section. (Watch the Greek $\tau$ "tau" — easy to misread as $r$ or $t$.)

10. Black–Scholes

As the number of binomial periods $\to\infty$ with appropriately chosen $u,d$, the binomial price converges to the Black–Scholes–Merton (BSM) price. BSM assumes a constant, flat risk-free rate, frictionless continuous trading, no dividends, and a stock that is lognormal with drift $\mu$ (real world) but drift $r-\tfrac12\sigma^2$ under the risk-neutral measure. The price of any payoff is its risk-neutral expectation discounted at $r$: $v(t)=e^{-r(T-t)}E^Q_t[h(S_T)]$. For calls/puts this yields the closed form below; $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$ are the risk-neutral deltas/probabilities, and $N(d_2)$ is (under $Q$) the probability the call finishes in the money. The BSM call/put are themselves delta-hedge portfolios: $\Delta_{\text{call}}=N(d_1)$, $\Delta_{\text{put}}=-N(-d_1)$.

11. Why insurers care

Insurance products often contain embedded options: a variable-annuity guaranteed minimum maturity benefit (GMMB) is a put on the policyholder's fund (the insurer guarantees a floor); an equity-indexed annuity's participation feature is effectively a call. Pricing and reserving for these guarantees uses exactly the option-pricing machinery above.

Formulas

Payoffs and profit

$$ \text{Long stock} = S_T, \qquad \text{Short stock} = -S_T, \qquad \text{Bond} = B $$ $$ \text{Call payoff} = (S_T-K)_+ = \max(S_T - K,\,0), \qquad \text{Put payoff} = (K-S_T)_+ = \max(K - S_T,\,0) $$ $$ \text{Short call} = -\max(S_T-K,0), \qquad \text{Short put} = -\max(K-S_T,0) $$ $$ \text{Long-option profit} = \text{payoff} - (\text{premium})\,e^{rT} $$
Symbols

$S_T$ — underlying price at expiry; $K$ — strike; $r$ — continuously-compounded risk-free rate; $T$ — time to expiry (years); $c(t),p(t)$ — time-$t$ call/put premiums.

Put–call parity

Non-dividend stock $$ C(K,T) - P(K,T) = S_0 - K e^{-rT} $$ Continuous dividend yield $\delta$ $$ C - P = S_0 e^{-\delta T} - K e^{-rT} $$ Discrete dividends with present value $\mathrm{PV}_{0,T}(\text{Div})$ $$ C - P = \big(S_0 - \mathrm{PV}_{0,T}(\text{Div})\big) - K e^{-rT} $$

$C, P$ — current call/put premiums; $\delta$ — continuous dividend yield; $S_0$ — current stock price. Same underlying, same strike, same expiry; European only.

One-period binomial — replicating portfolio

$$ \Delta = e^{-\delta h}\,\frac{V_u - V_d}{S_0(u - d)}, \qquad B = e^{-rh}\,\frac{u\,V_d - d\,V_u}{u - d} $$ $$ \boxed{\;\text{Option price} = V_0 = \Delta S_0 + B\;}$$

$h$ — length of the period (years); $u, d$ — up/down multiplicative factors ($S_u=S_0u,\ S_d=S_0d$); $V_u, V_d$ — option payoffs in the up/down state; $\delta$ — dividend yield ($\delta = 0$ if none, the case the exam emphasizes).

One-period binomial — risk-neutral probability

Writing $p^{*}$ for the risk-neutral probability of an up-move (equivalently $1-q$ in the down-move convention some texts use):

$$ p^{*} = \frac{e^{(r-\delta)h} - d}{u - d}, \qquad q = 1-p^{*} = \frac{u - e^{(r-\delta)h}}{u - d} $$ $$ \boxed{\;\text{Option price} = e^{-rh}\,E^Q[V_h] = e^{-rh}\big[\,p^{*} V_u + (1-p^{*}) V_d\,\big]\;}$$
Two conventions — same price

Coaching-Actuaries writes $q=\dfrac{u-e^{(r-\delta)h}}{u-d}$ for the down-move probability, so its formula reads $V_0=e^{-rh}[(1-q)V_u+qV_d]$. That is identical to the up-probability $p^{*}=1-q$ above. Whichever you use, weight $V_u$ by the up-probability and $V_d$ by the down-probability and discount at $r$.

For arbitrage-free pricing we need $d < e^{(r-\delta)h} < u$, which guarantees $0 < p^* < 1$.

Multi-period binomial

Build a recombining tree: after $n$ periods the stock can be $S_0 u^{j} d^{\,n-j}$ for $j = 0,\dots,n$. Work backwards from expiry, applying the one-period risk-neutral formula at each node:

$$ V_{\text{node}} = e^{-rh}\big[\,p^{*}V_{\text{up}} + (1-p^{*})V_{\text{down}}\,\big] $$

European shortcut — let $k$ be the number of down-jumps to an ending node; the probability of reaching it is binomial in the down-move probability $q=1-p^{*}$:

$$ \Pr(k\text{ down jumps}) = \binom{n}{k} q^{k}(1-q)^{\,n-k}, \quad k = 0,1,\dots,n $$ $$ V_0 = e^{-rnh}\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} q^{k}(1-q)^{\,n-k}\, V_{(n-k\ \text{up moves})} $$

For an American option, at each node take the larger of the continuation value and the immediate-exercise value:

$$ V_{\text{node}} = \max\big(\text{exercise value},\; e^{-rh}[p^{*}V_{\text{up}} + (1-p^{*})V_{\text{down}}]\big) $$

Forward (Cox-Ross-Rubinstein style) tree factors often given on the exam:

$$ u = e^{(r-\delta)h + \sigma\sqrt{h}}, \qquad d = e^{(r-\delta)h - \sigma\sqrt{h}} $$

with this choice, $p^{*} = \dfrac{1}{1+e^{\sigma\sqrt{h}}}$.

Lognormal model — real-world probabilities

Accumulation factor & log-return ($P$-measure) $$ \frac{S_{t+\tau}}{S_t}\sim \mathrm{LogN}(\mu\tau,\ \sigma^2\tau), \qquad \ln\!\frac{S_{t+\tau}}{S_t}\sim N(\mu\tau,\ \sigma^2\tau) $$ Probability the future price is below $K$ $$ \Pr[S_{t+\tau}\lt K] = \Pr\!\left[Z < \frac{\ln(K/S_t) - \mu\tau}{\sigma\sqrt{\tau}}\right] = N\!\left(\frac{\ln(K/S_t)-\mu\tau}{\sigma\sqrt{\tau}}\right) $$

$\mu$ — annual expected (continuously-compounded) return; $\sigma$ — annual volatility; $Z\sim N(0,1)$. Use $1-N(\cdot)$ for $\Pr[S_{t+\tau}>K]$. This is a real-world ($P$) calculation — it uses $\mu$, not $r$.

Black–Scholes (European options)

Stock with continuous dividend yield $\delta$ ($N=$ standard normal CDF):

$$ C = S_0 e^{-\delta T} N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2) $$ $$ P = K e^{-rT} N(-d_2) - S_0 e^{-\delta T} N(-d_1) $$ $$ d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + \big(r - \delta + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^{2}\big)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T} $$
  • $\sigma$ — annual volatility of the stock's continuously-compounded return; all other symbols as above. Set $\delta = 0$ for a non-dividend stock.
  • Call delta $= e^{-\delta T} N(d_1)$; put delta $= -e^{-\delta T} N(-d_1)$.
  • BS satisfies put–call parity automatically, so you can always get the put from the call.
Delta-hedge / replicating portfolio (non-dividend) $$ \text{Call: } \Delta = N(d_1),\ B = -Ke^{-rT}N(d_2) \;\Rightarrow\; \text{buy } N(d_1)\text{ shares, borrow } Ke^{-rT}N(d_2) $$ $$ \text{Put: } \Delta = -N(-d_1),\ B = Ke^{-rT}N(-d_2) \;\Rightarrow\; \text{short } N(-d_1)\text{ shares, lend } Ke^{-rT}N(-d_2) $$

Both BSM portfolios are self-financing, mirroring the multi-period binomial hedge.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Short-sale payoff ▾

A non-dividend stock trades at $\$100$; the continuously-compounded risk-free rate is $8\%$. An insurer short-sells one share and invests all proceeds in a 6-month risk-free bond. Find the payoff if the stock is $\$110$ in six months.

Solution. Bond proceeds: $100e^{0.08(0.5)} = 104.08$. Buying back the share to close the short: $-110$.

$$ \text{Total payoff} = 104.08 - 110 = -5.92 $$
Answer
A loss of $\boxed{\$5.92}$. (The short bet lost because the stock rose.)
Example 2 Moneyness of a call ▾

For a long call with strike $K=100$, classify the moneyness when the underlying is $80$, $100$, and $120$.

  • $S=80$: $80-100=-20<0$ → out-of-the-money.
  • $S=100$: $100-100=0$ → at-the-money.
  • $S=120$: $120-100=+20>0$ → in-the-money.
Answer
OTM, ATM, ITM respectively. (Classify by the would-be payoff, ignoring exercise style.)
Example 3 Embedded option in a guarantee ▾

The PB&J 300 Index ($S_0=2500$, no dividends) has historically returned $10\%$/yr. An insurer guarantees a customer she will earn at least $10\%$/yr over two years on the index, and plans to offset the risk with one share plus a put. Available 2-year European puts:

StrikePut price
$2500$$130.77$
$2750$$223.78$
$3025$$357.94$

Step 1 — Identify the guarantee. Guaranteed payoff $=\max\!\big(S_2,\ 2500\times1.10^2\big)=\max(S_2,3025)$.

Step 2 — Decompose. $\max(S_2,3025)=\max(3025-S_2,0)+S_2$ = a 2-year put struck at $K=3025$ plus one share.

Step 3 — Cost. Buy the $3025$-strike put ($357.94$) and one share of the index ($2500$):

$$ \text{Cost} = S_0 + p(0) = 2500 + 357.94 = 2{,}857.94 $$
Answer
Time-0 cost of the hedge $=\boxed{\$2{,}857.94}$. The embedded put is what makes the guarantee hedgeable.
Example 4 Put–call parity to back out $r$ ▾

Non-dividend stock at $\$105$. A 6-month $\$100$-strike European call costs $\$9.88$; the matching put costs $\$2.90$. Find the continuously-compounded risk-free rate.

Solution. Parity $C-P=S_0-Ke^{-rT}$:

$$ 9.88-2.90 = 105 - 100e^{-r(0.5)} \;\Rightarrow\; 98.02 = 100e^{-0.5r} \;\Rightarrow\; r = -2\ln(0.9802) = 0.04 $$
Answer
$\boxed{r = 4\%}$. (A call & put on the same underlying/strike/expiry is your cue to reach for parity.)
Example 5 Put–call parity to price the put ▾

A non-dividend-paying stock trades at $S_0 = 50$. A 1-year European call with strike $K=52$ costs $C = 4.50$. The continuously-compounded risk-free rate is $r = 6\%$. Find the price of the 1-year European put with the same strike.

Compute $Ke^{-rT} = 52\,e^{-0.06(1)} = 52(0.941765) = 48.9718$.

$$ P = C - S_0 + Ke^{-rT} = 4.50 - 50 + 48.9718 = 3.4718 $$
Answer
$\boxed{P \approx 3.47}$
Example 6 One-period binomial: replicating portfolio for a put ▾

$S_0=100$ (no dividends), in 6 months the stock is $110$ or $90$, $r=6\%$. Price an at-the-money put ($K=100$) by replication.

Factors: $u=110/100=1.1$, $d=90/100=0.9$, $h=0.5$. Payoffs: $V_u=\max(100-110,0)=0$, $V_d=\max(100-90,0)=10$.

$$ \Delta = \frac{V_u-V_d}{S_0(u-d)} = \frac{0-10}{100(0.2)} = -0.50 \quad(\text{negative}\Rightarrow\text{short-sell }0.5\text{ shares}) $$ $$ B = e^{-rh}\frac{uV_d - dV_u}{u-d} = e^{-0.06(0.5)}\frac{1.1(10)-0.9(0)}{0.2} = e^{-0.03}(55) = 53.3745 \quad(\text{positive}\Rightarrow\text{lend}) $$ $$ V_0 = \Delta S_0 + B = -0.5(100) + 53.3745 = 3.37 $$
Answer
$\boxed{P \approx 3.37}$. Sign check matches the table: put → short stock, lend cash.
Example 7 One-period binomial (both methods agree) ▾

A stock with $S_0 = 100$ pays no dividends. Over the next $h = 1$ year it moves to either $S_u = 120$ ($u=1.2$) or $S_d = 90$ ($d=0.9$). The risk-free rate is $r = 5\%$ (continuous). Price a 1-year European call with strike $K = 105$.

Step 1 — Payoffs. $V_u = \max(120-105,0) = 15$, $\quad V_d = \max(90-105,0) = 0$.

Step 2 — Risk-neutral (up) probability.

$$ p^{*} = \frac{e^{(0.05)(1)} - 0.9}{1.2 - 0.9} = \frac{1.051271 - 0.9}{0.3} = \frac{0.151271}{0.3} = 0.504237 $$

Step 3 — Price (risk-neutral).

$$ C = e^{-0.05}\big[0.504237(15) + 0.495763(0)\big] = 0.951229 \times 7.56356 = 7.1949 $$

Check via replicating portfolio.

$$ \Delta = \frac{V_u - V_d}{S_0(u-d)} = \frac{15-0}{100(0.3)} = 0.5 $$ $$ B = e^{-rh}\frac{uV_d - dV_u}{u-d} = e^{-0.05}\frac{(1.2)(0)-(0.9)(15)}{0.3} = 0.951229\times(-45) = -42.8053 $$ $$ \Delta S_0 + B = 0.5(100) - 42.8053 = 7.1947 $$

The two methods agree (rounding). The replicating portfolio is "buy 0.5 shares, borrow 42.81."

Answer
$\boxed{C \approx 7.19}$, with $\Delta = 0.5$.
Example 8 Two-period binomial via the binomial shortcut (European call) ▾

$S_0=100$ (no dividends), each year $u=1.05$ or $d=0.90$, $r=3\%$, $h=1$. Price a 2-year $K=100$ European call.

Down-move probability: $q=\dfrac{u-e^{rh}}{u-d}=\dfrac{1.05-e^{0.03}}{0.15}=0.13030$, so $1-q=0.86970$.

Terminal prices & payoffs: $S_{uu}=110.25\to10.25$; $S_{ud}=94.50\to0$; $S_{dd}=81\to0$.

Node probabilities ($n=2$): $\Pr(0\text{ down})=(1-q)^2=0.75637$, $\Pr(1)=2q(1-q)=0.22665$, $\Pr(2)=q^2=0.01698$.

$$ V_0 = e^{-0.03(2)}\big[0.75637(10.25) + 0.22665(0) + 0.01698(0)\big] = 7.30133 $$
Answer
$\boxed{C \approx 7.30}$ — identical to working node-by-node, but faster for European options.
Example 9 Two-period binomial (American put, early exercise matters) ▾

$S_0 = 100$, $u = 1.10$, $d = 0.90$, $r = 4\%$ continuous, each period $h = 1$ year, no dividends. Price a 2-year American put with strike $K = 105$.

Step 1 — Risk-neutral probability.

$$ p^{*} = \frac{e^{0.04} - 0.9}{1.10 - 0.90} = \frac{1.040811 - 0.9}{0.2} = \frac{0.140811}{0.2} = 0.704054 $$

So $1-p^{*} = 0.295946$ and discount factor $e^{-0.04} = 0.960789$.

Step 2 — Stock tree.

  • $S_{uu} = 100(1.1)^2 = 121$
  • $S_{ud} = 100(1.1)(0.9) = 99$
  • $S_{dd} = 100(0.9)^2 = 81$
  • Period-1: $S_u = 110$, $S_d = 90$.

Step 3 — Terminal payoffs $\max(105 - S,0)$:

  • $P_{uu} = \max(105-121,0) = 0$
  • $P_{ud} = \max(105-99,0) = 6$
  • $P_{dd} = \max(105-81,0) = 24$

Step 4 — Node $S_u = 110$. Continuation $= e^{-0.04}[0.704054(0) + 0.295946(6)] = 0.960789(1.775676) = 1.7061$. Exercise $= \max(105-110,0) = 0$. Take max $\Rightarrow P_u = 1.7061$.

Step 5 — Node $S_d = 90$. Continuation $= e^{-0.04}[0.704054(6) + 0.295946(24)] = 0.960789[4.224324 + 7.102704] = 0.960789(11.327028) = 10.8829$. Exercise $= \max(105-90,0) = 15$. Since $15 > 10.8829$, exercise early $\Rightarrow P_d = 15$.

Step 6 — Root node. Continuation $= e^{-0.04}[0.704054(1.7061) + 0.295946(15)] = 0.960789[1.201288 + 4.439190] = 0.960789(5.640478) = 5.4194$. Exercise $= \max(105-100,0) = 5$. Since $5.4194 > 5$, hold $\Rightarrow$ price $= 5.4194$.

Answer
$\boxed{P_{\text{American}} \approx 5.42}$. (Early exercise at the down node adds value; the European version would be worth less.)
Example 10 Lognormal: real-world exercise probability ▾

An at-the-money 2-year European put, $S_0=100$, no dividends; the stock is lognormal with $\mu=0.10$, $\sigma^2=0.09$. Find the real-world probability the put is exercised.

The put is exercised iff $S_2\lt K=100$. Use $\mu\tau=0.10(2)=0.20$, $\sigma\sqrt{\tau}=0.30\sqrt{2}=0.424264$.

$$ \Pr[S_2<100] = N\!\left(\frac{\ln(100/100)-0.20}{0.424264}\right) = N(-0.47140) = 0.31868 $$
Answer
$\boxed{\approx 0.3187}$. Note this uses $\mu$ (real world), not $r$ — it is not a pricing calculation.
Example 11 Black–Scholes call & put ▾

6-month $K=120$ European options on a non-dividend stock: $S_0=100$, $r=5\%$, $\sigma=30\%$, $\delta=0$, $T=0.5$.

$$ d_1=\frac{\ln(100/120)+(0.05+\tfrac12(0.3)^2)(0.5)}{0.3\sqrt{0.5}}=-0.63555, \qquad d_2=d_1-0.3\sqrt{0.5}=-0.84769 $$

$N(-0.63555)=0.26253$, $N(-0.84769)=0.19831$.

$$ C = 100(0.26253) - 120e^{-0.05(0.5)}(0.19831) = 3.04 $$

Put via parity (fastest): $P = C - S_0 + Ke^{-rT} = 3.04 - 100 + 120e^{-0.025} = 20.08$ (or directly with $N(0.63555),N(0.84769)$).

Answer
$\boxed{C \approx 3.04,\ P \approx 20.08}$.
Example 12 Black–Scholes delta-hedge for a written call ▾

An insurer shorts 1,000 one-year $K=200$ European calls on a non-dividend stock: $S_0=200$, $r=3\%$, $\sigma=50\%$. Build the delta-hedge.

$$ d_1=\frac{\ln(200/200)+(0.03+\tfrac12(0.5)^2)(1)}{0.5\sqrt1}=0.31, \qquad d_2=0.31-0.5=-0.19 $$

$N(0.31)=0.62172$, $N(-0.19)=0.42465$. Per call: buy $\Delta=0.62172$ shares and borrow $B=200e^{-0.03}(0.42465)=82.41994$.

Scale to 1,000 written calls (the insurer buys the replicating portfolio to offset the short):

$$ \text{Shares} = 1000(0.62172) = 621.72, \qquad \text{Borrow} = 1000(82.41994) = \$82{,}419.94 $$
Answer
Buy $\boxed{621.72}$ shares and borrow $\boxed{\$82{,}419.94}$ at the risk-free rate.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  1. Confusing payoff with profit. Payoff ignores the premium; profit subtracts the accumulated premium $e^{rT}\times$ premium. Read the question's word carefully.
  2. Confusing moneyness with whether you'd exercise. Moneyness pretends the option is exercised regardless of sign (so an OTM call has a "negative" would-be payoff); the actual payoff is still floored at $0$.
  3. Mixing up real-world and risk-neutral. Pricing uses the risk-neutral $p^{*}$ and discounts at $r$. Real-world probabilities $\Pr[S_T\lt K]$ use the drift $\mu$ — never put $r$ into a lognormal probability question, and never put $\mu$ into a pricing question.
  4. Up vs. down convention for the risk-neutral probability. Coaching-Actuaries' $q=\frac{u-e^{rh}}{u-d}$ is the down-move probability; the up-probability is $p^{*}=\frac{e^{rh}-d}{u-d}$. Make sure $V_u$ is weighted by the up-probability.
  5. Forgetting dividends in $p^{*}$ and in $\Delta$. With a dividend yield $\delta$, the risk-neutral probability uses $e^{(r-\delta)h}$ and delta carries a factor $e^{-\delta h}$. Dropping these is the most common binomial error.
  6. $u$ and $d$ as factors vs. returns. $u = 1.2$ means multiply by 1.2; "$\sigma\sqrt h$ style" means $u=e^{(r-\delta)h+\sigma\sqrt h}$. Confirm the convention. Also: $d$ need not be below $1$ (a stock can have two "up-ish" states and still satisfy no-arbitrage).
  7. Misreading replicating-portfolio signs. $\Delta>0$ buy / $\Delta<0$ short; $B>0$ lend / $B<0$ borrow. Call → $(+\Delta,-B)$; put → $(-\Delta,+B)$.
  8. Sign / direction in put–call parity. It is $C - P = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$ (call minus put). Memorize the LHS order. For dividends, subtract their PV from $S_0$ (or use $S_0e^{-\delta T}$), never add. Parity is European only.
  9. Skipping the early-exercise check on American options. At every node take $\max(\text{exercise}, \text{continuation})$. American calls on non-dividend stocks are never exercised early (so they equal European), but American puts frequently are — and FAM loves to test exactly this.
  10. Using the European shortcut on American options. The binomial-probability formula assumes no early exercise — for American options you must go node-by-node.
  11. $d_2$ sign and the put formula. $d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt T$ (subtract). For the put, use $N(-d_1)$ and $N(-d_2)$, or just compute the call and apply put–call parity.
  12. Discounting at the wrong rate. Binomial and BS both discount at the risk-free rate $r$, never at an expected stock return.
  13. Rounding $N(\cdot)$ too early. Carry $d_1, d_2$ to 4+ decimals before looking up the normal CDF; small errors blow up in the final price.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

State the three steps of short-selling and the payoff of a short stock.
(1) Borrow shares from a lender; (2) sell them now and invest the proceeds at $r$; (3) buy the shares back later and return them. The short-stock payoff is $-S_T$ — the opposite of a long stock's $+S_T$.
An option is in-the-money. What does that tell you about its would-be payoff, and is "in-the-money" the same as "will be exercised"?
ITM means the immediate (would-be) payoff is positive — for a call $S>K$, for a put $S\lt K$. It is a classification snapshot ignoring exercise style; the actual payoff is floored at $0$, so an OTM option simply isn't exercised.
How is a call "insurance," and how do you rewrite a "get at least your money back" guarantee as an option plus a share?
A call caps the price you pay for the asset at $K$ — insurance against a price increase (a put insures a decrease). Using $\max(S_0,S_T)=\max(S_0-S_T,0)+S_T$, the guarantee = one share of the asset plus a long put struck at the floor $K=S_0$ (an embedded option the insurer can buy to hedge).
A long call and a short put on the same stock, strike, and expiry together replicate what simple position, and what principle turns that into put–call parity?
A long forward to buy the stock at strike $K$ (payoff $S_T - K$), which also equals long stock + short bond. The law of one price (no arbitrage) equates their costs, giving $C - P = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$.
Write the one-period binomial $\Delta$ and $B$ for a call, then state whether you buy/short stock and lend/borrow cash for a call vs. a put.
$\Delta=\dfrac{V_u-V_d}{S_0(u-d)}$, $\;B=e^{-rh}\dfrac{uV_d-dV_u}{u-d}$. Call: $\Delta>0$ (buy stock), $B<0$ (borrow). Put: $\Delta<0$ (short stock), $B>0$ (lend).
Write the risk-neutral probability $p^{*}$ on a stock paying continuous dividend yield $\delta$, the no-arbitrage condition, and say what happens to a stock priced under the risk-neutral measure.
$p^{*} = \dfrac{e^{(r-\delta)h} - d}{u - d}$, valid when $d\lt e^{(r-\delta)h}\lt u$ (so $0\lt p^{*}<1$). Discounting the stock's risk-neutral expected price at $r$ returns $S_0$ exactly — every asset earns $r$ on average under $Q$.
True or false: a higher real-world probability of an up-move raises the price of a European call in the binomial model.
False. Real-world probabilities never enter the option price; only the risk-neutral $p^{*}$ (a function of $u, d, r, \delta, h$) does.
For a recombining $n$-period tree, what is the probability of reaching the node with $k$ down-jumps, and when can you NOT use this shortcut?
$\binom{n}{k}q^k(1-q)^{n-k}$ where $q=1-p^{*}$ is the down-move probability. It works for European options only; American options require node-by-node backward induction so you can test early exercise at each node.
For a non-dividend stock, why is an American call worth the same as the otherwise-identical European call?
Early exercise of a call forfeits the time value and the interest you could earn on the strike; with no dividends there is never a benefit to exercising early, so the early-exercise feature is worthless and the two prices coincide. (American puts often do benefit.)
State the distribution of the log-return and the formula for $\Pr[S_{t+\tau}\lt K]$. Which rate goes in it?
$\ln(S_{t+\tau}/S_t)\sim N(\mu\tau,\sigma^2\tau)$, so $\Pr[S_{t+\tau}\lt K]=N\!\big(\frac{\ln(K/S_t)-\mu\tau}{\sigma\sqrt{\tau}}\big)$. It uses the real-world drift $\mu$, not $r$.
In Black–Scholes, what does $N(d_2)$ represent, and which input shifts the call price the most for an at-the-money option?
$N(d_2)$ is the risk-neutral probability the call finishes in the money ($S_T > K$). Near at-the-money, the call value is most sensitive to volatility $\sigma$ (and to time $T$), since both increase the chance and magnitude of finishing in the money.
An insurer writes calls and wants to be hedged. Under BS, what does it buy and borrow per written call?
It replicates a long call: buy $N(d_1)$ shares and borrow $Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ per call. This self-financing delta-hedge offsets the short-call risk; multiply by the number of calls written.
