Pricing / Ratemaking for Short-Term Insurance

Ratemaking is prospective: take historical losses and premium, then aggregate, develop, trend, and on-level them forward so today's rate is adequate but not excessive. Master the data-prep pipeline and the two rate-change formulas drop out of one equation.

Reference framework: Werner & Modlin / Friedland, Fundamentals of General Insurance Ratemaking and Estimating Unpaid Claims Using Basic Techniques (the SOA FAM short-term ratemaking syllabus). Severity/frequency notation follows Klugman/Panjer/Willmot, Loss Models: From Data to Decisions. Learning Objectives

After studying this note, for the FAM exam you should be able to:

  • State and apply the fundamental insurance equation and explain what it means for a rate to be adequate, not excessive, and not unfairly discriminatory; list the essential and non-essential objectives of ratemaking.
  • Choose an appropriate exposure base and list the features of a good one.
  • Aggregate losses by calendar year, accident year, and policy year, and compute incurred losses (paid + change in reserves vs paid + reserves at valuation).
  • Develop aggregated losses to ultimate via age-to-age (link) ratios / chain ladder, rearranging calendar-year triangles into development-year form.
  • Trend losses: find the trend period from the average loss date of the experience period to the average loss date of the expected effective period (handling AY vs PY data and the "written-only-on-effective-date" case), and apply effective or continuously-compounded trend, including creative linear/log-slope forms.
  • Separate variable vs fixed expenses, classify ALAE/ULAE and DCC/AAO, handle profit implicitly or explicitly, and compute the permissible loss ratio (PLR).
  • Aggregate premium (CY vs PY) into written/earned/unearned, using the UEP recursion.
  • Bring premium to current rate level by the extension of exposures method (base rate × relativities) and the parallelogram method (5-step on-level factor), including non-annual policy terms.
  • Compute the indicated overall average rate (pure premium / loss cost method) and the indicated average rate change (loss ratio method), and reconcile the two from the single solved fundamental equation.
  • Apply credibility ($Z$) to blend an experience-based indication with a complement of credibility.

Key Concepts

1. What ratemaking is — and its objectives

Ratemaking is the determination of the rates/premiums to charge. Unlike a tangible product, the cost of an insurance policy is not known when it is sold, so the rate must be set prospectively: adequate to pay future losses and expenses and to generate profit on policies that will be written in a future period and earned over the policy term after that. Because we are predicting the future, we take historical data and adjust it forward. Four big adjustments recur:

  1. Aggregate losses and premium into comparable annual buckets (CY / AY / PY).
  2. Develop losses to ultimate (immature accident/policy periods are still growing).
  3. Trend losses to the future cost level (inflation, frequency/severity drift).
  4. On-level premium to the current rate level (old premiums reflect old, superseded rates).
Objectives of ratemaking

Essential: rates must (1) cover expected losses & expenses (income — premium plus investment income — must equal or exceed expected costs, per class and for the whole book); (2) make provision for contingencies (unexpected costs); (3) encourage loss control (safe-driver/good-student discounts); (4) satisfy regulators — be adequate, not excessive, not unfairly discriminatory, supported by data and documentation.

Non-essential (desirable): rates should be reasonably stable (reinsurance helps spread large losses), reasonably responsive to change, and simple/easy to understand.

The exam syllabus is the manual rating approach: risks with similar characteristics are grouped, and rates are based on the group's estimated future losses — not on an individual insured's experience. We compute the indicated overall average rate / rate change (the word "overall" is implied and usually dropped). "Indicated" means "as indicated by the data."

2. The fundamental insurance equation

The premium must cover everything plus a target profit:

$$\text{Premium} = \text{Losses} + \text{Expenses} + \text{Profit}\;\Longrightarrow\; P = L + (V\!\cdot\!P + E_F) + Q_T\!\cdot\!P.$$
  • $L$ here includes LAE (loss adjustment expense) — we group "losses and LAE" together throughout.
  • $V\!\cdot\!P$ = variable expense (a fraction $V$ of premium); $E_F$ = fixed expense (dollars, not a fraction of premium); $Q_T\!\cdot\!P$ = profit & contingency loading.
  • When the equation balances at the proposed rate, the rate is just adequate. Short → raise rates; surplus → can lower.

3. Exposure — the basic unit of risk

The exposure base measures the amount of risk; rates are quoted per unit of exposure. A good exposure base should: accurately measure quantitative exposure to loss; be easy to determine at quoting; not be easily manipulated by the insured; be simple to record/administer; be easily understood by the policyholder; and ideally auto-adjust to inflation. Examples: car-years (auto), payroll (workers' comp), per \$1,000 of home value (homeowners). So "home value" is a good base; "cubic millimetres of closet space" is not.

Example: a \$250,000 home rated at \$4.80 per \$1,000 of value → exposure base = home value, unit = \$1,000 of value, premium $=\frac{250{,}000}{1{,}000}\times 4.80 = \$1{,}200$. A one-year, one-car auto policy = 1 car-year; a three-year, two-car policy = 6 exposure units.

4. Aggregating losses: calendar / accident / policy year

Loss data are aggregated into 12-month buckets. (A fourth method, report year, is for claims-made policies and is not on this exam.) The 12-month window need not be Jan–Dec for AY/PY, but assume Jan 1–Dec 31 unless told otherwise.

  • Calendar year (CY): all loss transactions that occur Jan 1–Dec 31, regardless of accident or report date. CY figures are final at year-end (later development falls into the next CY).
  • Accident year (AY): all transactions for accidents occurring in the 12-month window, regardless of policy issue or report date. AY losses keep developing, so a valuation date is required (it plays the role of the development age in chain ladder).
  • Policy year (PY): all transactions for policies issued in the 12-month window, regardless of accident date. Also needs a valuation date.

Incurred-loss formulas. For calendar year $i$ (loss is the change across the year):

CY incurred $$L_i^{I} = L_i^{P} + R_i - R_{i-1},$$

where $L_i^{P}$ = paid in CY $i$ and $R_i$ = reserves at the end of CY $i$. For accident year / policy year at a chosen valuation date:

AY / PY incurred (at a valuation date) $$L_i^{I} = L_i^{P} + R_i,$$

where $L_i^{P}$ = cumulative paid as of the valuation date on that accident (or policy) year and $R_i$ = reserves as of that valuation date. (No subtraction of a prior reserve — AY/PY accumulate from the start of the cohort.)

Which to use, and what matches what

For ratemaking, AY and PY are the usual choices: AY is more responsive (recent data), PY is more complete (whole policy term) but less responsive (more outdated). CY is generally not used in ratemaking — premiums and losses are mismatched in timing. Matching rule: CY earned premium ↔ AY losses; PY earned premium ↔ PY losses.

5. Developing losses

Aggregated AY/PY losses are immature — more claims will be reported and known claims will change. Use age-to-age (link) ratios and the chain-ladder method (Topic 7) to project to ultimate. A practical step that often appears: the data may be given by calendar valuation date, so first rearrange the triangle into development-year form before computing link ratios. Since the same factors estimate reserves, pricing actuaries consult reserving actuaries when developing losses.

6. Trending losses

Even at ultimate, past losses reflect past cost levels. Trend projects the cost level from the experience (historical) period to the expected effective (forecast) period. Two ingredients: the trend period and the trend factor.

Trend period = (average loss occurrence date of the forecast period) − (average loss occurrence date of the experience period). The "average loss occurrence date" is the midpoint of each period.

  • Experience period (AY data): a one-year window, so the midpoint is the middle of the year (e.g. AY2015 → Jul 1, 2015).
  • Experience period (PY data): length $= 1 + (\text{policy term})$ years, because the last policy written must still run out its term. Annual policies, PY2015 → Jan 1, 2015 to Dec 31, 2016 (2 yrs), midpoint Jan 1, 2016. Six-month policies, PY2015 → Jan 1, 2015 to Jun 30, 2016 (18 mo), midpoint Oct 1, 2015.
  • Expected effective (forecast) period: begins on the rates' effective date; length $= (\text{policy term}) + (\text{time the rates are in effect})$. Annual policies effective for one year → a 2-year window; effective Oct 1, 2018 → Oct 1, 2018 to Sep 30, 2020, midpoint Oct 1, 2019.
Special case — policies written only on the effective date

The default assumption is uniform writing through the year. But if policies are written only on the effective date, they all expire together, so the forecast period is only as long as the policy term, and its midpoint moves earlier. (Same logic shortens a PY experience window in that case.)

Overlap fallacy (Coach's Remark): a common myth says developing and trending double-counts inflation. It does not. Development brings immature losses up to their ultimate value; trend moves the ultimate value from the experience cost level to the forecast cost level. They are two independent steps, both required.

You can equivalently trend pure premium (loss ÷ exposures) instead of losses — identical result if exposures are consistent. (Pure premium ≠ premium.)

7. Expenses, LAE classification, and profit

Loss adjustment expense (LAE) is the cost of settling claims and is grouped with losses, not with other expenses:

  • ALAE — allocated to a specific claim (e.g. a lawyer's fee). ULAE — overhead not tied to one claim (e.g. claims-manager salary).
  • A U.S. reporting refinement: DCC (defense & cost containment — claim-defense costs) is generally treated as ALAE; AAO (adjusting & other) is generally treated as ULAE.

Other (underwriting) expenses split into:

  • Variable expenses $E_V$ — scale with premium (commissions, brokerage, premium taxes, licenses): a % of premium, $V = E_V/P$.
  • Fixed expenses $E_F$ — roughly constant per exposure/policy (salaries outside claims, general overhead): a dollar amount, with fixed ratio $F = E_F/P$.

So the expense provision is $\text{Expenses}=E_V+E_F = V\!\cdot\!P + E_F$.

Profit can be loaded implicitly (conservative estimates for loss/premium/expense, so actual beats expected on average) or explicitly (add $Q_T\!\cdot\!P$, with $Q_T$ the target profit & contingencies %). The permissible (expected) loss ratio is the share of premium left for losses + LAE after variable expense and profit:

Permissible loss ratio $$\text{PLR} = 1 - V - Q_T.$$

Fixed expenses are accounted for separately (in the numerator), not inside the PLR.

8. Aggregating premium (written / earned / unearned)

Premium is aggregated by calendar year (premiums of all active policies in the CY) or policy year (premiums of policies issued in the window). Three definitions:

  • Written premium $P^{W}$ — full premium for all policies issued in the period (a "cash register" concept, recorded at sale).
  • Earned premium $P^{E}$ — the portion for coverage that has already elapsed (an "accrual" concept; earned pro rata over the term). Losses are matched to earned premium.
  • Unearned premium $P^{U}$ — the not-yet-earned remainder, $P^U = P^W - P^E$ at a point in time (a liability — refunded on cancellation).

The "point in time" is the end of the calendar year for CY aggregation, or the valuation date for PY. A handy roll-forward for total CY unearned premium:

UEP recursion $$P_i^{U} = P_i^{W} - P_i^{E} + P_{i-1}^{U}.$$

The same written/earned/unearned logic applies to exposures; ratemaking uses earned exposures to match premium and losses.

9. Premium at current rate level — two methods

Historical premium was charged at old rates; to use it we restate it at today's rates (the current rate level, CRL) — analogous to developing losses to ultimate. Two approaches:

(a) Extension of exposures — the most accurate. Take every historical policy's actual rating characteristics and recompute its premium at current rates. Each rating variable has a base cell (the most common class, relativity $1.00$) with relativities (multiplicative differentials); premium $=$ current base rate $\times$ relativities. The drawback: it needs granular historical rating data that companies often lack.

Mini-example: base rate $100$, gender {Male $1.00$, Female $0.80$}, age {Under 25 $0.90$, 25–65 $1.00$, Over 65 $1.15$}. A female over 65 is charged $100(0.80)(1.15)=92$. To on-level an old policy, recompute with the current base rate and current relativities.

(b) Parallelogram method — for aggregate premium when you lack granular data. It builds on-level factors from average rate changes, assuming policies are written uniformly through the year (so it is less accurate than extension of exposures). The diagram: x-axis = time, y-axis = % of policies earned; diagonal lines (ending one policy-term after each effective date) separate rate-level groups; each unit square is a calendar year.

Parallelogram method — the 5 steps
  1. Determine the timing and amount of each rate change; draw the diagram.
  2. Compute the cumulative rate level index of each group (product of $1+\text{change}$ factors).
  3. For each year, compute the portion of premium in each rate group via the geometry (triangle $\tfrac12 bh$, trapezoid $\tfrac12(b_1+b_2)h$, parallelogram $bh$).
  4. Compute the year's weighted-average cumulative rate level index (portions × indices).
  5. On-level factor $=\dfrac{\text{current cumulative rate level index}}{\text{year's average index}}$; multiply earned premium by it.

For non-annual policies the diagonals end after the policy term, not one year (semi-annual → twice as steep, rate change fully in effect after 6 months).

10. Indicated rate vs indicated rate change — one equation, two methods

Solve the fundamental equation for the indicated premium $P_I$ for the forecast period:

$$P_I = L + V P_I + E_F + Q_T P_I \;\Longrightarrow\; P_I = \frac{L + E_F}{\,1 - V - Q_T\,}.$$

The denominator is exactly the PLR. Both ratemaking methods are this one identity, divided by something:

  • Loss ratio method — divide by premium at current rate level $P^{C}$: produces an indicated rate change (a multiplier on the current rate). Needs on-level premium but not exposures; the common method.
  • Pure premium (loss cost) method — divide by exposures $n_e$: produces an indicated rate (level) in dollars per exposure. Needs well-defined exposures; used for new programs / no existing rate.

They are algebraically identical only when consistent data/assumptions are used. If the on-level premium comes from the parallelogram method, the two will generally disagree slightly (parallelogram uses average factors, not exact extension of exposures).

11. Credibility

Experience for a small segment is volatile, so we blend it with a complement of credibility (last year's rate, a trended overall indication, or an industry/competitor rate):

$$\text{Final} = Z \times (\text{Experience indication}) + (1-Z)\times(\text{Complement}),\qquad Z=\min\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{n}{n_{\text{full}}}},\,1\right),$$

with $n$ = observed exposures/claims, $n_{\text{full}}$ = full-credibility standard.

Formulas

Fundamental equation, solved

$$P_I=\frac{L+E_F}{1-V-Q_T},\qquad \text{denominator}=\text{PLR}=1-V-Q_T.$$

Loss aggregation (incurred)

$$\underbrace{L_i^{I}=L_i^{P}+R_i-R_{i-1}}_{\text{calendar year}}\qquad\qquad \underbrace{L_i^{I}=L_i^{P}+R_i}_{\text{accident / policy year, at valuation}}$$

Loss development (chain ladder)

$$f_{j\to j+1}=\frac{\sum \text{losses at age }(j{+}1)}{\sum \text{losses at age } j}\;(\text{matched years}),\quad \text{CDF}_j=\prod_{k\ge j} f_k,\quad \widehat{L}^{\text{ult}}=(\text{loss at age }j)\times\text{CDF}_j.$$

Trend

$$\widehat{L}^{\text{ult}}_{\text{trended}}=L^{\text{ult}}\times(1+t)^{\,n}\quad(\text{effective}),\qquad \widehat{L}^{\text{ult}}_{\text{trended}}=L^{\text{ult}}\, e^{\,r n}\quad(\text{continuous}),$$

$n$ = trend period in years (avg loss date, forecast − avg loss date, experience).

Creative trend forms (Coach's Remark) $$\text{linear slope }m:\;\; \widehat L = L + m\,n;\qquad \text{slope }m\text{ on }\ln L:\;\; \widehat L = L\, e^{\,m n}.$$

Premium aggregation & on-leveling

$$P^{U}=P^{W}-P^{E},\qquad P_i^{U}=P_i^{W}-P_i^{E}+P_{i-1}^{U}.$$ $$\text{On-Level Factor}_y=\frac{\text{current cumulative rate level index}}{\text{year's avg cumulative rate level index}},\qquad P^{C}_y = P^{E}_y\times\text{On-Level Factor}_y.$$

Extension of exposures: $\;\text{on-level premium} = (\text{current base rate})\times\prod(\text{current relativities}).$

Expenses, PLR

$$V=\frac{E_V}{P},\qquad F=\frac{E_F}{P},\qquad \text{Expenses}=V P+E_F,\qquad \text{PLR}=1-V-Q_T.$$

The two ratemaking methods

Loss ratio method (rate change) $$\text{Indicated Avg. Rate Change}=\frac{LR+F}{1-V-Q_T}-1,\qquad LR=\frac{L_{\text{dev,trend}}}{P^{C}}.$$
Pure premium / loss cost method (rate level) $$\text{Indicated Avg. Rate}=\frac{\bar L+\bar E_F}{1-V-Q_T},\qquad \bar L=\frac{L_{\text{dev,trend}}}{n_e},\quad \bar E_F=\frac{E_F}{n_e}.$$

Convert a change to a level: $\;\text{indicated rate}=(\text{current avg rate})\times(1+\text{indicated change}).$ With multiple years, compute $LR$ (or $\bar L$) per year, then take the weighted average.

Credibility

$$Z=\min\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{n}{n_{\text{full}}}},\,1\right),\qquad \text{Final}=Z\,A+(1-Z)\,C.$$

Example 6 Parallelogram — multiple changes in one year, annual vs semi-annual ▾

Rate changes: +3% (5/1/2015), −1% (1/1/2016), +5% (7/1/2016). CY2016 earned premium $=5{,}400$. Find CRL premium for CY2016.

Example 8 Both methods at once — loss ratio & pure premium ▾

Given: premium at current rate level $=120{,}000$; projected losses + LAE $=74{,}000$; exposures $=1{,}000$; $V=20\%$; fixed expense per exposure $=25$; $Q_T=5\%$; current average rate $=120$.

Loss ratio method: $LR=\frac{74{,}000}{120{,}000}=0.61667$, $F=\frac{25(1{,}000)}{120{,}000}=0.20833$, PLR $=1-0.20-0.05=0.75$:

$$\text{Indicated change}=\frac{0.61667+0.20833}{0.75}-1=\frac{0.825}{0.75}-1=0.10.$$

Pure premium method: $\bar L=\frac{74{,}000}{1{,}000}=74$, $\bar E_F=25$:

$$\text{Indicated rate}=\frac{74+25}{0.75}=\frac{99}{0.75}=132.$$

Reconcile: apply the +10% change to the current rate: $120(1.10)=132.$ ✓

Answer
+10% rate change (loss ratio) ⇔ \$132 indicated rate (pure premium). They match here because the data are consistent — they would not if $P^C$ came from the parallelogram method.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  1. CY vs AY/PY incurred formula. CY incurred uses the change in reserves ($+R_i-R_{i-1}$); AY/PY incurred adds the current reserve to cumulative paid ($+R_i$, no subtraction). Mixing them is the classic aggregation error.
  2. Overlap fallacy. Developing and trending do not double-count inflation — development brings immature losses to ultimate; trend moves the ultimate from the experience to the forecast cost level. Apply both.
  3. Wrong trend period / wrong midpoints. Trend runs from the average loss date of the experience period to the average loss date of the forecast period, not "midpoint to today." Remember PY experience length $=1+\text{term}$ and forecast length $=\text{term}+\text{time-in-effect}$. If policies are written only on the effective date, the forecast window collapses to the policy term (midpoint moves earlier).
  4. Effective vs continuous trend. $(1+t)^n$ vs $e^{rn}$ — use whichever the problem states; don't silently convert. Watch the "creative" forms (slope on $L$ → linear; slope on $\ln L$ → exponential).
  5. Matching losses to the wrong premium. Developed/trended losses go against on-level earned premium (CY EP ↔ AY losses; PY EP ↔ PY losses) — never written or as-booked premium.
  6. Double-counting fixed expenses. Fixed expenses belong in the numerator (as $E_F$ / fixed ratio $F$), not inside the PLR denominator. The denominator $1-V-Q_T$ holds only variable expense and profit.
  7. Misclassifying expenses. Commissions/taxes scale with premium → variable (% of premium). Overhead per policy → fixed (dollars). LAE (ALAE/ULAE, DCC/AAO) sits with losses, not with underwriting expenses.
  8. Parallelogram geometry. Areas use fractions of a year; a change with $m$ months remaining gives a leg $m/12$ and a corner area $\tfrac12(m/12)^2$. Non-annual terms change the slope (semi-annual → diagonals end at 6 months). Forgetting to use the current cumulative index in the numerator of the on-level factor is common.
  9. Indicated rate vs rate change. Pure premium method → a rate (level, dollars). Loss ratio method → a change factor (subtract 1 for %). Reporting one when asked for the other loses the point. The two agree only with consistent data — expect a small gap when $P^C$ comes from the parallelogram method.
  10. On-leveling is a premium step. Never apply on-level factors to losses; losses get development + trend only.
  11. PLR sign. $\text{PLR}=1-V-Q_T$. Profit/contingency is a loading, so it reduces the permissible loss ratio. Don't subtract the actual loss ratio.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write the fundamental insurance equation and name the four essential objectives of ratemaking.
$P = L + (V P + E_F) + Q_T P$ (losses incl. LAE, variable + fixed expenses, profit/contingency). Essential objectives: rates must cover expected losses & expenses, provide for contingencies, encourage loss control, and satisfy regulators (adequate, not excessive, not unfairly discriminatory).
Name the three loss-aggregation methods on the exam and the key difference among them.
Calendar year (all transactions in the CY — final at year-end), accident year (accidents occurring in the window — needs a valuation date), policy year (policies issued in the window — needs a valuation date). Report year (claims-made) is not tested.
Give the CY and AY/PY incurred-loss formulas.
CY: $L_i^I=L_i^P+R_i-R_{i-1}$ (change in reserves). AY/PY at a valuation date: $L_i^I=L_i^P+R_i$ (cumulative paid + current reserve, no subtraction).
A claim: paid 1,500 during 2017; case reserve 3,000 at 12/31/2017, 2,600 at 12/31/2016; accident in 2016, policy written 2015. Find CY2017, AY2016@12/31/17, and PY2016 incurred.
CY2017 $=1{,}500+3{,}000-2{,}600=1{,}900$. AY2016@12/31/17 (cumulative paid 1,500 + reserve 3,000) $=4{,}500$. PY2016 $=0$ (policy was written in 2015, not 2016).
What is the overlap fallacy, and why is it wrong?
The myth that developing and trending double-counts inflation. Wrong because development brings immature losses to ultimate, while trend moves the ultimate from the experience cost level to the forecast cost level — two independent adjustments.

Define the variable and fixed expense ratios, and state the PLR. Where do fixed expenses go in the rate formula?
