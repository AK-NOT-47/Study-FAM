Frequency Models

Frequency answers "how many claims?" — and four distributions, one recursion, the dispersion test, and the slope-of-ratios method cover almost everything the exam asks.

📖 Loss Models (KPW) ⏱️ ~3 hrs 🔑 Poisson · Binomial · NB · Geometric 🔑 (a,b,0) · (a,b,1) · PGF 🔑 mean vs variance · slope test 🔑 Poisson–Gamma · thinning
Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

  • Identify and parametrize the core frequency distributions: Poisson, binomial (and its Bernoulli special case), negative binomial, and geometric (a special case of the negative binomial).
  • Compute probabilities, means, and variances for each, and use the mean-to-variance relationship to recognize which distribution fits a data set.
  • Apply the closure (sum) properties: sums of independent Poissons, of binomials with a common $q$, and of geometrics, and recognize the resulting distribution.
  • Use the geometric memoryless property for shifted/conditional counts.
  • State the defining property of the (a, b, 0) class, identify which distributions belong to it, and back out parameters from the recursion coefficients $a$ and $b$.
  • Use the (a, b, 0) recursion $p_k = (a + b/k)\,p_{k-1}$ to compute successive probabilities.
  • Choose the best-fitting (a,b,0) distribution from data using (1) sample mean vs. variance and (2) the slope of $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$.
  • Recognize the Poisson–Gamma mixture that produces a negative binomial.
  • Understand the (a, b, 1) class: zero-truncated and zero-modified distributions, convert between base and modified probabilities, and compute their moments.
  • Work with probability generating functions (PGFs), extract moments, and use them for compound and mixed distributions.
  • Build and analyze compound frequency distributions (e.g., Neyman Type A).
  • Determine the effect of exposure changes and coverage modifications (deductibles, via thinning) on the frequency distribution.

Key Concepts

1. What is a frequency model?

A frequency model describes the number of claims $N$ (a non-negative integer random variable) over a fixed period. It is the "how many" half of the risk; the "how much" half is the severity $X$. Together they build the aggregate loss $S = X_1 + \dots + X_N$ (a compound distribution). Mastering frequency is the foundation for aggregate-loss modeling.

We always denote $p_k = \Pr(N = k)$ for $k = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ (the exam table uses $p_n$).

2. The four core distributions — intuition

  • Poisson $(\lambda)$: the "default" claim-count model. Counts of rare events over time/space. Its signature feature is mean = variance = $\lambda$ (equidispersion). Memorize this.
  • Binomial $(m, q)$: a finite number of independent trials $m$, each producing a claim with probability $q$. Count is capped at $m$. Variance < mean (underdispersion): $\text{Var} = mq(1-q) < mq = $ mean. The Bernoulli $(q)$ is just the $m=1$ case.
  • Negative binomial $(r, \beta)$: counts with variance > mean (overdispersion). Arises naturally as a Poisson mixed over a gamma-distributed $\lambda$ — i.e., heterogeneous risks. This is the workhorse when real data are more variable than Poisson allows.
  • Geometric $(\beta)$: the negative binomial with $r = 1$. The discrete analogue of the exponential; memoryless in the count sense.
Coach's Remark — Poisson process vs. Poisson distribution

You may see the term Poisson process. For this exam it can be used interchangeably with "Poisson distribution." The process is the broader idea of counting occurrences over time; the distribution is the count itself.

3. Closure (sum) properties

These "sum of independent copies" facts show up constantly and underpin exposure scaling.

  • Poisson: if $N_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i)$ are independent, then $\sum_i N_i \sim \text{Poisson}\!\left(\sum_i \lambda_i\right)$.
  • Binomial (common $q$): if $N_i \sim \text{Binomial}(m_i, q)$ are independent, then $\sum_i N_i \sim \text{Binomial}\!\left(\sum_i m_i,\, q\right)$. (The $q$ must match.)
  • Bernoulli → Binomial: a sum of $m$ i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(q)$ is $\text{Binomial}(m, q)$.
  • Negative binomial (common $\beta$): if $N_i \sim \text{NB}(r_i, \beta)$ are independent, then $\sum_i N_i \sim \text{NB}\!\left(\sum_i r_i,\, \beta\right)$.
  • Geometric → Negative binomial: a sum of $r$ i.i.d. $\text{Geometric}(\beta)$ is $\text{NB}(r, \beta)$.
Geometric memoryless property

For $N \sim \text{Geometric}(\beta)$, the shifted conditional count is again geometric with the same $\beta$:

$$ (N - d \mid N \ge d) \sim \text{Geometric}(\beta). $$

Consequences: $\mathrm{E}[N - d \mid N \ge d] = \mathrm{E}[N] = \beta$,   $\text{Var}(N - d \mid N \ge d) = \beta(1+\beta)$,   and $\Pr(N - d = a \mid N \ge d) = \Pr(N = a)$. It is the discrete twin of the exponential's memorylessness.

The dispersion test (a hugely common exam shortcut):

DistributionRelationship
BinomialVariance < Mean
PoissonVariance = Mean
Negative binomial / GeometricVariance > Mean

Given sample mean $\bar{x}$ and sample variance $s^2$, compare them to pick the family before fitting. (More on this — including a second, data-driven method — in §6.)

4. The (a, b, 0) class

A distribution belongs to the (a, b, 0) class if there exist constants $a$ and $b$ such that

$$\frac{p_k}{p_{k-1}} = a + \frac{b}{k}, \qquad k = 1, 2, 3, \dots$$

The "0" means the recursion starts at $k = 1$ and uses the natural $p_0$. There are exactly four members: Poisson, binomial, negative binomial, and geometric. (Geometric is the $r=1$ negative binomial; Bernoulli is the $m=1$ binomial — so really three families.) No other distribution satisfies this.

Why it matters: the values of $a$ and $b$ both identify the family and let you back out the parameters. The sign of $a$ is the tell:

Sign of $a$Distribution$a$$b$
$a = 0$Poisson$0$$\lambda$
$a < 0$Binomial$-\dfrac{q}{1-q}$$(m+1)\dfrac{q}{1-q}$
$a > 0$Negative binomial$\dfrac{\beta}{1+\beta}$$(r-1)\dfrac{\beta}{1+\beta}$
$a > 0,\ b = 0$Geometric$\dfrac{\beta}{1+\beta}$$0$

(In all four members $b > 0$ except the geometric, where $b = 0$.) This recursion is also the computational engine for compound (aggregate) distributions via the Panjer recursion.

5. The Poisson–Gamma mixture (how a negative binomial is born)

A negative binomial arises when each insured is Poisson but the Poisson mean itself varies across the population according to a gamma distribution — the classic model of heterogeneous risks:

$$(N \mid \Lambda = \lambda) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda), \quad \Lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \theta) \;\Longrightarrow\; N \sim \text{Negative Binomial}(r = \alpha,\ \beta = \theta).$$
Coach's Remark — why this is worth knowing

This is a continuous mixture (an infinite blend of Poissons). Continuous mixtures are not on the syllabus in general, but this specific one is in the prescribed reading for the negative binomial and appears in an SOA sample question — so treat it as testable. Special case: an exponential mixing distribution is $\text{Gamma}(\alpha = 1, \theta)$, which yields a geometric (NB with $r = 1$).

6. Choosing the best (a, b, 0) distribution from data

Given a frequency table, two methods identify the family. The exam table gives all the formulas you need, so neither requires memorizing inequalities.

Method 1 — sample mean vs. unbiased sample variance. Compare $\bar{x}$ and $s^2$:

If…RecommendWhy (from formulas)
$\bar{x} = s^2$Poisson$\lambda = \lambda$
$\bar{x} > s^2$Binomial$mq > mq(1-q)$
$\bar{x} < s^2$Negative binomial$r\beta < r\beta(1+\beta)$

Method 2 — slope of $k\,n_k / n_{k-1}$. Starting from the (a,b,0) ratio and multiplying by $k$,

$$\frac{p_k}{p_{k-1}} = a + \frac{b}{k} \;\Longrightarrow\; k\,\frac{p_k}{p_{k-1}} = ak + b,$$

so plotting $k\,p_k/p_{k-1}$ against $k$ gives a straight line with slope $a$. Estimating $\hat p_k = n_k/n$ (where $n_k$ is the observed count of $k$ and $n$ the total), the proportions cancel:

$$k\,\frac{\hat p_k}{\hat p_{k-1}} = k\,\frac{n_k}{n_{k-1}}.$$

Compute $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$ for successive $k$ and read the trend: constant ⇒ Poisson ($a=0$), decreasing ⇒ binomial ($a<0$), increasing ⇒ negative binomial ($a>0$). This works directly off raw counts, no proportions needed.

Coach's Remark — "how close is close enough?"

Real samples never give $\bar{x}$ exactly equal to $s^2$ or a perfectly constant ratio. When $\bar{x}$ and $s^2$ are close, don't rush to a verdict. A handy check: look at the ratio $\bar{x}/s^2$ (or its reciprocal). If it's around $1.00$, Poisson is the call; clearly above $1$ leans binomial, clearly below leans negative binomial. The convention is the unbiased $s^2$ (divide by $n-1$), but for large samples the bias is negligible.

7. The (a, b, 1) class — truncation and modification

Sometimes the probability of zero claims doesn't match an (a,b,0) model (e.g., a "claim/no-claim" gate, or data where zero is over- or under-represented). The (a, b, 1) class keeps the same recursion but only for $k \ge 2$:

$$\frac{p_k}{p_{k-1}} = a + \frac{b}{k}, \qquad k = 2, 3, 4, \dots$$

This frees up $p_0$ (and hence $p_1$) to be set arbitrarily. The core difference between (a,b,0) and (a,b,1) is entirely in $p_0$; the parameters $a$, $b$ (and hence the underlying $\lambda$, $q$, or $r,\beta$) are the same as the base distribution. Two important special cases:

  • Zero-truncated (ZT): $p_0^T = 0$. We condition on at least one claim.
  • Zero-modified (ZM): $p_0^M$ is an arbitrary chosen value in $(0,1)$; the rest is rescaled.

If $p_k$ is the original (a,b,0) probability, the modified probabilities are:

$$p_k^M = \frac{1 - p_0^M}{1 - p_0}\, p_k, \qquad k = 1, 2, 3, \dots$$

The zero-truncated case is just the special case $p_0^M = 0$:

$$p_k^T = \frac{p_k}{1 - p_0}, \qquad k \ge 1.$$

The factor rescales all the positive-mass probabilities so they sum (with $p_0^M$) to 1. Crucially, the $p_0$ in the denominator and the $p_k$ being scaled are the original base probabilities, not the modified ones.

Formulas

Throughout, $p_k = \Pr(N=k)$.

Poisson $(\lambda)$, $\lambda > 0$

$$p_k = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}, \qquad \mathrm{E}[N] = \lambda, \quad \text{Var}(N) = \lambda, \quad P_N(z) = e^{\lambda(z-1)}.$$ $$(a, b, 0):\quad a = 0,\quad b = \lambda.$$

Binomial $(m, q)$, $m \in \{1,2,\dots\}$, $0 < q < 1$

$$p_k = \binom{m}{k} q^k (1-q)^{m-k}, \quad k = 0,\dots,m, \qquad \mathrm{E}[N] = mq, \quad \text{Var}(N) = mq(1-q).$$ $$P_N(z) = \big(1 + q(z-1)\big)^m, \qquad a = -\frac{q}{1-q},\quad b = (m+1)\frac{q}{1-q}.$$

Bernoulli $(q)$ — binomial with $m = 1$

$$p_k = q^k (1-q)^{1-k}, \quad k = 0, 1, \qquad \mathrm{E}[N] = q, \quad \text{Var}(N) = q(1-q).$$

Negative binomial $(r, \beta)$, $r > 0$, $\beta > 0$

$$p_k = \binom{r + k - 1}{k}\left(\frac{1}{1+\beta}\right)^r \left(\frac{\beta}{1+\beta}\right)^k,$$ $$\mathrm{E}[N] = r\beta, \qquad \text{Var}(N) = r\beta(1+\beta), \qquad P_N(z) = \big(1 - \beta(z-1)\big)^{-r}.$$ $$a = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta},\qquad b = (r-1)\frac{\beta}{1+\beta}.$$

Equivalently, $p_0 = (1+\beta)^{-r}$ and for $k\ge 1$, $\;p_k = \dfrac{r(r+1)\cdots(r+k-1)}{k!}\cdot\dfrac{\beta^k}{(1+\beta)^{r+k}}$. The "success probability" parametrization sets $p = \frac{1}{1+\beta}$, so $\beta = \frac{1-p}{p}$ is the odds of failure.

Geometric $(\beta)$ — negative binomial with $r = 1$

$$p_k = \frac{\beta^k}{(1+\beta)^{k+1}}, \qquad \mathrm{E}[N] = \beta, \quad \text{Var}(N) = \beta(1+\beta), \quad P_N(z) = \big(1-\beta(z-1)\big)^{-1}.$$ $$a = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta},\qquad b = 0.$$
Symbol key

$\lambda$ = Poisson rate; $m$ = number of binomial trials; $q$ = per-trial success (claim) probability; $r$ = negative-binomial "shape"; $\beta$ = negative-binomial scale (odds-like parameter, not a probability); $z$ = PGF argument; $n_k$ = observed number of policies with exactly $k$ claims.

(a, b, 0) recursion

$$p_k = \left(a + \frac{b}{k}\right) p_{k-1}, \qquad k \ge 1,$$

with $p_0$ given by the distribution ($e^{-\lambda}$, $(1-q)^m$, or $(1+\beta)^{-r}$).

Choosing the distribution from data

$$\text{Mean vs. var: } \bar{x}=s^2 \Rightarrow \text{Poisson}, \quad \bar{x}>s^2 \Rightarrow \text{Binomial}, \quad \bar{x}\lt s^2 \Rightarrow \text{NB}.$$ $$\text{Slope: } k\,\frac{n_k}{n_{k-1}} = a k + b \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{constant}\,(a{=}0)\ \text{Poisson},\ \text{decr.}\,(a{<}0)\ \text{Binom.},\ \text{incr.}\,(a{>}0)\ \text{NB}.$$

(a, b, 1): zero-modified and zero-truncated

$$p_k^M = \frac{1 - p_0^M}{1 - p_0}\, p_k \ (k\ge 1), \qquad p_k^T = \frac{p_k}{1 - p_0}\ (k \ge 1).$$

Raw moments scale by the same factor. For zero-truncated, every $k$th raw moment is the base moment scaled by $1/(1-p_0)$; for zero-modified, by $(1-p_0^M)/(1-p_0)$:

$$\mathrm{E}\big[(N^T)^k\big] = \frac{1}{1-p_0}\,\mathrm{E}[N^k], \qquad \mathrm{E}\big[(N^M)^k\big] = \frac{1-p_0^M}{1-p_0}\,\mathrm{E}[N^k].$$

There is no direct shortcut for the variance — compute it from the (modified/truncated) first and second raw moments:

$$\text{Var}(N^T) = \mathrm{E}\big[(N^T)^2\big] - \big(\mathrm{E}[N^T]\big)^2, \qquad \text{Var}(N^M) = \mathrm{E}\big[(N^M)^2\big] - \big(\mathrm{E}[N^M]\big)^2.$$

The (a,b,1) recursion persists for the modified probabilities, just shifted to start at $k = 2$:

$$\frac{p_k^T}{p_{k-1}^T} = a + \frac{b}{k}, \qquad \frac{p_k^M}{p_{k-1}^M} = a + \frac{b}{k}, \qquad k = 2, 3, \dots$$

PGF moment extraction

$$P_N(z) = \mathrm{E}[z^N] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} p_k z^k, \qquad \mathrm{E}[N] = P_N'(1), \qquad \text{Var}(N) = P_N''(1) + P_N'(1) - \big[P_N'(1)\big]^2.$$

Also $P_N(0) = p_0$, $P_N(1) = 1$, $p_k = P_N^{(k)}(0)/k!$, the PGF of an independent sum is the product of PGFs, and a compound's PGF is the composition $P_K(P_M(z))$.

Compound frequency $N = \sum_{i=1}^{K} M_i$

$$P_N(z) = P_K\big(P_M(z)\big),$$ $$\mathrm{E}[N] = \mathrm{E}[K]\,\mathrm{E}[M], \qquad \text{Var}(N) = \mathrm{E}[K]\,\text{Var}(M) + \text{Var}(K)\,\big(\mathrm{E}[M]\big)^2.$$

(This is the conditional-variance / compound-variance formula.) Named cases: Poisson–Poisson = Neyman Type A; Poisson–logarithmic = negative binomial.

Exposure scaling (units $n_1 \to n_2$, ratio $c = n_2/n_1$)

$$\text{Poisson: } \lambda \to c\lambda; \qquad \text{NB: } r \to cr; \qquad \text{Binomial: } m \to cm.$$

Coverage modification / thinning (survival probability $v$)

$$\text{Poisson: } \lambda \to v\lambda; \qquad \text{Binomial: } q \to vq; \qquad \text{NB: } \beta \to v\beta.$$

Worked Examples

Example 1 Identify the (a, b, 0) member and recover the mean from two ratios ▾

For an (a,b,0) class distribution you are given $\dfrac{p_1}{p_0} = 2$ and $\dfrac{p_2}{p_1} = \dfrac{4}{3}$. Calculate the mean.

Solution. Each ratio is $a + b/k$:

$$\frac{p_1}{p_0} = a + b = 2, \qquad \frac{p_2}{p_1} = a + \frac{b}{2} = \frac{4}{3}.$$

Subtract: $\frac{b}{2} = 2 - \frac{4}{3} = \frac{2}{3} \Rightarrow b = \frac{4}{3}$. Then $a = 2 - \frac{4}{3} = \frac{2}{3}$. Both positive ⇒ negative binomial. Solve for parameters:

$$a = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} = \frac{2}{3} \implies \beta = 2, \qquad b = (r-1)\frac{\beta}{1+\beta} = \frac{4}{3} \implies (r-1)\cdot\tfrac{2}{3} = \tfrac{4}{3} \implies r = 3.$$ $$\mathrm{E}[N] = r\beta = 3 \times 2 = 6.$$
Answer
Negative binomial with $r = 3$, $\beta = 2$; mean $= \mathbf{6}$.
Example 2 Closure: summing binomials with a common $q$ ▾

An insured owns 3 cars in Year 1 and 4 cars in Year 2. Each car has a 10% chance of an accident and can have at most one accident per year; accidents are independent. Find $\Pr(\text{at most 1 accident in two years})$.

Solution. $N_1 \sim \text{Binomial}(3, 0.1)$ and $N_2 \sim \text{Binomial}(4, 0.1)$ share the same $q = 0.1$, so the two-year total is

$$N = N_1 + N_2 \sim \text{Binomial}(3+4,\ 0.1) = \text{Binomial}(7, 0.1).$$ $$\Pr(N \le 1) = p_0 + p_1 = \binom{7}{0}(0.9)^7 + \binom{7}{1}(0.1)(0.9)^6 = 0.4783 + 0.3720 = 0.8503.$$
Answer
$\Pr(N \le 1) \approx \mathbf{0.85}$.
Example 3 Poisson–Gamma mixture (exponential mixing) ▾

The annual number of claims per insured is $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, where $\lambda$ varies by insured and follows an exponential distribution with mean 4. (1) For a random insured, find $\Pr(N = 1)$. (2) For an insured known to average 4 claims per year, find $\Pr(N = 1)$.

Solution (1). An exponential with mean 4 is $\text{Gamma}(\alpha = 1, \theta = 4)$. By the Poisson–Gamma result,

$$N \sim \text{Negative Binomial}(r = 1,\ \beta = 4) = \text{Geometric}(\beta = 4).$$ $$\Pr(N = 1) = \frac{\beta^1}{(1+\beta)^{1+1}} = \frac{4}{5^2} = \frac{4}{25} = 0.16.$$

Solution (2). Now $\lambda$ is fixed at 4, so $N \mid \Lambda = 4 \sim \text{Poisson}(4)$:

$$\Pr(N = 1 \mid \Lambda = 4) = \frac{e^{-4} 4^1}{1!} = 0.0733.$$
Answer
(1) $0.16$ (geometric). (2) $0.0733$ (plain Poisson). The mixing inflates the chance of 1 claim because the population is heterogeneous.
Example 4 Choosing the distribution from data — both methods ▾

Auto-accident counts from 1,000 policies:

$k$ (accidents)012345
$n_k$ (policies)210367275115294

Method 1 — mean vs. variance.

$$\bar{x} = \frac{367(1)+275(2)+115(3)+29(4)+4(5)}{1000} = 1.3980,$$ $$s^2 = \frac{1000}{999}\left[\frac{367(1)+275(4)+115(9)+29(16)+4(25)}{1000} - 1.3980^2\right] = 1.1127.$$

Since $\bar{x} > s^2$ (ratio $1.398/1.113 = 1.26$), recommend the binomial.

Method 2 — slope of $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$.

$$1\cdot\tfrac{367}{210}=1.748,\quad 2\cdot\tfrac{275}{367}=1.499,\quad 3\cdot\tfrac{115}{275}=1.255,\quad 4\cdot\tfrac{29}{115}=1.009,\quad 5\cdot\tfrac{4}{29}=0.690.$$

The sequence decreases (slope $a < 0$), confirming the binomial.

Answer
Both methods agree: binomial.
Example 5 When mean ≈ variance: use the slope to break the tie ▾

Counts from 3,000 policies:

$k$012345
$n_k$1,4901,04336585152

$\bar{x} = 0.6993$ and $s^2 = 0.6972$. They are nearly equal ($\bar{x}/s^2 = 1.00$), so Method 1 is inconclusive — don't jump to "binomial" just because $\bar{x}$ is a hair larger. Apply Method 2:

$$1\cdot\tfrac{1043}{1490}=0.700,\ \ 2\cdot\tfrac{365}{1043}=0.700,\ \ 3\cdot\tfrac{85}{365}=0.699,\ \ 4\cdot\tfrac{15}{85}=0.706,\ \ 5\cdot\tfrac{2}{15}=0.667.$$

The ratio fluctuates around a constant ($\approx 0.70$, slope $\approx 0$).

Answer
Recommend the Poisson distribution.
Example 6 Zero-truncated and zero-modified Poisson variance ▾

Monthly claims $N \sim \text{Poisson}(3)$. Find (1) $\text{Var}(N)$; (2) $\text{Var}(N^T)$ assuming a zero-claim month is impossible; (3) $\text{Var}(N^M)$ assuming the probability of no claims is set to $0.3$.

(1) $\text{Var}(N) = \lambda = 3$. Base moments: $\mathrm{E}[N] = 3$, $\mathrm{E}[N^2] = \text{Var}+\text{mean}^2 = 3 + 9 = 12$, and $p_0 = e^{-3} = 0.049787$.

(2) Zero-truncated (factor $1/(1-p_0)$):

$$\mathrm{E}[N^T] = \frac{3}{1-e^{-3}} = 3.157, \qquad \mathrm{E}[(N^T)^2] = \frac{12}{1-e^{-3}} = 12.629,$$ $$\text{Var}(N^T) = 12.629 - 3.157^2 = 2.66.$$

(3) Zero-modified with $p_0^M = 0.3$ (factor $(1-0.3)/(1-e^{-3}) = 0.7/0.950213 = 0.73668$):

$$\mathrm{E}[N^M] = 0.73668 \times 3 = 2.21, \qquad \mathrm{E}[(N^M)^2] = 0.73668 \times 12 = 8.84,$$ $$\text{Var}(N^M) = 8.84 - 2.21^2 = 3.96.$$
Answer
$\text{Var}(N) = 3$; $\text{Var}(N^T) \approx 2.66$; $\text{Var}(N^M) \approx 3.96$. Note truncation lowered the variance and modification (adding zero mass back) raised it — always compute from the modified moments, never scale the base variance.
Example 7 Compound (Neyman Type A) and exposure + thinning ▾

(A) Storms per year $K \sim \text{Poisson}(3)$; each storm causes $M \sim \text{Poisson}(4)$ claims. Total claims $N$:

$$\mathrm{E}[N] = \mathrm{E}[K]\mathrm{E}[M] = 3\times 4 = 12,$$ $$\text{Var}(N) = \mathrm{E}[K]\text{Var}(M) + \text{Var}(K)(\mathrm{E}[M])^2 = 3(4) + 3(16) = 60.$$

(B) A 500-policy portfolio has loss count $\text{NB}(r=2, \beta=0.5)$. The book grows to 1,500 policies (exposure ratio $c=3$): $r \to 3(2) = 6$, $\beta$ unchanged ⇒ $\text{NB}(6, 0.5)$, mean $6(0.5)=3$. A deductible then passes only $v = \Pr(X>d) = 0.6$ of losses (thinning): $\beta \to 0.6(0.5) = 0.30$, $r$ unchanged ⇒ payment count $\text{NB}(6, 0.30)$, mean $6(0.30) = 1.80$.

Answer
(A) $\mathrm{E}[N]=12$, $\text{Var}(N)=60$. (B) Payment count is $\text{NB}(6,0.30)$, mean $1.80$ (= $0.6\times 3$, the thinning sanity check).

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Confusing the negative-binomial $\beta$ with a probability. $\beta$ is a scale/odds parameter, not in $[0,1]$. The "success probability" is $\frac{1}{1+\beta}$ and $\beta = \frac{1-p}{p}$ is the odds of failure. Loss Models uses $\beta$, not the Exam-P $p$.
  • Mis-signing $a$. Binomial $a < 0$; negative binomial/geometric $a > 0$; Poisson $a = 0$. Reversing these is the single most common identification error.
  • Forgetting geometric is $b = 0$. If $a > 0$ and $b = 0$, it's geometric (NB with $r = 1$), not "some other distribution."
  • Closure requires a matching parameter. Sums of binomials must share $q$; sums of negative binomials must share $\beta$. Without that, the sum is not in the same family.
  • Calling mean ≈ variance "binomial." When $\bar{x}$ and $s^2$ are close, the mean/variance test is inconclusive — fall back to the slope of $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$ before committing. A near-constant ratio means Poisson, even if $\bar{x}$ is slightly above $s^2$.
  • (a,b,1) starts the recursion at $k = 2$, not $k = 1$. $p_1$ relative to $p_0$ is not constrained — only $p_2, p_3, \dots$ are.
  • Using the wrong base $p_0$ in the rescale factor. In $p_k^M = \frac{1-p_0^M}{1-p_0}p_k$, the $p_0$ in the denominator and the $p_k$ are the original (untruncated) probabilities.
  • Trying to scale the truncated/modified variance directly. Raw moments scale by the factor, but variance does not — compute it from the modified first and second moments.
  • Scaling the wrong parameter. Exposure scales the "size" parameter ($\lambda$, $r$, or $m$); thinning/coverage scales the "intensity" parameter ($\lambda$, $q$, or $\beta$). For Poisson both are $\lambda$ — don't generalize that shortcut to NB/binomial.
  • Compound-variance formula order. It is $\mathrm{E}[K]\text{Var}(M) + \text{Var}(K)(\mathrm{E}[M])^2$ — the primary variance multiplies the squared secondary mean. Swapping $K$ and $M$ is wrong.
  • PGF vs MGF. $\mathrm{E}[N] = P'(1)$ (not $P'(0)$), and $P''(1) = \mathrm{E}[N(N-1)]$ is a factorial moment — add $P'(1)$ back for the variance.
  • Thinning stays in family only for plain (a,b,0) members. A zero-modified or compound distribution may not.

List the members of the (a, b, 0) class and the signs of $a$ and $b$ for each.
Poisson, binomial (incl. Bernoulli, $m{=}1$), negative binomial, geometric ($r{=}1$ NB). Signs: Poisson $a=0,\,b>0$; binomial $a<0,\,b>0$; negative binomial $a>0,\,b>0$; geometric $a>0,\,b=0$.
What is the distribution of a sum of independent negative binomials, and what condition is required?
If $N_i \sim \text{NB}(r_i, \beta)$ are independent and share the same $\beta$, then $\sum_i N_i \sim \text{NB}\!\left(\sum_i r_i,\ \beta\right)$. (Likewise a sum of $r$ i.i.d. geometrics is $\text{NB}(r, \beta)$.)
Describe the Poisson–Gamma mixture and its result.
If $N\mid\Lambda=\lambda \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and $\Lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \theta)$, then unconditionally $N \sim \text{Negative Binomial}(r=\alpha,\ \beta=\theta)$. Special case: exponential mixing ($\alpha=1$) gives a geometric.
A claim-count data set has sample mean 1.5 and sample variance 0.9. Which distribution is the natural candidate, and why?
Binomial. Variance (0.9) < mean (1.5) — underdispersion, which only the binomial exhibits. (Ratio $1.5/0.9 = 1.67$, comfortably above 1, so the call is safe.)
Explain the slope method for choosing an (a,b,0) distribution. What does a near-constant $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$ indicate?
Plot $k\,n_k/n_{k-1}$ against $k$; it equals $ak + b$, so the trend is the slope $a$. Constant ⇒ Poisson ($a=0$), decreasing ⇒ binomial ($a<0$), increasing ⇒ negative binomial ($a>0$). A near-constant ratio indicates Poisson.
$N \sim \text{Geometric}(\beta=2)$. Find $\mathrm{E}[N-3 \mid N \ge 3]$.
By memorylessness, $(N-3\mid N\ge 3)\sim\text{Geometric}(2)$, so $\mathrm{E}[N-3\mid N\ge 3] = \mathrm{E}[N] = \beta = \mathbf{2}$.
For a Poisson with $\lambda = 4$, give $a$ and $b$ and express $p_3$ via the recursion.
Poisson: $a = 0$, $b = \lambda = 4$. Recursion: $p_3 = \left(0 + \frac{4}{3}\right)p_2 = \frac{4}{3}p_2$.
Zero-truncated Poisson with base $\lambda = 1$: find $p_1^T$.
Base $p_0 = e^{-1}$, $p_1 = e^{-1}$. Then $p_1^T = \dfrac{p_1}{1-p_0} = \dfrac{e^{-1}}{1-e^{-1}} = \dfrac{0.367879}{0.632121} = \mathbf{0.5820}$.
Base $\text{Poisson}(\lambda=2)$ is zero-modified with $p_0^M = 0.4$. Find $\mathrm{E}[N^M]$.
Factor $c = \dfrac{1-0.4}{1-e^{-2}} = \dfrac{0.6}{0.864665} = 0.69391$. Then $\mathrm{E}[N^M] = c\,\mathrm{E}[N] = 0.69391 \times 2 = \mathbf{1.388}$.
Compare the (a,b,0) recursion to the zero-modified version.
Identical ratio $a + b/k$, but the (a,b,0) version holds for $k\ge 1$ while the ZT/ZM version holds only for $k \ge 2$ (since $p_0$, and hence the $p_1/p_0$ link, has been altered).
The PGF of $N$ is $P_N(z) = e^{2(z-1)}$. Identify the distribution and find $\text{Var}(N)$.
Poisson with $\lambda = 2$, so $\mathrm{E}[N] = 2$ and $\text{Var}(N) = \mathbf{2}$.
Storm count is $\text{NB}(r=5,\beta=0.4)$; each storm causes $\text{Poisson}(3)$ claims. Find the mean and variance of total claims.
$\mathrm{E}[K]=r\beta=2$, $\text{Var}(K)=r\beta(1+\beta)=2.8$; $\mathrm{E}[M]=\text{Var}(M)=3$. $\mathrm{E}[N]=2\times 3=\mathbf{6}$; $\text{Var}(N)=2(3)+2.8(9)=6+25.2=\mathbf{31.2}$.
