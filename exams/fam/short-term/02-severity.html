Severity Models

Severity models describe the size of a single loss. The real exam skill is not memorizing densities but fluently moving between pdf / cdf / survival / hazard, computing moments, and creating new distributions from old ones — then reading their tails.

📖 Klugman, Panjer & Willmot · Loss Models ⏱️ ~4–5 hrs 🔑 random-variable basics · scale families · tail weight 🔑 transformations · mixing · splicing · LEF

Severity models describe the size of an individual loss (the claim amount), as opposed to frequency models, which describe the number of losses. To get there you first need fluency with the basic descriptors of a random variable (pdf, cdf, survival, hazard, percentiles, modes, moments, conditional distributions, empirical distributions). Then on Exam FAM you are expected to know a small zoo of continuous parametric distributions, to compute their moments, to compare their tail weight, and — crucially — to create new distributions from old ones (transforming, scaling for inflation, inverting, exponentiating, mixing, and splicing).

Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

  1. Move freely between the four equivalent descriptors of a continuous random variable — pdf $f$, cdf $F$, survival $S$, hazard $h$ (and cumulative hazard $H$) — and recover any one from any other.
  2. Compute percentiles and modes (continuous via $F(\pi_p)=p$ and $f'(x)=0$; discrete via the full percentile definition).
  3. Compute moments: raw moments $\mu_k'=E[X^k]$, central moments $\mu_k$, variance, standard deviation, coefficient of variation, skewness, and kurtosis — and convert central moments to raw moments.
  4. Apply conditional-distribution machinery: conditional pdfs, the laws of total probability, total expectation, and total variance, plus independence.
  5. Build empirical distributions from data: empirical cdf, empirical percentiles, sample mean, and the biased vs. unbiased sample variance.
  6. Recognize and use the standard parametric severity distributions — exponential, gamma, Pareto (two-parameter, single-parameter, inverse), Weibull, lognormal, normal, beta, uniform, and the inverse families — using the parameterizations in the Loss Models exam tables.
  7. Use the transformation technique (cdf method) to find the distribution of $Y=g(X)$, and apply it to build inverse, transformed, scaled, and exponentiated distributions.
  8. Compute and interpret the hazard rate $h(x)$ and the mean excess / mean residual life $e(d)$, and use their behavior to classify tail weight via the four criteria.
  9. Identify scale families and the scale parameter $\theta$, and apply the correct scaling rule under inflation (general, normal, lognormal).
  10. Recognize the linear exponential family and use its mean/variance machinery, and handle discrete mixtures (including the Bernoulli shortcut) and splicing.

Key Concepts

1. What a severity distribution is

A severity random variable $X$ is the dollar amount of a single loss. It is continuous, non-negative, and almost always right-skewed with a long right tail — a few enormous claims dominate. The whole game is choosing a distribution whose tail behaves the way the data's tail behaves.

Exam-table orientation (read once, save minutes on exam day)

You get the Loss Models exam tables (same as STAM) plus a normal CDF / inverse-CDF calculator. The tables give the pdf, cdf, raw moments $E[X^k]$, and limited moments $E[X\wedge d]$ for every continuous distribution (except log-$t$), and add VaR/TVaR/mode for several. Appendix A is sorted by number of parameters, descending (A.2 three-param → A.3 two-param → ...); lognormal and single-parameter Pareto live in A.5; beta (finite support) in A.6. Appendix B holds the discrete $(a,b,0)$ and $(a,b,1)$ classes. Unless stated, every continuous distribution has domain $x>0$.

2. The four equivalent descriptors (and how they connect)

For a positive continuous $X$ you can describe it by any of:

  • pdf $f(x)$ — not a probability ($f(x)\ne P(X=x)$, and $P(X=a)=0$); it is the relative likelihood, and may exceed 1.
  • cdf $F(x) = P(X \le x)=\int_{-\infty}^x f(t)\,dt$, with $f(x)=F'(x)$,
  • survival function $S(x) = 1 - F(x) = P(X > x)$, with $f(x)=-S'(x)$,
  • hazard rate $h(x) = f(x)/S(x) = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln S(x)$, and cumulative hazard $H(x)=\int_{-\infty}^x h(t)\,dt=-\ln S(x)$, so $S(x)=e^{-H(x)}$.

They're interchangeable — given any one you can produce the other three. The survival function is the natural object for tails; the hazard rate is the natural object for "given you've survived to $x$, the instantaneous rate of failure." The cumulative-hazard relationship $S=e^{-H}$ is the cleanest way to rebuild $S$ from a hazard rate.

3. Percentiles and modes

The $100p$-th percentile $\pi_p$ satisfies $P(X<\pi_p)\le p$ and $P(X\le\pi_p)\ge p$. For a continuous $X$ this collapses to the friendly $F(\pi_p)=p$, i.e. $\pi_p=F^{-1}(p)$. Special names: 25th = first quartile, 50th = median, 75th = third quartile.

For a discrete $X$ the cdf is a step function, so you must use the full definition — and two surprises appear: multiple percentiles can share one value (a flat run of $p$ all map to the same $x$), and multiple values can be the same percentile (a horizontal hit on a jump). Sketch the step cdf and draw the line $F(x)=p$.

The mode maximizes the pdf/pmf. For continuous $X$, solve $f'(x)=0$ (optionally confirm $f''<0$); watch for the global max sitting at an endpoint of the domain.

4. Moments — the full toolkit

$E[g(X)]=\int g(x)f(x)\,dx$. For non-negative $X$ with $g(0)=0$ there is the slick survival-function form $E[g(X)]=\int_0^\infty g'(x)S(x)\,dx$; the special case $E[X]=\int_0^\infty S(x)\,dx$ is used constantly. Beyond the mean you should be able to produce:

  • Raw moment $\mu_k'=E[X^k]$ (the 1st is the mean $\mu$); central moment $\mu_k=E[(X-\mu)^k]$ (the 1st is always 0).
  • Variance $\mu_2=\text{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$; sd $\sigma=\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$; coefficient of variation $\text{CV}=\sigma/\mu$.
  • Skewness $=\mu_3/\sigma^3$ (positive ⇒ right-skewed / long right tail; the FAM severity world is overwhelmingly positively skewed) and kurtosis $=\mu_4/\sigma^4$ (normal $=3$; $>3$ ⇒ more outlier-prone).

The exam-friendly move is to express central moments in raw moments: $\mu_3=\mu_3'-3\mu_2'\mu+2\mu^3$ and $\mu_4=\mu_4'-4\mu_3'\mu+6\mu_2'\mu^2-3\mu^4$. CV, skewness, and kurtosis are all scale-invariant — multiplying $X$ by a positive constant leaves them unchanged.

5. Conditional distributions, independence, and the three laws

Conditioning $X$ on an event or another variable gives a renormalized density $f_{X\mid j\lt X\lt k}(x)=f_X(x)/P(j\lt X\lt k)$. The three laws drive almost every mixture/credibility computation:

Total probability / expectation / variance $$ P(X=x)=E_Y[P(X=x\mid Y)],\qquad E[X]=E_Y\big[E[X\mid Y]\big],$$ $$ \text{Var}(X)=E_Y\big[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)\big]+\text{Var}_Y\big(E[X\mid Y]\big).$$

If $X,Y$ are independent, then $f_{X,Y}=f_Xf_Y$, $E[g(X)h(Y)]=E[g(X)]E[h(Y)]$, $\text{Cov}=0$, and $\text{Var}(aX+bY)=a^2\text{Var}(X)+b^2\text{Var}(Y)$. The classic blunder is assuming $\text{Var}(X)=E_Y[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$ — you must add the variance of the conditional mean.

6. Empirical distributions

The empirical distribution puts mass $1/n$ on each of the $n$ data points. Its cdf is $F_n(x)=\frac{\#\{x_i\le x\}}{n}$ (a step function). The empirical $100p$-th percentile is the $\lceil np\rceil$-th order statistic, $\pi_p=x_{(\lceil np\rceil)}$. Moments come from $E[g(X)]=\frac1n\sum g(x_i)$:

$$\bar x=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i,\qquad \underbrace{\widehat{\text{Var}}(X)=\frac1n\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}_{\text{biased — describes the sample}},\qquad \underbrace{s^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=\tfrac{n}{n-1}\widehat{\text{Var}}(X)}_{\text{unbiased — estimates the population}}.$$

Rule of thumb: use the biased divisor $n$ when you literally want the variance of the empirical distribution; use the unbiased divisor $n-1$ when estimating an unknown population variance (the default when the method is unspecified).

7. Transformation technique — find the law of $Y=g(X)$

Not on the syllabus per se, but it is the engine behind every "create a new distribution" result. Three steps: (1) get $F_X$; (2) rewrite $F_Y(y)=P(g(X)\le y)$ in terms of $F_X$ (flip the inequality if $g$ is decreasing — e.g. $Y=X^{-1}$ uses $S_X$); (3) differentiate to get $f_Y$ (never forget the Jacobian). This single method derives the inverse, transformed, scaled, and exponentiated families below.

8. Scale families and the scale parameter $\theta$

A distribution is a scale family if multiplying $X$ by a positive constant $c$ keeps it in the same family, changing only the scale parameter and leaving every other (shape) parameter fixed. In Loss Models the scale parameter is $\theta$, and:

$$ Y = cX \implies \theta_Y = c\,\theta_X, \quad \text{other (shape) parameters unchanged.}$$

This is the single most useful structural fact in the topic, because uniform inflation of all losses by factor $(1+r)$ is exactly multiplication by a constant. Every distribution in the exam tables with a $\theta$ parameter is parameterized so $\theta$ is the scale parameter — except the inverse Gaussian. The other parameters are shape parameters. Lognormal, normal, inverse Gaussian, and log-$t$ are the exceptions to the "just multiply $\theta$" rule (see Formulas → Scaling).

Symbols are arbitrary — roles are not

The exam may hand you a Pareto as $f(x)=\dfrac{\psi\tau^\psi}{(x+\tau)^{\psi+1}}$ (here $\psi$ is shape, $\tau$ is scale) or even with $\theta$ and $\alpha$ swapped. Identify the distribution from the form of the pdf, then assign shape vs. scale by role, not by letter. Our materials always list shape before scale, e.g. Gamma$(2,50)$ means $\alpha=2,\theta=50$.

9. Tail weight — the central organizing idea

A heavier tail means more probability far out — bigger, more frequent extreme claims, and a greater chance the mean or variance is infinite. From lightest to heaviest, a rough ordering of the common severity models:

$$ \text{Weibull}(\tau>1) \prec \text{gamma}(\alpha>1) \prec \text{exponential} \prec \text{Weibull}(\tau<1),\ \text{lognormal} \prec \text{Pareto},\ \text{Burr}.$$

Four practical tools for comparing tail weight (memorize all four):

  1. Existence of moments. The more positive moments a distribution has, the lighter the tail. Pareto only has moments $E[X^k]$ for $k < \alpha$ — so a Pareto with small $\alpha$ has an infinite mean. Any distribution with all positive moments (gamma, Weibull, lognormal) is lighter-tailed than one missing some.
  2. Limiting ratio of survival functions (or densities). If $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{S_1(x)}{S_2(x)} = \infty$, then distribution 1 has the heavier tail. By L'Hôpital this equals $\lim f_1(x)/f_2(x)$.
  3. Hazard rate. A decreasing hazard rate $\Rightarrow$ heavy tail; an increasing hazard rate $\Rightarrow$ light tail. (Exponential is the boundary: constant hazard = memoryless.)
  4. Mean excess function. An increasing mean excess function $e(d)$ $\Rightarrow$ heavy tail; decreasing $\Rightarrow$ light tail. (Again exponential is the flat boundary: $e(d) = \theta$, constant.)

These four agree: heavy tail $\Leftrightarrow$ fewer moments $\Leftrightarrow$ $S$-ratio blows up $\Leftrightarrow$ decreasing hazard $\Leftrightarrow$ increasing mean excess.

10. Hazard rate (failure rate) and mean excess $e(d)$

Hazard: $h(x)=f(x)/S(x)$. Among policyholders whose loss exceeds $x$, $h(x)\,dx$ is the chance the loss lands in $(x,x+dx)$. For the exponential it's the constant $1/\theta$ (memoryless). Mean excess:

$$ e(d) = E[X - d \mid X > d] = \frac{\int_d^\infty S(x)\,dx}{S(d)}=\frac{E[X]-E[X\wedge d]}{S(d)}.$$

In insurance, $e(d)$ is the expected payment per loss that exceeds an ordinary deductible $d$, among losses big enough to pay; in reliability it is the mean residual life. For the exponential, memorylessness gives $e(d)=\theta$ for all $d$. Whether $e(d)$ rises or falls in $d$ is a clean tail-weight diagnostic.

11. Creating new distributions — the manufacturing operations

These are the operations the exam loves. Each takes a known distribution and manufactures another.

  • (a) Multiplication by a constant $Y = cX$ ($c>0$): scaling. Within a scale family just multiply $\theta$ by $c$. Density transforms as $f_Y(y) = \tfrac{1}{c} f_X(y/c)$. Used for inflation.
  • (b) Raising to a power $Y = X^{1/\tau}$: generates transformed ($\tau>0$), inverse ($\tau=-1$), and inverse transformed ($\tau<0$) families. Example: $X$ exponential, $Y = X^{1/\tau}$ → Weibull.
  • (c) Inversion $Y=X^{-1}$: a special, heavily-tested power transform. For any table distribution with an inverse counterpart, $X^{-1}$ follows the inverse distribution with the same parameters except $\theta\to\theta^{-1}$ (e.g. gamma → inverse gamma, exponential → inverse exponential, Weibull → inverse Weibull, Pareto → inverse Pareto).
  • (d) Exponentiation $Y = e^{X}$: e.g., $X \sim \text{Normal}(\mu,\sigma^2) \Rightarrow Y = e^X \sim \text{Lognormal}(\mu,\sigma^2)$. The lognormal's $k$-th moment uses the normal MGF: $E[Y^k] = e^{k\mu + k^2\sigma^2/2}$.
  • (e) Shifting $Y=X+c$: e.g. a two-parameter Pareto shifted right by $\theta$ becomes a single-parameter Pareto.
  • (f) Mixing. Let a parameter be random. If $X \mid \Lambda$ has cdf $F(x\mid\lambda)$ and $\Lambda$ has density $u(\lambda)$, the mixture is $F_X(x) = \int F(x\mid\lambda)\,u(\lambda)\,d\lambda$. Mixing always produces a heavier (or equal) tail and increases variance. (An exponential whose mean is gamma/inverse-gamma-mixed yields a Pareto.)
  • (g) Splicing. Glue distinct densities on disjoint intervals: $f(x)=a_jf_j(x)$ on piece $j$, with each $f_j$ a legitimate density on its sub-interval and weights $a_j\ge0$ summing to 1.

12. The linear exponential family (LEF)

A distribution belongs to the linear exponential family if its probability function (discrete or continuous), with support not depending on $\theta$, can be written

$$ f(x;\theta)=\frac{p(x)\,e^{r(\theta)\,x}}{q(\theta)},$$

where $\theta$ is the natural parameter. Members: Poisson, binomial (fixed $m$), gamma, normal, negative binomial (fixed $r$). Non-members: beta, lognormal, inverse gamma, Pareto. For LEF members the mean and variance follow from $r,q$:

$$ E[X]=\mu(\theta)=\frac{q'(\theta)}{r'(\theta)\,q(\theta)},\qquad \text{Var}(X)=v(\theta)=\frac{\mu'(\theta)}{r'(\theta)}.$$

13. Discrete mixtures and the Bernoulli shortcut

A random variable $Y$ is a discrete ($n$-point) mixture if $f_Y(y)=\sum_{i=1}^n w_i f_{X_i}(y)$ with $\sum w_i=1$, $0\le w_i\le1$. Then $F_Y=\sum w_iF_{X_i}$, $S_Y=\sum w_iS_{X_i}$, and crucially $E[Y^k]=\sum w_iE[X_i^k]$. A mixture is not a linear combination $w_1X_1+w_2X_2$: in a mixture $Y$ is one of the $X_i$ (chosen with probability $w_i$); in a linear combination its value depends on all components at once. Treat mixtures via the conditioning laws of §5.

When a quantity takes only two values, the Bernoulli shortcut gives its variance without computing moments:

$$ X=\begin{cases}a,&\text{prob }q\\ b,&\text{prob }1-q\end{cases}\ \Longrightarrow\ \text{Var}(X)=(a-b)^2\,q(1-q).$$

It is the fast way to get $\text{Var}\big(E[X\mid \cdot]\big)$ in any two-point mixture.

Formulas

Notation

Throughout, $\theta>0$ is the scale parameter; $\alpha,\tau,a,b>0$ are shape parameters. $\Gamma(\cdot)$ is the gamma function, $\Gamma(\alpha;x)$ the incomplete gamma function, $\Phi$ the standard normal cdf.

Exponential$(\theta)$

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\theta}e^{-x/\theta}, \quad S(x) = e^{-x/\theta}, \quad h(x) = \frac{1}{\theta}, \quad e(d) = \theta.$$ $$ E[X] = \theta, \quad \text{Var}(X) = \theta^2, \quad E[X^k] = \theta^k\,k! \ (k \text{ integer}).$$
  • $\theta$: mean loss. Memoryless — the only continuous distribution that is (geometric is the discrete one): $X-d\mid X>d\sim\text{Exponential}(\theta)$, so $E[X-d\mid X>d]=\theta$, $\text{Var}=\theta^2$, and $P(X-d\le a\mid X>d)=P(X\le a)$.
  • Sum of $n$ i.i.d. Exponential$(\theta)$ is Gamma$(n,\theta)$.

Gamma$(\alpha,\theta)$

$$ f(x) = \frac{(x/\theta)^\alpha e^{-x/\theta}}{x\,\Gamma(\alpha)}, \quad F(x)=\Gamma\!\left(\alpha;\tfrac{x}{\theta}\right),\quad E[X]=\alpha\theta, \quad \text{Var}(X)=\alpha\theta^2, \quad E[X^k]=\frac{\theta^k\,\Gamma(\alpha+k)}{\Gamma(\alpha)}.$$
  • $\alpha$: shape. $\alpha>1$ → increasing hazard (lighter tail); $\alpha<1$ → decreasing hazard (heavier); $\alpha=1$ → exponential. $\Gamma(n)=(n-1)!$ for integer $n$ (one input), distinct from the incomplete gamma $\Gamma(\alpha;x)$ (two inputs).
  • Poisson shortcut for integer $\alpha$: $\Gamma(\alpha;x)=1-P(N<\alpha)$ where $N\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda=x)$. (Avoids the messy incomplete-gamma integral.)
  • Sum of independent gammas sharing $\theta$: $\sum_i\text{Gamma}(\alpha_i,\theta)=\text{Gamma}(\sum_i\alpha_i,\theta)$.

Pareto$(\alpha,\theta)$ (two-parameter, Loss Models)

$$ f(x)=\frac{\alpha\theta^\alpha}{(x+\theta)^{\alpha+1}}, \quad S(x)=\left(\frac{\theta}{x+\theta}\right)^{\alpha}, \quad h(x)=\frac{\alpha}{x+\theta}\ (\text{decreasing}), \quad e(d)=\frac{d+\theta}{\alpha-1}\ (\alpha>1).$$ $$ E[X]=\frac{\theta}{\alpha-1}\ (\alpha>1), \quad \text{Var}(X)=E[X]^2\cdot\frac{\alpha}{\alpha-2}=\frac{\alpha\theta^2}{(\alpha-1)^2(\alpha-2)}\ (\alpha>2).$$
  • Recognizable pdf shape: $f(x)=\dfrac{c}{(x+\theta)^{\alpha+1}}$ (constant over "$x$ plus a constant" to a power). Only moments of order $k<\alpha$ exist → heavy tail; $e(d)$ increases linearly → heavy tail. Mean needs $\alpha>1$, variance $\alpha>2$.
  • Special excess property: $X-d\mid X>d\sim\text{Pareto}(\alpha,\theta+d)$ — only $\theta$ shifts by $d$, $\alpha$ unchanged.

Single-parameter & inverse Pareto

Single-parameter Pareto$(\alpha,\theta)$, support $x>\theta$ $$ f(x)=\frac{\alpha\theta^\alpha}{x^{\alpha+1}},\quad E[X]=\frac{\alpha\theta}{\alpha-1},\quad \text{Var}(X)=\left(\frac{\theta}{\alpha-1}\right)^2\frac{\alpha}{\alpha-2}.$$ Inverse Pareto$(\tau,\theta)$ $$ f(x)=\frac{\tau\theta x^{\tau-1}}{(x+\theta)^{\tau+1}},\quad x>0.$$
  • S-P Pareto is a two-param Pareto shifted right by $\theta$ ($X=Y+\theta$): same variance, mean is $\theta$ larger. Its only true parameter is $\alpha$; $\theta$ is the (known) shift. Domain $x>\theta$ is the giveaway.
  • For S-P Pareto with $d>\theta$: $X\mid X>d\sim\text{S-P Pareto}(\alpha,d)$ and $X-d\mid X>d\sim\text{Pareto}(\alpha,d)$.
  • Inverse Pareto $=$ Pareto inverted ($X=Y^{-1}$, $\theta\to\theta^{-1}$); its pdf carries an extra $x^{\tau-1}$ in the numerator vs. a Pareto.

Weibull$(\theta,\tau)$ and inverse families

$$ F(x)=1-e^{-(x/\theta)^\tau}, \quad S(x)=e^{-(x/\theta)^\tau}, \quad f(x)=\frac{\tau (x/\theta)^\tau}{x}e^{-(x/\theta)^\tau},\quad h(x)=\frac{\tau}{\theta}\left(\frac{x}{\theta}\right)^{\tau-1}, \quad E[X^k]=\theta^k\,\Gamma\!\left(1+\frac{k}{\tau}\right).$$
  • Weibull is a power-transformed exponential: $Y\sim\text{Exp}(\mu)$, $X=Y^{1/\tau}\Rightarrow X\sim\text{Weibull}(\theta=\mu^{1/\tau},\tau)$. $\tau=1$ → exponential; $\tau>1$ → increasing hazard (light); $\tau<1$ → decreasing hazard (heavy). The pdf differs from gamma's by raising $x/\theta$ to a power in the exponent.
  • Inverse rule (general): if $X$ is in a table family with an inverse counterpart, $X^{-1}$ follows it with $\theta\to\theta^{-1}$ and other parameters unchanged — gamma→inverse gamma$(\alpha,\theta^{-1})$, exponential→inverse exponential$(\theta^{-1})$, Weibull→inverse Weibull$(\theta^{-1},\tau)$.

Lognormal$(\mu,\sigma^2)$

$$ f(x)=\frac{1}{x\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left[-\frac{(\ln x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right], \quad F(x)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln x-\mu}{\sigma}\right),\quad \pi_p=e^{\mu+z_p\sigma}.$$ $$ E[X^k]=\exp\!\left(k\mu+\tfrac{1}{2}k^2\sigma^2\right), \quad E[X]=e^{\mu+\sigma^2/2}, \quad \text{Var}(X)=E[X]^2\!\left(e^{\sigma^2}-1\right).$$
  • $X=e^Y$ with $Y\sim\text{Normal}(\mu,\sigma^2)$; $\mu,\sigma^2$ are the mean/variance of $\ln X$, not of $X$. $e^\mu$ is the scale (median). Probabilities/percentiles via $\Phi$; moments via the exponential formula. Heavier than exponential, lighter than Pareto.
  • Product of independent lognormals is lognormal: parameters add, $\text{LN}(\mu_1+\mu_2,\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)$.

Normal$(\mu,\sigma^2)$, standard normal, sums

$$ f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)},\quad P(X\le x)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right),\quad \pi_p=\mu+z_p\sigma,\quad z_p=\Phi^{-1}(p).$$
  • Parameters are the mean and variance. Standardize $Z=(X-\mu)/\sigma$; by symmetry $\Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z)$. Sum of independent normals is normal with summed means and summed variances.

Beta$(a,b,\theta)$ and Uniform$(a,b)$

Beta — finite support $0\lt x<\theta$ $$ f(x)=\frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}\left(\frac{x}{\theta}\right)^a\!\left(1-\frac{x}{\theta}\right)^{b-1}\frac1x,\quad E[X]=\frac{a}{a+b}\theta,\quad \text{Var}(X)=\frac{ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)}\theta^2.$$ Uniform$(a,b)$ $$ f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a},\quad E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2},\quad \text{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12},\quad E[X^2]=\frac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}.$$
  • Beta is recognized by finite support and purely polynomial terms (no exponentials/logs). Don't drop the trailing $\tfrac1x$. With $\theta=1$: $f(x)=c\,x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1}$, $E[X^2]=\dfrac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}$.
  • Uniform$(0,\theta)\equiv$ Beta$(a=1,b=1,\theta)$. Excess: $X\mid X>d\sim\text{Uniform}(d,b)$ and $X-d\mid X>d\sim\text{Uniform}(0,b-d)$.

Hazard rate and mean excess — general

$$ h(x)=\frac{f(x)}{S(x)}=-\frac{d}{dx}\ln S(x), \qquad S(x)=\exp\!\left(-\int_0^x h(t)\,dt\right),\qquad e(d)=\frac{\int_d^\infty S(x)\,dx}{S(d)}=\frac{E[X]-E[X\wedge d]}{S(d)},$$

where $E[X\wedge d]=\int_0^d S(x)\,dx$ is the limited expected value (mean of $X$ capped at $d$).

Tail-weight comparison criteria

  • Moments: distribution with more finite positive moments is lighter-tailed.
  • Survival ratio: $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{S_1(x)}{S_2(x)}=\infty \Rightarrow$ dist. 1 heavier; $=0\Rightarrow$ dist. 1 lighter.
  • Hazard: $h\downarrow \Rightarrow$ heavy; $h\uparrow \Rightarrow$ light.
  • Mean excess: $e(d)\uparrow \Rightarrow$ heavy; $e(d)\downarrow \Rightarrow$ light.

Scaling / inflation rules

Inflate losses by factor $c=(1+r)$
  • Any table distribution except lognormal, inverse Gaussian, log-$t$: $\theta\to c\theta$, other parameters unchanged. Density $f_Y(y)=\tfrac1c f_X(y/c)$.
  • Normal: $\mu\to c\mu$, $\sigma\to c\sigma$ (so variance $\to c^2\sigma^2$).
  • Lognormal: $\mu\to\mu+\ln c$, $\sigma^2$ unchanged.
  • Everything else: fall back to the transformation technique.

Manufacturing operations & mixtures

Build-a-distribution toolbox
  • Power: $Y=X^{1/\tau}$, density $f_Y(y)=f_X(y^\tau)\,\tau y^{\tau-1}$.
  • Inversion: $Y=X^{-1}$ → inverse counterpart with $\theta\to\theta^{-1}$.
  • Exponentiation: $Y=e^X$, density $f_Y(y)=f_X(\ln y)/y$.
  • Continuous mixture: $f_X(x)=\int f(x\mid\lambda)u(\lambda)\,d\lambda$; $E[X^k]=E_\Lambda\!\big[E[X^k\mid\Lambda]\big]$.
  • Law of total variance: $\text{Var}(X)=E[\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda)]+\text{Var}(E[X\mid\Lambda])$.
  • Discrete mixture: $f_Y=\sum w_if_{X_i}$, $\sum w_i=1$; $E[Y^k]=\sum w_iE[X_i^k]$.
  • Bernoulli shortcut: two-value $X$ → $\text{Var}(X)=(a-b)^2q(1-q)$.
  • Splicing: $f(x)=a_jf_j(x)$ on interval $j$, $\sum_j a_j=1$, each $f_j$ a density on its sub-interval.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Inflation in a scale family (Pareto) ▾

Losses follow a Pareto with $\alpha=3$ and $\theta=2000$. Next year all losses inflate by 5%. Find next year's mean loss and the probability a loss exceeds 5000 next year.

Step 1 — Scale the parameter. Pareto is a scale family, $\theta$ is the scale. Inflation by 5%: $\theta_{\text{new}}=1.05\times 2000=2100$; $\alpha=3$ unchanged.

Step 2 — Mean. $E[X]=\dfrac{\theta}{\alpha-1}=\dfrac{2100}{3-1}=1050.$

Step 3 — Tail probability. $S(5000)=\left(\dfrac{\theta}{x+\theta}\right)^\alpha=\left(\dfrac{2100}{5000+2100}\right)^3=(0.29577)^3=0.025878.$

Answer
Mean $=\boxed{1050}$; $P(X>5000)=\boxed{0.0259}$ (≈ 2.59%).

Sanity check via old parameters: old $E[X]=2000/2=1000$; inflating the mean directly gives $1000\times1.05=1050$. ✓ (Mean inflates by the same factor — true in any scale family.)

Example 2 Mean excess and tail comparison ▾

(a) An exponential has $\theta=500$. Find $e(1000)$.
(b) A Pareto has $\alpha=2.5,\ \theta=600$. Find $e(1000)$.
(c) Which has the heavier tail, and is the mean-excess behavior consistent?

Part (a). Exponential is memoryless: $e(d)=\theta=500$ for every $d$. So $e(1000)=500$.

Part (b). Pareto: $e(d)=\dfrac{d+\theta}{\alpha-1}=\dfrac{1000+600}{2.5-1}=\dfrac{1600}{1.5}=1066.67.$

Part (c). For the exponential $e(d)$ is constant; for the Pareto $e(d)=\dfrac{d+600}{1.5}$ increases without bound in $d$. An increasing mean-excess function signals a heavier tail, so the Pareto is heavier. Confirm with moments: the Pareto with $\alpha=2.5$ has finite mean and variance but no moments of order $\ge 2.5$, whereas the exponential has all moments — consistent with the Pareto being heavier-tailed.

Answer
$e_{\text{exp}}(1000)=\boxed{500}$; $e_{\text{Par}}(1000)=\boxed{1066.67}$; the Pareto has the heavier tail (increasing $e(d)$, fewer moments). ✓ consistent.
Example 3 Mixing: exponential rate gamma-mixed gives Pareto (and total variance) ▾

The conditional loss is $X \mid \Theta \sim \text{Exponential}(\Theta)$ (mean $\Theta$). The scale $\Theta$ itself is random with an inverse-gamma distribution such that $E[\Theta]=400$ and the mixture is Pareto with $\alpha=3,\ \theta=800$.

(a) Verify $E[X]$ using both the mixture and the Pareto.
(b) Suppose instead $X\mid\Lambda \sim \text{Exponential}$ with mean $\Lambda$, where $\Lambda$ takes value 200 w.p. 0.6 and 700 w.p. 0.4 (a discrete mixture). Find $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.

Part (a). Pareto mean: $E[X]=\dfrac{\theta}{\alpha-1}=\dfrac{800}{3-1}=400$. Via mixing, $E[X]=E_\Theta[E[X\mid\Theta]]=E[\Theta]=400$. They match.

Part (b) — discrete mixture, use total expectation/variance.

Conditional on $\Lambda=\lambda$: $X$ is exponential with mean $\lambda$, so $E[X\mid\Lambda]=\lambda$ and $\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda)=\lambda^2$.

Mean: $E[X]=E[\Lambda]=0.6(200)+0.4(700)=400.$

Total variance:

  • $E[\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda)]=E[\Lambda^2]=0.6(200^2)+0.4(700^2)=24000+196000=220000.$
  • $\text{Var}(E[X\mid\Lambda])=\text{Var}(\Lambda)$. Via the Bernoulli shortcut: $(200-700)^2(0.6)(0.4)=250000(0.24)=60000.$
  • $\text{Var}(X)=220000+60000=280000.$
Answer
(a) Both give $E[X]=\boxed{400}$. (b) $E[X]=\boxed{400}$, $\text{Var}(X)=\boxed{280000}$ (sd ≈ 529.2).

Heavy-tail signature of mixing: a single exponential with mean 400 would have variance $400^2=160000$; mixing inflated it to 280000. Note the Bernoulli shortcut reproduced $\text{Var}(\Lambda)$ instantly — no need for $E[\Lambda^2]-(E[\Lambda])^2$.

Example 4 Lognormal probability, percentile, and inflation ▾

Losses are lognormal with $\mu=7,\ \sigma=1.5$. (a) Find $P(X>3000)$. (b) Find $E[X]$. (c) After 10% inflation, give the new parameters and the new mean. (d) Find the 95th percentile of the original losses.

Part (a). $P(X>3000)=1-\Phi\!\left(\dfrac{\ln 3000-\mu}{\sigma}\right)$. $\ln 3000=8.00637$, $z=\dfrac{8.00637-7}{1.5}=0.6709$, $\Phi(0.6709)\approx0.7488$, so $P(X>3000)=0.2512.$

Part (b). $E[X]=e^{\mu+\sigma^2/2}=e^{7+1.125}=e^{8.125}=3380.0.$

Part (c). Inflation 10%: $\mu\to7+\ln(1.1)=7.09531$; $\sigma=1.5$ unchanged. New mean $=e^{7.09531+1.125}=e^{8.22031}=3718.0=1.1\times3380.$

Part (d). $z_{0.95}=1.64485$, $y_{0.95}=\mu+z_{0.95}\sigma=7+1.64485(1.5)=9.46728$, $\pi_{0.95}=e^{9.46728}=12{,}912.$

Answer
(a) $\boxed{0.2512}$; (b) $\approx\boxed{3380}$; (c) new $\mu=\boxed{7.0953}$, $\sigma=1.5$, new mean $\approx\boxed{3718}$; (d) $\pi_{0.95}\approx\boxed{12{,}912}$.
Example 5 Gamma cdf via the Poisson shortcut ▾

$X\sim\text{Gamma}(\alpha=2,\theta=5)$. Compute $F(20)$.

The gamma cdf is $F(20)=\Gamma\!\left(2;\tfrac{20}{5}\right)=\Gamma(2;4)$. Since $\alpha=2$ is a positive integer, use the Poisson shortcut with $N\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda=4)$:

$$F(20)=1-P(N<2)=1-\big[p_N(0)+p_N(1)\big]=1-\big(e^{-4}+4e^{-4}\big)=1-5e^{-4}=0.9084.$$

Answer
$F(20)=\boxed{0.9084}$ — no incomplete-gamma integral required.
Example 6 Pareto excess property — payment above a deductible ▾

Loss size $X\sim\text{Pareto}(\alpha=3,\theta=500)$. Insurance covers the excess over 200. Find $P(\text{insurer pays} >500 \mid \text{loss} >200)$, i.e. $P(X-200>500\mid X>200)$.

Method 1 — special property. $X-200\mid X>200\sim\text{Pareto}(3,\,500+200)=\text{Pareto}(3,700)$. Then $$P(X-200>500\mid X>200)=\left(\frac{700}{500+700}\right)^3=(0.58333)^3=0.1985.$$

Method 2 — direct. $\dfrac{P(X>700)}{P(X>200)}=\dfrac{(500/1200)^3}{(500/700)^3}=\dfrac{0.0723}{0.3644}=0.1985.$ ✓

Answer
$\boxed{0.1985}$. Under the Pareto only $\theta$ shifts ($\to\theta+d$); $\alpha$ stays put.
Example 7 Identify a beta from its pdf, then get a moment ▾

$f(x)=105\,x^4(1-x)^2,\ 0\lt x<1$. Find $E[X^2]$.

Finite support $[0,1]$ and purely polynomial ⇒ beta with $\theta=1$, of form $c\,x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1}$. Match exponents: $a-1=4\Rightarrow a=5$; $b-1=2\Rightarrow b=3$. So $X\sim\text{Beta}(5,3,1)$.

$$E[X^2]=\frac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}=\frac{5\cdot6}{8\cdot9}=\frac{30}{72}=\frac{5}{12}.$$

Answer
$E[X^2]=\boxed{5/12}$. Recognizing the family beats grinding the integral $105\int_0^1 x^6(1-x)^2dx$.
Example 8 Three-point mixture of exponentials (mean, sd, probability) ▾

Annual loss is a mixture of exponentials by risk class: Low (mean 300, $P=0.40$), Medium (mean 540, $P=0.25$), High (mean 800, $P=0.35$). Find (1) $P(X<500)$, (2) $E[X]$, (3) the standard deviation.

(1) $P(X<500)=\sum w_i\big(1-e^{-500/\theta_i}\big)=0.4(1-e^{-500/300})+0.25(1-e^{-500/540})+0.35(1-e^{-500/800})=0.6381.$

(2) $E[X]=\sum w_i\theta_i=0.4(300)+0.25(540)+0.35(800)=535.$

(3) Mix the second moments ($E[X^2\mid i]=2\theta_i^2$ for an exponential): $E[X^2]=0.4(2\cdot300^2)+0.25(2\cdot540^2)+0.35(2\cdot800^2)=665{,}800.$ Then $\text{Var}(X)=665{,}800-535^2=379{,}575$, sd $=616.10.$

Answer
(1) $\boxed{0.6381}$, (2) $E[X]=\boxed{535}$, (3) sd $\approx\boxed{616.10}$. Mix moments, never first-moments-squared.
Example 9 Mixture vs. linear combination — same weights, different variance ▾

$X\sim\text{Pareto}(4,240)$ and $Y\sim\text{Pareto}(3,300)$, independent. Find (1) $\text{Var}(Z)$ where $f_Z=0.4f_X+0.6f_Y$, and (2) $\text{Var}(T)$ where $T=0.4X+0.6Y$.

Component moments (Pareto): $E[X]=240/3=80$, $\text{Var}(X)=80^2(4/2)=12{,}800$; $E[Y]=300/2=150$, $\text{Var}(Y)=150^2(3/1)=67{,}500$.

(1) Mixture. $E[\text{Var}(Z\mid M)]=0.4(12{,}800)+0.6(67{,}500)=45{,}620.$ Bernoulli shortcut on the conditional means $\{80,150\}$: $\text{Var}(E[Z\mid M])=(80-150)^2(0.4)(0.6)=1{,}176.$ So $\text{Var}(Z)=45{,}620+1{,}176=46{,}796.$

(2) Linear combination. $\text{Var}(T)=0.4^2(12{,}800)+0.6^2(67{,}500)+2(0.4)(0.6)\underbrace{\text{Cov}}_{=0}=2{,}048+24{,}300=26{,}348.$

Answer
$\text{Var}(Z)=\boxed{46{,}796}$ (mixture) vs. $\text{Var}(T)=\boxed{26{,}348}$ (linear combo). Same weights — different objects, different variance.
Example 10 Empirical distribution from a small sample ▾

Sample of size 6: $\{1,4,5,8,8,10\}$. Find (1) $F_6(6)$, (2) the empirical 75th percentile, (3) the unbiased sample variance.

(1) Observations $\le6$: $\{1,4,5\}$ ⇒ $F_6(6)=3/6=0.5.$

(2) $\lceil 6(0.75)\rceil=\lceil4.5\rceil=5$, so $\pi_{0.75}=x_{(5)}=8.$

Example 11 CV, skewness, kurtosis from a density ▾

$f(x)=x/8,\ 0\le x\le4$. Find the CV, skewness, and kurtosis.

Moments: $E[X]=\int_0^4 x\cdot\tfrac{x}{8}dx=\tfrac{8}{3}$, $E[X^2]=8$, $E[X^3]=25.6$, $E[X^4]=85.333$. So $\text{Var}=8-(8/3)^2=8/9$.

CV $=\dfrac{\sqrt{8/9}}{8/3}=0.3536.$

Skewness: $\mu_3=\mu_3'-3\mu_2'\mu+2\mu^3=25.6-3(8)(\tfrac83)+2(\tfrac83)^3=-0.4741$; skew $=\dfrac{-0.4741}{(8/9)^{3/2}}=-0.5657.$

Kurtosis: $\mu_4=\mu_4'-4\mu_3'\mu+6\mu_2'\mu^2-3\mu^4=85.333-4(25.6)(\tfrac83)+6(8)(\tfrac83)^2-3(\tfrac83)^4=1.8963$; kurt $=\dfrac{1.8963}{(8/9)^2}=2.4.$

Answer
CV $=\boxed{0.3536}$, skewness $=\boxed{-0.5657}$ (left-skewed), kurtosis $=\boxed{2.4}$ ($<3$, fewer outliers than normal).
Example 12 Transformation technique — pdf of $Y=X^{-1}$ for an exponential ▾

$X$ has pdf $f_X(x)=\tfrac15 e^{-x/5},\ x>0$ (exponential with $\theta=5$). Let $Y=X^{-1}$. Find the pdf of $Y$ from first principles, then name the distribution.

Step 1 — cdf of $X$. $F_X(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x \tfrac15 e^{-t/5}\,dt = 1-e^{-x/5}$, so $S_X(x)=e^{-x/5}$.

Step 2 — restate the cdf of $Y$ using $F_X$. Because $g(x)=x^{-1}$ is decreasing, the inequality flips:

$$F_Y(y)=\Pr(Y\le y)=\Pr\!\big(X^{-1}\le y\big)=\Pr\!\big(X\ge y^{-1}\big)=S_X\!\big(y^{-1}\big)=e^{-1/(5y)},\quad y>0.$$

Step 3 — differentiate (mind the Jacobian).

$$f_Y(y)=\frac{d}{dy}\,e^{-(1/5)y^{-1}} = e^{-1/(5y)}\cdot\frac{d}{dy}\!\left(-\tfrac15 y^{-1}\right)=\frac{1}{5y^{2}}\,e^{-1/(5y)},\quad y>0.$$
Answer
$f_Y(y)=\dfrac{1}{5y^{2}}e^{-1/(5y)},\ y>0$ — an inverse exponential. Matches the inverse rule: $X\sim\text{Exponential}(\theta)\Rightarrow X^{-1}\sim\text{Inverse Exponential}(\theta^{-1})$, here $\theta^{-1}=1/5$.

Increasing-$g$ variant ($Y=e^{X}$). If instead $F_X(x)=x/10$ on $0\le x\le10$ and $Y=e^{X}$, then $g$ is increasing so the inequality does not flip: $F_Y(y)=\Pr(X\le\ln y)=\tfrac{\ln y}{10}$, giving $f_Y(y)=\dfrac{1}{10y}$ on $1\le y\le e^{10}$. The recipe is identical — only the inequality direction (and the Jacobian) change.

Example 13 Mode of a continuous density via $f'(x)=0$ with the $f''<0$ check ▾

$f(x)=0.75\big[1-(x-1)^2\big],\ 0<x<2$. Find the mode.

Step 1 — first-order condition. The mode maximizes the pdf, so set $f'(x)=0$:

$$f'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\,0.75\big[1-(x-1)^2\big]=-1.5\,(x-1)=0\;\Rightarrow\;x=1.$$

Step 2 — confirm it is a maximum. Second-derivative test:

$$f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\big[-1.5(x-1)\big]=-1.5<0,$$

which is negative everywhere, so the critical point $x=1$ is a local (here global) maximum, not a minimum.

Answer
Mode $=\boxed{1}$. The $f''<0$ check is what certifies it as a max — and you should still scan the domain endpoints, since a global maximum can sit at an endpoint where $f'$ never vanishes.
Example 14 The hidden $(X\mid q)$ table — law of total expectation over $q$ ▾

A claim-size table is given as

Claim size $x$$\Pr(X=x)$
$50$$0.30$
$100$$0.70-q$
$150$$q$

where $q$ varies by insured with density $f(q)=\dfrac{q}{0.175},\ 0.1<q<0.6$. Find (a) $E[X]$ and (b) $\Pr(X=100)$.

Spot the trap

The table secretly describes $(X\mid q)$, not $X$ — the probabilities depend on the random $q$. You cannot read $E[X]$ straight off the table; you must average over $q$ with the law of total expectation. (Bayes' Theorem, $\Pr(A\mid B)=\dfrac{\Pr(B\mid A)\Pr(A)}{\Pr(B)}$, is the conditioning identity these total-probability laws are built on.)

Sanity check $f(q)$ is a density: $\displaystyle\int_{0.1}^{0.6}\frac{q}{0.175}\,dq=\frac{1}{0.175}\cdot\frac{0.6^2-0.1^2}{2}=\frac{0.175}{0.175}=1.$ ✓ Also $E[q]=\displaystyle\int_{0.1}^{0.6}\frac{q^2}{0.175}\,dq=\frac{1}{0.175}\cdot\frac{0.6^3-0.1^3}{3}=\frac{0.215}{0.525}=\frac{43}{105}=0.40952.$

(a) Condition first, then average.

$$E[X\mid q]=50(0.30)+100(0.70-q)+150q=15+70-100q+150q=85+50q.$$ $$E[X]=E_q\big[E[X\mid q]\big]=E_q[85+50q]=85+50\,E[q]=85+50\!\left(\tfrac{43}{105}\right)=105.48.$$

(b) Same idea for a probability (law of total probability).

$$\Pr(X=100)=E_q\big[\Pr(X=100\mid q)\big]=E_q[0.70-q]=0.70-\tfrac{43}{105}=0.2905.$$
Answer
(a) $E[X]=\boxed{105.48}$; (b) $\Pr(X=100)=\boxed{0.2905}$. Reading $E[X]$ as $50(0.3)+100(0.7-q)+150q$ and leaving $q$ in the answer is the classic miss — the table is conditional on $q$.

Discrete inequality reminder. For this discrete $X$, keep $S(x)=\Pr(X>x)$ and $F(x)=\Pr(X\le x)$ strict-vs-non-strict apart: e.g. $F(100)=\Pr(X\le100)$ includes the mass at $100$, whereas $S(100)=\Pr(X>100)=q$ excludes it, so $F(x)=\Pr(X<x)+p(x)\ne\Pr(X<x)$. That distinction is harmless for continuous $X$ but decisive here.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  1. Inflating shape parameters. Under inflation only the scale $\theta$ changes ($\to(1+r)\theta$). Shape parameters ($\alpha$, $\tau$, $a,b$) do not move. Lognormal: $\mu\to\mu+\ln(1+r)$, never $e^\mu$ literally and never $\sigma$. Normal: scale both $\mu$ and $\sigma$.
  2. Using a moment that doesn't exist. Pareto $E[X]$ needs $\alpha>1$, $\text{Var}(X)$ needs $\alpha>2$. If $\alpha\le1$ the mean is infinite — a negative $\theta/(\alpha-1)$ is the tell, not an answer.
  3. Confusing the two "Pareto" parameterizations. Two-parameter: $S=(\theta/(x+\theta))^\alpha$ on $x>0$. Single-parameter: support $x>\theta$, $S=(\theta/x)^\alpha$, mean $\alpha\theta/(\alpha-1)$. Same variance as the two-param, mean larger by $\theta$. Domain is the giveaway.
  4. $f(x)$ is not a probability. A density can exceed 1; $P(X=a)=0$ for continuous $X$. Don't read $f(x)$ as $P(X=x)$ or compare densities as if they were probabilities.
  5. Discrete percentiles aren't $F^{-1}(p)$. The shortcut $F(\pi_p)=p$ only holds for continuous $X$. For discrete (and empirical) data, use the full definition / order statistics; a value can be many percentiles and a percentile can be a whole interval.
  6. Central vs. raw moments. $\text{Var}=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$ uses raw moments; skewness/kurtosis use central moments $\mu_3,\mu_4$. Convert via $\mu_3=\mu_3'-3\mu_2'\mu+2\mu^3$, etc. Don't divide a raw moment by $\sigma^3$.
  7. Biased vs. unbiased sample variance. Divide by $n$ for the variance of the empirical distribution; by $n-1$ when estimating the population. Mixing them up flips an answer choice.
  8. Hazard vs. mean-excess direction. Heavy tail ⇒ hazard decreasing, mean excess increasing — opposite directions. Attach "increasing/decreasing" to the right function.
  9. Forgetting the Jacobian. For $Y=X^{1/\tau}$, $Y=e^X$, or $Y=X^{-1}$, $f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\,|dg^{-1}/dy|$. Dropping the derivative factor yields a non-density. For decreasing $g$ (like inversion) work through $S_X$ and flip the inequality.
  10. Mixture variance ≠ average of variances. Always use the law of total variance; the term $\text{Var}(E[X\mid\Lambda])$ is what makes mixtures heavier-tailed. The two-point Bernoulli shortcut $(a-b)^2q(1-q)$ delivers it fast.
  11. Mixture ≠ linear combination. $f_Z=w_1f_{X_1}+w_2f_{X_2}$ (one component is realized) is a different object from $T=w_1X_1+w_2X_2$ (all components at once) — different variances even with identical weights (Example 9).
  12. Mixing first moments instead of second. $E[Z^2]=\sum w_iE[X_i^2]\ne\sum w_iE[X_i]^2$. For exponentials remember $E[X^2]=2\theta^2$.
  13. Lognormal moments via $\Phi$ — don't. Probabilities/percentiles use $\Phi$; moments use $E[X^k]=e^{k\mu+k^2\sigma^2/2}$. Keep the two computations separate, and remember $\mu,\sigma^2$ are the moments of $\ln X$, not $X$.
  14. Incomplete vs. ordinary gamma. $\Gamma(\alpha;x)$ (two inputs) is the gamma cdf factor; $\Gamma(\alpha)$ (one input) is the constant $=(\alpha-1)!$ for integer $\alpha$. Use the Poisson shortcut $\Gamma(\alpha;x)=1-P(N<\alpha)$, $N\sim\text{Poisson}(x)$, only for integer $\alpha$.
  15. Splicing weights must give a valid density. Each piece $a_jf_j$ must integrate to $a_j$ over its sub-interval (so $f_j$ is a real density there), and the $a_j$ sum to 1. No renormalizing = invalid.
  16. Beta pdf housekeeping. Don't drop the trailing $\tfrac1x$ in the table form, and remember most exam betas set $\theta=1$ (support $0\lt x<1$).

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write down how $f$, $S$, $h$, and $H$ relate, and recover $S$ from a hazard rate.
$f=F'=-S'$; $S=1-F$; $h=f/S=-\frac{d}{dx}\ln S$; $H=\int h=-\ln S$, so $S(x)=e^{-H(x)}=\exp(-\int_0^x h(t)\,dt)$. Given $h$, integrate to $H$, then exponentiate the negative.
For a continuous $X$ with $f(x)=0.5x$ on $(0,2)$, find the median and 3rd quartile.
$F(x)=0.25x^2$ on $(0,2)$. Median: $0.25\pi_{0.5}^2=0.5\Rightarrow\pi_{0.5}=\sqrt2$. Third quartile: $0.25\pi_{0.75}^2=0.75\Rightarrow\pi_{0.75}=\sqrt3$.
Losses are exponential with $\theta=250$. What is $e(800)$, and why doesn't the deductible matter?
Memoryless: $e(d)=\theta$ for all $d$, so $e(800)=\boxed{250}$. The excess $X-d\mid X>d$ is again Exponential$(\theta)$, independent of $d$.
A Pareto has $\alpha=1.5,\ \theta=1000$. Does its mean exist? Its variance?
Mean exists ($\alpha>1$): $E[X]=1000/0.5=2000$. Variance does not (needs $\alpha>2$; here $1.5$). Pareto has $E[X^k]$ finite only for $k<\alpha$.
If $X\sim\text{Pareto}(\alpha,\theta)$, what is the distribution of $X-d\mid X>d$? Of $X^{-1}$?
$X-d\mid X>d\sim\text{Pareto}(\alpha,\theta+d)$ (only $\theta$ shifts). $X^{-1}\sim\text{Inverse Pareto}(\tau=\alpha,\theta^{-1})$ (inverse rule: $\theta\to\theta^{-1}$, other params unchanged).
$X\sim\text{Gamma}(3,\theta=2)$. Express $F(8)$ using the Poisson shortcut.
$F(8)=\Gamma(3;8/2)=\Gamma(3;4)=1-P(N<3)$ with $N\sim\text{Poisson}(4)$ $=1-(e^{-4}+4e^{-4}+8e^{-4})=1-13e^{-4}=0.7619.$
Which of gamma$(\alpha=0.5)$ and gamma$(\alpha=3)$ has the heavier tail, and by which criterion?
gamma$(\alpha=0.5)$. For gamma, $\alpha<1$ ⇒ decreasing hazard (heavy), $\alpha>1$ ⇒ increasing hazard (light). Decreasing hazard ⇒ heavier tail.
$X$ is lognormal with $\mu=6,\ \sigma=2$. Compute $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.
$E[X]=e^{6+2}=e^8=2980.96$. $\text{Var}(X)=E[X]^2(e^{\sigma^2}-1)=e^{16}(e^4-1)\approx 4.76\times10^{8}$.
$X$ is lognormal with $\mu=0.4,\ \sigma^2=0.25$. Find the 95th percentile and $P(X\le2)$.
$z_{0.95}=1.64485$, $y_{0.95}=0.4+1.64485(0.5)=1.22242$, $\pi_{0.95}=e^{1.22242}=3.3954$. $P(X\le2)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln2-0.4}{0.5}\right)=\Phi(0.58629)=0.7212.$
Identify the family: $f(x)=c\,x^{2}e^{-x/4}$ on $x>0$ vs. $g(x)=c\,x^{2}e^{-(x/4)^3}$. Name both.
$f$: $x$ to a power times $e^{-x/\theta}$ ⇒ gamma ($\alpha-1=2\Rightarrow\alpha=3$, $\theta=4$). $g$: the exponent has $(x/\theta)$ raised to a power ⇒ Weibull ($\tau=3$, $\theta=4$). That power in the exponent is the gamma-vs-Weibull tell.
$X\mid\Lambda$ is exponential with mean $\Lambda$; $\Lambda=100$ w.p. 0.5 and $500$ w.p. 0.5. Find $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.
$E[X]=E[\Lambda]=300$. $E[\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda)]=E[\Lambda^2]=0.5(100^2)+0.5(500^2)=130000$. Bernoulli shortcut: $\text{Var}(\Lambda)=(100-500)^2(0.5)(0.5)=40000$. Total $\text{Var}(X)=170000$.
Sample $\{2,3,3,7\}$: give $F_4(3)$, the empirical 50th percentile, and the biased sample variance.
$F_4(3)=3/4=0.75$ (three obs $\le3$). $\pi_{0.5}=x_{(\lceil4\cdot0.5\rceil)}=x_{(2)}=3$. $\bar x=15/4=3.75$; biased var $=\frac{4+9+9+49}{4}-3.75^2=17.75-14.0625=3.6875.$
Name a distribution in the linear exponential family and one that is not, and state why LEF matters.
In LEF: gamma, normal, Poisson, binomial (fixed $m$), negative binomial (fixed $r$). Not: Pareto, lognormal, beta, inverse gamma. It matters because LEF members have tidy mean/variance via $\mu(\theta)=q'/(r'q)$, $v(\theta)=\mu'/r'$, and the family underlies later credibility/GLM theory.
Why is a mixture $f_Z=0.5f_X+0.5f_Y$ generally heavier-tailed and higher-variance than either component?
By the law of total variance, $\text{Var}(Z)=E[\text{Var}(Z\mid \text{component})]+\text{Var}(E[Z\mid\text{component}])$. The second term (spread of the conditional means) is added on top of the average component variance, so mixing never reduces variance and fattens the tail.
