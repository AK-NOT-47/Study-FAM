Loss Reserving for Short-Term Insurance

A claim incurred today may not be paid for years — reserving is the discipline of estimating those future payments from a development triangle.

📖 Loss Reserving · Chain-Ladder & BF ⏱️ ~3 hrs 🔑 CDF · IBNR · ELR · BF · Cape Cod 🔁 BF = weighted avg of CL & ELR
Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic, the SOA expects you to be able to:

  • Define paid losses, case reserve, and incurred (reported) loss, and trace a single claim's loss-development history through time.
  • Distinguish between case reserves, IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported), and the total loss reserve, and list the four components that make up the gross IBNR (bulk) reserve.
  • Construct and interpret a loss development (run-off) triangle — reading its rows (accident year), columns (development year), and diagonals (calendar year) — and convert between incremental and cumulative losses.
  • Estimate ultimate losses and reserves using:
    • The Expected Loss Ratio (Expected Claims) Method
    • The Chain-Ladder (Development) Method
    • The Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) Method — both the standard form and the weighted-average form
    • The Cape Cod (Stanard-Bühlmann) Method
    • The Frequency-Severity Method
  • Compute age-to-age (link-ratio) factors and age-to-ultimate / cumulative development factors (CDFs), and select them using the simple average, n-year average, and volume-weighted average models.
  • Develop paid losses with paid factors (→ loss reserve) and incurred losses with incurred factors (→ IBNR reserve), and incorporate a tail factor for development beyond the observed triangle.
  • Compare methods and recognize when each is most appropriate (mature vs. immature years, stable vs. volatile experience, new lines with thin data).

Key Concepts

The vocabulary of a claim

Before any method, nail the three building-block terms:

  • Paid losses — amounts already paid to claimants.
  • Case reserve — an adjuster's estimate of the remaining money needed to settle a known claim.
  • Incurred (reported) loss — paid losses plus the current case reserve: $\text{Incurred} = \text{Paid} + \text{Case Reserve}$.
  • Ultimate losses — the total amount the insurer eventually pays to fully close the claim.

Watch how a single claim evolves: the loss event occurs, then is reported (often months later), a case reserve is set, partial payments are made, the reserve is revised up or down as facts emerge, and finally the claim closes. At closure, incurred = paid (the case reserve drops to zero). The table below traces one such claim through three year-end valuations — note the case reserve absorbing uncertainty until the final settlement:

Valuation dateIncurred-to-DatePaid-to-DateCase Reserve

The ultimate here is 285,000 — the final settled amount that both paid and incurred converge to.

Why reserve at all? Case reserves vs. IBNR

Summing case reserves over all known claims is not enough — it misses claims the insurer doesn't yet know about and future development on the ones it does. So actuaries add a gross IBNR reserve (a.k.a. bulk reserve) with four components:

  1. Future development on known claims — adjustments (usually upward) to existing case reserves (this piece is IBNER: Incurred But Not Enough Reported).
  2. Reopened claims — files closed but that may reopen (e.g., workers' compensation).
  3. Pure IBNR — claims incurred but not yet reported to the insurer at all.
  4. RBNR — claims reported but not yet recorded in the system.
Loss reserve decomposition$$\text{Loss Reserve} = \text{Case Reserve} + \text{IBNR Reserve}$$

Every method below shares the same engine — project ultimate losses, then strip out what's already been paid:

$$\text{Loss Reserve} = \text{Estimated Ultimate Losses} - \text{Losses Paid-to-Date}$$
Three reserves, one diagram

The base you subtract decides which reserve you get:

  • $\text{Loss Reserve} = \hat L^{ult} - L^{P}$  (subtract paid-to-date)
  • $\text{IBNR Reserve} = \hat L^{ult} - L^{I}$  (subtract incurred-to-date)
  • $\text{Loss Reserve} - \text{IBNR Reserve} = L^{I} - L^{P} = \text{Case Reserve}$

The development (run-off) triangle

Losses are grouped by accident year (AY) — the year the loss event occurred (rows) — and tracked as they develop over development years (DY) — how many years after the accident year we are observing (columns). Because recent accident years have had fewer years to develop, the data forms a triangle:

AY \ DY0123

The diagonal (lower-right edge) is the most recent valuation date — what we know today. The chain-ladder method fills in the missing lower-right corner.

Reading the three dimensions

Denote a single cell $l_{i,k}$ (incremental) or $L_{i,k}$ (cumulative), where $i$ = accident year and $k$ = development year:

  • Row = accident year. Summing a full row gives all payments on that AY's claims (even though those payments were spread across many later calendar years).
  • Column = development year (age). Column 0 is paid in the accident year itself; column 1 the next calendar year, etc. $l_{2014,3}$ is paid in DY 3 on AY-2014 claims.
  • Upward-sloping diagonal = calendar year. All cells with $i + k = \text{CY}$ were paid in the same calendar year. For example, $l_{2013,0}$, $l_{2012,1}$, and $l_{2011,2}$ are all CY-2013 payments.

Triangles can be cumulative (running totals, used for chain-ladder) or incremental (period-by-period payments). Chain-ladder factors need cumulative data — if you're handed incrementals, accumulate each row first: $L_{i,k} = \sum_{j=0}^{k} l_{i,j}$.

The Expected Loss Ratio (Expected Claims) Method — intuition

Ignore the actual reported losses entirely. Set ultimate equal to an a priori expectation: earned premium × expected loss ratio (ELR), or exposure × pure premium. The loss ratio is simply losses ÷ premium — the slice of premium the insurer pays out in losses; the ELR comes from analyzing historical loss ratios.

  • Strength: Stable; immune to noisy early development. Often the only option for a brand-new line with no claims history, and a handy sanity check on fancier methods.
  • Weakness: Ignores actual emerging experience, and the ELR is easy to manipulate — so it can produce illogical or insufficient reserves if the year is genuinely off.

The Chain-Ladder (Development) Method — intuition

Also called the loss development method or loss triangle method. Assume each accident year develops in the same proportional pattern. If, historically, cumulative losses grow by a factor of 1.5 from age 0 to age 1, we apply 1.5 to the latest year too. Multiply the current diagonal value by all the future age-to-age factors to reach ultimate.

  • Strength: Purely data-driven; great for mature years with stable patterns.
  • Weakness: Extremely sensitive to the latest diagonal for immature years — a small reported amount times a large CDF produces a wildly leveraged (and unstable) ultimate. A few outliers can swing the result, and structural changes (e.g., management deciding to settle claims faster mid-study) inflate recent age-to-age factors and over-state the reserve. Actuarial judgment is needed before trusting the data.

Selecting age-to-age factors

Each cell of the factor triangle is $f_{i,k} = L_{i,k}/L_{i,k-1}$. You then select one factor per column. Three standard models:

  • Simple (straight) average — arithmetic mean of all individual factors in the column.
  • n-year average — arithmetic mean of only the n most recent factors in the column (responsive to recent experience; common when older years aren't representative).
  • Volume-weighted average (a.k.a. "mean") — sum of the later cumulative losses ÷ sum of the earlier cumulative losses, over the AYs that have both entries.

The Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method — the compromise

BF blends chain-ladder and expected loss ratio. The key insight: trust actual reported losses for the portion already developed, and use the a priori expectation for the unreported portion. From chain-ladder, $L^P/\hat L^{ult} = 1/f^{ult}$ is the proportion paid, so $1 - 1/f^{ult}$ is the proportion unpaid. BF applies that unpaid proportion to the expected (ELR) ultimate:

$$R_{BF} = \hat L^{ult}_{LR}\left(1 - \frac{1}{f^{ult}_{CL}}\right), \qquad \hat L^{ult}_{LR} = P^E \times \text{ELR}$$

As a year matures ($f^{ult} \to 1$), BF converges to the chain-ladder; for green years (large $f^{ult}$), BF leans on the expected claims. This is why BF is the workhorse for recent accident years.

BF = a credibility weighting of CL and ELR

The BF reserve is exactly a weighted average of the two parent reserves, with the weight equal to the proportion already developed:

$$R_{BF} = w\,R_{CL} + (1-w)\,R_{LR}, \qquad w = \frac{1}{f^{ult}_{CL}}$$

The more mature the year (small $f^{ult}$, so $w \to 1$), the more weight chain-ladder gets — because more actual data is available. Brand-new years lean on the expected-loss-ratio reserve.

The Cape Cod (Stanard-Bühlmann) Method

Cape Cod is BF but with the expected loss ratio estimated from the triangle itself rather than assumed externally. It computes a single ELR for all years:

$$\text{ELR} = \frac{\sum \text{Reported Losses}}{\sum (\text{Premium} \times \%\text{ reported})}$$

The denominator is the "used-up premium" — premium weighted by how developed each year is. Then it applies the BF formula using this fitted ELR. Cape Cod is more responsive than pure BF (it lets the data set the loss ratio) but more stable than chain-ladder (it pools across years).

The Frequency-Severity Method

Estimate ultimate claim counts (frequency) and ultimate average severity separately, then multiply:

$$\text{Ultimate Losses} = \text{Ultimate Counts} \times \text{Ultimate Severity}$$

Each component can be developed with its own triangle. Useful when counts develop more stably than dollars, or for trending severity with inflation.

Paid vs. incurred triangles, and tail factors

In theory paid and incurred losses converge to the same ultimate (both reach the final settlement amount), but in practice they don't — so never assume they're equal unless told. The rule: develop paid losses only with paid factors, and incurred losses only with incurred factors. Subtract paid-to-date for the loss reserve; subtract incurred-to-date for the IBNR reserve.

The triangle only shows development up to the oldest available age. If losses keep developing beyond that (e.g., long-tail liability), multiply the last cumulative development factor by a tail factor > 1.0 to capture development past the observed window.

Formulas

Notation

$L_{i,k}$ = cumulative reported (paid or incurred) loss for accident year $i$ at development age $k$; $l_{i,k}$ = the corresponding incremental loss; $f_k$ = selected age-to-age (link-ratio) factor from age $k-1$ to age $k$; $f^{ult}$ (a.k.a. $\text{CDF}$) = age-to-ultimate / cumulative development factor; $t$ = age of claims at valuation; $\hat L^{ult}_i$ = estimated ultimate loss for AY $i$; $P^E$ = earned premium; $\text{ELR}$ = expected loss ratio; $L^P$ = losses paid-to-date; $L^I$ = losses incurred-to-date.

Reserve identities

$$\text{Loss Reserve} = \text{Case Reserve} + \text{IBNR Reserve}$$ $$R = \hat L^{ult} - L^{P}, \qquad \text{IBNR} = \hat L^{ult} - L^{I}, \qquad R - \text{IBNR} = L^{I} - L^{P} = \text{Case Reserve}$$

Age-to-age (link ratio) factors

For a single cell, $f_{i,k} = L_{i,k}/L_{i,k-1}$. The three selection models:

Simple average $$f_k = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \frac{L_{i,k}}{L_{i,k-1}}$$ n-year average (most recent $n$ factors) $$f_k = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in \text{last } n} \frac{L_{i,k}}{L_{i,k-1}}$$ Volume-weighted average ("mean") $$f_k = \frac{\sum_i L_{i,k}}{\sum_i L_{i,k-1}}$$

Volume-weighted is the most common chain-ladder default; in every model sum only over accident years $i$ that have both $L_{i,k-1}$ and $L_{i,k}$ available.

Age-to-ultimate factor (CDF) and projecting ultimate

$$f^{ult}_i = \prod_{k=t+1}^{\infty} f_k \;=\; f_{t+1}\, f_{t+2}\cdots f_{n}\, f_{\text{tail}}$$ $$\hat L^{ult}_i = L_{i,t}\cdot f^{ult}_i, \qquad L^P_i = L_{i,t}$$

$f_{\text{tail}}$ = tail factor (use 1.0 if no tail development). If the triangle assumes no development past DY $n$, then $f_{n+1}=f_{n+2}=\cdots=1$, so the product stops at $f_n$.

Percent reported (developed)

$$\%\text{ reported at age } t = \frac{1}{f^{ult}_t}, \qquad \%\text{ unreported at age } t = 1 - \frac{1}{f^{ult}_t}$$

Expected Loss Ratio Method

$$\hat L^{ult}_i = P^E_i \times \text{ELR}_i \quad(\text{or}\quad \text{Exposure}_i \times \text{Pure Premium})$$ $$R_i = \hat L^{ult}_i - L^P_i$$

Chain-Ladder (Development) Method

$$\hat L^{ult}_i = L_{i,t}\cdot f^{ult}_i, \qquad R_i = \hat L^{ult}_i - L^P_i$$

Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method

Standard form $$R_{BF} = \hat L^{ult}_{LR}\left(1 - \frac{1}{f^{ult}_{CL}}\right), \qquad \hat L^{ult}_{LR} = P^E\times\text{ELR}$$ BF ultimate (do NOT use $P^E\times\text{ELR}$ as the BF ultimate) $$\hat L^{ult}_{BF} = L^P + R_{BF}$$ Weighted-average form $$R_{BF} = w\,R_{CL} + (1-w)\,R_{LR}, \qquad w = \frac{1}{f^{ult}_{CL}}$$

Use the paid age-to-ultimate factor to get the total loss reserve; use the incurred age-to-ultimate factor to get the IBNR reserve.

Cape Cod (Stanard-Bühlmann) Method

Fitted expected loss ratio, then BF with this single pooled ELR:

$$\widehat{\text{ELR}} = \frac{\sum_i L_{i,t}}{\sum_i P^E_i \cdot \dfrac{1}{f^{ult}_i}}, \qquad \hat L^{ult}_i = L_{i,t} + P^E_i\,\widehat{\text{ELR}}\left(1 - \frac{1}{f^{ult}_i}\right)$$

The denominator term $P^E_i / f^{ult}_i$ is the used-up (earned-developed) premium for AY $i$.

Frequency-Severity Method

$$\hat L^{ult}_i = \hat N_i \times \hat S_i$$
  • $\hat N_i$ = ultimate claim count for AY $i$ (developed from a count triangle).
  • $\hat S_i$ = ultimate average severity (developed from a severity triangle, possibly trended for inflation).

Total reserve

$$\text{Total Reserve} = \sum_i \left(\hat L^{ult}_i - L^P_i\right)$$

Example 2 Chain-Ladder Ultimate and Reserve ▾

Given the following cumulative paid loss triangle (\$000s):

AY \ DY012
20231,0001,5001,650
20241,2001,800
20251,100

Find the estimated reserve for all accident years combined using the chain-ladder method (no tail).

Step 1 — Volume-weighted age-to-age factors.

Age 0→1: years 2023 and 2024 have both columns.

$$f_1 = \frac{1{,}500 + 1{,}800}{1{,}000 + 1{,}200} = \frac{3{,}300}{2{,}200} = 1.500$$

Age 1→2: only 2023 has both columns.

$$f_2 = \frac{1{,}650}{1{,}500} = 1.100$$

Step 2 — Age-to-ultimate factors.

  • At age 1: $f^{ult} = f_2 = 1.100$
  • At age 0: $f^{ult} = f_1 \times f_2 = 1.500 \times 1.100 = 1.650$

Step 3 — Develop each diagonal to ultimate.

  • 2023 (at age 2, fully mature): $U = 1{,}650 \times 1.000 = 1{,}650$
  • 2024 (at age 1): $U = 1{,}800 \times 1.100 = 1{,}980$
  • 2025 (at age 0): $U = 1{,}100 \times 1.650 = 1{,}815$

Step 4 — Reserve = Ultimate − Paid to date.

  • 2023: $1{,}650 - 1{,}650 = 0$
  • 2024: $1{,}980 - 1{,}800 = 180$
  • 2025: $1{,}815 - 1{,}100 = 715$

Total reserve $= 0 + 180 + 715 = 895$.

Answer
Total estimated reserve = \$895,000.

Example 7 Back-solving for the chain-ladder reserve ▾

Given: BF reserve $=77{,}900$, ELR reserve $=78{,}600$, earned premium $=250{,}000$, ELR $=0.65$. Find the chain-ladder reserve.

Step 1 — Recover $f^{ult}$ from the BF formula.

$$77{,}900 = 250{,}000(0.65)\left(1 - \frac{1}{f^{ult}}\right) \;\Rightarrow\; f^{ult} = 1.9208$$

Step 2 — Recover paid-to-date from the ELR reserve.

$$78{,}600 = 250{,}000(0.65) - L^P \;\Rightarrow\; L^P = 83{,}900$$

Step 3 — Chain-ladder ultimate and reserve.

$$\hat L^{ult}_{CL} = 83{,}900(1.9208) = 161{,}155 \;\Rightarrow\; R_{CL} = 161{,}155 - 83{,}900 = 77{,}255$$
Answer
$R_{CL} = $ 77,255. (Cross-check via $R_{BF}=wR_{CL}+(1-w)R_{LR}$ with $w=1/1.9208$ gives the same value.)
Example 8 Cape Cod (Stanard-Bühlmann) ▾

Premiums 2,500 and 2,400; reported diagonals 1,800 (AY 2024, age 1) and 1,100 (AY 2025, age 0); age-to-ultimate factors 1.100 and 1.650.

Step 1 — Used-up premium = Premium / $f^{ult}$.

  • 2024: $2{,}500 / 1.100 = 2{,}272.7$
  • 2025: $2{,}400 / 1.650 = 1{,}454.5$
  • Total $= 3{,}727.2$

Step 2 — Sum of reported losses: $1{,}800 + 1{,}100 = 2{,}900$.

Step 3 — Fitted ELR: $\widehat{\text{ELR}} = 2{,}900 / 3{,}727.2 = 0.7780$.

Step 4 — Cape Cod ultimate for 2025:

$$1{,}100 + 2{,}400(0.7780)(1 - 1/1.650) = 1{,}100 + 735.4 = 1{,}835.4$$
Answer
Cape Cod ELR ≈ 0.778; 2025 ultimate ≈ $1,835.4K, reserve ≈ $735.4K.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Cumulative vs. incremental confusion. Chain-ladder factors require cumulative triangles. If the problem gives incremental payments, accumulate the rows first. This is the #1 error.
  • Match the AY to its age, not its row position. An accident year's age at valuation is (valuation year − accident year). Apply the age-to-ultimate factor for that age (see Example 4).
  • Wrong entries in the volume-weighted average. Numerator and denominator must have the same number of entries — only include AYs that have both adjacent columns. Including a year missing the next column understates the denominator.
  • Forgetting the tail factor. A given tail factor multiplies into every CDF, even the oldest "fully developed" year — so a mature year can still carry a reserve.
  • $P^E\times\text{ELR}$ is NOT the BF ultimate. It's only the expected ultimate used inside the unreported term. The BF ultimate is $\hat L^{ult}_{BF} = L^P + R_{BF}$, and the first piece of any BF figure is actual reported losses, not the chain-ladder ultimate.
  • $1/f^{ult}$ (reported) vs. $1 - 1/f^{ult}$ (unreported). BF and Cape Cod multiply expected losses by the unreported fraction; Cape Cod's used-up premium uses the reported fraction $1/f^{ult}$; the BF weight is $w = 1/f^{ult}$.
  • Reserve vs. ultimate vs. IBNR. Subtract paid-to-date for the loss reserve, incurred-to-date for IBNR. The question may ask for any of the three — read carefully.
  • Don't mix paid and incurred development. Develop paid losses with paid factors and incurred losses with incurred factors. Never assume paid ultimate = incurred ultimate unless told.
  • Cape Cod uses a single pooled ELR, not a per-year ELR — total reported ÷ total used-up premium across all years.
  • n-year vs. all-year average. A "3-year average" uses only the three most recent link ratios per column — don't average the whole column.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Define paid losses, case reserve, and incurred (reported) loss, and give the relationship between them.
Paid losses are amounts already paid to claimants. Case reserve is an adjuster's estimate of the remaining money needed to settle a known claim. Incurred (reported) loss = paid losses + current case reserve. At final settlement the case reserve goes to zero, so incurred = paid = ultimate.
Name the four components of the gross IBNR (bulk) reserve.
(1) Future development on known claims (IBNER — adjustments to case reserves); (2) reopened claims (closed files that may reopen, e.g., workers' comp); (3) pure IBNR (claims incurred but not yet reported); (4) RBNR (claims reported but not yet recorded). Together: $\text{Loss Reserve} = \text{Case Reserve} + \text{IBNR}$.
If you subtract losses-incurred-to-date from estimated ultimate, which reserve do you get? What about subtracting paid-to-date?
Subtracting incurred-to-date gives the IBNR reserve. Subtracting paid-to-date gives the loss reserve. Their difference, $L^I - L^P$, is the case reserve.
In a loss triangle, how are accident year, development year, and calendar year related to a cell $L_{i,k}$?
The row $i$ is the accident year (when the loss occurred), the column $k$ is the development year (age of the claim), and the upward-sloping diagonal is the calendar year $i+k$ (when the payment was actually made).
Name the three age-to-age factor selection models and how each is computed.
Simple average: arithmetic mean of all link ratios in the column. n-year average: arithmetic mean of only the $n$ most recent link ratios. Volume-weighted average: $\sum L_{i,k} / \sum L_{i,k-1}$ over AYs with both columns.
Can ultimate losses be estimated from paid losses, incurred losses, or both? Any rules?
Both. In theory they converge to the same ultimate, but in practice they differ — so don't assume they're equal. Rule: develop paid losses only with paid factors and incurred losses only with incurred factors.
Write the BF reserve formula and state what each term represents.
$R_{BF} = \hat L^{ult}_{LR}\,(1 - 1/f^{ult}_{CL})$ where $\hat L^{ult}_{LR} = P^E\times\text{ELR}$ is the a-priori expected ultimate and $1 - 1/f^{ult}$ is the chain-ladder estimate of the unreported proportion. The BF ultimate is $L^P + R_{BF}$.
Write the weighted-average form of the BF reserve. Which factor sets the weight, and what does that imply for mature years?
$R_{BF} = w\,R_{CL} + (1-w)\,R_{LR}$ with $w = 1/f^{ult}_{CL}$. As a year matures, $f^{ult}\to 1$ so $w\to 1$ — more weight to the chain-ladder (actual-experience) reserve, because more real data is available.
A cumulative paid triangle shows AY 2024 at \$4,000 at age 1, age-1-to-ultimate CDF 1.25. Chain-ladder ultimate and reserve?
Ultimate $= 4{,}000 \times 1.25 = 5{,}000$. Reserve $= 5{,}000 - 4{,}000 = 1{,}000$.
How does Cape Cod differ from a standard BF application?
Cape Cod estimates the ELR from the triangle itself (total reported losses ÷ total used-up premium) instead of using an externally assumed a-priori ELR, then plugs that single fitted ELR into the BF formula. More responsive than pure BF, more stable than chain-ladder.
When is the expected loss ratio method the best (or only) choice, and what is its main weakness?
Best when there's little or no claims data (a new line of business) or as a reasonableness check on more sophisticated methods. Weakness: it relies entirely on the ELR — which is easy to manipulate and ignores actual emerging experience — so it can give illogical or insufficient reserves.
