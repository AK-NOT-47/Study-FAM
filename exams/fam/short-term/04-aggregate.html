Aggregate Loss Models & Risk Measures

Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic, the SOA expects you to be able to:

  • Construct and distinguish between the collective risk model ($S = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_N$, with primary frequency and secondary severity distributions) and the individual risk model ($S = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n$ with fixed $n$).
  • Compute the probability function $p_S(s)$ of aggregate losses directly via the Law of Total Probability / $n$-fold convolution when $n$ is small.
  • Compute the mean and variance of aggregate losses $S$ using the compound moment formulas.
  • Recognize and exploit the special properties of the compound Poisson distribution (additivity, splitting, simplified variance).
  • Compute the distribution of $S$ via convolution for discrete severities, and find probabilities such as $\Pr(S = k)$ and $\Pr(S \le k)$.
  • Apply the Panjer (a, b, 0) recursion to build the aggregate distribution efficiently.
  • Compute stop-loss premiums $E[(S - d)_+]$ via the limited-expected-value relationship $E[S]-E[S\wedge d]$, both exactly (discrete) and from approximations.
  • Approximate the distribution of $S$ using the normal (with continuity correction) and lognormal approximations, and compute probabilities and stop-loss premiums from them.
  • Model aggregate payments consistently ŌĆö either number-of-losses with payment-per-loss $Y^L$, or number-of-payments with payment-per-payment $Y^P$.
  • Compute and interpret risk measures: Value-at-Risk (VaR), Tail-Value-at-Risk (TVaR/CTE), evaluate coherence (translation invariance, positive homogeneity, subadditivity, monotonicity), and compare tail weight.

Key Concepts

Two ways to build aggregate losses

Aggregate losses $S$ for a portfolio over a period can be modeled two ways:

Collective risk model (compound model):

$$S = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_N,$$

where $N$ is a random number of claims (the frequency) and each $X_i$ is the severity (size) of an individual claim. Key assumptions:

  1. $N, X_1, X_2, \dots$ are mutually independent.
  2. The $X_i$ are i.i.d. with the same severity distribution.
  3. $N$ is independent of the values of the $X_i$.

Here $S$ is a random sum ŌĆö both the number of terms and the size of each term are random. When $N = 0$, we define $S = 0$. The distribution of the frequency $N$ is called the primary distribution, the severity $X$ is the secondary distribution, and the resulting distribution of $S$ is the compound distribution.

Coach's Remark

On the exam, most aggregate questions use the collective risk model. The independence requirement is usually as simple as confirming that frequency and severity are independent.

Individual risk model:

$$S = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n,$$

where $n$ is a fixed, known number of policies (we simplify the frequency to a constant but generalize the severity). Each $X_i$ is the total loss on policyholder $i$. The $X_i$ are independent but not necessarily identically distributed (different policies can have different distributions). Often each $X_i = I_i B_i$, where $I_i$ is a Bernoulli "did a claim occur" indicator and $B_i$ is the claim amount given a claim occurs.

Intuition: the collective model thinks "how many claims hit the whole book, and how big is each"; the individual model thinks "policy by policy, what's the loss on each."

Probability function via the Law of Total Probability

The exact distribution of a discrete $S$ comes from conditioning on the number of claims:

$$p_S(s) = \sum_{n>0} p_N(n)\,\Pr\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = s \,\Big|\, N=n\Big), \quad s>0.$$

The probability in the sum is the $n$-fold convolution of the severity. Because $S = 0$ when there are no losses or all losses are zero,

$$p_S(0) = p_N(0) + \sum_{n>0} p_N(n)\,\Pr\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = 0 \,\Big|\, N=n\Big).$$

The same conditioning gives the aggregate CDF ŌĆö and this form works whether the severity is discrete or continuous, which is exactly what you need when each $X_i$ is, say, Normal or Uniform:

Aggregate CDF $$F_S(s) = p_N(0) + \sum_{n>0} p_N(n)\,\Pr\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \le s \,\Big|\, N=n\Big).$$

The $p_N(0)$ term is the mass at $S=0$ (no claims), and each remaining term weights the CDF of an $n$-fold sum of severities by $p_N(n)$. With a small frequency support (e.g. $N\in\{0,1,2\}$) this collapses to $p_N(0)+p_N(1)F_X(s)+p_N(2)F_{X_1+X_2}(s)+\dots$ ŌĆö a one-line Law-of-Total-Probability calculation.

Coach's Remark

These equations are very general ŌĆö don't memorize them blindly. Exam questions usually limit the possible values of $n$ (e.g. $N\le 2$) so you only need to enumerate the handful of ways the claims can sum to $s$. Understand the structure and you can rebuild it on the fly.

Compound distributions

When $N$ is random, $S$ has a compound distribution. Its name comes from the frequency: a compound Poisson has $N \sim \text{Poisson}$, a compound negative binomial has $N \sim$ negative binomial, etc.

The mean and variance come from conditioning on $N$ (double expectation / law of total variance). The key results are the most-tested formulas on this entire topic.

Why compound Poisson is special

If $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, three beautiful things happen:

  1. Variance simplifies: $\text{Var}(S) = \lambda \, E[X^2]$ (the second raw moment of severity, not the variance).
  2. Additivity: The sum of independent compound Poisson random variables is again compound Poisson, with $\lambda = \sum \lambda_j$ and a severity distribution that is the $\lambda$-weighted mixture of the individual severities.
  3. Splitting: The reverse of additivity. Categorize each claim independently (e.g. "below deductible" vs "above deductible") with probabilities $q_j$; the count in each category is independent Poisson with mean $\lambda q_j$.

Splitting is the engine behind the "number of payments" approach to aggregate payments: scale the Poisson mean by $\Pr(\text{loss exceeds deductible})$. This works only for Poisson, not binomial or negative binomial.

Convolutions

To get the exact distribution of a sum of independent random variables, you convolve. For two independent discrete random variables $X$ and $Y$:

$$\Pr(X + Y = k) = \sum_{j} \Pr(X = j)\Pr(Y = k - j).$$

The $n$-fold convolution $f_X^{*n}$ is the distribution of $X_1 + \dots + X_n$. The aggregate distribution is then a frequency-weighted mixture of convolutions:

$$\Pr(S = x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \Pr(N = n)\, f_X^{*n}(x).$$

This is exact but slow. Panjer recursion replaces it with a fast recursion for an important class of frequencies.

Stop-loss insurance

A stop-loss (or aggregate excess-of-loss) policy imposes an aggregate deductible $d$ ŌĆö applied to the aggregate loss $S$, in contrast to an ordinary per-claim deductible applied to each individual loss. It pays $(S - d)_+ = \max(S - d, 0)$. The net stop-loss premium is the expected payment:

$$E[(S - d)_+] = E[S] - E[S \wedge d].$$

Because the distribution of $S$ can be hard to pin down, there is no single universal formula ŌĆö the rule of thumb is to identify the pattern and apply basic probability. Fortunately $S$ is usually discrete in these questions, so a small table of $\Pr(S=s)$ and $S\wedge d$ does the job.

Aggregate payments

Beyond aggregate losses, insurers model aggregate payments. There are two equivalent constructions ŌĆö the key is that frequency and severity must be consistent (both "loss" or both "payment"):

  • Number of losses ├Ś payment per loss: $S = \sum_{i=1}^{N} Y_i^{L}$. Here $N$ counts all losses and $Y^L$ is the payment per loss ŌĆö which can be $0$ when a loss falls below the deductible.
  • Number of payments ├Ś payment per payment: $S = \sum_{i=1}^{N'} Y_i^{P}$. Here $N'$ counts only losses producing a positive payment, and $Y^P$ is the payment given a payment occurs.

Both give the same $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$. When there is no deductible, $Y^P$ and $Y^L$ are identical and the two approaches coincide.

Approximations

For large portfolios, computing the exact distribution is impractical, so we moment-match a simple distribution to $S$:

  • Normal approximation: match $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$; treat $S \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Justified by the CLT (sum of i.i.d. severities); improves as the number of claims grows. Simple but can be poor in the right tail because $S$ is usually right-skewed.
  • Lognormal approximation: match $E[S]$ and $E[S^2]$ to a lognormal, capturing skewness better.

Continuity correction. The normal/lognormal are continuous, so when the aggregate model is discrete (both frequency and severity discrete) you should shift by half a "step" to avoid assigning zero probability to point masses. With unit integer support, $\Pr(S=s)\approx\Pr(s-0.5\le S\le s+0.5)$, so $\Pr(S\le s)\Rightarrow\Pr(S\le s+0.5)$ and $\Pr(S\ge s)\Rightarrow\Pr(S\ge s-0.5)$. When $S$ only takes spread-out values (e.g. $200, 400, 1000$), use the midpoints between adjacent possible values as the correction boundaries.

Risk measures

A risk measure $\rho(X)$ maps a loss random variable to a single number quantifying risk ŌĆö used to gauge solvency. The two headline measures are Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Tail-Value-at-Risk (TVaR).

  • VaR $=\text{VaR}_p(X)=\pi_p$ is just the $100p$th percentile: the value the loss exceeds with probability $1-p$. (Do not confuse with variance!)
  • TVaR (a.k.a. CTE, conditional tail expectation) is the expected loss given the loss already exceeds its VaR ŌĆö the average severity of a bad tail event.

A measure is coherent if it satisfies four properties (translation invariance, positive homogeneity, subadditivity, monotonicity). TVaR is coherent; VaR is not (it can fail subadditivity). Tail weight compares how likely extreme losses are: fewer finite positive raw moments ŌćÆ heavier tail.

Formulas

Mean and variance (general compound model)

Let $E[N], \text{Var}(N)$ be the frequency moments and $E[X], \text{Var}(X)$ the severity moments.

Mean $$E[S] = E[N]\,E[X] \qquad(\mu_S=\mu_N\mu_X)$$ Variance $$\text{Var}(S) = E[N]\,\text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(N)\,\big(E[X]\big)^2 \qquad(\sigma_S^2=\mu_N\sigma_X^2+\sigma_N^2\mu_X^2)$$
Symbols
  • $S$ ŌĆö aggregate loss (random sum).
  • $N$ ŌĆö claim count (frequency, the primary distribution).
  • $X$ ŌĆö individual claim size (severity, the secondary distribution); $E[X]$ first moment, $E[X^2] = \text{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2$ second raw moment.

Equivalent form using the second moment of severity (handy when you have $E[X^2]$):

$$\text{Var}(S) = E[N]\,E[X^2] + \big(\text{Var}(N) - E[N]\big)\big(E[X]\big)^2.$$

Compound Poisson ($N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$)

Since $E[N] = \text{Var}(N) = \lambda$:

$$E[S] = \lambda\,E[X], \qquad \text{Var}(S) = \lambda\,\big(\text{Var}(X)+E[X]^2\big)=\lambda\,E[X^2].$$

Third central moment (for skewness):

$$E\big[(S - E[S])^3\big] = \lambda\,E[X^3], \qquad \text{Skew}(S) = \frac{\lambda E[X^3]}{\big(\lambda E[X^2]\big)^{3/2}}.$$
Additivity & splitting

If $S_1, \dots, S_m$ are independent compound Poisson with parameters $\lambda_j$ and severity cdfs $F_j$, then $S = \sum_j S_j$ is compound Poisson with

$$\lambda = \sum_{j=1}^{m}\lambda_j, \qquad F_X(x) = \sum_{j=1}^{m}\frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda}\,F_j(x).$$

Conversely (splitting), if claims are independently sorted into category $j$ with probability $q_j$, the count in category $j$ is independent Poisson with mean $\lambda q_j$.

Compound negative binomial / binomial frequency

If $N \sim \text{Negative Binomial}(r, \beta)$: $E[N] = r\beta$, $\text{Var}(N) = r\beta(1+\beta)$.

If $N \sim \text{Binomial}(m, q)$: $E[N] = mq$, $\text{Var}(N) = mq(1-q)$.

Plug into the general $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$ formulas above.

Individual risk model

For $S = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$ with independent (not necessarily identical) $X_i$:

$$E[S] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} E[X_i], \qquad \text{Var}(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{Var}(X_i).$$

If $X_i = I_i B_i$ with $\Pr(I_i = 1) = q_i$ (claim probability) and $B_i$ the claim amount (independent of $I_i$):

$$E[X_i] = q_i\,E[B_i],$$ $$\text{Var}(X_i) = q_i\,\text{Var}(B_i) + q_i(1 - q_i)\big(E[B_i]\big)^2.$$

If the claim amount is a fixed constant $b_i$ (so $\text{Var}(B_i)=0$):

$$E[X_i] = q_i b_i, \qquad \text{Var}(X_i) = q_i(1-q_i)\,b_i^2.$$
Bernoulli shortcut

For any two-valued variable $X=\begin{cases}a,&q\\ b,&1-q\end{cases}$ the variance is $\text{Var}(X)=(a-b)^2\,q(1-q)$ ŌĆö no need to compute first and second moments separately.

Aggregate payments ŌĆö compound formulas

The compound mean and variance hold for payments too, in either consistent parameterization:

$$E[S]=E[N]\,E[Y^L]=E[N']\,E[Y^P],$$ $$\text{Var}(S)=E[N]\,\text{Var}(Y^L)+\text{Var}(N)\,E[Y^L]^2=E[N']\,\text{Var}(Y^P)+\text{Var}(N')\,E[Y^P]^2.$$

Convolution

Discrete severity on $0, 1, 2, \dots$ with pmf $f_X$:

$$f_X^{*n}(x) = \sum_{y} f_X^{*(n-1)}(x - y)\,f_X(y), \qquad f_X^{*0}(0) = 1.$$ $$f_S(x) = \Pr(S = x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\Pr(N = n)\,f_X^{*n}(x).$$

Panjer recursion ŌĆö the $(a, b, 0)$ class

A frequency $N$ is in the $(a, b, 0)$ class if its pmf $p_n = \Pr(N = n)$ satisfies

$$\frac{p_n}{p_{n-1}} = a + \frac{b}{n}, \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$$
Distribution$a$$b$$p_0$
Poisson$(\lambda)$$0$$\lambda$$e^{-\lambda}$

With a severity defined on $0, 1, 2, \dots$ with pmf $f_X(j)$ (let $f_k = f_X(k)$), the aggregate pmf $g_s = \Pr(S = s)$ is built by:

Starting value (probability of zero aggregate loss):

$$g_0 = P_N\big(f_X(0)\big),$$

the probability generating function of $N$ evaluated at $f_X(0)$. Common cases:

  • Poisson: $g_0 = e^{-\lambda(1 - f_0)}$.
  • If $f_X(0) = 0$ (severity has no mass at 0): $g_0 = p_0 = \Pr(N=0)$.

Recursion for $s = 1, 2, 3, \dots$:

$$g_s = \frac{1}{1 - a\,f_0}\sum_{j=1}^{s}\left(a + \frac{b\,j}{s}\right) f_j\, g_{s-j}.$$
  • $a, b$ ŌĆö the frequency's $(a,b,0)$ parameters.
  • $f_j = f_X(j)$ ŌĆö severity pmf at $j$.
  • $g_{s-j}$ ŌĆö previously computed aggregate probabilities.

(If $f_0 = 0$, the $\frac{1}{1 - a f_0}$ factor is just $1$.)

Stop-loss premium

General definition:

$$E[(S - d)_+] = \int_d^\infty \big(1 - F_S(x)\big)\,dx = \int_d^\infty (x - d)\,f_S(x)\,dx.$$

Relation to the mean and the limited expected value $E[S \wedge d]$ (the most useful exam form):

$$E[(S - d)_+] = E[S] - E[S \wedge d].$$

Discrete $S$ (mass points $0, 1, 2, \dots$), integer retention $d$:

$$E[(S - d)_+] = \sum_{x > d}(x - d)\,f_S(x) = \sum_{x=d}^{\infty}\big(1 - F_S(x)\big).$$
Recursion across integer retentions (very efficient on exams)
$$E[(S - (d+1))_+] = E[(S - d)_+] - \big(1 - F_S(d)\big).$$

Each unit increase in the deductible reduces the stop-loss premium by the survival probability at $d$.

Normal approximation

Set $\mu = E[S]$, $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(S)$. Then $S \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$\Pr(S \le s) \approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{s - \mu}{\sigma}\right).$$

Stop-loss premium under the normal approximation:

$$E[(S - d)_+] \approx \sigma\,\phi\!\left(\frac{d - \mu}{\sigma}\right) - (d - \mu)\left[1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{d - \mu}{\sigma}\right)\right],$$

where $\phi$ is the standard normal pdf and $\Phi$ the standard normal cdf.

Lognormal approximation

Match the first two moments of $S$ to a lognormal with parameters $\mu_L, \sigma_L$. Using $m_1 = E[S]$, $m_2 = E[S^2]$:

$$\sigma_L^2 = \ln\!\left(\frac{m_2}{m_1^2}\right), \qquad \mu_L = \ln m_1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_L^2.$$

Equivalently, equate to the lognormal moment formulas $E[S]=e^{\mu_L+\sigma_L^2/2}$ and $\text{Var}(S)=e^{2\mu_L+\sigma_L^2}(e^{\sigma_L^2}-1)$. Then for a lognormal,

$$\Pr(S \le s) \approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln s - \mu_L}{\sigma_L}\right),$$ $$E[(S - d)_+] \approx e^{\mu_L + \sigma_L^2/2}\,\Phi\!\left(\frac{\mu_L + \sigma_L^2 - \ln d}{\sigma_L}\right) - d\left[1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln d - \mu_L}{\sigma_L}\right)\right].$$

Value-at-Risk (VaR)

The VaR of $X$ at the $100p\%$ security level is its $100p$th percentile:

$$\text{VaR}_p(X) = \pi_p = F_X^{-1}(p), \qquad 0\lt p\lt 1.$$

It is the loss exceeded with probability $1-p$. The exam tables provide VaR (percentile) formulas for most distributions, so you rarely need to invert the CDF by hand. For a monotone transform $Y=e^X$, $\text{VaR}_p(e^X)=e^{\text{VaR}_p(X)}$.

Tail-Value-at-Risk (TVaR / CTE)

The TVaR at the $100p\%$ level is the expected loss given the loss exceeds its VaR:

$$\text{TVaR}_p(X)=E\big[X \mid X>\text{VaR}_p(X)\big]=\frac{\int_{\text{VaR}_p(X)}^{\infty} x\,f(x)\,dx}{1-p}.$$

To dodge the integral, write it via the mean excess loss $e(d)=\dfrac{E[X]-E[X\wedge d]}{1-F(d)}$ evaluated at the VaR:

$$\text{TVaR}_p(X)=\text{VaR}_p(X)+e\big(\text{VaR}_p(X)\big)=\text{VaR}_p(X)+\frac{E[X]-E[X\wedge \text{VaR}_p(X)]}{1-p}.$$

Memorize these two table-free shortcuts (let $z_p=\Phi^{-1}(p)$):

Normal $(\mu,\sigma^2)$ $$\text{TVaR}_p(X)=\mu+\sigma\,\frac{\phi(z_p)}{1-p}.$$ Lognormal $(\mu,\sigma^2)$ $$\text{TVaR}_p(X)=E[X]\cdot\frac{\Phi(\sigma-z_p)}{1-p}, \qquad E[X]=e^{\mu+\sigma^2/2}.$$

Always $\text{TVaR}_p(X)\ge\text{VaR}_p(X)$ (the tail average is at least the threshold).

Tail weight

A heavier right tail ŌćÆ extreme losses more likely. Compare two models with the number of positive raw moments test:

$$\text{Fewer finite positive raw moments} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{heavier tail}.$$

E.g. Pareto$(\alpha,\theta)$ has finite $E[X^k]$ only for $k<\alpha$ (finitely many), whereas the exponential has all positive moments finite ŌĆö so Pareto is heavier-tailed.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Mean and variance of a compound distribution Ō¢Š

The number of claims $N$ has $E[N] = 5$ and $\text{Var}(N) = 8$ (negative-binomial-type). Claim severity $X$ has mean $E[X] = 200$ and variance $\text{Var}(X) = 30{,}000$. Find $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$.

Solution.

Mean:

$$E[S] = E[N]E[X] = 5 \times 200 = 1000.$$

Variance, using $\text{Var}(S) = E[N]\text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(N)(E[X])^2$:

$$\text{Var}(S) = 5(30{,}000) + 8(200)^2 = 150{,}000 + 8(40{,}000) = 150{,}000 + 320{,}000 = 470{,}000.$$
Answer
$E[S] = 1000$, $\text{Var}(S) = 470{,}000$ (so $\text{SD}(S) \approx 685.6$).
Example 2 Probability function from the Law of Total Probability Ō¢Š

$N\sim\text{Poisson}(3)$. Each claim amount has $\Pr(X=x)=0.25$ for $x=1,2,3,4$. Frequency and severity are independent. Find $\Pr(S=3)$.

Solution. Enumerate every way the claims can sum to $3$.

One claim of size 3:

$$p_N(1)p_X(3)=e^{-3}\frac{3^1}{1!}(0.25)=0.75\,e^{-3}.$$

Two claims summing to 3 (sizes $1{+}2$ or $2{+}1$):

$$p_N(2)\big[2\,p_X(1)p_X(2)\big]=e^{-3}\frac{3^2}{2!}\big[2(0.25)(0.25)\big]=0.5625\,e^{-3}.$$

Three claims of size 1 each:

$$p_N(3)\,p_X(1)^3=e^{-3}\frac{3^3}{3!}(0.25^3)=0.0703\,e^{-3}.$$

Add them:

$$\Pr(S=3)=(0.75+0.5625+0.0703)\,e^{-3}=1.3828\,e^{-3}=0.0688.$$
Answer
$\Pr(S=3)\approx 0.0688$. The trick is counting ordered combinations correctly (the $1{+}2$ split has multiplicity 2).
Example 3 Compound Poisson with additivity and normal approximation Ō¢Š

A book has two independent compound Poisson lines:

  • Line A: $\lambda_A = 3$, severity is exponential with mean $100$ (so $E[X_A] = 100$, $E[X_A^2] = 2(100)^2 = 20{,}000$).
  • Line B: $\lambda_B = 2$, severity is a constant $500$ (so $E[X_B] = 500$, $E[X_B^2] = 250{,}000$).

(a) Find $E[S]$ and $\text{Var}(S)$ for the combined book $S = S_A + S_B$.
(b) Use the normal approximation to estimate $\Pr(S > 3000)$.

Solution.

By additivity, $S$ is compound Poisson with $\lambda = 3 + 2 = 5$ and a mixed severity. We can just add moments of the two independent compound Poissons.

Means:

$$E[S_A] = 3(100) = 300, \quad E[S_B] = 2(500) = 1000, \quad E[S] = 1300.$$

Variances (compound Poisson: $\text{Var} = \lambda E[X^2]$):

$$\text{Var}(S_A) = 3(20{,}000) = 60{,}000,$$ $$\text{Var}(S_B) = 2(250{,}000) = 500{,}000,$$ $$\text{Var}(S) = 60{,}000 + 500{,}000 = 560{,}000.$$

So $\text{SD}(S) = \sqrt{560{,}000} \approx 748.33$.

(b) Normal approximation:

$$\Pr(S > 3000) \approx 1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{3000 - 1300}{748.33}\right) = 1 - \Phi(2.272).$$

$\Phi(2.27) \approx 0.9884$, so the probability $\approx 1 - 0.9884 = 0.0116$.

Answer
$E[S] = 1300$, $\text{Var}(S) = 560{,}000$; $\Pr(S > 3000) \approx 0.0116$.
Example 4 Panjer recursion and a stop-loss premium Ō¢Š

Claim frequency $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda = 1.5)$. Severity $X$ is discrete with

$$f_X(1) = 0.5, \quad f_X(2) = 0.3, \quad f_X(3) = 0.2, \quad f_X(0) = 0.$$

(a) Use Panjer recursion to find $\Pr(S = 0), \Pr(S = 1), \Pr(S = 2), \Pr(S = 3)$.
(b) Find the stop-loss premium $E[(S - 2)_+]$.

Solution.

Poisson $(a, b, 0)$ parameters: $a = 0$, $b = \lambda = 1.5$. Since $f_0 = 0$, the recursion is

$$g_s = \sum_{j=1}^{s}\frac{b\,j}{s}\,f_j\,g_{s-j} = \frac{1.5}{s}\sum_{j=1}^{s} j\,f_j\,g_{s-j}.$$

Starting value: $g_0 = e^{-\lambda(1 - f_0)} = e^{-1.5} = 0.223130$.

$s = 1$:

$$g_1 = \frac{1.5}{1}\big[(1)(0.5)(g_0)\big] = 1.5(0.5)(0.223130) = 0.167348.$$

$s = 2$:

$$g_2 = \frac{1.5}{2}\big[(1)(0.5)(g_1) + (2)(0.3)(g_0)\big].$$

Inside: $(0.5)(0.167348) + (0.6)(0.223130) = 0.083674 + 0.133878 = 0.217552.$

$$g_2 = 0.75 \times 0.217552 = 0.163164.$$

$s = 3$:

Example 11 Which coherence properties fail? Ō¢Š

For $\rho(X)=E[X]+k\,\text{Var}(X)$ with $k>0$, determine which coherence properties fail.

Translation invariance Ō£ō: $\rho(X+c)=E[X]+c+k\text{Var}(X)=\rho(X)+c$. Holds.

Positive homogeneity Ō£Ś: $\rho(cX)=cE[X]+k c^2\text{Var}(X)\ne c\,\rho(X)$ (the variance scales by $c^2$). Fails.

Subadditivity Ō£Ś: $\rho(X+Y)=\rho(X)+\rho(Y)+2k\,\text{Cov}(X,Y)$; since covariance can be positive, the inequality can break. Fails.

Monotonicity Ō£Ś: Counterexample with $\Pr(X=0)=0.9,\Pr(X=100)=0.1$ and $Y\equiv 100$, so $\Pr(X\le Y)=1$. Using the Bernoulli shortcut $\text{Var}(X)=(100-0)^2(0.9)(0.1)=900$:

$$\rho(X)=10+1(900)=910 > \rho(Y)=100+0=100.$$

So $\rho(X)>\rho(Y)$ despite $X\le Y$. Fails.

Answer
Fails positive homogeneity, subadditivity, and monotonicity (only translation invariance holds). To disprove a property one counterexample suffices; to prove one you need a general argument.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Using $\text{Var}(X)$ instead of $E[X^2]$ in the compound Poisson variance. For compound Poisson, $\text{Var}(S) = \lambda\,E[X^2]$ ŌĆö the second raw moment, not the variance. A frequent slip is plugging in $\text{Var}(X)$.
  • Forgetting the $(E[X])^2$ term in the general variance. $\text{Var}(S) = E[N]\text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(N)(E[X])^2$. Candidates routinely drop the second term.
  • Confusing the two models. Collective: $N$ random, $X_i$ i.i.d. Individual: $n$ fixed, $X_i$ independent but possibly different. Their variance formulas are completely different ŌĆö don't apply the compound variance formula to an individual-risk problem.
  • Severity not independent of count. The compound variance formula assumes severity is i.i.d. and independent of $N$. If the payment distribution changes with the number of claims/visits, you must build the per-unit distribution by enumeration instead.
  • Miscounting convolution combinations. When summing $\Pr(S=s)$ by hand, account for ordering: two claims of sizes $1$ and $2$ contribute $2f_X(1)f_X(2)$, not $f_X(1)f_X(2)$.
  • Panjer starting value. $g_0 = P_N(f_0)$, the PGF of $N$ at $f_0 = \Pr(X=0)$. It equals $p_0 = \Pr(N=0)$ only when $f_0 = 0$. If severity has mass at 0 (e.g. a deductible truncates small losses to 0), use the full PGF, e.g. $e^{-\lambda(1-f_0)}$ for Poisson.
  • Panjer index/severity at 0. The recursion sum runs $j = 1$ to $s$, and you need $f_0$ in the leading factor $1/(1 - a f_0)$. For Poisson $a = 0$, so that factor is 1 ŌĆö but don't forget it for binomial/negative binomial where $a \ne 0$.
  • Severity must start at the right support. Panjer requires severity on $0, 1, 2, \dots$. Rescale/round to a common monetary unit ("span") first; mismatched spans give wrong $g_s$.
  • Stop-loss sign and which tail. $E[(S-d)_+]=E[S]-E[S\wedge d]$ integrates the survival function $1 - F_S$ above $d$. Don't accidentally use $F_S$ or include the mass at/below $d$.
  • Continuity correction. Apply it only when the aggregate is discrete (both frequency and severity discrete). Decide the half-step direction by whether the point $s$ should be included: $\Pr(S\le s)\Rightarrow s+0.5$, $\Pr(S>s)\Rightarrow s+0.5$, $\Pr(S\ge s)\Rightarrow s-0.5$. With spread-out support use midpoints between possible values, not $\pm 0.5$.
  • Aggregate payments inconsistency. Don't mix "number of losses" with "payment per payment." Pair $N$ with $Y^L$, or $N'$ with $Y^P$ ŌĆö never crossed.
  • Splitting only for Poisson. Scaling the frequency mean by $\Pr(\text{above deductible})$ to get $N'$ works for Poisson only; for binomial/negative binomial use the number-of-losses approach.
  • Lognormal parameterization. $\sigma_L^2 = \ln(E[S^2]/E[S]^2)$ and $\mu_L = \ln E[S] - \tfrac12\sigma_L^2$. Mixing up $\mu_L$ vs $\sigma_L$, or using $\text{Var}(S)$ where $E[S^2]$ is needed, is a classic error.
  • Additivity only for compound Poisson. You can merge independent compound Poissons into one compound Poisson. This does not hold for compound negative binomial or compound binomial.
  • VaR is not variance. $\text{VaR}_p(X)=F_X^{-1}(p)$ is a percentile; $\text{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-E[X]^2$ is a spread. Different objects entirely.
  • TVaR shortcut signs. For the lognormal it's $\Phi(\sigma-z_p)$ (not $\Phi(z_p-\sigma)$); for the normal it's $\mu+\sigma\,\phi(z_p)/(1-p)$ using the standard normal pdf $\phi$, not the cdf $\Phi$.
  • Coherence of VaR. VaR satisfies three of four properties but fails subadditivity, so it is not coherent; TVaR is coherent. Memorize the table.
  • Tail weight direction. Fewer finite positive raw moments means a heavier tail ŌĆö the inverse relationship trips people up.

State the Panjer starting value $g_0$ for a compound Poisson with $\lambda = 2$ when the severity has $f_X(0) = 0.25$.
$g_0 = e^{-\lambda(1 - f_0)} = e^{-2(1 - 0.25)} = e^{-1.5} \approx 0.22313$.
Two independent compound Poissons have $\lambda_1 = 4, \lambda_2 = 6$. What is $\lambda$ for the combined compound Poisson, and how is the combined severity cdf formed?
$\lambda = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 10$. The combined severity is the mixture $F_X(x) = \frac{4}{10}F_1(x) + \frac{6}{10}F_2(x)$ (weights proportional to the $\lambda_j$).
Claims $N\sim\text{Poisson}(20)$; each loss exceeds a deductible with probability $0.6$. What is the distribution of the number of payments $N'$?
By Poisson splitting, $N'\sim\text{Poisson}(20\times 0.6)=\text{Poisson}(12)$. (This shortcut is Poisson-only.)

An integer-valued $S$ has $E[S]=504$, $\text{Var}(S)=205{,}344$, and you want $\Pr(S\le 400)$ by normal approximation. $S$ can equal $\dots,200,400,1000,\dots$ ŌĆö what continuity-corrected boundary do you use?
Use the midpoints around 400: lower $\tfrac{200+400}{2}=300$, upper $\tfrac{400+1000}{2}=700$. Since we want to include $S=400$, $\Pr(S\le 400)\Rightarrow\Pr(S\le 700)=\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{700-504}{\sqrt{205344}}\big)=\Phi(0.4325)\approx 0.667$.
$X\sim\text{Weibull}(\theta=5000,\tau=3)$. Find $\text{VaR}_{0.95}(X)$.
$\text{VaR}_{0.95}=\theta[-\ln(1-0.95)]^{1/\tau}=5000[-\ln 0.05]^{1/3}=5000(2.9957)^{1/3}\approx 7{,}207.83$. (Solve $F(x)=1-e^{-(x/\theta)^\tau}=0.95$.)

$\text{TVaR}_{0.95}=\mu+\sigma\dfrac{\phi(z_{0.95})}{1-0.95}=1000+200\cdot\dfrac{0.1031}{0.05}=1000+200(2.062)=1{,}412.4$.
Is VaR a coherent risk measure? If not, which property does it violate?
No. VaR satisfies translation invariance, positive homogeneity, and monotonicity, but can violate subadditivity, so it is not coherent. TVaR satisfies all four and is coherent.
Compare the tail weight of a Pareto$(\alpha=2,\theta)$ and an exponential. Which is heavier-tailed, and why?
The Pareto. It has finite positive raw moments only for $k<\alpha=2$ (finitely many), while the exponential has all positive moments finite. Fewer finite moments ŌćÆ heavier tail.
Write the aggregate CDF $F_S(s)$ in Law-of-Total-Probability form, and specialize it to $N\in\{0,1,2\}$.
$F_S(s)=p_N(0)+\sum_{n>0}p_N(n)\,\Pr\!\big(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\le s\big)$. With $N\le 2$: $F_S(s)=p_N(0)+p_N(1)F_X(s)+p_N(2)F_{X_1+X_2}(s)$. This holds for continuous severity too ŌĆö you weight the severity CDF.

$X_1,X_2$ are independent $\text{Normal}(180,100^2)$. What is the distribution of $X_1+X_2$, and what is $F_{X_1+X_2}(200)$?
Sum of independent normals is normal with means and variances added: $X_1+X_2\sim\text{Normal}(360,\,20{,}000)$. $F_{X_1+X_2}(200)=\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{200-360}{\sqrt{20{,}000}}\big)=\Phi(-1.131)\approx 0.129$. (Never add standard deviations.)
