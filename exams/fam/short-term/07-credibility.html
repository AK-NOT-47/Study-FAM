Introduction to Credibility

How much should next year's premium trust this policyholder's own experience versus the manual rate? Credibility is the weight $Z$ that answers it.

📖 Loss Models · Ch. 16–17 ⏱️ ~3.5 hrs 🔑 full credibility · $n_e$ vs $n_c$ · square-root rule · Bühlmann · EPV/VHM
Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic, the SOA expects you to be able to:

  • Limited fluctuation (classical) credibility
    • State the full-credibility condition $\Pr(|\bar S - \mu_S| \le k\mu_S) \ge p$ and identify the basis (aggregate / frequency / severity), the tolerance $k$, and the probability $p$ from exam wording.
    • Compute the full credibility standard as a number of exposures $n_e$ and as an expected number of claims $n_c$ for claim frequency, claim severity, and aggregate losses / pure premium, and convert between them with $n_c = n_e\,\mu_N$.
    • Apply the standard normal "within $\pm 100k\%$ of the true mean with probability $p$" requirement to derive $n_c = \left(z_{(1+p)/2}/k\right)^2\!\left(\sigma_N^2/\mu_N + \mathrm{CV}_X^2\right)$.
    • Determine the partial credibility factor $Z$ using the square-root rule (in either exposures or claims) and form the credibility-weighted estimate $P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)M$.
  • Greatest-accuracy (Bühlmann / Bayesian) credibility
    • Define and compute the hypothetical mean $\mu(\Theta)$ and process variance $v(\Theta)$.
    • Compute the expected hypothetical mean $\mu = E[\mu(\Theta)]$, the expected process variance $v = \text{EPV} = E[v(\Theta)]$, and the variance of hypothetical means $a = \text{VHM} = \text{Var}[\mu(\Theta)]$.
    • Compute the Bühlmann credibility factor $Z = \dfrac{n}{n+k}$ where $k = v/a$, and the credibility premium $Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu$.
    • Apply Bühlmann–Straub credibility when exposures differ by year/policyholder.
    • Recognize that the Bühlmann estimate is the best linear approximation to the Bayesian (posterior mean) estimate.

Key Concepts

What problem does credibility solve?

You observe a policyholder (or class) for a while and get a sample mean $\bar{X}$ of their losses. You also have a manual rate $M$ — a prior/collective estimate from a big book of business. How much should next year's premium rely on this policyholder's own experience versus the manual rate?

The answer is a weighted average:

$$\text{Credibility premium} = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)M, \qquad 0 \le Z \le 1.$$

$Z$ is the credibility factor. More data → larger $Z$ → trust the policyholder's own experience more.

A motivating scenario (and three properties of $Z$)

All-Care Insurance prices new car insurance in Iowa. Its internal data says each insured averages a claim of $100$/year; industry-wide data says $200$/year. The internal data fits All-Care's book better but has a small sample; the industry data is huge but generic. With $Z = 0.3$ credibility on the internal data,

$$\text{Estimate} = 0.3(100) + 0.7(200) = 170.$$

Letting $n$ be the number of observations, $Z$ always obeys:

  • $0 \le Z \le 1$ — worst case discard the data ($Z=0$); best case rely on it entirely ($Z=1$).
  • $\dfrac{dZ}{dn} > 0$ — more data always raises $Z$.
  • $\dfrac{d}{dn}\!\left(\dfrac{Z}{n}\right) < 0$ — $Z$ rises faster when the sample is small. Adding $500$ observations to a sample of $250$ matters far more than adding $500$ to a sample of $1{,}000{,}000$.

There are two philosophies for choosing $Z$:

  1. Limited fluctuation (classical) credibility — "How much data do I need before the observed mean is stable enough (small random fluctuation) to be fully trusted?" Heuristic, based on the normal approximation.
  2. Greatest accuracy (Bühlmann) credibility — "What $Z$ minimizes squared error?" Rigorous, derived from variance components. This is the one that connects to Bayesian estimation.

Limited Fluctuation (Classical) Credibility — the full-credibility condition

The observations earn full credibility ($Z=1$) once the sample is large enough that the sample mean $\bar S$ is reliably close to the true mean $\mu_S$. "Close" is made precise: for a small tolerance $k$ and a large probability $p$, we require the sample mean to land within $100k\%$ of the true mean at least $100p\%$ of the time:

$$\Pr\!\left(|\bar S - \mu_S| \le k\mu_S\right) \ge p, \qquad z_{(1+p)/2} = \Phi^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1+p}{2}\right).$$
Don't confuse $k$ and $p$
  • $k$ — the permitted fluctuation of $\bar S$ from $\mu_S$; usually small (e.g. 0.05).
  • $p$ — the minimum probability the bound holds; usually large (e.g. 0.90).

Applying the Central Limit Theorem to $\bar S$ and solving the inequality gives the minimum sample size. That size can be reported in two units — and keeping them straight is the heart of every classical credibility problem.

Two units for the standard: exposures $n_e$ vs. expected claims $n_c$

  • Exposure $=$ one data point. One exposure could be "one insured for one year." Two exposures could be two insureds for one year or one insured for two years. The number of exposures for full credibility is $n_e$.
  • Expected number of claims $n_c$ restates the same standard in claim units. If every exposure produces $\mu_N$ claims on average, then $n_c = n_e\,\mu_N$.
Master formula — exposures for full credibility of aggregate claims $$n_e = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\mathrm{CV}_S^2$$ Master formula — expected claims for full credibility $$n_c = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N} + \mathrm{CV}_X^2\right), \qquad n_c = n_e\,\mu_N.$$

Here $z_{(1+p)/2} = \Phi^{-1}\!\big(\tfrac{1+p}{2}\big)$ is the two-sided normal quantile; $S$ is aggregate loss per exposure, $N$ is frequency per exposure, $X$ is severity, and $\mathrm{CV}$ is the coefficient of variation. The two master formulas plus the conversion $n_c = n_e\mu_N$ are all you must memorize — everything else (frequency-only, severity-only, exposure forms) is a special case.

Choosing the basis: aggregate vs. frequency vs. severity

The standard can be set on aggregate losses, on frequency alone, or on severity alone. You modify the expected-claims master formula by switching components on or off:

  • Aggregate / pure premium: keep both terms, $\dfrac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N} + \mathrm{CV}_X^2$. Based on $\Pr(|\bar S - \mu_S| \le k\mu_S) \ge p$.
  • Frequency only: set the severity term to $0$ → $\dfrac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N}$. Based on $\Pr(|\bar N - \mu_N| \le k\mu_N) \ge p$.
  • Severity only: set the frequency term to $0$ → $\mathrm{CV}_X^2$. Based on $\Pr(|\bar X - \mu_X| \le k\mu_X) \ge p$ with frequency held fixed.
Strategy for any full-credibility question
  1. Identify the basis (aggregate, frequency, or severity), and read off $k$ and $p$.
  2. Compute $n_c$ from the master $n_c$ formula, switching off whichever term the basis excludes.
  3. Need exposures? Divide: $n_e = n_c/\mu_N$. (For an aggregate basis you may instead use the $n_e$ master formula directly.)

"Pure premium" = aggregate claims per exposure, so a pure-premium standard is just the aggregate standard.

Partial Credibility — the square-root rule

If the experience falls short of full credibility, we assign $Z < 1$ and blend with the manual premium $M$ to form the credibility premium $P_C$:

$$P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)M = M + Z(\bar X - M).$$

Classical credibility sets $Z$ by the square-root rule, available in two consistent unit-pairs:

$$Z = \sqrt{\frac{n}{n_e}} = \sqrt{\frac{n'}{n_c}} \quad(\text{capped at } 1),$$

where $n$ is the actual exposures, $n_e$ the exposure standard, $n'$ the actual claims, and $n_c$ the claim standard. Use whichever pair matches the data you are given — numerator and denominator must share units.

Coach's Remarks — $\bar X$, $M$, and $n'$
  • $\bar X$ is the average of the observations; $M$ must be given by the problem (it is set "manually," ignoring the experience).
  • $\bar X$ and $M$ must be comparable quantities. If $M$ is an annual aggregate per insured, then $\bar X$ must be the average annual aggregate too — not total claims divided by the number of claims. (E.g. one year of data with $12{,}000$ total losses gives $\bar X = 12{,}000/1 = 12{,}000$, not $12{,}000/200$.)
  • Technically $n' = n\,\mu_N$, but $\mu_N$ is often unknown, so the observed claim count is used for $n'$.

Greatest Accuracy (Bühlmann) Credibility — intuition

Beyond FAM scope — read for understanding, not for the exam

Everything Bühlmann-related on this page — this section, Bühlmann–Straub, the Bühlmann formulas, and Worked Examples 10–11 — is greatest-accuracy credibility, which sits on ASTAM, not FAM. The FAM syllabus (Topic 4) tests limited-fluctuation (classical) credibility only, and the FAM readings stop at Loss Models Ch. 16. It's kept here because it makes classical credibility click and is genuinely useful background — but don't spend exam-prep time mastering it for FAM.

Each policyholder has a hidden risk parameter $\Theta$ (their true riskiness). Given $\Theta = \theta$:

  • Hypothetical mean: $\mu(\theta) = E[X \mid \Theta = \theta]$ — the long-run mean for that policyholder.
  • Process variance: $v(\theta) = \text{Var}[X \mid \Theta = \theta]$ — the year-to-year randomness within that policyholder.

Across the population, $\Theta$ is random, giving three structural quantities:

  • $\mu = E[\mu(\Theta)]$ — the collective/overall mean (this is what $M$ becomes in Bühlmann).
  • $v = \text{EPV} = E[v(\Theta)]$ — Expected Process Variance: average within-risk noise.
  • $a = \text{VHM} = \text{Var}[\mu(\Theta)]$ — Variance of Hypothetical Means: how much policyholders differ from each other.

The credibility factor is

$$Z = \frac{n}{n+k}, \qquad k = \frac{v}{a} = \frac{\text{EPV}}{\text{VHM}}.$$
Intuition for $k = v/a$
  • Large EPV (noisy individual data) → large $k$ → small $Z$ → trust the data less.
  • Large VHM (policyholders are very different) → small $k$ → large $Z$ → an individual's own data is very informative.

The Bühlmann estimate $Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu$ is the least-squares linear approximation to the Bayesian posterior mean $E[\mu(\Theta)\mid \mathbf{X}]$. For some conjugate models (Poisson–gamma, Bernoulli–beta, Normal–Normal) the Bühlmann and exact Bayesian estimates coincide.

Bühlmann–Straub

When each year (or policyholder) has a different exposure $m_i$ (e.g., number of policies, payroll units), use Bühlmann–Straub. Replace counts with total exposure $m = \sum m_i$:

$$Z = \frac{m}{m+k}, \qquad \bar{X} = \frac{\sum_i m_i X_i}{\sum_i m_i},$$

where $X_i$ is the loss per unit of exposure in cell $i$. The estimate is still $Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu$.

Formulas

Limited fluctuation credibility — master formulas

Full-credibility condition $$\Pr\!\left(|\bar S - \mu_S| \le k\mu_S\right) \ge p, \qquad z_{(1+p)/2} = \Phi^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1+p}{2}\right).$$ Exposures (aggregate claims) $$n_e = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\mathrm{CV}_S^2$$ Expected claims (aggregate / pure premium) $$n_c = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N} + \mathrm{CV}_X^2\right)$$ Conversion between the two units $$n_c = n_e\,\mu_N \;\Longleftrightarrow\; n_e = \frac{n_c}{\mu_N}.$$

Poisson shortcut

If frequency is Poisson, then $\sigma_N^2 = \mu_N$, so $\dfrac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N} = 1$. The aggregate claim standard collapses to the familiar

$$n_c = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\!\left(1 + \mathrm{CV}_X^2\right),$$

and the pure frequency standard becomes $n_c = \big(z_{(1+p)/2}/k\big)^2$.

Partial credibility

Square-root rule (two equivalent forms) $$Z = \min\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{n}{n_e}},\,1\right) = \min\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{n'}{n_c}},\,1\right)$$
  • $n$ = actual exposures, $n_e$ = exposure standard; or $n'$ = actual claims, $n_c$ = claim standard.
  • Numerator and denominator must be in the same units.
Credibility-weighted estimate (credibility premium) $$P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\,M = M + Z(\bar X - M)$$
  • $\bar{X}$ = observed mean (frequency, severity, or pure premium) — must match the units of $M$.
  • $M$ = manual / prior rate, supplied by the problem.

Bühlmann credibility

Conditional moments (per risk) $$\mu(\theta) = E[X\mid \Theta=\theta], \qquad v(\theta) = \text{Var}[X\mid \Theta=\theta].$$ Structural parameters $$\mu = E[\mu(\Theta)] \quad(\text{overall mean})$$ $$v = \text{EPV} = E[v(\Theta)]$$ $$a = \text{VHM} = \text{Var}[\mu(\Theta)] = E\!\big[\mu(\Theta)^2\big] - \mu^2$$ Total variance check (law of total variance) $$\text{Var}(X) = E[v(\Theta)] + \text{Var}[\mu(\Theta)] = v + a.$$ Credibility factor and premium $$k = \frac{v}{a} = \frac{\text{EPV}}{\text{VHM}}, \qquad Z = \frac{n}{n+k},$$ $$P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu,$$
  • $n$ = number of observations (years/exposures) for the policyholder.
  • $\bar{X}$ = average of the policyholder's $n$ observations.
  • $\mu$ = collective mean (the credibility complement).

Bühlmann–Straub credibility

For cells $i=1,\dots,n$ with exposures $m_i$ and per-exposure loss $X_i$:

$$m = \sum_{i} m_i, \qquad \bar{X} = \frac{\sum_i m_i X_i}{m}, \qquad Z = \frac{m}{m+k}, \qquad k=\frac{v}{a},$$ $$P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu.$$

Here $v(\theta)$ and $a$ refer to the process variance and VHM per unit of exposure.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Exposures for full credibility (aggregate, $\mathrm{CV}_S$ given) ▾

Annual total claims per policyholder follow a gamma distribution with mean 4 and variance 9. The standard is for aggregate claims to be within 5% of the mean 95% of the time. Find the number of exposures for full credibility.

Solution. Read off the basis (aggregate), $k = 0.05$, $p = 0.95$.

$$z_{(1+0.95)/2} = z_{0.975} = 1.95996, \qquad \mathrm{CV}_S = \frac{\sigma_S}{\mu_S} = \frac{\sqrt{9}}{4} = 0.75.$$

Apply the $n_e$ master formula:

$$n_e = \left(\frac{1.95996}{0.05}\right)^{\!2}(0.75)^2 = 864.32.$$
Answer
$\approx \mathbf{864.32}$ exposures. (E.g. 865 insureds for 1 year, or 173 insureds for 5 years.) Graphically: with 864.32 exposures, the average claim lands in $[3.8,\,4.2]$ with 95% probability.
Example 2 Converting exposures to expected claims ($n_c = n_e\mu_N$) ▾

Same gamma severity model as Example 1 ($n_e = 864.32$), and now the annual claim frequency per policyholder has mean $\mu_N = 5$. Find the expected number of claims for full credibility.

Solution. Each exposure averages 5 claims, so

$$n_c = n_e\,\mu_N = 864.32 \times 5 = 4{,}321.62.$$
Answer
$\approx \mathbf{4{,}321.62}$ expected claims. Note $\mu_N$ (claims per exposure) is not the same as $n_c$ (claims for the whole standard) — keep them distinct.
Example 3 Expected claims with a discrete frequency (compound moments) ▾

Weekly claim counts $N$ follow $P(0)=0.3,\,P(1)=0.25,\,P(2)=0.35,\,P(3)=0.1$; each claim has size exactly $10$. Standard: aggregate within 6% of the mean 95% of the time. Find the exposures for full credibility.

Solution. Since severity is constant, work the aggregate $S = 10N$ directly.

$$E[N] = 0(0.3)+1(0.25)+2(0.35)+3(0.1) = 1.25,$$ $$E[N^2] = 0+0.25+1.40+0.90 = 2.55,\quad \mathrm{Var}[N] = 2.55 - 1.25^2 = 0.9875.$$ $$E[S] = 10(1.25) = 12.5, \qquad \mathrm{Var}[S] = 10^2(0.9875) = 98.75.$$ $$\mathrm{CV}_S = \frac{\sqrt{98.75}}{12.5} = 0.795, \qquad z_{0.975} = 1.95996,\ k = 0.06.$$ $$n_e = \left(\frac{1.95996}{0.06}\right)^{\!2}(0.795)^2 = 674.39.$$
Answer
$\approx \mathbf{674.4}$ exposures.
Example 4 Frequency-only vs. aggregate standard (Poisson) ▾

Claims per year are Poisson; severity has mean 100 and variance 600, independent of frequency. Find the expected claims for full credibility if (1) frequency is within 2% with 95% probability; (2) aggregate claims within 8% with 99% probability.

(1) Frequency only. Poisson ⇒ $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N = 1$; severity term off ($\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = 0$). $z_{0.975}=1.95996$, $k=0.02$.

$$n_c = \left(\frac{1.95996}{0.02}\right)^{\!2}(1 + 0) = 9{,}604.$$

(2) Aggregate. $\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = 600/100^2 = 0.06$, $z_{0.995}=2.57583$, $k=0.08$.

$$n_c = \left(\frac{2.57583}{0.08}\right)^{\!2}(1 + 0.06) = 1{,}099.$$
Answer
(1) $\mathbf{9{,}604}$ claims; (2) $\approx \mathbf{1{,}099}$ claims.
Example 5 Lognormal severity — $n_c$ then $n_e$ ▾

Annual counts: $P(0)=0.3,\,P(1)=0.4,\,P(2)=0.1,\,P(3)=0.2$. Severity is lognormal with $\mu=5,\ \sigma^2=2$. Independent. Standard: aggregate within 5% of the mean 90% of the time. Find $n_c$ and $n_e$.

Frequency: $E[N] = 0.4 + 0.2 + 0.6 = 1.2$, $E[N^2] = 0.4 + 0.4 + 1.8 = 2.6$, $\mathrm{Var}[N] = 2.6 - 1.2^2 = 1.16$.

Severity (lognormal $\mu=5,\sigma^2=2$):

$$E[X] = e^{5 + 0.5(2)} = 403.43,\quad E[X^2] = e^{2(5)+2(2)} = 1{,}202{,}604.28,$$ $$\mathrm{Var}[X] = 1{,}202{,}604.28 - 403.43^2 = 1{,}039{,}849.49.$$

$z_{0.95} = 1.64485$, $k = 0.05$. Apply the $n_c$ master formula:

$$n_c = \left(\frac{1.64485}{0.05}\right)^{\!2}\!\left(\frac{1.16}{1.2} + \frac{1{,}039{,}849.49}{403.43^2}\right) = 7{,}960.46.$$

Convert to exposures: $n_e = n_c/\mu_N = 7{,}960.46 / 1.2 = 6{,}633.71$.

Answer
$n_c \approx \mathbf{7{,}960.5}$ claims, $n_e \approx \mathbf{6{,}633.7}$ exposures.
Example 6 Solving backward for the tolerance $k$ (negative binomial frequency) ▾

Counts are negative binomial $r=t,\ \beta=5$; severity is gamma $\alpha=4$, unknown $\theta$; independent. Given that 1,691 expected claims achieve full credibility for aggregate claims within $100k\%$ with 90% probability, find $k$.

Frequency: $\mu_N = r\beta = 5t$, $\sigma_N^2 = r\beta(1+\beta) = 5t(6) = 30t$, so $\dfrac{\sigma_N^2}{\mu_N} = \dfrac{30t}{5t} = 6$.

Severity: $\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = \dfrac{\alpha\theta^2}{(\alpha\theta)^2} = \dfrac{1}{\alpha} = \dfrac{1}{4} = 0.25$ ($\theta$ cancels).

$$1{,}691 = \left(\frac{1.64485}{k}\right)^{\!2}(6 + 0.25) \;\Rightarrow\; \left(\frac{1.64485}{k}\right)^{\!2} = 270.56.$$
Answer
$k = \dfrac{1.64485}{\sqrt{270.56}} = \mathbf{0.1}$.
Example 7 Solving backward for severity $\mathrm{CV}$ (binomial frequency) ▾

Counts per insured are binomial $(m,\,q=0.2)$; severity is independent with $\mathrm{CV}_X = \lambda$. Given 3,000 claims are needed for full credibility, find $\lambda$ when the standard is (1) severity within 5% with 95%; (2) pure premium within 5% with 95%.

$z_{0.975} = 1.95996$, $k = 0.05$, so $(z/k)^2 = 1{,}536.6$.

(1) Severity only (frequency term off):

$$3{,}000 = 1{,}536.6\,\lambda^2 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda = \sqrt{3{,}000/1{,}536.6} = 1.397.$$

(2) Pure premium (= aggregate; binomial $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N = \dfrac{mq(1-q)}{mq} = 1-q = 0.8$):

$$3{,}000 = 1{,}536.6\,(0.8 + \lambda^2) \;\Rightarrow\; 0.8 + \lambda^2 = 1.952 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda = \sqrt{1.152} = 1.073.$$
Answer
(1) $\lambda \approx \mathbf{1.397}$; (2) $\lambda \approx \mathbf{1.073}$.
Example 8 Revising $Z$ when the standard changes ▾

Standard: claim frequency within 3% of the true value 90% of the time. A group of 800 insureds has $Z = 0.80$. The standard is then tightened to 99%. Find the revised $Z$.

Step 1 — back out the original $n_e$ from the square-root rule:

$$0.80 = \sqrt{\frac{800}{n_e}} \;\Rightarrow\; n_e = \frac{800}{0.64} = 1{,}250.$$

Step 2 — the standard scales with $(z/k)^2$. Only $z$ changes ($k$ and the distribution stay fixed), so

$$\text{revised } n_e = 1{,}250 \times \frac{z_{0.995}^2}{z_{0.95}^2} = 1{,}250 \times \frac{2.57583^2}{1.64485^2} = 3{,}065.43.$$

Step 3 — re-apply the square-root rule with 800 insureds:

$$\text{revised } Z = \sqrt{\frac{800}{3{,}065.43}} = 0.5109.$$
Answer
Revised $Z \approx \mathbf{0.511}$. Shortcut: $Z_{\text{new}} = Z_{\text{old}}\cdot z_{\text{old}}/z_{\text{new}} = 0.80(1.64485/2.57583) = 0.511$.
Example 9 Full classical credibility premium (Poisson, inverse Gaussian) ▾

Frequency is Poisson; severity is inverse Gaussian $\mu=4,\ \theta=8$; independent. Standard: aggregate within 10% of the mean 90% of the time. Manual aggregate premium $M = 10{,}000$. Last year: 200 claims, total losses $12{,}000$. Find $P_C$.

Standard in claims (data is in claims, so use $n_c$). Poisson ⇒ $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N = 1$. For inverse Gaussian, $E[X]=\mu=4$, $\mathrm{Var}[X]=\mu^3/\theta = 4^3/8 = 8$, so $\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = 8/4^2 = 0.5$. $z_{0.95}=1.64485$, $k=0.1$.

$$n_c = \left(\frac{1.64485}{0.1}\right)^{\!2}(1 + 0.5) = 405.83.$$

Partial credibility from 200 observed claims:

$$Z = \sqrt{\frac{200}{405.83}} = 0.702.$$

Credibility premium. $M$ is the annual aggregate, so $\bar X$ is the annual aggregate too: $\bar X = 12{,}000/1 = 12{,}000$ (one year of data).

$$P_C = 0.702(12{,}000) + 0.298(10{,}000) = 11{,}404.$$
Answer
$Z \approx 0.702$, $P_C \approx \boxed{\mathbf{11{,}404}}$. Trap: $\bar X \ne 12{,}000/200$ — that would be average severity, not the aggregate that matches $M$.
Example 10 Bühlmann credibility from a conditional model ▾

A risk parameter $\Theta$ takes values with the following distribution. Given $\Theta=\theta$, annual claims $X$ have mean $\mu(\theta)=\theta$ and variance $v(\theta)=\theta$ (Poisson-type). The prior on $\Theta$ is:

$\theta$$P(\Theta=\theta)$
10.5
30.3
50.2

A policyholder is observed for n = 4 years with mean $\bar{X} = 2.5$. Find the Bühlmann credibility premium.

Solution.

Overall mean:

$$\mu = E[\mu(\Theta)] = 1(0.5)+3(0.3)+5(0.2) = 0.5+0.9+1.0 = 2.4.$$

EPV (here $v(\theta)=\theta$, so $E[v(\Theta)]=E[\Theta]$):

$$v = E[v(\Theta)] = 2.4.$$

VHM: $E[\mu(\Theta)^2] = 1^2(0.5)+3^2(0.3)+5^2(0.2)=0.5+2.7+5.0=8.2.$

$$a = \text{VHM} = E[\mu(\Theta)^2]-\mu^2 = 8.2 - 2.4^2 = 8.2 - 5.76 = 2.44.$$

Credibility:

$$k = \frac{v}{a} = \frac{2.4}{2.44} = 0.98361, \qquad Z = \frac{n}{n+k} = \frac{4}{4+0.98361} = \frac{4}{4.98361}=0.80263.$$

Premium:

$$P_C = Z\bar{X}+(1-Z)\mu = 0.80263(2.5)+0.19737(2.4) = 2.00658 + 0.47369 = 2.48027.$$
Answer
$k \approx 0.984$, $Z \approx 0.803$, credibility premium $\approx \boxed{\mathbf{2.480}}$.

Total exposure: $m = 100+150+250 = 500.$

Exposure-weighted mean (= total losses / total exposures):

$$\bar{X} = \frac{24+27+60}{500} = \frac{111}{500} = 0.222.$$ $$k = \frac{v}{a} = \frac{0.60}{0.0025} = 240, \qquad Z = \frac{m}{m+k} = \frac{500}{500+240} = \frac{500}{740} = 0.67568.$$ $$P_C = Z\bar{X} + (1-Z)\mu = 0.67568(0.222) + 0.32432(0.20).$$ $$P_C = 0.150000 + 0.064865 = 0.214865.$$
Answer
$Z \approx 0.676$; estimated loss per exposure $\approx \boxed{\mathbf{0.2149}}$ per unit (so $\approx 0.2149 \times 500 = 107.4$ total expected losses).
Example 12 Deriving $\mathrm{CV}_S$ from compound moments end-to-end (Poisson × Gamma) ▾

Annual claim frequency is Poisson(5); individual claim severity is Gamma$(\alpha=3,\theta=10)$; frequency and severity are independent. The full-credibility standard is for the aggregate loss to be within 3% of its mean with probability 90%. Find the number of exposures $n_e$.

Why this example. Earlier problems handed you $\mathrm{CV}_S$ or a constant severity. Here neither moment of $S$ is given — you must build $\mu_S$ and $\sigma_S^2$ from the frequency and severity moments using the compound (collective-risk) formulas:

$$\mu_S = \mu_N\,\mu_X, \qquad \sigma_S^2 = \mu_N\,\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_N^2\,\mu_X^2.$$

Step 1 — frequency moments (Poisson). For Poisson$(\lambda=5)$, $\mu_N = \sigma_N^2 = 5$.

Step 2 — severity moments (Gamma). For Gamma$(\alpha=3,\theta=10)$, $\mu_X = \alpha\theta = 30$ and $\sigma_X^2 = \alpha\theta^2 = 3(10^2) = 300$.

Step 3 — aggregate moments. Plug into the compound formulas:

$$\mu_S = \mu_N\mu_X = 5(30) = 150,$$ $$\sigma_S^2 = \mu_N\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_N^2\mu_X^2 = 5(300) + 5(30^2) = 1{,}500 + 4{,}500 = 6{,}000.$$

Step 4 — coefficient of variation of $S$.

$$\mathrm{CV}_S = \frac{\sigma_S}{\mu_S} = \frac{\sqrt{6{,}000}}{150} = \frac{77.4597}{150} = 0.5164.$$

Step 5 — apply the $n_e$ master formula. Basis is aggregate, $k = 0.03$, $p = 0.90 \Rightarrow z_{0.95} = 1.64485$:

$$n_e = \left(\frac{z_{(1+p)/2}}{k}\right)^{\!2}\mathrm{CV}_S^2 = \left(\frac{1.64485}{0.03}\right)^{\!2}(0.5164)^2 = 3{,}006.14 \times 0.26667 = 801.64.$$
Answer
$n_e \approx \mathbf{801.6}$ exposures. The whole problem is just "moments of $S$ → $\mathrm{CV}_S$ → master formula." Once you can build $\mu_S$ and $\sigma_S^2$ from $\mu_N,\sigma_N^2,\mu_X,\sigma_X^2$, no aggregate full-credibility question can surprise you.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • $n_e$ vs. $n_c$ units. Exposures and expected claims are different standards linked by $n_c = n_e\,\mu_N$. Match the unit of the standard to the unit of the data: claims observed → compare to $n_c$; exposures observed → compare to $n_e$. Mixing them is the single most common error here.
  • $z_{(1+p)/2}$, not $z_p$. The condition is two-sided: $\Phi(z) = (1+p)/2$. For $p=0.90$ use $z = 1.645$ (the 0.95 quantile), not 1.282; for $p=0.95$ use $1.960$; for $p=0.99$ use $2.576$.
  • Don't confuse $k$ and $p$. $k$ is the small tolerance (e.g. 0.05); $p$ is the large probability (e.g. 0.90). Re-read the wording — "within 5% … 90% of the time" means $k=0.05,\ p=0.90$.
  • Read the basis. Aggregate keeps both terms $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N + \mathrm{CV}_X^2$; frequency only drops $\mathrm{CV}_X^2$; severity only drops $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N$. "Pure premium" = aggregate per exposure, so use the aggregate formula.
  • Poisson is an assumption, not a default. $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N = 1$ only if frequency is Poisson. For binomial it is $1-q$; for negative binomial it is $1+\beta$. Compute $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N$ from the actual distribution.
  • Severity uses $\mathrm{CV}^2$, not variance. The severity multiplier is $\sigma_X^2/\mu_X^2 = \mathrm{CV}_X^2$ (dimensionless), not $\sigma_X^2$.
  • $\bar X$ and $M$ must be comparable. If $M$ is an annual aggregate per insured, $\bar X$ is the average annual aggregate — total losses over the number of exposures, not over the number of claims.
  • Confusing EPV and VHM. EPV $= E[\text{Var}(X\mid\Theta)]$ (average within-risk variance); VHM $= \text{Var}[E(X\mid\Theta)]$ (variance between risks). $k = \text{EPV}/\text{VHM}$. Swapping them inverts $Z$.
  • VHM via the shortcut. $a = E[\mu(\Theta)^2] - \mu^2$. The first term is $E[\mu(\Theta)^2]$, not $E[\mu(\Theta)]^2$.
  • Bühlmann–Straub weighting. $\bar X$ is the exposure-weighted average and $Z = m/(m+k)$ uses total exposure $m$, not the number of cells.
  • Credibility complement. Classical uses the manual rate $M$; Bühlmann uses the collective mean $\mu$. Don't mix them.
  • Bühlmann never reaches full trust. $Z\in(0,1)$ always for finite $n$ — it never equals 1 (unlike classical, which caps at 1).
Decode the SOA wording → basis, $k$, $p$

Real FAM-S problems hide the three inputs inside deliberately bland sentences. Every classical-credibility prompt is one phrase that pins down the basis (which terms of the master formula survive), the tolerance $k$ (the "within $X\%$"), and the probability $p$ (the "$Y\%$ of the time" / "probability $Y$"). Train your eye on the actual SOA phrasings:

SOA wording (sample problems)Basis$k$$p$
"The observed pure premium should be within 2% of the expected pure premium 90% of the time." (Q1)Aggregate (pure premium = aggregate per exposure → keep both terms)$0.02$$0.90$
"The full credibility standard is for aggregate losses to be within 5% of the expected with probability 0.90." (Q8)Aggregate (keep both terms)$0.05$$0.90$
"The full credibility standard is to be within 5% of the expected aggregate losses 90% of the time." (Q12)Aggregate (keep both terms)$0.05$$0.90$

Reading rules: "pure premium" and "aggregate losses" both mean the aggregate basis (keep $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N + \mathrm{CV}_X^2$); only the words "claim frequency / number of claims" force the frequency basis (drop $\mathrm{CV}_X^2$), and only "claim size / severity" forces the severity basis (drop $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N$). The "within $k$" number is always $k$ — never halve it; the two-sided split into $\tfrac{1+p}{2}$ happens inside $z_{(1+p)/2}$, not in $k$. The "$\%$ of the time" or "probability" number is $p$ — feed it through $z_{(1+p)/2}=\Phi^{-1}(\tfrac{1+p}{2})$, so $p=0.90\Rightarrow z=1.645$.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Why do we even need credibility theory? State the motivation in one sentence.
Individual experience targets the specific risk but has a small, noisy sample; the broad group has a large sample but is too generic. Credibility blends them — $Z\bar X + (1-Z)M$ — so we get a stable yet relevant estimate of the future outcome.
Write the three properties of the credibility factor $Z$.
(1) $0 \le Z \le 1$; (2) $dZ/dn > 0$ (more data raises $Z$); (3) $\dfrac{d}{dn}(Z/n) < 0$ ($Z$ rises faster when the sample is small).
For $p = 0.95$ and $k = 0.10$, what is the expected-claims standard for frequency under Poisson counts?
$z_{0.975} = 1.96$, severity term off, Poisson ⇒ $\sigma_N^2/\mu_N = 1$. $n_c = (1.96/0.10)^2(1) = 19.6^2 = 384.16 \approx \mathbf{384}$ claims.
Severity has mean 500 and standard deviation 1,500. By what factor is the severity standard larger than the base $(z/k)^2$?
$\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = (1500/500)^2 = 3^2 = \mathbf{9}$. The severity standard is $9\times$ the base.
How do you convert between the exposure standard $n_e$ and the expected-claims standard $n_c$?
Multiply or divide by the mean frequency per exposure $\mu_N$: $\;n_c = n_e\,\mu_N \Leftrightarrow n_e = n_c/\mu_N$.
A risk needs 3,000 expected claims for full credibility but only 750 are observed. What is $Z$?
$Z = \sqrt{750/3000} = \sqrt{0.25} = \mathbf{0.5}.$
$n_e = 80{,}000$ exposures are needed for full credibility. What is $Z$ for (a) 60,000 exposures, (b) 120,000 exposures?
(a) $Z = \sqrt{60{,}000/80{,}000} = \mathbf{0.866}$. (b) $120{,}000 > 80{,}000$, so full credibility: $Z = \mathbf{1}$.
Frequency is Poisson; the aggregate standard uses 90% probability and 5% tolerance, with $\mathrm{CV}_X^2 = 1$. Find $n_c$.
$z_{0.95} = 1.64485$. $n_c = (1.64485/0.05)^2(1 + 1) = 1082.4 \times 2 = \mathbf{2{,}164.8}$ claims.
Given EPV $= 8$, VHM $= 2$, and $n = 5$ observations, find $k$ and the Bühlmann $Z$.
$k = \text{EPV}/\text{VHM} = 8/2 = 4.$ $Z = 5/(5+4) = 5/9 \approx \mathbf{0.556}.$
In Bühlmann–Straub, total exposure is $m = 800$, $k = 200$, exposure-weighted mean $\bar{X} = 0.15$, collective mean $\mu = 0.10$. Find the estimate.
$Z = 800/(800+200) = 0.8.$ $P_C = 0.8(0.15) + 0.2(0.10) = 0.12 + 0.02 = \mathbf{0.14}.$
