Insurance and Reinsurance Coverages

Every coverage modification — deductible, limit, coinsurance, reinsurance — is just algebra on the same two layers: what stays below a retention and what spills above it. This page maps the contract structures (especially reinsurance) and the high-level risk framing; the heavy per-loss / per-payment severity math lives on the Coverage Modifications page.

Learning Objectives

After studying this topic, you should be able to:

  • Describe the standard coverage modifications — deductibles (fixed-dollar / ordinary, fixed-percentage, disappearing, franchise, per-calendar-year, elimination period), policy limits, and coinsurance — and state the objectives (and drawbacks) of each.
  • Combine an ordinary deductible $d$, policy limit / maximum covered loss, and coinsurance $\alpha$ into the standard three-modification payment structure and compute the maximum covered loss $m = \tfrac{u}{\alpha}+d$.
  • Distinguish the per-loss random variable $Y^L$ from the per-payment random variable $Y^P$, and link them via $\Pr(X>d)$.
  • Explain why insurers buy reinsurance (capital relief, underwriting capacity, catastrophe protection, stabilizing experience, diversification, expertise, withdrawal) and define cedant, reinsurer, retrocession, and ceding commission.
  • Classify reinsurance two ways: treaty vs facultative, and pro rata (proportional) vs excess of loss (non-proportional).
  • Split a loss between cedant and reinsurer under quota share, surplus share, excess of loss (per-risk, per-occurrence, aggregate/stop-loss), and layered XL treaties.
  • Define the individual risk model and the collective risk model and compute the mean and variance of aggregate losses under each.
  • Explain the key ways short-term insurance differs from long-term (life-contingent) insurance.

Key Concepts

1. Coverage modifications — the high-level menu

Most real insurance contracts combine some mix of deductibles, policy limits, and coinsurance — and so do the reinsurance treaties insurers buy. A deductible is the amount of each claim the policyholder pays before the insurer pays anything. The exam-relevant deductible types:

TypeWhat it doesInsurer pays
Fixed-dollar (ordinary)Fixed amount $d$ regardless of loss size. The default — and the most exam-likely.$\max(0, X-d)$
Franchise"Cliff" deductible: once the loss exceeds $d$, the deductible vanishes and the full loss is paid. Exam-likely; was common in ocean marine.$0$ if $X\le d$, else $X$
Fixed-percentageDeductible is $\delta X$ but with a dollar floor $d$ (so small claims are still excluded).see Formulas
DisappearingDeductible decreases linearly from $d$ to $0$ as the loss grows from $a$ to $b$ ($d\le a < b$).see Formulas
Per-calendar-year (health)An individual amount and a family amount; coverage starts when either is met. Rare outside health / major-medical.—
Elimination periodDisability/WC: a waiting period before benefits begin. If the retroactive-qualification period equals the elimination period, the policy behaves like a franchise deductible.—
Coach's remark — focus your effort

For the exam, master fixed-dollar (ordinary) and franchise deductibles. The fixed-percentage, disappearing, calendar-year, and elimination-period flavours are mostly qualitative recognition — know what they are and which one a word problem is describing.

Elimination period vs. retroactive qualification period (WC / disability)

In workers-compensation and disability cover, the elimination period is the waiting time between the date of the disability/accident and the date benefits begin — during it the insured receives nothing. If the disability lasts long enough, benefits may be paid retroactively back over the elimination period. The retroactive qualification period is the length of time the disability must continue before those retroactive benefits are triggered.

The exam rule: when the retroactive qualification period equals the elimination period, the policy behaves exactly like a franchise deductible — short disabilities (below the threshold) get $0$, but once the threshold is crossed the insured is paid in full back to day one (the "deductible" disappears), just as a franchise pays the whole loss $X$ once $X\gt d$.

A policy limit $u$ caps the insurer's payment at $u$. Coinsurance $\alpha$ ($0<\alpha\le1$) is the fraction of the (post-deductible, post-limit) loss the insurer pays; the insured retains $1-\alpha$. Order of application: cap → subtract deductible → apply coinsurance.

Objectives & trade-offs (qualitative — examiners love these)
  • Deductibles eliminate small, expensive-to-administer claims, reduce average large-claim payments (lowering premiums), and give the insured "skin in the game" (less moral hazard). Downsides: claims not paid in full (dissatisfaction), harder marketing, and insureds may inflate claims to recover the deductible.
  • Policy limits clarify and bound the insurer's obligation, reduce insolvency risk, and let buyers trade lower limits for lower premiums. A limit is the mirror image of a deductible — the insured's payment $\max(0,X-u)$ looks just like an insurer's payment under a deductible of $u$, and the two payments always sum to $X$.

2. The three-modification payment structure & maximum covered loss

When an ordinary deductible $d$, policy limit $u$, and coinsurance $\alpha$ are all present, the insurer's payment has three pieces (zero below $d$, linear in the middle, capped at the limit). The pivotal quantity is the maximum covered loss $m$ — the smallest ground-up loss at which the insurer first pays the full policy limit:

Maximum covered loss $$m = \frac{u}{\alpha} + d.$$

With a franchise deductible instead (full loss paid above $d$), the maximum covered loss equals the limit itself, $m=u$ — there is no $d$ added on. (See Example 1.)

Turning modifications on/off

Use one master structure and switch features off: set $d=0$ (no deductible), $u=\infty$ (no limit), or $\alpha=1$ (no coinsurance). If — rarely — coinsurance is applied before the deductible, the deductible is effectively met at $d^*=d/\alpha$, and $m=\tfrac{u}{\alpha}+d^*$.

3. Ground-up loss: per-loss vs. per-payment

Start with the ground-up loss $X\ge0$ — the full economic loss before any provision. Two perspectives you must never confuse:

  • Per-loss variable $Y^L$: payment per loss event, including zeros for losses that produce no payment. The natural variable for the insurer (every loss is one observation).
  • Per-payment variable $Y^P$: payment conditional on a payment being made ($Y^P=Y^L\mid Y^L>0$). Zeros discarded — this is what a claims database shows.
Mantra

Per-loss includes the zeros; per-payment conditions them away.

$$E[Y^P]=\frac{E[Y^L]}{\Pr(X>d)}.$$

The detailed moments, LER, and inflation algebra are on the Coverage Modifications page — here just keep the distinction crisp.

4. Reinsurance — what it is and why insurers buy it

Reinsurance is insurance for insurance companies. The cedant (primary / direct insurer) cedes some or all of its risk to a reinsurer. A reinsurer can in turn cede risk onward to another reinsurer — this is called retrocession. Beyond sharing premiums and losses, the reinsurer often pays the cedant a ceding commission to reimburse part of the cedant's cost of writing the business.

Functions of reinsurance (memorize the list — pure exam fodder)
  • Capital relief — reserves aren't required for risk that's reinsured, freeing capital for other uses.
  • Increase underwriting capacity — cede part of a risk that's bigger than your appetite, so you can still write it.
  • Catastrophe protection — shields against rare, severe events.
  • Stabilize loss experience — smooths fluctuations even in non-catastrophic claims.
  • Risk diversification — avoids over-concentration in one region or peril.
  • Technical expertise — reinsurers have deep experience with large/complex claims.
  • Withdrawal — an insurer can exit a market by ceding all of its risk.

5. Two classifications of reinsurance

Every treaty is described two independent ways: how risks are selected (treaty vs facultative) and how the loss is shared (pro rata vs excess of loss).

AxisTypeMeaning
Risk selectionTreatyAll risks in a specified line/class are automatically ceded; the reinsurer cannot underwrite each one individually.
FacultativeRisks ceded individually; the reinsurer underwrites and accepts/rejects each one.
Loss sharingPro rata (proportional)Cedant and reinsurer share risk, premium, and losses in fixed proportions. Two flavours: quota share and surplus share.
Excess of loss (non-proportional)Reinsurer pays only amounts above the cedant's retention — structurally a deductible held by the cedant.

6. Proportional reinsurance: quota share & surplus share

  • Quota share — both parties share a fixed percentage of every risk. A "70%/30%" treaty means the cedant pays 70% and the reinsurer 30% of all covered claims. Mathematically identical to coinsurance: if $Y$ is the insurer's gross payment and the cedant keeps fraction $a$, $$Z_I=aY,\qquad Z_R=(1-a)Y,\qquad Z_I+Z_R=Y.$$
  • Surplus share — proportional sharing, but only for policies whose coverage exceeds a stated amount called the cedant's net retention (retention limit), a.k.a. the reinsurer's attachment point. Policies below the retention are kept 100% by the cedant; above it, the cedant and reinsurer split per agreed percentages (which in practice vary by each policy's retention and limit).
Two exam conventions to lock in
  • Surplus share is qualitative on FAM. Because the ceded percentage varies policy-by-policy, expect only conceptual questions — don't expect to compute a surplus-share split numerically.
  • Quota-share ambiguity. If a question says "70% quota share" without saying whose share it is, assume the percentage is the reinsurer's share (so the reinsurer covers 70%).

7. Excess-of-loss reinsurance: per-risk, per-occurrence, aggregate

Under excess of loss (XL) the reinsurer pays everything above the cedant's retention (attachment point) $\delta$ — just like a deductible the cedant holds:

$$Z_I=\min(Y,\delta),\qquad Z_R=(Y-\delta)_+.$$

The crucial distinction is what quantity the retention attaches to:

  • Per-risk XL — the retention applies to each individual policy/risk's total loss. Sum the claims belonging to a single policy, then apply $\delta$ to that sum.
  • Per-occurrence XL — the retention applies to the combined losses of one event (e.g. one earthquake) across all affected policies. Sum all claims from the event, then apply $\delta$ once.
  • Aggregate XL = stop-loss — the retention applies to the total of all losses over the policy period (the aggregate $S$). The reinsurer pays $(S-\delta)_+$.

Layered XL. Real treaties often split the excess into layers, each with its own reinsurer share and band of loss (e.g. "70% of 500,000 in excess of 1,000,000"). Apply each layer's percentage to the portion of the loss that falls inside that layer, then add (see Example 4).

The recurring algebra

Every retention/deductible/attachment splits a quantity into an upper layer $(\cdot-\delta)_+$ and a lower layer $\min(\cdot,\delta)$, and the two always sum to the whole. Deductibles, policy limits, XL, and stop-loss are all the same two-layer split applied to different quantities ($X$ vs $S$ vs a per-policy or per-event sum).

8. Two aggregate-loss models

Aggregate loss $S$ = total of all claims in a period. Two standard constructions:

  • Individual risk model: $S=X_1+\cdots+X_n$, a fixed number $n$ of policies, each $X_i$ the (possibly zero) loss on policy $i$. The $X_i$ need not be identically distributed. Natural for a group of $n$ distinct insureds (e.g. group life).
  • Collective risk model: $S=X_1+\cdots+X_N$, where the claim count $N$ (frequency) is itself random and the $X_i$ (severities) are i.i.d. and independent of $N$. This is a compound distribution (compound Poisson, etc.).

Formulas

Setup

$X\ge0$ is the ground-up loss; $d$ = deductible, $u$ = policy limit, $\alpha$ = coinsurance. Detailed expected-value / LER / inflation formulas live on the Coverage Modifications page — here are the structural payment functions and the reinsurance splits.

Deductible payment functions

Ordinary (fixed-dollar) deductible $d$ $$Y=\max(0,X-d)=\begin{cases}0,& X\le d\\ X-d,& X>d.\end{cases}$$ Franchise deductible $d$ $$Y=\begin{cases}0,& X\le d\\ X,& X>d.\end{cases}$$ Fixed-percentage $\delta$ with floor $d$ — effective deductible $D=\max(d,\delta X)$ $$Y=\begin{cases}0,& X\le d\\ X-d,& d\lt X\le d/\delta\\ (1-\delta)X,& X> d/\delta.\end{cases}$$ Disappearing deductible: $d\to0$ linearly over $[a,b]$ ($d\le a\lt b$) $$Y=\begin{cases}0,& X\lt d\\ X-d,& d\le X\le a\\ X-d\!\left(\dfrac{b-X}{b-a}\right),& a\lt X\le b\\ X,& X>b.\end{cases}$$

Policy limit & coinsurance

Policy limit $u$ $$Y=\min(X,u)=\begin{cases}X,& X\le u\\ u,& X>u.\end{cases}\qquad \text{Insured pays }\max(0,X-u).$$ Coinsurance $\alpha$ (insurer's share) $$Y=\alpha X.$$

All three together (ordinary $d$, limit $u$, coinsurance $\alpha$)

$$Y=\begin{cases}0,& X\le d\\ \alpha(X-d),& d\lt X\le m\\ u,& X>m\end{cases}\qquad\text{where } m=\frac{u}{\alpha}+d \text{ (maximum covered loss).}$$

Per-payment link: $E[Y^P]=\dfrac{E[Y^L]}{\Pr(X>d)}$.

Reinsurance splits

Quota share — reinsurer share $1-a$ (cedant keeps fraction $a$ of every loss $Y$) $$Z_I=aY,\quad Z_R=(1-a)Y,\quad \operatorname{Var}(Z_R)=(1-a)^2\operatorname{Var}(Y).$$ Excess of loss — retention (attachment) $\delta$ $$Z_I=\min(Y,\delta),\quad Z_R=(Y-\delta)_+,\quad E[Z_R]=E[Y]-E[Y\wedge\delta].$$ Stop-loss (aggregate XL) on $S$, retention $d$ $$Z_R=(S-d)_+,\qquad E[(S-d)_+]=E[S]-E[S\wedge d]=\sum_{s>d}(s-d)\Pr(S=s)\ \ (\text{discrete }S).$$ Layered XL — reinsurer pays $\sum_k p_k\cdot(\text{loss falling in layer }k)$ $$Z_R=\sum_k p_k\big[(Y\wedge t_k)-(Y\wedge t_{k-1})\big].$$

Individual risk model

$$S=\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i,\quad E[S]=\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i],\quad \operatorname{Var}(S)=\sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{Var}(X_i)\ \ (\text{independent }X_i).$$ Each policy pays benefit $b_i$ with prob. $q_i$, else 0 $$E[X_i]=b_i q_i,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X_i)=b_i^2 q_i(1-q_i).$$

Collective risk model (compound distribution)

$$S=\sum_{i=1}^{N}X_i,\quad N\perp\{X_i\},\ X_i \text{ i.i.d.}$$ $$E[S]=E[N]\,E[X],\qquad \operatorname{Var}(S)=E[N]\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(N)\big(E[X]\big)^2.$$

For the compound Poisson ($N\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, so $\operatorname{Var}(N)=E[N]=\lambda$):

$$E[S]=\lambda E[X],\qquad \operatorname{Var}(S)=\lambda\,E[X^2].$$

Worked Examples

Example 1 One loss, four deductible designs (+ franchise max covered loss) ▾

Dorothy incurs a loss of $X=2{,}350$. Find the insurer's payout under each design.

(1) Ordinary deductible 500. Loss exceeds $d$, so $Y=2{,}350-500=\boxed{1{,}850}$.

(2) Fixed-percentage 30% with floor 500. $0.30(2{,}350)=705>500$, so the effective deductible is $705$ and $Y=2{,}350-705=\boxed{1{,}645}$.

(3) Disappearing deductible of 500 for losses below 2,000, decreasing linearly to 0 between 2,000 and 3,000. Since $2{,}000<2{,}350<3{,}000$, $$Y=2{,}350-500\!\left(\frac{3{,}000-2{,}350}{3{,}000-2{,}000}\right)=2{,}350-500(0.65)=\boxed{2{,}025}.$$

(4) Franchise deductible 500. Loss exceeds $d$, so the insurer pays the full loss $Y=\boxed{2{,}350}$.

Franchise + limit twist

If instead a policy "pays the full amount of losses above 500, up to a maximum payment of 1,500," that's a franchise deductible with limit 1,500 — so the maximum covered loss is $m=u=1{,}500$ (not $2{,}000$). A claim of 2,100 exceeds $m$, so the payment is capped at $\boxed{1{,}500}$.

Example 2 Deductible + coinsurance + limit (maximum covered loss) ▾

A policy: the policyholder is fully responsible for claims below 200; the policy reimburses 80% of claims in excess of 200; the payment is capped at 2,000. So $d=200$, $\alpha=0.80$, $u=2{,}000$. Find the payment for a claim of $X=2{,}100$.

Maximum covered loss: $m=\dfrac{u}{\alpha}+d=\dfrac{2{,}000}{0.8}+200=2{,}500+200=2{,}700.$

Since $200<2{,}100\le2{,}700$, use the middle piece:

$$Y=\alpha(X-d)=0.8(2{,}100-200)=0.8(1{,}900)=\boxed{1{,}520}.$$
Why $m$ matters

If you had forgotten the cap and plugged $X=2{,}100$ blindly you'd still be fine here (because $2{,}100\lt m$), but if the claim were 3,000 the answer would be the limit $u=2{,}000$, not $0.8(3{,}000-200)=2{,}240$. Always check against $m$.

Example 3 Excess of loss: per-risk vs. per-occurrence ▾

An insurer's claims from a single earthquake, with an XL treaty at retention $\delta=500{,}000$:

  • Policy A3001: $250{,}000+325{,}000=575{,}000>500{,}000$ → cedant retains $500{,}000$.
  • Policy A2936: $236{,}500<500{,}000$ → cedant retains all $236{,}500$.

(2) Reinsurer's loss on a per-occurrence basis. Apply $\delta$ once to the whole event:

Same numbers, very different answers depending on what the retention attaches to. Read "per-risk" vs "per-occurrence" vs "aggregate" carefully.

Example 4 Layered excess-of-loss treaty ▾

A primary insurer has an XL treaty with net retention 1,000,000, structured in layers (percentages are the reinsurer's share):

  • Layer 1: 70% of 500,000 in excess of 1,000,000 (covers loss in $[1.0\text{M}, 1.5\text{M}]$)
  • Layer 2: 80% of 500,000 in excess of 1,500,000 (covers $[1.5\text{M}, 2.0\text{M}]$)
  • Layer 3: 90% in excess of 2,000,000 (covers everything above 2.0M)

Find the reinsurer's payment for a loss of 2,750,000. The reinsurer pays nothing up to 1.0M, then a share of each layer's filled amount:

$$ \begin{aligned} Z_R&=0.70(500{,}000)+0.80(500{,}000)+0.90(2{,}750{,}000-2{,}000{,}000)\\ &=350{,}000+400{,}000+0.90(750{,}000)\\ &=350{,}000+400{,}000+675{,}000=1{,}425{,}000. \end{aligned} $$
Answer
$Z_R=\boxed{1{,}425{,}000}$ (the cedant keeps the 1.0M retention plus the un-ceded slivers of each layer).

Example 8 Inverse disappearing deductible — recover $b$ from a claims table, then price a new claim ▾

You are not told the parameters $(d,a,b)$ of a linearly disappearing deductible. Instead you are given recorded (claim amount, payment) pairs and must reverse-engineer the design. The trick: effective deductible $D=X-\text{payment}$, and inside the disappearing band $D$ is linear in $X$, falling to $0$ at the upper bound $b$.

Claim amount $X$Payment $Y$Effective deductible $D=X-Y$
2000$\ge 200$ (still fully retained)
750600150
900800100
1,3001,3000

Find the payment for a claim of $X=1{,}000$.

Step 1 — recover the upper bound $b$ by linear interpolation. Use the two interior points that sit inside the band, $(750,150)$ and $(900,100)$. The deductible line has slope

$$\frac{900-750}{100-150}=\frac{150}{-50}=-3,$$

and the deductible hits $0$ at $X=b$, so extend the same line from $(900,100)$ down to $D=0$:

$$-3=\frac{b-900}{0-100}\;\Longrightarrow\;b-900=300\;\Longrightarrow\;b=1{,}200.$$

Step 2 — confirm $1{,}000$ lies in the band ($a\lt 1{,}000\lt b=1{,}200$): yes, so the deductible still applies on a sliding scale (it has not yet disappeared).

Step 3 — price the new claim. Interpolate the deductible at $X=1{,}000$ along the same line through $(900,100)$:

$$-3=\frac{1{,}000-900}{D-100}\;\Longrightarrow\;D-100=\frac{100}{-3}=-33.3333\;\Longrightarrow\;D=66.6667.$$ $$Y=1{,}000-D=1{,}000-66.6667=933.3333.$$
Answer

$b=1{,}200$ and the payment for the $1{,}000$ claim is $Y=\boxed{933.33}$.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Maximum covered loss formula. With ordinary deductible, limit, and coinsurance, $m=\tfrac{u}{\alpha}+d$ — not just $u$. With a franchise deductible, though, $m=u$ (no $d$ added). Mixing these up caps the payment in the wrong place.
  • Order of operations. Always cap → subtract deductible → apply coinsurance. Inflation (if present) scales the loss first. Applying coinsurance before the deductible, or inflating the deductible itself, is wrong.
  • Franchise vs ordinary deductible. Once the loss clears $d$, a franchise pays the full loss — $d$ more per payment than an ordinary deductible. Candidates routinely treat it like an ordinary deductible.
  • Per-loss vs per-payment. "Per payment" → divide $E[Y^L]$ by $\Pr(X>d)$; "per loss" → don't divide. The single most common point lost on this material.
  • Inverse disappearing deductible — don't skip the band check. When you reverse-engineer a disappearing deductible from a (claim, payment) table, use effective deductible $D=X-Y$ and recover $b$ by interpolating $D$ to $0$. Then verify the new claim is actually inside the band $a\lt X\lt b$ before interpolating its deductible. If $X\ge b$ the deductible has already vanished and the answer is the full loss; if $X\le a$ the full fixed deductible $d$ applies. Blindly interpolating a claim that lies outside the band (see Example 8, where $b=1{,}200$) gives a wrong payment. Also remember the slope is negative — the deductible decreases as the loss grows.
  • XL basis: per-risk vs per-occurrence vs aggregate. The retention attaches to a single policy's total (per-risk), one event's total across policies (per-occurrence), or the whole period's total $S$ (aggregate/stop-loss). Applying it to the wrong quantity is a guaranteed wrong answer (see Example 3).
  • Layered XL. Apply each layer's percentage only to the loss inside that layer's band, and remember the reinsurer pays $0$ below the retention. Don't apply one percentage to the whole excess.
  • Quota-share ambiguity. If the question doesn't say whose share a quoted percentage is, assume it's the reinsurer's share.
  • Surplus share is qualitative. Don't waste time hunting for a numeric surplus-share split — FAM tests it conceptually (retention line / attachment point), not computationally.
  • Quota-share variance. The reinsurer's variance is $(1-a)^2\operatorname{Var}(Y)$ — the proportion is squared.
  • Compound variance formula. $\operatorname{Var}(S)=E[N]\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(N)(E[X])^2$ — never $\operatorname{Var}(N)\operatorname{Var}(X)$. Compound Poisson shortcut: $\lambda E[X^2]$ (second moment of severity, not variance).
  • Individual vs collective model. Individual = fixed $n$, possibly non-identical $X_i$, sum variances directly. Collective = random $N$, i.i.d. severities, use the compound formula.
  • Cedant terminology. The cedant is the primary/direct insurer that buys reinsurance; retrocession is a reinsurer ceding onward; a ceding commission flows from reinsurer to cedant.

A policy has ordinary deductible 200, coinsurance 80%, and policy limit 2,000. What is the maximum covered loss, and what does it represent?
$m=\dfrac{u}{\alpha}+d=\dfrac{2{,}000}{0.8}+200=\mathbf{2{,}700}$ — the smallest ground-up loss at which the insurer first pays the full policy limit.
How does the maximum covered loss differ when the deductible is a franchise rather than ordinary?
A franchise pays the full loss above $d$, so the maximum covered loss equals the limit itself, $m=u$ — you do not add $d$. (Example 1's franchise-plus-limit policy caps at 1,500, not 2,000.)
Define the retroactive qualification period and state when an elimination-period policy behaves like a franchise deductible.
The retroactive qualification period is how long a disability must continue before benefits are paid retroactively over the elimination period (the initial waiting period during which nothing is paid). When the retroactive qualification period equals the elimination period, the policy acts like a franchise deductible: nothing below the threshold, but full payment back to day one once the threshold is crossed.
A family policy has a $100$ per-person and a $250$ per-family annual deductible. Claims occur in order: Dad 70, Chloe 110, Chloe 90, Anne 180. How much does the insurer pay in total?
Insurer pays $0+10+90+100=\mathbf{200}$ (family pays $70+100+0+80=250$, exactly the family cap). Key moves: Chloe's first claim meets her individual $100$ (insurer pays the $10$ excess); her second is then paid in full ($90$); Anne's claim only needs $80$ more to reach the $250$ family cap, so the insurer covers the remaining $100$.
You are given (claim, payment) pairs $(750,600)$ and $(900,800)$ for a linearly disappearing deductible. Find the upper bound $b$ at which the deductible disappears.
Effective deductibles are $D=X-Y$: $150$ and $100$. The line has slope $\frac{900-750}{100-150}=-3$; extending to $D=0$ gives $-3=\frac{b-900}{0-100}$, so $b-900=300$ and $b=\mathbf{1{,}200}$. (Then check any new claim is below $b$ before interpolating its deductible.)
Name four functions (benefits) of reinsurance for a primary insurer.
Any four of: capital relief, increased underwriting capacity, catastrophe protection, stabilizing loss experience, risk diversification, access to technical expertise, and orderly withdrawal from a market.
Distinguish treaty from facultative reinsurance.
Treaty cedes all risks in a specified line/class automatically (the reinsurer can't underwrite each one); facultative cedes individual risks, each underwritten and accepted/rejected by the reinsurer.
Name the two types of pro rata reinsurance and the key difference between them.
Quota share (both parties share a fixed % of every risk) and surplus share (proportional sharing applies only to policies whose coverage exceeds the cedant's net retention / the reinsurer's attachment point).
For excess-of-loss reinsurance, how do per-risk, per-occurrence, and aggregate (stop-loss) differ?
The retention attaches to: a single policy's total loss (per-risk); the combined loss of one event across multiple policies (per-occurrence); or the total of all losses over the policy period $S$ (aggregate/stop-loss, reinsurer pays $(S-d)_+$).
A question says "70% quota share" but doesn't state whose share it is. What should you assume?
Assume the percentage is the reinsurer's share — the reinsurer covers 70%, the cedant 30%.
A quota-share treaty cedes 30% of every loss. If $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=4{,}000{,}000$, what is the variance of the reinsurer's payment?
Reinsurer pays $0.30Y$, so variance $=(0.30)^2(4{,}000{,}000)=\mathbf{360{,}000}$ — square the proportion.
An XL treaty has retention 500,000. Two claims of 250,000 and 325,000 come from the same policy. What does the cedant retain on a per-risk basis?
Sum the policy's claims first: $250{,}000+325{,}000=575{,}000>500{,}000$, so the cedant retains the full retention $\mathbf{500{,}000}$ and the reinsurer pays 75,000.
In the collective risk model with $N\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, write $\operatorname{Var}(S)$. Which moment of $X$ appears?
$\operatorname{Var}(S)=\lambda\,E[X^2]$ — the second raw moment of severity (because $\operatorname{Var}(N)=E[N]=\lambda$ collapses the general formula).
