Parametric and Non-Parametric Estimation

Two words decide every estimation problem on this exam — truncation conditions the density and drops data; censoring keeps the point but swaps $f(x)$ for $S(u)$. Master the four-step likelihood recipe, then learn the shortcuts that turn a page of algebra into one line.

📖 Loss Models · KPW ⏱️ ~3–4 hrs 🔑 MLE recipe ✂️ Truncation · Censoring · Grouping ⚡ Closed-form shortcuts 🔑 Kaplan–Meier · Greenwood
Learning Objectives

After studying this topic you should be able to:

  • Construct and use the empirical distribution (and empirical survival/cdf) for complete, individual data.
  • Estimate parameters via the method of moments and percentile matching.
  • Execute the four-step MLE recipe (likelihood → log-likelihood → derivative → set to zero) for complete, censored, truncated, and grouped data.
  • Write the correct likelihood factor for left/right truncation, left/right censoring, and grouped intervals, including mixed policies with different deductibles and limits.
  • Derive MLEs for common parametric distributions (exponential, gamma scale, Pareto, single-parameter Pareto, Weibull, lognormal, inverse exponential, beta, Poisson, binomial, negative binomial, uniform).
  • Apply the matching-moments shortcuts for severity and $(a,b,0)$ distributions, plus the $(a,b,1)$ zero-truncated / zero-modified shortcuts.
  • Use the invariance property $\hat{g}(\theta)=g(\hat\theta)$ to estimate any function of a parameter (e.g. a VaR).
  • Construct the Kaplan–Meier product-limit estimator and the Nelson–Aalen estimator of the cumulative hazard from left-truncated, right-censored data.
  • Estimate the variance of estimators: empirical, Greenwood's formula, Aalen's variance, and the asymptotic variance of MLEs via the information matrix; build confidence intervals (linear and log-transformed).

Key Concepts

Data types: the four words that change everything

  • Complete (individual) data: every observation is a single exact value $x_i$. No information loss.
  • Grouped data: you only know counts in intervals.
  • Truncated data: observations below (left) or above (right) a threshold are not in the data set at all — you don't even know how many there were. A deductible $d$ produces left truncation: claims below $d$ are never reported. You must condition on the value exceeding the truncation point.
  • Censored data: the observation is in the data set, but you only know it exceeds (right-censored) or is below (left-censored) some value. A policy limit $u$ produces right censoring: any loss $\geq u$ is recorded as exactly $u$. You know it happened and that it was at least $u$.
The single most important distinction on this exam
  • Truncation affects the denominator (it conditions the density/probability) and removes data points.
  • Censoring keeps the data point but replaces a density factor $f(x)$ with a survival factor $S(u)$.

What "likelihood" means

The likelihood of a single data value is the probability (or density) function evaluated at that value. Assuming the observations are i.i.d., the likelihood of the whole sample is the product of the individual likelihoods. The MLE is the parameter value that makes this product as large as possible — i.e. that makes the observed data "most probable."

Coach's Remark — likelihood vs. joint probability

The likelihood function looks like a joint probability evaluated at all the observations, and for this exam you may treat it that way. But they are conceptually different: a likelihood is a function of the parameter(s) (the data are fixed), whereas a joint probability is a function of the random-variable outcomes (the parameter is fixed). Evaluating a PDF at a point isn't a probability — it's a relative likelihood — so the product of PDFs serves as a proxy for the probability of the set of observations.

The four-step MLE recipe

Every MLE problem — complete, censored, truncated, or grouped — follows the same four steps. The only thing that changes is which factor you write for each observation.

  1. Construct the likelihood $L(\theta)=\prod_i (\text{factor}_i)$.
  2. Take logs to get the log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)=\ln L(\theta)$ (turns the product into a sum).
  3. Differentiate: compute $\ell'(\theta)=\dfrac{d}{d\theta}\ell(\theta)$.
  4. Set to zero and solve: $\ell'(\theta)=0$, then check it's a maximum and that the solution is in the valid parameter range.
Coach's Remark — why logs, and dropping constants
  • Logs are optional but smart. $\ln$ is increasing, so it peaks at the same $\hat\theta$. Differentiating a sum (log-likelihood) avoids the product rule you'd need on $L$ itself.
  • Drop multiplicative constants early. Anything that does not depend on the parameter (a factor like $c=\prod x_i^3/6^5$) is multiplicative in $L$ — it vanishes when you differentiate, so you may discard it from the start. Caution: if a question needs the actual value of $\ell$ (e.g. a likelihood-ratio test later), you must keep the constants.

Non-parametric (empirical) models

The empirical distribution puts probability mass $1/n$ on each of the $n$ data points. It makes no distributional assumption.

$$F_n(x) = \frac{\#\{x_i \le x\}}{n}, \qquad S_n(x) = 1 - F_n(x).$$

When data are left-truncated and/or right-censored, the empirical cdf is generalized to the Kaplan–Meier estimator (for $S(x)$) and the cumulative hazard to the Nelson–Aalen estimator.

Borderline FAM scope — prioritise MLE

The Kaplan–Meier, Nelson–Aalen, Greenwood, and Aalen-variance material (here, plus the matching Formulas and Worked Examples 10 & 12) is non-parametric estimation. FAM's Topic 3 learning outcome names Maximum Likelihood Estimation only — there is no non-parametric outcome — so this is at most light/background for FAM. (It's left in because the Loss Models Ch. 11 reading borders on it and it reinforces censoring/truncation intuition.) Spend your time on the MLE recipe; treat K–M / N–A as understanding-only unless your prep source says otherwise.

Parametric estimation methods (in order of exam importance)

  1. Maximum likelihood (MLE) — by far the most tested. Pick parameters that maximize the probability of the observed data. Works cleanly for censored/truncated data and has known asymptotic variance.
  2. Method of moments (MoM) — set sample raw moments equal to theoretical raw moments and solve. Simple but inefficient; uses only complete data.
  3. Percentile matching — set the fitted percentile equal to the smoothed empirical percentile and solve.

Likelihood building blocks

For each observation, write one factor:

SituationLikelihood factor
Exact observation at $x$ (complete)$f(x)$  (or $p(x)$ if discrete)
Right-censored at $u$ (e.g. policy limit)$S(u)=\Pr(X\ge u)$
Left-censored at $d$$F(d)=\Pr(X\le d)$
Left-truncated at $d$ (e.g. deductible), exact loss $x$$\dfrac{f(x)}{S(d)}=\dfrac{f(x)}{\Pr(X>d)}$
Right-truncated at $u$, exact value $x$$\dfrac{f(x)}{F(u)}=\dfrac{f(x)}{\Pr(X\lt u)}$
Left-truncated at $d$, right-censored at $u$$\dfrac{S(u)}{S(d)}$
Interval / grouped $(a,\,b]$$F(b) - F(a)=S(a)-S(b)$

Multiply all factors, take logs, differentiate, set to zero. For discrete grouped data the interval factor is the sum of pmf values, e.g. $\Pr(1\le N\le 2)=p(1)+p(2)$.

The invariance property of the MLE

One of the most useful facts on this exam: the MLE of any function of a parameter is that function of the MLE.

$$\widehat{g(\theta)} = g(\hat\theta).$$

So once you have $\hat\theta$, you can immediately read off the MLE of the fitted mean, variance, a percentile/VaR, a limited expected value, etc. — just plug $\hat\theta$ into the formula for that quantity.

Special cases & shortcuts

For complete data, many MLEs reduce to matching raw moments — set the fitted $k$-th raw moment equal to the empirical one, $\mathrm E[X^k]=\frac1n\sum x_i^k$, and solve. These shortcuts are the fastest route through severity and $(a,b,0)$/$(a,b,1)$ frequency problems (see the Formulas section). Some closed forms (exponential, Pareto, Weibull) extend to truncated/censored data; others (uniform, inverse exponential, beta) are complete-data only.

Kaplan–Meier and Nelson–Aalen

These estimate the survival function $S(t)$ when each subject can enter late (left truncation) and leave early (right censoring). Define at each observed death/loss time $y_j$:

  • $s_j$ = number of observed events (deaths) at $y_j$,
  • $r_j$ = risk set = number under observation at time $y_j$ (those who have entered and not yet exited).

The conditional probability of surviving past $y_j$, given alive just before, is estimated by $1 - s_j/r_j$. The product of these gives Kaplan–Meier; summing the hazard contributions $s_j/r_j$ gives Nelson–Aalen (cumulative hazard $\hat H$), and $\hat S(t) = e^{-\hat H(t)}$.

Formulas

Empirical distribution (complete, individual data)

$$F_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf{1}(x_i \le x), \qquad S_n(x) = 1 - F_n(x).$$ Empirical raw moment (used for MoM) $$\hat\mu_k' = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^k.$$
Notation

$n$ = sample size; $x_i$ = $i$-th observation; $\mathbf{1}(\cdot)$ = indicator.

Method of moments

Solve, for $p$ parameters, the first $p$ equations:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^k = \mathrm{E}[X^k], \quad k = 1, 2, \dots, p.$$

Percentile matching (smoothed empirical percentile)

Order the data $x_{(1)} \le \dots \le x_{(n)}$. The smoothed empirical estimate of the $100p$-th percentile uses

$$\hat\pi_p = x_{(j)} + h\,(x_{(j+1)} - x_{(j)}), \qquad \text{where } (n+1)p = j + h,$$

$j$ = integer part, $0 \le h < 1$. Then set the fitted percentile equal to $\hat\pi_p$: solve $F(\hat\pi_p \mid \theta) = p$ for the parameter(s).

Notation

$p$ = chosen probability level; $j$ = integer index; $h$ = fractional part.

Maximum likelihood

Likelihood and log-likelihood:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n (\text{factor}_i), \qquad \ell(\theta) = \ln L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \ln(\text{factor}_i).$$

The MLE $\hat\theta$ solves $\dfrac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta} = 0$ (and is a maximum). For two parameters, take partial derivatives with respect to each, set both to zero, and solve the system.

Two-parameter MLE (general form) $$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta_1} = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta_2} = 0.$$

MLEs for common distributions (complete data) — matching moments

For complete data, set the fitted raw moment equal to the empirical raw moment. Let $\bar x = \frac1n\sum x_i$.

Closed-form MLEs to memorize
  • Exponential (mean $\theta$): $\quad \hat\theta = \bar x.$  (gamma special case, $\alpha=1$).
  • Gamma with known $\alpha$, scale $\theta$: $\quad \mathrm E[X]=\alpha\theta=\bar x \Rightarrow \hat\theta = \bar x / \alpha.$
  • Normal (the only distribution where you match the first two moments to get both parameters): $\hat\mu = \bar x, \quad \hat\sigma^2 = \frac1n\sum x_i^2 - \bar x^2 = \frac1n\sum (x_i - \bar x)^2$ (divides by $n$, not $n-1$).
  • Lognormal: let $y_i = \ln x_i$, apply the normal shortcuts: $\hat\mu = \frac1n\sum y_i$, $\hat\sigma^2 = \frac1n\sum(y_i - \hat\mu)^2$.
  • Poisson (mean $\lambda$): $\quad \hat\lambda = \bar x.$
  • Binomial with known $m$: $\quad m\hat q=\bar x \Rightarrow \hat q = \bar x / m.$
  • Negative binomial with known $r$: $\quad r\hat\beta=\bar x \Rightarrow \hat\beta = \bar x / r.$  (geometric is $r=1$, so $\hat\beta=\bar x$).
  • Uniform $(0,\theta)$: $\quad \hat\theta = \max(x_i) = x_{(n)}$ (boundary MLE, not from a derivative).
Coach's Remark — fitted mean equals sample mean

For the gamma, binomial, and negative binomial, if $\theta$, $q$, or $\beta$ is the MLE, then the fitted mean equals the sample mean — regardless of how $\alpha$, $m$, $r$ were obtained. So: given $\alpha/m/r$, get the scale instantly by equating fitted mean to $\bar x$; or given the scale's MLE, recover the missing shape parameter the same way. Also note: matching the 2nd moment for $\sigma^2$ is valid only when $\mu$ is also estimated by MLE; if $\mu$ is fixed, use $\hat\sigma^2=\frac1n\sum (x_i-\mu)^2$.

$(a,b,1)$ class shortcuts — zero-truncated & zero-modified

The matching-moments idea extends to the $(a,b,1)$ family; just match the appropriate mean. Let $X$ be the parent $(a,b,0)$ variable.

Zero-truncated: match $\mathrm E[X^T]=\dfrac{1}{1-p_0}\,\mathrm E[X]=\bar x$ $$\text{ZT Poisson: }\ \frac{\hat\lambda}{1-e^{-\hat\lambda}}=\bar x, \qquad \text{ZT binomial: }\ \frac{m\hat q}{1-(1-\hat q)^m}=\bar x,$$ $$\text{ZT negative binomial: }\ \frac{r\hat\beta}{1-(1+\hat\beta)^{-r}}=\bar x.$$ Zero-modified: two matches $$\hat p_0^M=\frac{n_0}{n}\quad(\text{proportion of zeros}), \qquad \mathrm E[X^M]=\frac{1-\hat p_0^M}{1-p_0}\,\mathrm E[X]=\bar x.$$

Here $n_0$ = number of observed zeros. Solve the mean match for the parent parameter, then use $\hat g(\theta)=g(\hat\theta)$ for any modified moment you need.

Uniform $(0,\theta)$ — boundary MLE

The uniform PDF $f(x)=1/\theta$ has no interior critical point: $\ell(\theta)=-n\ln\theta$ gives $\ell'(\theta)=-n/\theta\ne 0$. Since $L=1/\theta^n$ increases as $\theta$ shrinks, push $\theta$ as small as the data allow — but $\theta$ must be at least the largest observation.

$$\hat\theta = \max(x_1,\dots,x_n)=x_{(n)}.$$

Other closed-form shortcuts (truncation/censoring allowed where noted)

Let $n$ = number of uncensored points, $c$ = number of censored points, $x_i$ = observed value (or censoring point), $d_i$ = truncation point ($0$ if none).

Exponential (handles truncation + censoring) $$\hat\theta = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}(x_i-d_i)}{n}=\frac{\text{total amount above truncation, all obs}}{\text{number of uncensored (exact) obs}}.$$ Pareto, fixed $\theta$ (handles truncation + censoring) $$\hat\alpha = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}\big[\ln(x_i+\theta)-\ln(d_i+\theta)\big]}.$$ Single-parameter Pareto, fixed $\theta$ (handles truncation + censoring) $$\hat\alpha = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}\big\{\ln x_i-\ln[\max(\theta,d_i)]\big\}}.$$ Weibull, fixed $\tau$ (handles truncation + censoring) $$\hat\theta = \left(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}x_i^{\tau}-\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}d_i^{\tau}}{n}\right)^{1/\tau}.$$
Complete-data only $$\text{Inverse exponential: }\ \hat\theta=\frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}1/x_i}, \qquad \text{Beta }(b{=}1,\text{ fixed }\theta):\ \hat a=\frac{n}{\,n\ln\theta-\sum\ln x_i\,},$$ $$\text{Beta }(a{=}1,\text{ fixed }\theta):\ \hat b=\frac{n}{\,n\ln\theta-\sum\ln(\theta-x_i)\,}, \qquad \text{Uniform }[0,\theta]\text{ grouped: }\ \hat\theta=c_j\!\left(\frac{n}{\,n-n_j\,}\right).$$
Coach's Remark — how much to memorize

Don't memorize the whole table. The exponential shortcut θ̂ = total payment ÷ number of uncensored observations is the one worth knowing cold. The rest exist for completeness — you're expected to be able to do any of them from first principles (build $L$, log, differentiate, solve). The uniform grouped shortcut and the inverse-exponential/beta forms are complete-data only (uniform also can't handle truncation). In the grouped-uniform formula, $n$ = total observations and $n_j$ = number in the last interval $[c_j,\infty)$.

Kaplan–Meier (product-limit) estimator of $S(t)$

For observed event times $y_1 < y_2 < \dots < y_k$, with $s_j$ events and risk set $r_j$ at $y_j$:

$$\hat S(t) = \prod_{j:\,y_j \le t}\left(1 - \frac{s_j}{r_j}\right), \qquad \hat S(t) = 1 \text{ for } t < y_1.$$

Nelson–Aalen estimator of cumulative hazard $H(t)$

$$\hat H(t) = \sum_{j:\,y_j \le t} \frac{s_j}{r_j}, \qquad \hat S(t) = e^{-\hat H(t)}.$$

Variance estimates and confidence intervals

Greenwood's formula (variance of the Kaplan–Meier $\hat S(t)$) $$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}\big[\hat S(t)\big] = \big[\hat S(t)\big]^2 \sum_{j:\,y_j \le t} \frac{s_j}{r_j\,(r_j - s_j)}.$$ Aalen's variance (variance of the Nelson–Aalen $\hat H(t)$) $$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}\big[\hat H(t)\big] = \sum_{j:\,y_j \le t} \frac{s_j}{r_j^{2}} \quad\text{(textbook form)} \;\;\Big(\text{some texts: } \sum \tfrac{s_j (r_j - s_j)}{r_j^3}\Big).$$

On FAM use $\sum_{j} s_j / r_j^2$ unless told otherwise.

Linear (normal) CI for $S(t)$ $$\hat S(t) \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\,\sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat S(t)]}.$$ Log-transformed CI for $S(t)$ (keeps endpoints in $(0,1)$) $$\big(\hat S(t)^{1/U},\ \hat S(t)^{U}\big), \qquad U = \exp\!\left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}\sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat S(t)]}}{\hat S(t)\,\ln \hat S(t)}\right).$$

Asymptotic variance of MLEs via Fisher information. For a single parameter,

$$I(\theta) = -\mathrm{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial\theta^2}\right], \qquad \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat\theta] \approx \frac{1}{I(\hat\theta)} \approx \left(-\frac{\partial^2 \ell}{\partial\theta^2}\bigg|_{\hat\theta}\right)^{-1}.$$

For multiple parameters, $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}) = I(\hat{\boldsymbol\theta})^{-1}$ (inverse of the information matrix). The delta method gives the variance of a function $g(\hat\theta)$:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[g(\hat\theta)] \approx [g'(\hat\theta)]^2\,\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat\theta].$$
Notation

$z_{1-\alpha/2}$ = standard normal quantile (1.96 for 95%); $\ell$ = log-likelihood; $I$ = Fisher information.

Worked Examples

Example 1 The four-step MLE recipe (exponential, complete data) ▾

You observe 4 claims: $20,\ 50,\ 150,\ 380$. Claim amounts follow an exponential with mean $\theta$. Find the MLE of $\theta$ from first principles.

Step 1 — likelihood. For continuous data each factor is the PDF $f(x)=\frac1\theta e^{-x/\theta}$:

$$L(\theta)=\prod_{i=1}^4\frac1\theta e^{-x_i/\theta}=\frac{1}{\theta^4}\,e^{-(20+50+150+380)/\theta}=\frac{1}{\theta^4}\,e^{-600/\theta}.$$

Step 2 — log-likelihood. $\ \ell(\theta)=-4\ln\theta-\dfrac{600}{\theta}.$

Step 3 — derivative. $\ \ell'(\theta)=-\dfrac{4}{\theta}+\dfrac{600}{\theta^2}.$

Step 4 — set to zero. $\ -\dfrac{4}{\theta}+\dfrac{600}{\theta^2}=0 \Rightarrow -4\theta+600=0 \Rightarrow \hat\theta=\dfrac{600}{4}=150.$

Answer
$\boxed{\hat\theta=150}$ — identical to the shortcut $\hat\theta=\bar x=600/4=150$.
Example 2 Recognizing a gamma from its PDF (the shape trick) ▾

Losses follow $f(x)=\dfrac{x^3 e^{-x/\theta}}{6\,\theta^4},\ x>0$. You observe 5 losses totaling 700. Find the MLE of $\theta$.

First principles. The product of PDFs is $L(\theta)=c\cdot\theta^{-20}e^{-700/\theta}$, where $c=\big(\prod x_i^3\big)/6^5$ is a constant (no $\theta$), so drop it:

$$\ell(\theta)=-20\ln\theta-\frac{700}{\theta}\ \Rightarrow\ \ell'(\theta)=-\frac{20}{\theta}+\frac{700}{\theta^2}=0\ \Rightarrow\ \hat\theta=\frac{700}{20}=35.$$

Shortcut. A PDF of the form $x^{\alpha-1}e^{-x/\theta}$ is a gamma. Comparing $x^3 e^{-x/\theta}$ to $(x/\theta)^\alpha e^{-x/\theta}/(x\,\Gamma(\alpha))$ gives $\alpha=4$. With fixed $\alpha$ the fitted mean equals the sample mean:

$$\alpha\hat\theta=\bar x\ \Rightarrow\ 4\hat\theta=\frac{700}{5}=140\ \Rightarrow\ \hat\theta=\frac{140}{4}=35.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\hat\theta=35}$ — and notice the constant $c$ never mattered.
Example 3 Left truncation (deductible): exponential MLE ▾

Losses $6,\ 7,\ 9,\ 10$ are observed; no information is available for losses of 5 or less (left-truncated at $d=5$). Losses are exponential with mean $\theta$. Find $\hat\theta$.

Solution. Each factor is conditioned: $\dfrac{f(x)}{S(5)}$. With $f(x)=\frac1\theta e^{-x/\theta}$ and $S(5)=e^{-5/\theta}$:

$$L(\theta)=\frac{f(6)f(7)f(9)f(10)}{S(5)^4}=\frac{1}{\theta^4}\,e^{-(6+7+9+10)/\theta}\,\big(e^{-5/\theta}\big)^{-4}=\frac{1}{\theta^4}\,e^{-12/\theta}.$$

The four $-5$'s in the exponent cancel $\tfrac{20}{\theta}$ of the numerator's $\tfrac{32}{\theta}$, leaving $\tfrac{12}{\theta}$. Then $\ell(\theta)=-4\ln\theta-\frac{12}{\theta}$, so $\hat\theta=\frac{12}{4}=3$.

Cross-check (shortcut). $\hat\theta=\frac{\sum(x_i-d)}{n}=\frac{(6{-}5)+(7{-}5)+(9{-}5)+(10{-}5)}{4}=\frac{12}{4}=3.$

Answer
$\boxed{\hat\theta=3}$
Example 4 Right censoring + invariance: a VaR from a single-parameter Pareto ▾

Policy limit $=10$. Observed claim payments: $2,\ 3,\ 5,\ 8,\ 10,\ 10$. Claim sizes have $S(x)=x^{-\alpha},\ 1\lt x<\infty$ (single-parameter Pareto, $\theta=1$). The two $10$'s are at the limit (right-censored). Find the MLE of $\mathrm{VaR}_{0.90}(X)$.

Solution. The first four are exact (use $f$); the two $10$'s are right-censored (use $S(10)$). With $f(x)=\alpha x^{-\alpha-1}$:

$$L(\alpha)=f(2)f(3)f(5)f(8)\,S(10)^2=\frac{\alpha^4}{(2\cdot3\cdot5\cdot8)^{\alpha+1}}\cdot 10^{-2\alpha}=\frac{\alpha^4}{240^{\alpha+1}\,100^{\alpha}}.$$ $$\ell(\alpha)=4\ln\alpha-(\alpha+1)\ln 240-\alpha\ln 100\ \Rightarrow\ \ell'(\alpha)=\frac{4}{\alpha}-\ln 240-\ln 100=0.$$ $$\hat\alpha=\frac{4}{\ln 240+\ln 100}=0.3966.$$

By invariance, $\widehat{\mathrm{VaR}}_{0.90}=g(\hat\alpha)$ with $\mathrm{VaR}_{0.90}=\theta(1-0.90)^{-1/\alpha}=0.1^{-1/\alpha}$:

$$\widehat{\mathrm{VaR}}_{0.90}(X)=0.1^{-1/0.3966}=332.24.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\hat\alpha=0.3966,\quad \widehat{\mathrm{VaR}}_{0.90}\approx 332.24}$
Example 5 Mixed policies: the exponential shortcut on two policy types ▾

Policy I: no deductible, limit 100. Policy II: deductible 20, no limit. Losses below the deductible are not recorded. A ground-up exponential is fit by MLE.

PolicyObserved losses
I (limit 100)$50,\ 60,\ 60,\ 70,\ 80,\ 100^+,\ 100^+$
II (deductible 20)$30,\ 50,\ 60,\ 70,\ 90,\ 120$

$^+$ = censored at the limit. Estimate the mean of the ground-up distribution.

Solution. Use $\hat\theta=\dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^{n+c}(x_i-d_i)}{n}$, where $n$ counts only uncensored observations.

  • Policy I ($d=0$): the two $100^+$ are censored. Numerator pieces (all 7): $50+60+60+70+80+100+100=520$. Uncensored count = 5.
  • Policy II ($d=20$): all 6 exact. Numerator pieces: $(30{-}20)+(50{-}20)+(60{-}20)+(70{-}20)+(90{-}20)+(120{-}20)=10+30+40+50+70+100=300$. Uncensored count = 6.
$$\hat\theta=\frac{520+300}{5+6}=\frac{820}{11}=74.55.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\hat\theta=820/11\approx 74.55}$ — i.e. total payment $\div$ number of uncensored observations.
Example 6 Lognormal mean via the normal shortcut + invariance ▾

Claims $25,\ 70,\ 215,\ 535$ are fit by a lognormal $(\mu,\sigma^2)$ via MLE. Find the mean of the fitted distribution.

Solution. $\ln X\sim$ Normal$(\mu,\sigma^2)$, so apply the normal shortcuts to $y_i=\ln x_i$:

$$\hat\mu=\frac{\ln 25+\ln 70+\ln 215+\ln 535}{4}=4.7801,$$ $$\hat\sigma^2=\frac{(\ln 25)^2+(\ln 70)^2+(\ln 215)^2+(\ln 535)^2}{4}-\hat\mu^2=1.3313.$$

By invariance, the fitted mean is $\mathrm E[X]=e^{\mu+\sigma^2/2}$ evaluated at the MLEs:

$$\widehat{\mathrm E}[X]=e^{4.7801+0.5(1.3313)}=231.77.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\widehat{\mathrm E}[X]\approx 231.77}$
Example 7 Grouped discrete data: geometric from accident counts ▾

Annual accidents per car $\sim$ Geometric$(\beta)$. From 20 cars: 9 had 0 accidents, 6 had $[1,2]$, 5 had $[3,4]$. Find the MLE of $\beta$.

Solution. Grouped likelihood uses interval probabilities (sums of pmf for discrete data). With $p_N(k)=\frac{\beta^k}{(1+\beta)^{k+1}}$:

$$L(\beta)=p_N(0)^9\,[p_N(1)+p_N(2)]^6\,[p_N(3)+p_N(4)]^5=\frac{\beta^{21}(1+2\beta)^{11}}{(1+\beta)^{52}}.$$ $$\ell(\beta)=21\ln\beta+11\ln(1+2\beta)-52\ln(1+\beta).$$ $$\ell'(\beta)=\frac{21}{\beta}+\frac{22}{1+2\beta}-\frac{52}{1+\beta}=0\ \Rightarrow\ 40\beta^2-33\beta-21=0.$$

Quadratic formula: $\hat\beta=\dfrac{33\pm\sqrt{33^2+4(40)(21)}}{2(40)}=1.2463$ or $-0.4213$. Since $\beta>0$:

Answer
$\boxed{\hat\beta=1.2463}$
Example 8 Zero-modified binomial: the two-match recipe ▾

From 100 policies, the accident counts are: 45 zeros, 37 ones, 16 twos, 2 threes. Fit a zero-modified binomial with $m=3$ by MLE; find $\mathrm E[(N^M)^2]$.

Match 1 — probability of zero = proportion of zeros. $\ \hat p_0^M=\frac{45}{100}=0.45.$

Match 2 — fitted ZM mean = sample mean. Sample mean $=\frac{0(45)+1(37)+2(16)+3(2)}{100}=0.75.$ With $\mathrm E[N]=mq=3q$ and $p_0=(1-q)^3$:

$$\frac{1-0.45}{1-(1-q)^3}\cdot 3q=0.75\ \Rightarrow\ q^2-3q+0.8=0\ \Rightarrow\ \hat q=0.2958\ (\text{the root in }(0,1)).$$

The ZM second moment scales by the same factor as the mean:

$$\widehat{\mathrm E}[(N^M)^2]=\frac{1-\hat p_0^M}{1-(1-\hat q)^3}\big[3\hat q(1-\hat q)+(3\hat q)^2\big]=1.1938.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\hat q=0.2958,\quad \widehat{\mathrm E}[(N^M)^2]\approx 1.1938}$
Example 9 Building a mixed likelihood (Weibull, multiple policies) ▾

Losses $\sim$ Weibull$(\theta=1,\tau)$. Policy A: no deductible/limit, claims $x_1,x_2$. Policy B: deductible $a$, claim $x_3$. Policy C: deductible $b$, limit $c$, claims $x_4,x_5$, and one at the limit $b+c$. Write $\ell(\tau)$.

Solution. A → plain $f$; B → $f/S(a)$; C → $f/S(b)$ for the two exact, and the limit claim → $S(b+c)/S(b)$. With $\theta=1$, $f(x)=\tau x^{\tau-1}e^{-x^\tau}$ and $S(x)=e^{-x^\tau}$:

$$L(\tau)=\big[f(x_1)f(x_2)\big]\cdot\frac{f(x_3)}{S(a)}\cdot\frac{f(x_4)f(x_5)\,S(b+c)}{S(b)^3}.$$ $$\ell(\tau)=5\ln\tau+(\tau-1)\sum_{i=1}^{5}\ln x_i-\sum_{i=1}^{5}x_i^{\tau}-(b+c)^{\tau}+a^{\tau}+3b^{\tau}.$$
Key idea
The censored/limit claim adds $-(b+c)^\tau$ (a survival factor), while each truncation denominator adds back a positive term: $+a^\tau$ once and $+b^\tau$ three times (three Policy-C claims each divided by $S(b)$).
Example 10 Kaplan–Meier and Greenwood confidence interval ▾

Ten policies are observed for time to first claim. Exact claim times (in months): 2, 3, 3, 5, 8. The remaining 5 policies were censored (no claim) at times 4, 6, 6, 9, 10. No left truncation. Estimate $S(5)$ by Kaplan–Meier and give a 95% linear confidence interval.

Solution. Order the event times and build the risk sets. Total $n = 10$.

$y_j$$s_j$ (events)$r_j$ (risk set)$1 - s_j/r_j$
2110$9/10 = 0.9$
329$7/9 \approx 0.7778$
516$5/6 \approx 0.8333$

Risk sets: at $t=2$, all 10 are at risk. After $t=2$: one event removed, 9 remain. At $t=3$, $r=9$, two events; one censored at 4 leaves. At $t=5$: from 9, subtract 2 events (at 3) and 1 censored (at 4) = 6 at risk. Good.

$$\hat S(5) = 0.9 \times \tfrac{7}{9} \times \tfrac{5}{6} = 0.9 \times 0.77778 \times 0.83333 = 0.5833.$$

Greenwood sum:

$$\sum \frac{s_j}{r_j(r_j - s_j)} = \frac{1}{10\cdot 9} + \frac{2}{9\cdot 7} + \frac{1}{6\cdot 5} = 0.011111 + 0.031746 + 0.033333 = 0.076190.$$ $$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat S(5)] = (0.5833)^2 (0.076190) = 0.34028 \times 0.076190 = 0.025927.$$

Standard error $= \sqrt{0.025927} = 0.16102$.

95% linear CI: $0.5833 \pm 1.96(0.16102) = 0.5833 \pm 0.3156 = (0.2677,\ 0.8989).$

Answer
$\boxed{\hat S(5) = 0.5833,\quad \text{95\% CI } \approx (0.268,\ 0.899)}$
Example 11 Method of moments and percentile matching for a Pareto ▾

Five complete observations: 100, 150, 200, 400, 700.

(a) Fit a Pareto with known $\alpha = 3$ by method of moments (solve for $\theta$).
(b) Estimate the median of an exponential by percentile matching using the same data.

Solution (a).

Sample mean $\bar x = (100+150+200+400+700)/5 = 1550/5 = 310.$

Pareto mean (for $\alpha>1$): $\mathrm{E}[X] = \dfrac{\theta}{\alpha - 1} = \dfrac{\theta}{2}.$

Set equal: $\theta/2 = 310 \Rightarrow \hat\theta = 620.$

Answer (a)
$\boxed{\hat\theta = 620}$

Solution (b).

Smoothed empirical median: $p = 0.5$, $(n+1)p = 6(0.5) = 3.0$, so $j=3$, $h=0$. The smoothed 50th percentile is the 3rd order statistic $= 200$.

For an exponential with mean $\theta$, the median solves $F(\hat\pi_{0.5}) = 0.5$:

$$1 - e^{-200/\theta} = 0.5 \;\Rightarrow\; e^{-200/\theta} = 0.5 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{200}{\theta} = \ln 2 \;\Rightarrow\; \hat\theta = \frac{200}{0.6931} = 288.5.$$
Answer (b)
$\boxed{\hat\theta = 200/\ln 2 \approx 288.5}$
Example 12 Nelson–Aalen with Aalen variance ▾

Using the same data as Example 10 (events at 2, 3, 3, 5; risk sets 10, 9, 6), estimate $H(5)$ and $S(5)$ by Nelson–Aalen, and give the variance of $\hat H(5)$.

Solution.

$$\hat H(5) = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{2}{9} + \frac{1}{6} = 0.10000 + 0.22222 + 0.16667 = 0.48889.$$ $$\hat S(5) = e^{-0.48889} = 0.6134.$$

Aalen variance ($\sum s_j/r_j^2$):

$$\frac{1}{10^2} + \frac{2}{9^2} + \frac{1}{6^2} = 0.01000 + 0.024691 + 0.027778 = 0.062469.$$
Answer
$\boxed{\hat H(5) = 0.4889,\;\; \hat S(5) = 0.6134,\;\; \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat H(5)] = 0.06247}$

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Truncation vs. censoring confusion. A deductible truncates (divide by $S(d)$, drop unobserved small claims); a policy limit censors (replace $f$ with $S(u)$, keep the point). Mixing these up is the #1 error.
  • Dropping the wrong "constants." You may discard factors that don't depend on the parameter, since they vanish on differentiation. But never drop them if the problem asks for the actual log-likelihood value (e.g. a likelihood-ratio test). And remember a "constant" must be multiplicative in $L$ (additive in $\ell$), not just a number.
  • Forgetting to subtract the deductible in the exponential MLE numerator. You sum amounts above the truncation point $(x_i - d)$, not the raw $x_i$. Same mistake: forgetting censored observations contribute $(u-d)$ to the numerator but 0 to the denominator.
  • Counting the wrong denominator in the exponential MLE: it is the number of exact (uncensored) deaths, never the total sample size, when censoring is present.
  • Payments vs. losses. If a table gives claim payments, add back the deductible to recover the loss; a limited payment of $u-d$ means the loss reached the maximum covered loss $u$. Fit the loss distribution, not the payment.
  • MLE of $\sigma^2$ divides by $n$, not $n-1$. The MLE is biased; do not "correct" it unless asked. And you may match the 2nd moment for $\sigma^2$ only when $\mu$ is also MLE-estimated — if $\mu$ is fixed, use $\frac1n\sum(x_i-\mu)^2$ directly.
  • Uniform / shifted distributions: the MLE is at the boundary ($\hat\theta = \max x_i$ for $U(0,\theta)$). Setting a derivative to zero gives no solution. The uniform/inverse-exp/beta shortcuts are complete-data only.
  • Forgetting the invariance property. To estimate a VaR, fitted mean, or any $g(\theta)$, plug in $\hat\theta$: $\widehat{g(\theta)}=g(\hat\theta)$. Don't re-derive a new MLE for the function.
  • Grouped discrete data uses a sum of pmf values over the interval, e.g. $p(1)+p(2)$ — not a single pmf and not a continuous $F(b)-F(a)$.
  • $(a,b,1)$ matching. For zero-truncated, match $\frac{1}{1-p_0}\mathrm E[X]$ to $\bar x$; for zero-modified, first set $\hat p_0^M=n_0/n$, then match the modified mean. Using the plain $(a,b,0)$ mean here is wrong.
  • Risk-set bookkeeping in Kaplan–Meier. Censored observations leave the risk set but do not create a factor. Only event times produce factors $(1 - s_j/r_j)$. With left truncation, subjects enter the risk set late; the risk set is not monotonically decreasing.
  • Greenwood vs. Aalen. Greenwood's denominator is $r_j(r_j - s_j)$ and multiplies by $\hat S(t)^2$; Aalen's is $r_j^2$ with no leading factor. Don't swap them.
  • Percentile matching index. Use $(n+1)p = j + h$ for the smoothed empirical percentile, not $np$. Interpolate between order statistics.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Describe the four-step process of maximum likelihood estimation for complete data.
(1) Build the likelihood $L(\theta)=\prod f(x_i)$ (or $\prod p(x_i)$ for discrete). (2) Take logs: $\ell(\theta)=\ln L(\theta)$. (3) Differentiate: $\ell'(\theta)$. (4) Set $\ell'(\theta)=0$ and solve, checking it's a maximum and in the valid range. For two parameters, set both partial derivatives to zero and solve the system.
Why is it valid to take logs, and why can you drop multiplicative constants before differentiating?
$\ln$ is strictly increasing, so $\ell$ peaks at the same $\hat\theta$ as $L$ — and a sum is far easier to differentiate than a product. Constants that don't depend on $\theta$ are multiplicative in $L$ (additive in $\ell$) and vanish on differentiation, so they don't affect $\hat\theta$. Caveat: keep them if you need the actual value of $\ell$ (e.g. likelihood-ratio test).
Write the likelihood factor for each case: left-truncated at $d$; right-truncated at $u$; left-censored at $d$; right-censored at $u$; grouped on $(a,b]$.
Left-truncated: $f(x)/\Pr(X>d)$. Right-truncated: $f(x)/\Pr(X\lt u)$. Left-censored: $\Pr(X\le d)=F(d)$. Right-censored: $\Pr(X\ge u)=S(u)$. Grouped: $\Pr(a\lt X\le b)=F(b)-F(a)$ (sum of pmf values if discrete).
A loss is recorded as exactly 1000 because the policy has a 1000 limit. Is this observation censored or truncated, and what likelihood factor does it contribute?
Censored (right-censored at $u=1000$). It contributes the survival factor $S(1000)$ to the likelihood.
For an exponential fit with deductible $d$ on every policy, three exact claims (above $d$) and two claims censored at limit $u$, write the MLE numerator and denominator in words.
Numerator = $\sum(\text{exact } x_i - d) + \sum(\text{censored } u - d)$ = total amount exposed above the deductible across all five policies. Denominator = number of exact claims = 3. (Memory hook: total payment $\div$ number of uncensored observations.)
You are given claim payments together with each policy's deductible and limit. What two adjustments translate them into the loss likelihood?
Add the deductible back to each payment to get the loss amount, and add the deductible to the limit to get the maximum covered loss. Then write $f(\text{loss})/S(\text{deductible})$ for exact losses and $S(\text{max covered loss})/S(\text{deductible})$ for losses at the limit.
State the invariance property and use it: if $\hat\alpha=0.4$ for a single-parameter Pareto $(\theta=1)$, what is the MLE of $\mathrm{VaR}_{0.90}$?
$\widehat{g(\theta)}=g(\hat\theta)$ — plug the MLE into the function. $\widehat{\mathrm{VaR}}_{0.90}=0.1^{-1/0.4}=0.1^{-2.5}\approx 316.2.$
A PDF is $f(x)=c\,x^{3}e^{-x/\theta}$. Which distribution is it, and what's the quick MLE of $\theta$ if 5 losses total 700?
$x^{\alpha-1}e^{-x/\theta}$ identifies a gamma with $\alpha=4$. With fixed $\alpha$, fitted mean = sample mean: $4\hat\theta=700/5=140\Rightarrow\hat\theta=35$.
How do you MLE-fit a zero-modified distribution?
Two matches: (1) set the fitted probability of zero equal to the sample proportion of zeros, $\hat p_0^M=n_0/n$; (2) set the fitted modified mean $\frac{1-\hat p_0^M}{1-p_0}\mathrm E[X]$ equal to $\bar x$ and solve for the parent parameter. (Zero-truncated needs only the second match with $p_0$ removed.)
Give the MLE of $\theta$ for a $U(0,\theta)$ sample $\{4, 7, 2, 9, 5\}$, and explain why no derivative is used.
$\hat\theta = \max\{4,7,2,9,5\} = \mathbf{9}$. $L=1/\theta^n$ increases as $\theta$ shrinks, but $\theta$ can't be below the largest observation — so the maximum sits at the boundary $\max x_i$, not at a derivative root.
In Kaplan–Meier, what happens to the survival estimate at a censoring time (no event)?
Nothing — the survival estimate $\hat S$ is unchanged (flat) at a censoring time; only the risk set $r_j$ for later event times is reduced. Factors $(1 - s_j/r_j)$ arise only at event times.
State Greenwood's formula and what it estimates.
$\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\hat S(t)] = [\hat S(t)]^2 \sum_{y_j \le t} \dfrac{s_j}{r_j(r_j - s_j)}$. It estimates the variance of the Kaplan–Meier survival estimator $\hat S(t)$.
