Coverage Modifications

Deductibles, limits, coinsurance, and inflation all reshape one ground-up loss $X$ — and almost every answer is a difference of limited expected values $E[X \wedge u]$. Master one Ultimate Formula and the whole topic collapses into looking up $E[X\wedge\cdot]$ from the exam table.

📖 Loss Models · KPW ⏱️ ~3.5 hrs 🔑 $Y^L$ vs $Y^P$ · LEV · LER · ILF 🔑 franchise · $e(d)$ · max covered loss 🔑 Ultimate Formula · inflation
Learning Objectives

After mastering this topic you should be able to:

  • Define and compute the per-loss random variable $Y^L$ and the per-payment random variable $Y^P$, and explain how they differ.
  • Calculate the effect of a policy limit $u$, an ordinary deductible $d$, a coinsurance factor $\alpha$, and inflation $(1+r)$ on both the severity distribution and aggregate losses.
  • Compute expected cost per loss and expected cost per payment, and the higher moments / variance of each.
  • Compute and interpret the Loss Elimination Ratio (LER) and the Increased Limit Factor (ILF).
  • Work with the limited expected value $E[X \wedge u]$ and use it as the building block for almost every coverage-modification computation, including in discrete / empirical form.
  • Handle a franchise deductible via the mean excess loss $e(d)$, and contrast it with an ordinary deductible.
  • Recognize the other deductible flavours — fixed-percentage, disappearing, per-calendar-year, and elimination periods — and find the effective deductible for each.
  • Apply coinsurance before vs. after the deductible, computing the maximum covered loss $m = \frac{u}{\alpha}+d$.
  • Combine deductible, limit, coinsurance, and inflation in the correct order using the Ultimate Formula to find expected payments, variances, and moments.

Key Concepts

The ground-up loss $X$

Everything starts with the ground-up loss $X$ — the full economic loss to the policyholder before any policy provisions are applied ($X \ge 0$ always; negative losses make no sense). The insurer rarely pays $X$ in full; the policy modifies coverage through deductibles, limits, and coinsurance. Throughout, "payment" means the insurer's payment unless stated otherwise — an actuary always sits on the insurer's side of the table.

Policy limit $u$ — the limited loss variable

The policy limit $u$ is the maximum the insurer will pay for a single loss. The insurer pays whichever is smaller, the loss or the limit:

$$ X \wedge u = \min(X,u) = \begin{cases} X & X < u \\ u & X \ge u \end{cases} $$

$(X\wedge u)$ is the limited loss variable; its mean $E[X\wedge u]$ is the limited expected value (LEV) — the single most reused quantity in this entire topic.

The ground-up loss split: $(X\wedge d) + (X-d)_+ = X$

An ordinary deductible $d$ and a policy limit $d$ partition the loss: the policyholder eats $X\wedge d$, the insurer pays the rest, and they sum to the full loss.

$$ (X \wedge d) + (X-d)_+ = X \;\Longrightarrow\; E[X\wedge d] + E[(X-d)_+] = E[X]. $$
A deductible is a limit, flipped

What the insurer pays under a deductible $d$ is exactly what the policyholder pays under a limit $d$, and vice versa. That symmetry is why $E[(X-d)_+] = E[X]-E[X\wedge d]$ — and why nearly every answer reduces to looking up LEVs from the exam table.

Ordinary deductible $d$

With an ordinary deductible $d$ (the default — if a problem just says "deductible," it means this), the insurer pays nothing on the first $d$ and the excess above $d$:

  • If $X \le d$: payment is $0$.
  • If $X > d$: payment is $X - d$.

There are two natural ways to record payments, and keeping them straight is the single most important idea in this topic.

Per-loss vs. per-payment: the central distinction

  • Per-loss variable $Y^L$: defined for every loss event, including those that produce a payment of $0$. So $Y^L = 0$ whenever $X \le d$. This is a mixed random variable (a point mass at 0 plus a continuous piece).
$$ Y^L = (X-d)_+ = \begin{cases} 0 & X \le d \\ X - d & X > d \end{cases} $$
  • Per-payment variable $Y^P$: defined only when a payment actually occurs, i.e. conditional on $X > d$. It is $Y^L$ conditioned on $Y^L > 0$:
$$ Y^P = X - d \mid X > d $$
Why insurers care about "per payment"

Losses below the deductible are usually never reported — the policyholder gets nothing, so why bother filing? The insurer therefore lacks data on small losses and is often forced to work with the payment-per-payment view. Note too: $Y^L$ is general notation for "payment per loss" and is not always $(X-d)_+$ — with a limit, $Y^L = X\wedge u$; with both, $Y^L = (X\wedge m)-(X\wedge d)$. And with no deductible, $Y^P = Y^L$ (every loss is paid).

Intuition — and the link between the two

$Y^L$ answers "averaged over all losses, how much do we pay?" $Y^P$ answers "given that we wrote a check, how big was it?" Because $Y^P$ excludes the zero payments, its mean is always at least as large as the mean of $Y^L$. The link is the survival probability $S_X(d) = P(X > d)$:

$$ E[Y^P] = \frac{E[Y^L]}{S_X(d)} \ge E[Y^L]. $$

This is just conditional expectation: $E[Y^L] = E[Y^P]\cdot P(\text{payment}) + 0 \cdot P(\text{no payment})$. The relationship holds for every moment: $E[(Y^P)^k] = E[(Y^L)^k]/S_X(d)$ — divide by $S_X(d) \le 1$ to get the larger per-payment value.

Expected cost per loss vs. expected cost per payment

  • Expected cost per loss $= E[Y^L]$ — the right quantity for aggregate / pure premium calculations, because you can multiply it by the expected number of losses.
  • Expected cost per payment $= E[Y^P]$ — the right quantity when you only observe (or only care about) the claims that generate a payment, e.g. when the frequency is the number of payments.

The excess loss variable and the mean excess loss $e(d)$

The conditional variable $(X-d \mid X>d)$ has its own name — the excess loss variable (contrast with the limited loss variable $X\wedge d$). Its mean is the mean excess loss (a.k.a. mean residual life) function:

$$ e(d) = E[X-d \mid X>d] = \frac{E[(X-d)_+]}{S_X(d)} = E[Y^P_{\text{ordinary}}]. $$

So $e(d)$ is just the expected cost per payment under an ordinary deductible. Several distributions have beautiful closed-form excess-loss behaviour, which lets you skip the integral entirely:

Limited expected value $E[X \wedge u]$ — your master tool

The mean of the limited loss variable is

$$ E[X \wedge u] = \int_0^u x\,f_X(x)\,dx + u\,S_X(u) = \int_0^u S_X(x)\,dx \quad\text{(for } X \ge 0\text{)}. $$

Almost every coverage-modification expectation can be written as a difference of LEVs. The key identity:

The identity that powers the whole topic
$$ E[(X-d)_+] = E[X] - E[X \wedge d]. $$

So the expected cost per loss with deductible $d$ equals $E[X] - E[X\wedge d]$. With a policy limit too (see below), everything becomes $E[X\wedge \cdot] - E[X\wedge \cdot]$. This first-moment shortcut is recommended because the exam table gives $E[X]$ and $E[X\wedge u]$ for almost every distribution.

The shortcut fails for higher moments

$E[(X-d)_+]=E[X]-E[X\wedge d]$ is a first-moment-only trick. For the second moment, $E[(X-d)_+^2] \ne E[X^2]-E[(X\wedge d)^2]$ — the algebra leaves a cross term. When you need a variance, go back to first principles $E[(X-d)_+^k]=\int_d^\infty (x-d)^k f(x)\,dx = \int_d^\infty k(x-d)^{k-1}S(x)\,dx$.

Increased Limit Factor (ILF)

The increased limit factor measures how much more the insurer expects to pay if the policy limit is raised from $b$ to $u$. An ILF of $1.10$ means "expect to pay 10% more."

ILF$$ \mathrm{ILF} = \frac{E[X\wedge u]}{E[X\wedge b]}, \qquad b = \text{original limit},\ u = \text{increased limit}. $$

Policy limit vs. maximum covered loss $m$

A common point of confusion. Let $m$ be the maximum covered loss — the smallest ground-up loss at which the insurer hits the policy limit (above $m$, extra loss buys no extra payment). With deductible $d$, policy limit $u$, and coinsurance $\alpha$:

Maximum covered loss$$ m = \frac{u}{\alpha} + d. $$
  • Limit only: $m = u$.
  • Limit + deductible (no coinsurance): $m = u + d$.
  • Limit + deductible + coinsurance: $m = \dfrac{u}{\alpha} + d$.

In the formulas, the upper LEV is taken at the maximum covered loss $m$, not the policy limit. Many students mis-plug here.

Coinsurance $\alpha$ — and "before vs. after" the deductible

A coinsurance factor $\alpha$ ($0 < \alpha \le 1$) is the proportion of the (modified) loss the insurer pays; the policyholder pays $(1-\alpha)X$. It multiplies the whole payment. With only coinsurance, $E[\alpha X] = \alpha E[X]$.

When coinsurance accompanies a deductible, there are two conventions:

  • After the deductible (default): the policyholder pays the first $d$, then shares the excess. $Y^L = \alpha(X-d)_+$ and $E[Y^L]=\alpha\big(E[X]-E[X\wedge d]\big)$.
  • Before the deductible (rare, must be stated): only the coinsured portion $\alpha X$ counts toward the deductible, so the deductible is met when $\alpha X = d$, i.e. at the loss $d^\ast = d/\alpha$. Just replace $d$ by $d^\ast = d/\alpha$ and solve exactly as the "after" case.
Coinsurance before deductible$$ d^\ast = \frac{d}{\alpha}, \qquad E[Y^L] = \alpha\big(E[X] - E[X\wedge d^\ast]\big). $$

Inflation $(1+r)$

Inflation by rate $r$ scales the ground-up loss before any provisions: losses become $(1+r)X$. Because deductibles and limits are fixed dollar amounts, inflation does not scale them — it effectively makes the (fixed) deductible and limit "smaller" relative to losses. The standard identity:

$$ E\big[(1+r)X \wedge u\big] = (1+r)\,E\!\left[X \wedge \frac{u}{1+r}\right]. $$

The threshold gets divided by $(1+r)$ inside the LEV; the factor $(1+r)$ comes outside. Equivalently, if $X$ belongs to a scale family (e.g. Pareto, exponential, single-parameter Pareto), inflate by scaling the scale parameter: $\theta^\ast=(1+r)\theta$.

Loss Elimination Ratio (LER)

The LER measures the fraction of expected ground-up loss that is eliminated (no longer paid by the insurer) by introducing a deductible $d$:

$$ \mathrm{LER}(d) = \frac{E[X \wedge d]}{E[X]} = \frac{E[X] - E[(X-d)_+]}{E[X]}. $$

It ranges from $0$ (no deductible) to $1$ (deductible so large nothing is ever paid). A generalized LER handles any modification (limit, coinsurance, franchise): if $E[Y]$ is the expected insurer payment under the modification, then

Generalized LER$$ \mathrm{LER} = \frac{E[X] - E[Y]}{E[X]}. $$

Franchise deductible

A franchise deductible $d$ (a "cliff disappearing deductible") pays nothing if $X \le d$, but pays the full loss $X$ (not $X-d$) once $X > d$:

$$ Y^L_{\text{franchise}} = \begin{cases} 0 & X \le d \\ X & X > d \end{cases} $$

Compared to the ordinary deductible, the franchise pays an extra $d$ on every payment. Hence (per payment, this is just $e(d)+d$):

$$ E[Y^L_{\text{fr}}] = E[(X-d)_+] + d\,S_X(d), \qquad E[Y^P_{\text{fr}}] = \frac{E[(X-d)_+]}{S_X(d)} + d = e(d)+d. $$

Other deductible flavours (qualitative — know the effective deductible)

The exam math centres on ordinary and franchise deductibles, but the syllabus also defines several others. For each, the trick is to find the effective deductible $D$, then the insurer pays $X - D$.

  • Fixed-dollar (ordinary): $D=d$ regardless of loss size; pay $\max(0,X-d)$.
  • Fixed-percentage ($100\delta\%$ of loss, floor $d$): effective deductible $D=\max(d,\delta X)$. Payment $=0$ for $X\le d$; $X-d$ for $d\lt X\le d/\delta$; $(1-\delta)X$ for $X>d/\delta$.
  • Disappearing (shrinks linearly from $d$ to $0$ as loss runs from $a$ to $b$, with $d\le a\lt b$): $D = d\cdot\frac{b-X}{b-a}$ on $a\lt X\le b$; $D=d$ below $a$; $D=0$ above $b$. Solve for $D$ by linear interpolation.
  • Fixed-dollar per calendar year (health): a per-person amount and a per-family amount; coverage starts once either is met.
  • Elimination period (disability): no benefit during a waiting period; if the disability lasts past the retroactive qualification period the benefit is paid back to day one — if that equals the elimination period, the policy effectively has a franchise deductible.
Why deductibles exist (a favourite qualitative MCQ)

Deductibles eliminate small-claim handling expense, lower average large-claim payments (cheaper premiums), and make policyholders more careful (less moral hazard); higher deductibles let buyers trade coverage for lower premium. Downsides: policyholders dislike unpaid claims, marketing gets complicated, and insureds may inflate claims to recover the deductible.

Formulas

Symbols used throughout
  • $X$ = ground-up loss ($X \ge 0$); $f_X, F_X, S_X = 1-F_X$ are its pdf, cdf, survival function.
  • $d$ = ordinary deductible; $u$ = policy limit; $m$ = maximum covered loss; $\alpha$ = coinsurance; $r$ = inflation rate.
  • $Y^L$ = per-loss payment; $Y^P$ = per-payment payment; $e(d)$ = mean excess loss.
  • $X \wedge u = \min(X,u)$; $(X-d)_+ = \max(X-d,0)$.
  • Set $d=0$ (no deductible), $u=\infty$ (no limit), $\alpha=1$ (no coinsurance), $r=0$ (no inflation). Note $E[X\wedge\infty]=E[X]$ and $E[X\wedge 0]=0$.

Limited expected value (and discrete form)

$$ E[X \wedge u] = \int_0^u x\,f_X(x)\,dx + u\,S_X(u) = \int_0^u S_X(x)\,dx. $$ $$ E[(X \wedge u)^k] = \int_0^u x^k f_X(x)\,dx + u^k S_X(u) = \int_0^u k\,x^{k-1}S_X(x)\,dx. $$
Coach's Remark — discrete / empirical losses

If $X$ is discrete, swap integrals for sums: $E[(X\wedge u)^k]=\sum_{x\le u} x^k p(x) + u^k S(u)$. For an empirical sample of $n$ losses, $E[X\wedge u]=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i\wedge u)$ — just cap every observation at $u$ and average.

Deductible only (ordinary)

$$ \boxed{E[Y^L] = E[(X-d)_+] = E[X] - E[X\wedge d]} $$ $$ \boxed{E[Y^P] = e(d) = \frac{E[X]-E[X\wedge d]}{S_X(d)}} $$

Deductible + limit (no coinsurance, no inflation)

Payment per loss $= (X\wedge m) - (X \wedge d)$ with $m = u+d$. Therefore:

$$ E[Y^L] = E[X\wedge m] - E[X\wedge d], \qquad E[Y^P] = \frac{E[X\wedge m] - E[X\wedge d]}{S_X(d)}. $$

The Ultimate Formula — deductible, limit, coinsurance, inflation

Apply provisions in this order: (1) inflate the loss, (2) deductible, (3) limit/censor at $m$, (4) coinsurance. The expected cost per loss is

Ultimate Formula (per loss) $$ \boxed{\,E[Y^L] = \alpha(1+r)\left[\,E\!\left[X \wedge \tfrac{m}{1+r}\right] - E\!\left[X \wedge \tfrac{d}{1+r}\right]\right]\,}, \qquad m = \frac{u}{\alpha}+d. $$

and the expected cost per payment divides by the probability that a payment occurs, $S_X\!\left(\tfrac{d}{1+r}\right)$:

Ultimate Formula (per payment) $$ \boxed{\,E[Y^P] = \frac{\alpha(1+r)\left[\,E\!\left[X \wedge \tfrac{m}{1+r}\right] - E\!\left[X \wedge \tfrac{d}{1+r}\right]\right]}{S_X\!\left(\tfrac{d}{1+r}\right)}\,} $$

Here $d/(1+r)$ and $m/(1+r)$ are the deductible and maximum covered loss expressed in pre-inflation loss units.

Two exceptions to the Ultimate Formula
  • Franchise deductible: the maximum covered loss drops to $m=u/\alpha$ (not $+d$, since the full loss is paid), and you must add back $\alpha\cdot d\cdot S_X\!\big(\tfrac{d}{1+r}\big)$.
  • Coinsurance before the deductible: replace $d$ with $d/\alpha$ everywhere.

Second moment and variance (per loss, with $d, u, \alpha, r$)

Let $\tilde d = d/(1+r)$, $\tilde m = m/(1+r)$. Then

$$ E[(Y^L)^2] = \alpha^2 (1+r)^2 \Big( E[(X\wedge \tilde m)^2] - E[(X\wedge \tilde d)^2] - 2\tilde d\big(E[X\wedge \tilde m]-E[X\wedge \tilde d]\big)\Big). $$ $$ \mathrm{Var}(Y^L) = E[(Y^L)^2] - \big(E[Y^L]\big)^2. $$

For the per-payment moments, divide each per-loss moment by $S_X(\tilde d)$ before taking the variance:

$$ E[(Y^P)^k] = \frac{E[(Y^L)^k]}{S_X(\tilde d)}, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y^P) = E[(Y^P)^2] - \big(E[Y^P]\big)^2. $$

LER, ILF, and franchise

$$ \mathrm{LER}(d) = \frac{E[X\wedge d]}{E[X]}, \qquad \mathrm{ILF} = \frac{E[X\wedge u]}{E[X\wedge b]}. $$ $$ E[Y^L_{\text{fr}}] = E[(X-d)_+] + d\,S_X(d), \qquad E[Y^P_{\text{fr}}] = e(d) + d. $$

Worked Examples

Example 1 Per-loss vs. per-payment with an exponential severity ▾

Losses $X$ are exponential with mean $\theta = 1000$, so $S_X(x)=e^{-x/1000}$. A policy has an ordinary deductible $d = 500$. Find (a) the expected cost per loss, (b) the expected cost per payment.

Step 1 — LEV of an exponential. For $X\sim$ Exponential($\theta$),

$$ E[X\wedge d] = \theta\left(1 - e^{-d/\theta}\right). $$

With $\theta=1000,\ d=500$: $E[X\wedge 500] = 1000(1-e^{-0.5}) = 1000(1-0.606531) = 393.469.$

Step 2 — Expected cost per loss.

$$ E[Y^L] = E[X]-E[X\wedge d] = 1000 - 393.469 = 606.531. $$

(Sanity check: for an exponential, the memoryless property gives $E[(X-d)_+] = \theta e^{-d/\theta} = 1000 e^{-0.5} = 606.531.$ ✓)

Step 3 — Expected cost per payment. Divide by $S_X(500)=e^{-0.5}=0.606531$:

$$ E[Y^P] = e(500) = \frac{606.531}{0.606531} = 1000. $$

(Memoryless property: the conditional excess of an exponential is again Exponential($\theta$), mean $1000$. ✓)

Answer
$E[Y^L] = \mathbf{606.53}$, $E[Y^P] = \mathbf{1000}$.
Example 2 Policy limit and the Increased Limit Factor (Pareto) ▾

Medical claim sizes follow Pareto$(\alpha=5,\ \theta=1000)$. The exam-table LEV is $E[X\wedge u]=\frac{\theta}{\alpha-1}\left[1-\left(\frac{\theta}{u+\theta}\right)^{\alpha-1}\right]$.
(a) Find the expected payment under a policy limit of $500$. (b) The insurer considers raising the limit from $500$ to $800$; find the increased limit factor.

Step 1 — (a) LEV at 500.

$$ E[X\wedge 500] = \frac{1000}{4}\left[1-\left(\frac{1000}{1500}\right)^{4}\right] = 250\big(1-0.197531\big) = 200.62. $$

Step 2 — LEV at 800.

$$ E[X\wedge 800] = 250\left[1-\left(\frac{1000}{1800}\right)^{4}\right] = 250\big(1-0.095260\big) = 226.19. $$

Step 3 — (b) ILF.

$$ \mathrm{ILF} = \frac{E[X\wedge 800]}{E[X\wedge 500]} = \frac{226.19}{200.62} = 1.1274. $$
Answer
(a) $E[X\wedge 500] = \mathbf{200.62}$. (b) $\mathrm{ILF}=\mathbf{1.1274}$ — the insurer expects to pay about 12.74% more per claim.
Example 3 Deductible + limit + coinsurance, exponential severity ▾

Losses are exponential with mean $1000$. Each claim has an ordinary deductible of $200$; the policy reimburses $80\%$ of the excess; and the payment is capped at $2000$. Find the expected payment per loss. (No inflation.)

Step 1 — Maximum covered loss. With $u=2000,\ d=200,\ \alpha=0.8$,

$$ m = \frac{u}{\alpha}+d = \frac{2000}{0.8}+200 = 2700. $$

Step 2 — LEVs ($E[X\wedge u]=\theta(1-e^{-u/\theta})$):

$$ E[X\wedge 2700] = 1000(1-e^{-2.7}) = 932.79, \qquad E[X\wedge 200] = 1000(1-e^{-0.2}) = 181.27. $$

Step 3 — Ultimate Formula (no inflation).

$$ E[Y^L] = \alpha\big(E[X\wedge m]-E[X\wedge d]\big) = 0.8(932.79 - 181.27) = 601.22. $$
Answer
Expected payment per loss $= \mathbf{601.22}$.
Example 4 Full combination: deductible, limit, coinsurance, inflation (uniform) ▾

Ground-up losses $X$ are uniform on $[0, 10000]$. Next year a policy has deductible $d = 1000$, maximum covered loss $m = 8000$, coinsurance $\alpha = 0.80$ (so policy limit $u=\alpha(m-d)=5600$), and losses inflate by $r = 0.05$. Find the expected cost per loss.

Step 1 — Pre-inflation thresholds.

$$ \tilde d = \frac{1000}{1.05} = 952.381, \qquad \tilde m = \frac{8000}{1.05} = 7619.048. $$

Step 2 — LEV of a uniform $(0,b)$. For $X\sim U(0,b)$ with $b=10000$,

$$ E[X\wedge t] = t - \frac{t^2}{2b}, \quad 0\le t \le b. $$ $$ E[X\wedge \tilde m] = 7619.048 - \frac{7619.048^2}{20000} = 7619.048 - 2902.494 = 4716.554. $$ $$ E[X\wedge \tilde d] = 952.381 - \frac{952.381^2}{20000} = 952.381 - 45.351 = 907.030. $$

Step 3 — Ultimate Formula.

$$ E[Y^L] = \alpha(1+r)\big(E[X\wedge\tilde m] - E[X\wedge \tilde d]\big) = 0.80(1.05)(4716.554 - 907.030) = 0.84 \times 3809.524 = 3200.00. $$
Answer
Expected cost per loss $= \mathbf{3200.00}$.
Example 5 Loss Elimination Ratio and a franchise deductible comparison (Pareto) ▾

Losses $X$ follow a Pareto distribution with $\alpha = 3$, $\theta = 2000$ (so $S_X(x) = \left(\frac{2000}{x+2000}\right)^3$). A deductible $d = 1000$ is introduced.
(a) Find the LER. (b) Find the expected cost per payment under an ordinary deductible. (c) Find the expected cost per payment under a franchise deductible.

Step 1 — Pareto moments. Mean: $E[X] = \dfrac{\theta}{\alpha-1} = \dfrac{2000}{2} = 1000.$ Limited expected value:

$$ E[X\wedge d] = \frac{\theta}{\alpha-1}\left[1 - \left(\frac{\theta}{d+\theta}\right)^{\alpha-1}\right]. $$ $$ \frac{\theta}{d+\theta} = \frac{2000}{3000} = 0.66667, \quad (0.66667)^{2} = 0.44444, \quad E[X\wedge 1000] = 1000(1 - 0.44444) = 555.556. $$

Step 2 — (a) LER. $\mathrm{LER}(1000) = \dfrac{555.556}{1000} = \mathbf{0.5556}$ (about 55.6% of expected loss eliminated).

Step 3 — (b) Ordinary, per payment. $E[(X-d)_+] = 1000 - 555.556 = 444.444$; $S_X(1000) = (0.66667)^3 = 0.296296$. Or use the excess-loss shortcut $e(d)=\frac{\theta+d}{\alpha-1}=\frac{3000}{2}=1500$:

$$ E[Y^P_{\text{ord}}] = e(1000) = \frac{444.444}{0.296296} = 1500.00. $$

Step 4 — (c) Franchise, per payment. Franchise adds $d=1000$:

$$ E[Y^P_{\text{fr}}] = e(d) + d = 1500.00 + 1000 = 2500.00. $$
Answers
(a) $\mathbf{0.5556}$ · (b) ordinary $\mathbf{1500.00}$ · (c) franchise $\mathbf{2500.00}$.
Example 6 Variance of $Y^P$ from first principles (uniform, deductible + limit) ▾

Losses have survival function $S(x) = 1-\frac{x}{200}$ on $0\le x\le 200$ (so $X\sim U(0,200)$). A policy has deductible $20$ and maximum covered loss $100$ (policy limit $u=m-d=80$). Find $\mathrm{Var}[Y^P]$.

Step 1 — payment-per-loss variable.

$$ Y^L = \begin{cases} 0 & X\le 20 \\ X-20 & 20\lt X<100 \\ 80 & X\ge 100 \end{cases} $$

Step 2 — first two moments of $Y^L$ (first principles, $f(x)=1/200$, $S(100)=0.5$):

$$ E[Y^L] = \int_{20}^{100}(x-20)\tfrac{1}{200}\,dx + 80\,S(100) = \frac{(x-20)^2}{400}\Big|_{20}^{100} + 80(0.5) = 16 + 40 = 56. $$ $$ E[(Y^L)^2] = \int_{20}^{100}(x-20)^2\tfrac{1}{200}\,dx + 80^2 S(100) = \frac{(x-20)^3}{600}\Big|_{20}^{100} + 6400(0.5) = 853.333 + 3200 = 4053.333. $$

Step 3 — convert to per payment using $S(d)=S(20)=1-\frac{20}{200}=0.9$:

$$ E[Y^P] = \frac{56}{0.9} = 62.2222, \qquad E[(Y^P)^2] = \frac{4053.333}{0.9} = 4503.7037. $$

Step 4 — variance.

$$ \mathrm{Var}[Y^P] = 4503.7037 - (62.2222)^2 = 632.10. $$
Answer
$\mathrm{Var}[Y^P] = \mathbf{632.10}$.
Example 7 A bonus question: define the payment variable, then it's just LEVs ▾

An agent earns a bonus equal to $15\%$ of the amount by which the year's total incurred losses fall below $500$; nothing if losses reach $500$. Total losses $X\sim$ Pareto$(\alpha=3,\ \theta=400)$. Find the expected bonus.

Step 1 — define the bonus variable. This is a deductible "from above" — a $(d-X)_+$ structure:

$$ B = 0.15(500-X)_+ = \begin{cases} 0.15(500-X) & X<500 \\ 0 & X\ge 500 \end{cases}. $$

Step 2 — rewrite via LEV. Since $(500-X)_+ = 500 - (X\wedge 500)$,

$$ E[B] = 0.15\big(500 - E[X\wedge 500]\big). $$

Step 3 — Pareto LEV.

$$ E[X\wedge 500] = \frac{400}{3-1}\left[1-\left(\frac{400}{500+400}\right)^{2}\right] = 200\big(1-0.197531\big) = 160.49. $$ $$ E[B] = 0.15(500 - 160.49) = 50.93. $$
Answer
Expected bonus $= \mathbf{50.93}$. (Any "bonus" payoff reduces to defining the variable, then computing $E[X\wedge\cdot]$.)
Example 8 Disappearing deductible — find the effective deductible ▾

A policy has a linearly disappearing deductible. Observed claims give: $200\to$ pays $0$; $750\to 600$; $900\to 800$; $1300\to 1300$. Find the payment for a claim of $1000$.

Step 1 — back out effective deductibles ($D = X - \text{payment}$): $750\to D=150$; $900\to D=100$; $1300\to D=0$.

Step 2 — find the upper bound $b$ where $D$ hits $0$, using the slope between the two interior points:

$$ \frac{900-750}{100-150} = -3 = \frac{b-900}{0-100} \;\Rightarrow\; b - 900 = 300 \;\Rightarrow\; b = 1200. $$

Since $1000 < 1200$, the claim is still in the disappearing range.

Step 3 — interpolate $D$ at $X=1000$.

$$ \frac{D-100}{1000-900} = -3 \;\Rightarrow\; D - 100 = -33.333 \;\Rightarrow\; D = 66.667. $$

Step 4 — payment. $Y = 1000 - 66.667 = 933.33$.

Answer
Payment $= \mathbf{933.33}$.
Example 9 Replace a policy limit with an equivalent coinsurance $c$ (Gamma LEV via the Poisson shortcut) ▾

Losses $X\sim$ Gamma$(\alpha=2,\ \theta=100)$. The current policy has a policy limit of $250$. The insurer wants to drop the limit and instead pay a fixed proportion $c$ of every loss (coinsurance, no limit), keeping the expected cost unchanged. Find $c$.

Step 1 — equate the two expected costs. Limit-only is $E[X\wedge 250]$; coinsurance-only is $c\,E[X]$. Setting them equal,

$$ c\,E[X] = E[X\wedge 250] \;\Longrightarrow\; c = \frac{E[X\wedge 250]}{E[X]}. $$

Step 2 — the easy piece. For Gamma$(\alpha,\theta)$, $E[X]=\alpha\theta = 2(100) = 200.$

Step 3 — the Gamma LEV. The exam-table formula (with $k=1$) is

$$ E[X\wedge u] = \alpha\theta\,\Gamma(\alpha+1;\,u/\theta) + u\big[1-\Gamma(\alpha;\,u/\theta)\big]. $$ $$ E[X\wedge 250] = 2(100)\,\Gamma(3;\,2.5) + 250\big[1-\Gamma(2;\,2.5)\big], \qquad \frac{u}{\theta}=\frac{250}{100}=2.5. $$

Step 4 — evaluate the incomplete gammas with the Poisson shortcut. For integer first argument $k$, $\Gamma(k;\lambda) = 1 - \Pr(N\lt k)$ where $N\sim$ Poisson$(\lambda)$. Here $\lambda = 2.5$:

$$ \Gamma(3;2.5) = 1-\Pr(N\lt 3) = 1-e^{-2.5}\!\left(1+2.5+\tfrac{2.5^2}{2}\right) = 1-e^{-2.5}(6.625) = 0.4562. $$ $$ \Gamma(2;2.5) = 1-\Pr(N\lt 2) = 1-e^{-2.5}\!\left(1+2.5\right) = 1-e^{-2.5}(3.5) = 0.7127. $$

Step 5 — assemble the LEV and solve.

$$ E[X\wedge 250] = 200(0.4562) + 250(1-0.7127) = 91.24 + 71.82 \approx 163. $$ $$ c = \frac{163}{200} \approx 0.815. $$
Answer
$c \approx \mathbf{81.5\%}$. A coinsurance of about $0.815$ on every loss costs the insurer the same as the original $250$ limit.
Don't memorize, recognize

The Poisson shortcut $\Gamma(k;\lambda)=1-\Pr(N\lt k)$ only works when the first argument is a positive integer — which it is whenever $\alpha$ (or $\alpha+1$) is a whole number, the usual exam case. Otherwise integrate the Gamma pdf by parts. And note $\Gamma(\alpha;u/\theta)$ here is the cdf-style incomplete gamma the exam table uses, so $1-\Gamma(\alpha;u/\theta)$ is the survival weight on the limit $u$.

Example 10 Equate two options under inflation — deflate the argument before the table lookup ▾

This year's losses follow a distribution tabulated only at the points below. A policy has an ordinary deductible of $600$. Losses next year are $20\%$ higher ($r=0.2$). The insurer will add either a policy limit of $3{,}000$ or a coinsurance $\alpha$. Find the $\alpha$ that makes the two options cost the same expected payment per loss next year.

$x$$F(x)$$E[X\wedge x]$
$500$$0.44$$375$
$600$$0.49$$429$
$1{,}800$$0.79$$818$
$2{,}415$$0.85$$925$
$3{,}000$$0.89$$1{,}000$
$4{,}350$$0.93$$1{,}115$
$\infty$$1.00$$1{,}500$

Step 1 — Ultimate Formula with inflation. $E[Y^L]=\alpha(1+r)\big(E[X\wedge \tfrac{m}{1+r}]-E[X\wedge \tfrac{d}{1+r}]\big)$. The table is in this year's units, so we must deflate every argument by $1.2$ before looking it up.

Step 2 — Option A (policy limit $3{,}000$). Here $u=3000,\ d=600,\ \alpha=1$, so $m=u+d=3{,}600$. Deflate:

$$ \frac{m}{1.2}=\frac{3600}{1.2}=3000, \qquad \frac{d}{1.2}=\frac{600}{1.2}=500. $$

Now the arguments land exactly on table rows — that is the point of deflating first:

$$ E[Y^L_A] = 1.2\big(E[X\wedge 3000]-E[X\wedge 500]\big) = 1.2(1000-375) = 1.2(625) = 750. $$

Step 3 — Option B (coinsurance $\alpha$, no limit). Here $u=\infty$ so $m=\infty$; $d=600,\ \alpha=\alpha$. Deflate: $\tfrac{m}{1.2}=\infty$ (use $E[X\wedge\infty]=E[X]=1500$) and $\tfrac{d}{1.2}=500$:

$$ E[Y^L_B] = \alpha(1.2)\big(E[X]-E[X\wedge 500]\big) = \alpha(1.2)(1500-375) = 1{,}350\,\alpha. $$

Step 4 — equate and solve.

$$ 750 = 1{,}350\,\alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; \alpha = \frac{750}{1350} = 0.5556. $$
Answer
$\alpha \approx \mathbf{0.5556}$.
Deflate the argument, never the table value

The exam's whole trap here is that $E[X\wedge 3600/1.2]=E[X\wedge 3000]$ — you divide the threshold by $(1+r)$ before the lookup, and the lone $(1+r)$ stays outside. If you had blindly read $E[X\wedge 3600]$ (not even a table row) or scaled a table value by $1.2$ inside the bracket, you'd be wrong. Deflate first, look up second.

Example 11 LEV by integrating $S(x)$ — a non-table mixed-exponential severity ▾

Auto claim amounts follow a distribution that is not on the exam table, with CDF

$$ F(x) = 1 - 0.6\,e^{-0.01x} - 0.4\,e^{-0.002x}, \qquad x \ge 0. $$

(a two-point mixture of exponentials with means $100$ and $500$). The policy has a limit of $200$. Find the expected payment per claim, $E[X\wedge 200]$.

Step 1 — write the survival function. Since $S=1-F$,

$$ S(x) = 0.6\,e^{-0.01x} + 0.4\,e^{-0.002x}. $$

Step 2 — use the survival-function LEV (cleaner than $\int x f\,dx + uS(u)$ because there's no table entry to borrow):

$$ E[X\wedge 200] = \int_0^{200} S(x)\,dx = \int_0^{200}\big(0.6\,e^{-0.01x} + 0.4\,e^{-0.002x}\big)\,dx. $$

Step 3 — integrate term by term ($\int_0^u e^{-cx}\,dx = \tfrac{1}{c}(1-e^{-cu})$, or carry the antiderivative $-\tfrac{1}{c}e^{-cx}$):

$$ \int_0^{200} S(x)\,dx = \left[-\frac{0.6}{0.01}e^{-0.01x} - \frac{0.4}{0.002}e^{-0.002x}\right]_0^{200} = \Big[-60\,e^{-0.01x} - 200\,e^{-0.002x}\Big]_0^{200}. $$

Step 4 — evaluate at the endpoints ($e^{-2}=0.135335,\ e^{-0.4}=0.670320$):

$$ = \big(-60\,e^{-2} - 200\,e^{-0.4}\big) - \big(-60 - 200\big) = (-8.120 - 134.064) + 260 = 117.82. $$
Answer
$E[X\wedge 200] = \mathbf{117.82}$.
When to reach for $\int_0^u S(x)\,dx$

Whenever the distribution is off-table (a mixture, a spliced model, a bare survival function), the survival-function form turns the LEV into one easy integral — no need to find $f(x)$, no separate $u\,S(u)$ tail term to add. For sums/mixtures of exponentials it is almost always the fastest route.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  1. Confusing maximum covered loss $m$ with the policy limit. The upper LEV is taken at $m = \frac{u}{\alpha}+d$, not at $u$. If given the policy limit, build $m$ first ($m=u+d$ when $\alpha=1$; $m=u$ when there's no deductible). "Many students make this mistake" — don't be one.
  2. Dividing by the wrong survival probability. $E[Y^P] = E[Y^L]/S_X(d)$. With inflation the divisor is $S_X\big(\tfrac{d}{1+r}\big)$ — evaluated at the deflated deductible. Also remember $E[Y^P]=\int_d^\infty (x-d)\frac{f(x)}{S(d)}dx$ uses the conditional pdf; $\int_d^\infty(x-d)f(x)\,dx$ is $E[Y^L]$, not $E[Y^P]$.
  3. Applying provisions in the wrong order. Inflate, then deductible, then limit, then coinsurance. $\alpha$ and $(1+r)$ multiply outside the bracketed LEV difference; $d$ and $m$ get divided by $(1+r)$ inside.
  4. Scaling the deductible/limit by inflation. Deductibles and limits are fixed contract dollars — only $X$ inflates. The $(1+r)$ moves outward and the thresholds get divided.
  5. Using $E[Y^P]$ where $E[Y^L]$ belongs in aggregate calculations. Aggregate loss = (frequency of losses) × severity uses $E[Y^L]$. Use $E[Y^P]$ only when frequency counts payments.
  6. First-moment shortcut on higher moments. $E[(X-d)_+^2] \ne E[X^2]-E[(X\wedge d)^2]$ (a cross term survives). For variances use first principles or the $-2\tilde d(\cdots)$ cross term in $E[(Y^L)^2]$.
  7. Variance shortcuts that don't exist. $\mathrm{Var}(Y^P) \ne \mathrm{Var}(Y^L)/S_X(d)$. Compute $E[(Y^P)^2] = E[(Y^L)^2]/S_X(d)$ first, then subtract $(E[Y^P])^2$. Coinsurance contributes $\alpha^2$ to second moments, not $\alpha$.
  8. Franchise deductible: forgetting the extra $d$. Per payment it is exactly $d$ more than ordinary ($e(d)+d$); per loss it is $E[(X-d)_+] + d\,S_X(d)$. And in the Ultimate Formula its $m=u/\alpha$ (no $+d$).
  9. Coinsurance before vs. after the deductible. "After" is the default. If the question says coinsurance applies before, replace $d$ by $d^\ast=d/\alpha$ — the deductible isn't met until $\alpha X = d$.
  10. Forgetting the exam-table excess-loss shortcuts. For Exponential/Pareto, $e(d)$ has a closed form (memoryless $\theta$; Pareto $\frac{\theta+d}{\alpha-1}$) — far faster than integrating, and it is the per-payment ordinary mean.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write down two expressions for the expected payment when a policy limit $u$ applies.
$E[X\wedge u] = \int_0^u x f(x)\,dx + u\,S(u) = \int_0^u S(x)\,dx.$ The second (survival-function) form is usually the faster integral.
What is an increased limit factor, and how is it calculated? If $E[X\wedge 500]=200.62$ and $E[X\wedge 800]=226.19$, find the ILF.
The ILF is the ratio of expected payments at the new vs. old limit: $\mathrm{ILF}=\frac{E[X\wedge u]}{E[X\wedge b]}$. Here $\mathrm{ILF}=226.19/200.62=\mathbf{1.1274}$ — about 12.74% more expected payment.
State the relationship between $E[Y^L]$ and $E[Y^P]$ for an ordinary deductible $d$, and explain why $E[Y^P]$ is always at least as large.
$E[Y^P] = E[Y^L]/S_X(d)$. Since $0 < S_X(d) \le 1$, dividing by it can only increase (or keep equal) the value. Intuitively, $Y^P$ excludes the zero-payment losses ($X\le d$), so averaging only over actual payments raises the mean.
Five losses are $2,3,7,9,14$ with an ordinary deductible of $5$. Find the average payment per loss and per payment.
Payments: $0,0,2,4,9$ (total $15$). Per loss $=15/5=\mathbf{3}$. Per payment divides by the $3$ losses that actually pay: $15/3=\mathbf{5}$. Per payment $\ge$ per loss, always.
Define the mean excess loss $e(d)$ and connect it to the per-payment mean. For Pareto$(\alpha=2.5,\theta=100)$ with a franchise deductible of $50$, find the expected payment per payment.
$e(d)=E[X-d\mid X>d]=\frac{E[(X-d)_+]}{S(d)}=E[Y^P_{\text{ordinary}}]$. For Pareto, $e(d)=\frac{\theta+d}{\alpha-1}=\frac{100+50}{1.5}=100$. Franchise adds $d$: $E[Y^P_{\text{fr}}]=e(d)+d=100+50=\mathbf{150}$.
A policy has deductible 500 and maximum covered loss 5000. What is the policy limit (largest possible payment, ignoring coinsurance)?
With $\alpha=1$, $m=u+d$, so policy limit $u = m - d = 5000 - 500 = \mathbf{4500}$.
Losses are exponential with mean 2000. Compute the LER for a deductible of 1000.
$E[X\wedge 1000] = 2000(1-e^{-0.5}) = 786.939.$ $\mathrm{LER} = 786.939/2000 = \mathbf{0.3935}.$
With deductible $d$, limit $u$, coinsurance $\alpha$, and inflation $r$, write the expected cost per loss (the Ultimate Formula) and the maximum covered loss.
$E[Y^L] = \alpha(1+r)\Big(E\big[X\wedge \tfrac{m}{1+r}\big] - E\big[X\wedge \tfrac{d}{1+r}\big]\Big)$ with $m=\frac{u}{\alpha}+d$.
A question says coinsurance $\alpha=0.5$ is applied before a deductible of $d=300$. How do you adjust, and why?
Use $d^\ast = d/\alpha = 300/0.5 = 600$ in place of $d$, then solve as the standard "after" case. Only the coinsured portion $\alpha X$ counts toward the deductible, so the deductible is met when $\alpha X = d$, i.e. at loss $X = d/\alpha$.
An elimination period equals the retroactive qualification period in a disability policy. What familiar coverage modification does the policy effectively have?
A franchise deductible: pay nothing during the waiting period, but once the disability lasts past it, benefits are paid retroactively to day one — the full amount, just like a franchise pays the full loss once $X>d$.
