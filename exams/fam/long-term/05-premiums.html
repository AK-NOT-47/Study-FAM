Premium Calculation

Set the premium so the insurer breaks even on average — then build the loss random variable $L_0$ and everything from variance to percentile premiums follows.

📖 DHW · Ch. 6 ⏱️ ~3–4 hrs 🔑 equivalence principle · $L_0$ · net & gross premiums 📈 variance · percentiles · portfolio premium 🧩 semi-continuous · m-thly · refund of premiums
Learning Objectives

After mastering this topic, you should be able to:

  • Define the present value of net future loss at issue random variable $L_0$ (and the gross version $L_0^g$): write it in terms of present-value random variables, and compute its expectation and variance.
  • State and apply the equivalence principle to compute net (benefit) premiums for whole life, term, and endowment insurances — fully continuous, fully discrete, and semi-continuous.
  • Express each net premium three equivalent ways: $\dfrac{A}{\ddot a}$ (core), in terms of $A$'s only, and in terms of $\ddot a$'s only.
  • Compute net premiums funded by annual, m-thly, and continuous premium streams, and handle limited-pay, term, and deferred-annuity products plus the return-of-premium feature.
  • Compute gross (expense-loaded) premiums that incorporate per-premium, per-policy, per-1000-of-face, and settlement expenses, with different first-year and renewal levels, and find the variance of the gross loss.
  • Compute percentiles and probabilities of $L_0$ for a single policy, and apply the portfolio percentile premium principle to a block of independent policies using the Normal approximation.
  • Price substandard (extra-risk) lives by adjusting the mortality basis three standard ways — age rating, a constant addition to the force of mortality ($\mu_x+c$), and a constant multiple of the mortality rate ($k\,q_x$).

These map to the SOA FAM-L learning outcomes on premium calculation and the loss random variable, following Actuarial Mathematics for Life Contingent Risks (Dickson, Hardy & Waters, "DHW"), Chapter 6.

Key Concepts

1. Why premiums exist — the loss random variable

When an insurer issues a policy, it promises a benefit (paid at death or maturity) in exchange for premiums. Both sides are random because the timing of death is random. The fundamental object is the present value of net future loss at issue ("net" means expenses are excluded):

Present value of net future loss at issue $$L_0 = \text{PV}_0(\text{future benefits}) - \text{PV}_0(\text{future premiums}).$$

$L_0 > 0$ means the policy lost money (in PV terms); $L_0 < 0$ means it made money. The insurer wants this to be zero on average (the equivalence principle) or, more conservatively, to be positive only with small probability (percentile premiums).

Exam notation for the loss random variable

The SOA may write the present-value-of-future-loss as $L$, ${}_0L$, or $L_0$ for loss at issue, and ${}_tL$ or $L_t$ for loss $t$ years after issue (the reserve work in the next topic). Superscripts such as $L_0^g$ flag the gross version. When the bare symbol $L$ appears, the question text defines exactly what it means — read the definition.

2. The equivalence principle

The equivalence principle sets the premium so that the expected present value of loss is zero:

Equivalence principle (expected loss is zero) $$E[L_0] = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{EPV(benefits + expenses)} = \text{EPV(premiums)}.$$

For a net premium we ignore expenses; for a gross premium we include them. Build the equation of value, solve for the premium.

What "net premium" silently tells you

The moment a question says "net premium", three things are automatically true and should fire in your head: (1) the equivalence principle is assumed, (2) expenses are ignored (even if some are stated), and (3) therefore $E[L_0]=0$, i.e. $\text{EPV(premiums)} = \text{EPV(benefits)}$. The same object was called the benefit premium on older exams — that term is retired but you may meet it in old problems. By default, unless expenses are explicitly given, "loss at issue" means net loss and "premium by the equivalence principle" means the net premium.

Why annuities-due, not -immediate?

In practice premiums are paid in advance, the first one at issue. This stops a policyholder from getting cover without having paid for it. So the EPV of premiums uses an annuity-due ($\ddot a$, or $\bar a$ in the continuous case) for the premium-paying period — never an annuity-immediate.

3. Net premiums for the standard contracts

Every net premium has the same shape — and this is the single most reused idea in the course:

The master recipe
$$\boxed{\;\text{Net premium} = \frac{\text{EPV(benefit)}}{\text{EPV(annuity for the premium-paying period)}}\;}$$

Benefit EPV on top; the EPV of a life annuity over exactly the years premiums are actually paid on the bottom.

  • Whole life (benefit 1 at death, premiums for life): $P = A_x / \ddot a_x$.
  • n-year term (benefit 1 if death within n, premiums for at most n years): $P = A^{\,1}_{x:\overline{n}|} / \ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}$.
  • n-year endowment (benefit 1 on death within n OR survival to n, premiums for at most n years): $P = A_{x:\overline{n}|} / \ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}$.

The premium-paying period and the benefit period need not coincide. A whole life with premiums limited to $h$ years (an "$h$-pay") has $P = A_x / \ddot a_{x:\overline{h}|}$ — full benefit on top, but only the $h$-year annuity below. Always match the denominator to the actual premium pattern.

4. Continuous, m-thly, and semi-continuous premiums

  • Fully continuous (continuous benefit $\bar A$, continuous premium): $\bar P(\bar A_x) = \bar A_x / \bar a_x$.
  • Fully discrete (benefit at end of year of death, annual premiums): $P_x = A_x / \ddot a_x$.
  • Semi-continuous (death benefit at the moment of death, but premiums paid annually): $P = \bar A_x / \ddot a_x$. The benefit is continuous ($\bar A$) while the premium annuity is discrete ($\ddot a$) — mix and match.
  • m-thly premiums (paid $m$ times per year): denominator becomes $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$. So $P^{(m)} = A_x / \ddot a_x^{(m)}$ is the annualized premium; each installment is $P^{(m)}/m$.
The m-thly factor-of-m rule

If a question gives you the m-thly premium $P$, the annualized premium is $mP$, and the EPV of premiums is $mP\,\ddot a_x^{(m)}$. Going the other way, divide the annualized premium by $m$. Forgetting the $\times m$ (e.g. writing $P\,\ddot a_x^{(4)}$ instead of $4P\,\ddot a_x^{(4)}$ for quarterly premiums of $P$) is a classic slip.

A common exam shortcut uses the Woolhouse (2-term) relationships to get $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ and $\bar a_x$ from $\ddot a_x$ (see Formulas).

5. Other products — term, n-pay, deferred annuities, special

For anything beyond the three standard contracts, fall back on the master recipe: set EPV(premiums) = EPV(benefits) and solve.

  • Term & n-pay whole life: built above — only the annuity in the denominator changes (it is the temporary annuity $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}$).
  • Deferred whole life annuity: the policyholder pays premiums only during the deferral period (you don't pay in while collecting). For an $n$-year deferred annuity, $P\,\ddot a_{x:\overline n|} = {}_{n|}\ddot a_x$, so $P = {}_{n|}\ddot a_x / \ddot a_{x:\overline n|}$.
  • Special insurance: a policy with non-level benefits and/or non-level premiums — the non-level pattern is spelled out in the question. If a policy is not called "special," assume benefits and premiums are level.
  • Return (refund) of premiums: if death triggers a refund of premiums paid (usually without interest), the refund is itself a benefit — model it as an arithmetically increasing term insurance $P(IA)_{x:\overline n|}^{\,1}$ and add it to the benefit side (see Worked Examples).

6. Gross premiums and expenses

A gross premium is the premium the insurer actually charges; it must cover expenses as well as benefits. Expenses come in four flavors, and most are heavier in the first year (acquisition) than at renewal:

  • Per-premium (percentage): a percent of the gross premium — e.g. commissions, 40% year 1 then 5%. These scale with $G$.
  • Per-policy: a flat dollar amount each year regardless of size — e.g. \$50 issue, \$10 renewal.
  • Per-face (per-1000): a dollar amount per \$1000 of face — multiply by $S/1000$.
  • Settlement (termination) expense $E$: incurred when the claim is paid; it rides on top of the death benefit, so it behaves like a small addition to the benefit amount $b$.
The two SOA expense default assumptions

Unless a question says otherwise, the SOA tells you to assume both of the following:

  1. Expenses are payable at the START of each period (the same advance timing as premiums — hence they ride the annuity-due, and the first year's expenses fall at $t=0$).
  2. Percent-of-premium expenses are payable only while premiums are payable — they begin with the first premium and stop the moment premiums stop. So for an $h$-pay or limited-pay policy, commissions run over the $h$-year annuity $\ddot a_{x:\overline h|}$, never the full $\ddot a_x$. (Per-policy and per-1000 expenses are not automatically tied to the premium period — read the question for when those end.)

Conceptually expenses sort into initial/acquisition (commissions, underwriting — at issue), renewal/maintenance (each later premium), and termination/settlement (at claim). The gross equation of value is:

Gross equation of value $$\text{EPV(benefits)} + \text{EPV(expenses)} = \text{EPV(gross premiums)} = G\,\ddot a.$$

Because per-premium expenses are multiples of $G$, you typically move them across to the premium side and divide.

Gross premium ≠ "equivalence" automatically

Unlike "net premium," the word "gross premium" does NOT imply the equivalence principle. Insurers often charge more than breakeven (e.g. 110% of the equivalence gross premium) for profit. Only assume equivalence if the question says "gross premium calculated using the equivalence principle." A tell-tale sign the equivalence principle was not used: $E[L_0^g] \neq 0$.

The renewal-level + first-year-excess trick

Instead of carrying "$e_1$ in year 1, $e$ thereafter," charge the renewal level $e$ for every year via the full annuity $e\,\ddot a$, then add the excess $(e_1 - e)$ once at $t=0$. This lets you use the annuity-due values straight off the Standard Ultimate Life Table (which tabulates $\ddot a$, not $a$) and kills most first-year double-counting errors. The same trick applies to percentage commissions: $e\,\ddot a$ becomes $r\,G\,\ddot a + (r_1-r)G$.

7. The variance of $L_0$ and the "annuity-trick"

For fully continuous whole life with annual rate of premium $P$, write $L_0$ in terms of the single PV random variable $Z = v^{T_x}$. Using $\bar a_{\overline{T}|} = (1 - v^{T})/\delta$:

$L_0$ as an affine function of $Z=v^T$ (fully continuous whole life) $$L_0 = v^{T} - P\,\bar a_{\overline{T}|} = \Big(1 + \frac{P}{\delta}\Big)v^{T} - \frac{P}{\delta}.$$

So $L_0$ is a linear (affine) function of $Z = v^T$, hence

Variance via the affine form $$\mathrm{Var}(L_0) = \Big(1 + \frac{P}{\delta}\Big)^2 \mathrm{Var}(Z) = \Big(1 + \frac{P}{\delta}\Big)^2\big({}^2\bar A_x - (\bar A_x)^2\big).$$

The discrete version uses $d$ in place of $\delta$ and $A_x$ in place of $\bar A_x$. This "express $L_0$ as $a + b\cdot Z$" technique is the key to every variance problem on this topic, and it works for endowment insurance too because the endowment PV $v^{\min(K+1,\,n)}$ is also a single $Z$. It does not work for term insurance (see Traps).

Two variance formulas — know when each is legal

The general form $\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = (b+\tfrac{P}{d})^2[{}^2A_x-(A_x)^2]$ works for any premium $P$ — equivalence or not. The shortcut $\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = b^2\dfrac{{}^2A_x-(A_x)^2}{(1-A_x)^2}$ is only valid when $P$ is the equivalence-principle premium. On the exam the general form is usually the safer default.

8. Percentile premiums and the portfolio principle

The equivalence principle gives roughly a 50% chance of loss. Two more prudent rules:

  • Single-policy percentile premium: choose $P$ so that $\Pr(L_0 > 0) = \alpha$ for some small $\alpha$. Because $L_0$ is monotonic in $T_x$, find the relevant percentile of $T_x$ and set $L_0 = 0$ at that lifetime.
  • Portfolio percentile premium principle: for $N$ independent, identical policies, choose $P$ so that the aggregate loss $S = \sum L_{0,i}$ satisfies $\Pr(S > 0) = \alpha$ (equivalently $\Pr(S<0)=1-\alpha$, the insurer's "probability of profit"). Apply the Central Limit Theorem: $E[S]=N\,E[L]$, $\mathrm{Var}[S]=N\,\mathrm{Var}[L]$, $S \approx \text{Normal}$, and solve $E[S] + \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]} = 0$. As $N \to \infty$ the loading per policy shrinks toward the equivalence premium.
$\Pr[S<0]$ vs. $\Pr[S>0]$ — don't get turned around

$S$ is the loss. $S<0$ is a profit (good), so the insurer wants $\Pr[S<0]$ large — 90%, 95%, 99%. $S>0$ is a real loss (bad), so $\Pr[S>0]$ should be small — 1%, 5%, 10%. They are complements: $\Pr[S<0] = 1 - \Pr[S>0]$.

9. Modelling substandard (extra-risk) lives

An applicant whose mortality is heavier than the standard table — a smoker, a hazardous occupation, an existing health condition — is substandard (rated). The insurer charges more by feeding a worse mortality basis into the same equivalence-principle machinery: nothing about the premium formula changes, only the $A$'s and $\ddot a$'s (or $\mu$) you plug in. There are three standard ways to model the extra risk:

Three ways to model extra/substandard mortality
  • (a) Age rating (rate-up). Treat the life as if it were older by a fixed number of years $j$: evaluate every function at age $x+j$. So a rated $(45)$ with a "+5 year" rating is priced as a standard $(50)$ — use $A_{x+j}$, $\ddot a_{x+j}$, ${}_tp_{x+j}$ throughout. The simplest to apply with a table.
  • (b) Constant addition to the force of mortality. Add a constant $c$ at every age: $\mu^{\,*}_{x+t} = \mu_{x+t} + c$. Survival is then scaled by an exponential: ${}_tp^{\,*}_x = {}_tp_x\,e^{-ct}$. In an EPV integral this multiplies the discount by an extra $e^{-ct}$, i.e. it acts exactly like adding $c$ to the force of interest $\delta$ in the survival part.
  • (c) Constant multiple of the mortality rate. Multiply the standard rate by a factor $k\gt 1$: $q^{\,*}_{x+t} = k\,q_{x+t}$ (cap at 1). A "200% of standard" or "rating factor 2" table means $k=2$. Rebuild ${}_tp^{\,*}_x = \prod(1-k\,q_{x+t})$ from the rated $q$'s, then compute the EPVs as usual.

Whichever you use, the recipe is unchanged: $\text{premium} = \dfrac{\text{EPV(benefit)}}{\text{EPV(annuity)}}$, with every EPV computed on the rated basis. A heavier basis raises $A$ and lowers $\ddot a$, so the rated premium is higher — as it should be.

Constant-force shortcut for method (b)

If the standard force is constant at $\mu$, adding $c$ gives a constant rated force $\mu^{\,*}=\mu+c$. Then fully continuous whole life has $\bar A^{\,*}_x = \dfrac{\mu^{\,*}}{\mu^{\,*}+\delta}$ and $\bar a^{\,*}_x = \dfrac{1}{\mu^{\,*}+\delta}$, so the net premium rate is simply $\;P = b\,\dfrac{\bar A^{\,*}_x}{\bar a^{\,*}_x} = b\,\mu^{\,*}.$ Under a constant force the continuous whole-life net premium rate equals the benefit times the force of mortality (see Example 15).

Formulas

Notation reminder

$v = 1/(1+i)$, $d = iv = 1 - v$, $\delta = \ln(1+i)$. $T_x$ is the future lifetime of $(x)$; $K_x = \lfloor T_x\rfloor$ is the curtate future lifetime. $A$'s are insurance EPVs, $\ddot a$'s annuity-due EPVs, $\bar a$ continuous-annuity EPVs. A leading superscript "2" (e.g. ${}^2A_x$) means the EPV computed at double the force of interest, i.e. using $i^* = 2i + i^2$ (equivalently $v^2$, $\delta^* = 2\delta$).

Net premiums (equivalence principle)

Discrete (annual) premiums, benefit 1 — the standard symbols you may see on the exam:

Whole life · $n$-year term · pure endowment · endowment insurance $$P_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot a_x}, \qquad P^{\,1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{A^{\,1}_{x:\overline{n}|}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}}, \qquad P_{x:\overline{n}|}^{\;\;1} = \frac{A_{x:\overline{n}|}^{\;\;1}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}}, \qquad P_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}}.$$
  • $A_x$: EPV of whole life insurance of 1 on $(x)$, paid end of year of death.
  • $A^{\,1}_{x:\overline{n}|}$: EPV of $n$-year term insurance (death benefit only); $A_{x:\overline{n}|}^{\;\;1} = {}_nE_x$ is the pure endowment.
  • $A_{x:\overline{n}|} = A^{\,1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_nE_x$: EPV of $n$-year endowment insurance; ${}_nE_x = v^n\,{}_np_x$.
  • If $P$ is not one of these symbols, the question defines it in its text.

Limited / $h$-pay whole life: $\;{}_hP_x = A_x / \ddot a_{x:\overline{h}|}$. Deferred annuity (pay during deferral only): $\;P = {}_{n|}\ddot a_x / \ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}$.

Each premium has three equivalent algebraic faces (core, $A$-only, $\ddot a$-only). For whole life, using $A_x = 1 - d\,\ddot a_x$:

Whole life — three equivalent forms (core · $\ddot a$-only · $A$-only) $$P_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot a_x} = \frac{1}{\ddot a_x} - d = \frac{d\,A_x}{1 - A_x}.$$

Endowment (same identities with the $x:\overline n|$ subscript): $\;P_{x:\overline{n}|} = \dfrac{A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}} = \dfrac{1}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}} - d = \dfrac{d\,A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{1-A_{x:\overline{n}|}}.$

Memorize one, derive the rest

The core form $A/\ddot a$ is the one to truly know; the $A$-only and $\ddot a$-only versions are just $A = 1 - d\,\ddot a$ substituted in. Less to memorize, fewer ways to slip.

Continuous, semi-continuous, and m-thly

Fully continuous whole life — three equivalent forms $$\bar P(\bar A_x) = \frac{\bar A_x}{\bar a_x} = \frac{1}{\bar a_x} - \delta = \frac{\delta\,\bar A_x}{1 - \bar A_x}, \qquad \bar A_x = 1 - \delta\,\bar a_x.$$ Semi-continuous and $m$-thly premiums $$P^{\text{semi}} = \frac{\bar A_x}{\ddot a_x}\;\;(\text{continuous benefit, annual premiums}), \qquad P^{(m)}_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot a_x^{(m)}}\;\;(\text{annualized}); \quad \text{each installment} = \frac{P^{(m)}_x}{m}.$$

Conversion (Woolhouse, 2-term — exam standard):

Woolhouse (2-term) annuity conversions $$\ddot a_x^{(m)} \approx \ddot a_x - \frac{m-1}{2m}, \qquad \bar a_x \approx \ddot a_x - \tfrac12.$$

Conversion (UDD): $\;\ddot a_x^{(m)} = \alpha(m)\,\ddot a_x - \beta(m)$, and $\bar A_x = \dfrac{i}{\delta}A_x$, $\;A_x^{(m)} = \dfrac{i}{i^{(m)}}A_x$.

Gross (expense-loaded) premium $G$

Let benefit face amount be $S$ (per-1000 expenses use $S/1000$). General equation of value:

General equation of value (face $S$) $$\underbrace{S\cdot A}_{\text{benefits}} + \underbrace{\text{EPV expenses}}_{E} = G\,\ddot a.$$

With first-year per-premium rate $r_1$, renewal $r$; first-year per-policy $e_1$, renewal $e$; first-year per-1000 $f_1$, renewal $f$ (using the renewal-level + first-year-excess trick):

Expanded equation with first-year and renewal expense levels $$G\,\ddot a = S\cdot A \;+\; \underbrace{\big[e\,\ddot a + (e_1 - e)\big]}_{\text{per-policy}} \;+\; \underbrace{\Big[f\tfrac{S}{1000}\ddot a + (f_1 - f)\tfrac{S}{1000}\Big]}_{\text{per-1000}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big[rG\,\ddot a + (r_1 - r)G\big]}_{\text{per-premium}}.$$
Gross premium master formula
$$\boxed{\;G = \frac{S\cdot A + e\,\ddot a + (e_1-e) + f\frac{S}{1000}\ddot a + (f_1-f)\frac{S}{1000}}{\ddot a - \big[r\,\ddot a + (r_1 - r)\big]} = \frac{\text{EPV benefits} + \text{EPV expenses (ex per-premium)}}{\ddot a - \text{EPV of per-premium factor}}\;}$$

In practice: put everything that is a multiple of $G$ (the per-premium expenses) on the premium side, then divide.

Loss-at-issue random variable $L_0$ (benefit $b$)

Fully continuous whole life, premium rate $P$:

$L_0$ and its mean (fully continuous whole life) $$L_0 = b\,v^{T_x} - P\,\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|} = \Big(b+\frac{P}{\delta}\Big)v^{T_x} - \frac{P}{\delta}, \qquad E[L_0] = b\,\bar A_x - P\,\bar a_x.$$ Variance of $L_0$ $$\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = \Big(b+\frac{P}{\delta}\Big)^2\Big({}^2\bar A_x - (\bar A_x)^2\Big).$$

Fully discrete whole life, annual premium $P$:

$L_0$ and its mean (fully discrete whole life) $$L_0 = b\,v^{K_x+1} - P\,\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|} = \Big(b+\frac{P}{d}\Big)v^{K_x+1} - \frac{P}{d}, \qquad E[L_0]=b\,A_x - P\,\ddot a_x.$$ Variance of $L_0$ $$\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = \Big(b+\frac{P}{d}\Big)^2\Big({}^2A_x - (A_x)^2\Big).$$

Fully discrete $n$-year endowment (PV is still a single $Z = v^{\min(K_x+1,\,n)}$):

Variance of $L_0$ (fully discrete $n$-year endowment) $$\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = \Big(b+\frac{P}{d}\Big)^2\Big({}^2A_{x:\overline{n}|} - (A_{x:\overline{n}|})^2\Big).$$

Under the equivalence-principle premium only, the clean shortcut is:

Equivalence-principle variance shortcut
$$\mathrm{Var}[L_0]\Big|_{\text{equiv}} = b^2\,\frac{{}^2A_x - (A_x)^2}{(1 - A_x)^2} = b^2\,\frac{{}^2A_x - (A_x)^2}{(d\,\ddot a_x)^2}.$$

(Endowment: replace $A_x$ by $A_{x:\overline{n}|}$ throughout. Continuous: replace $A$ by $\bar A$, $d$ by $\delta$.) Valid only when $P$ is the equivalence premium.

Gross loss $L_0^g$ and its variance

Let $e_i$ = initial (year-1) expense, $e_r$ = renewal expense, $b$ = benefit, $E$ = settlement expense, $G$ = gross premium. For fully discrete whole life:

Gross loss $L_0^g$ (fully discrete whole life) $$L_0^g = (b+E)v^{K_x+1} + \big[e_r\,\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|} + (e_i - e_r)\big] - G\,\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|} = \Big(b+E+\frac{G-e_r}{d}\Big)v^{K_x+1} - \frac{G-e_r}{d} + (e_i - e_r).$$
Gross variance shortcut (whole life / endowment only)
$$\mathrm{Var}[L_0^g] = \Big(b + E + \frac{G - e_r}{d}\Big)^2\big({}^2A_x - (A_x)^2\big).$$

Endowment: use $A_{x:\overline n|}$. Continuous: replace $d\to\delta$ and the $A$'s with $\bar A$'s. The initial expense $e_i$ vanishes from the variance — it is a known constant incurred at issue, and a constant has zero variance.

Percentile and portfolio premiums

Single-policy percentile. The $100p$th percentile $\pi_p$ of $L_0$ satisfies $\Pr[L_0 \le \pi_p] = p$. Because $L_0$ decreases in $T_x$ (longer life ⇒ smaller loss), graph $L_0$ vs. $T_x$, find the lifetime $h$ with $\Pr[T_x > h] = {}_hp_x = p$, then evaluate $L_0$ at $T_x = h$.

Probability $\Pr[L_0 > X]$ (single policy). Write $L_0$ with the only unknown being $v^{T_x}$ or $v^{K_x+1}$, solve the inequality for $T_x$ (or $K_x$), then read off a survival/death probability from the life table.

Portfolio ($N$ independent identical policies), aggregate loss $S = \sum_{i=1}^N L_{0,i}$:

Portfolio percentile premium condition (Normal approximation) $$\Pr(S > 0) = \alpha \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{0 - E[S]}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]}} = \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha) \;\Rightarrow\; E[S] + \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]} = 0,$$

with $E[S]=N\,E[L]$, $\mathrm{Var}[S]=N\,\mathrm{Var}[L]$, and $\Phi^{-1}(0.95)=1.645$. Substitute $E[L]$ and $\mathrm{Var}[L]$ as functions of the premium and solve. Per-policy form: $E[L] = -\,\Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\,\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[L]}/\sqrt N$.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Net annual premium, whole life, with variance ▾

A fully discrete whole life insurance of \$100,000 is issued to $(40)$. You are given $i = 0.05$, $A_{40} = 0.20$, ${}^2A_{40} = 0.06$. Find (a) the annual net premium and (b) the standard deviation of $L_0$.

Solution.
First $\ddot a_{40} = (1 - A_{40})/d$ with $d = 0.05/1.05 = 0.047619$.
$\ddot a_{40} = (1 - 0.20)/0.047619 = 0.80/0.047619 = 16.80$.

(a) $P = 100000 \cdot A_{40}/\ddot a_{40} = 100000 \cdot 0.20/16.80 = 1190.48$.

(b) Equivalence-principle variance, scaled by face 100000:
$\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = (100000)^2 \cdot \dfrac{{}^2A_{40} - (A_{40})^2}{(1 - A_{40})^2}$.
Numerator $0.06 - 0.04 = 0.02$; denominator $(0.80)^2 = 0.64$; ratio $= 0.03125$.
$\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = 10^{10}\cdot 0.03125 = 3.125\times 10^{8}$, so $\mathrm{SD} = 17677$.

Answer
(a) $P = \$1{,}190.48$ per year. (b) $\mathrm{SD}(L_0) \approx \$17{,}677$.
Example 2 SD of $L$ — equivalence premium from $A$'s only ▾

[MLC-S07:4 style] For a fully discrete whole life insurance of \$150,000 on $(x)$: ${}^2A_x = 0.0143$, $A_x = 0.0653$, premium by the equivalence principle. Find $\mathrm{SD}(L)$.

Solution. Use the equivalence shortcut directly — no annuity needed:

$$\mathrm{Var}[L] = b^2\,\frac{{}^2A_x - (A_x)^2}{(1 - A_x)^2} = 150000^2\cdot\frac{0.0143 - 0.0653^2}{(1-0.0653)^2} = 258{,}460{,}863.$$

$\mathrm{SD}(L) = \sqrt{258{,}460{,}863} = 16{,}076.72$.

Check (general form): $P = 150000\,\dfrac{dA_x}{1-A_x}$, then $\mathrm{Var}[L] = \big(150000 + \tfrac{P}{d}\big)^2[{}^2A_x-(A_x)^2]$ gives the same number — the shortcut is just this with $P$ substituted.

Answer
$\mathrm{SD}(L) \approx \$16{,}076.72$.
Example 3 Fully continuous WL with a mixed mortality density ▾

[SOA Sample 6.31] Fully continuous whole life of \$100,000 on $(35)$. The newborn future-lifetime density is $f(t)=0.01e^{-0.01t}$ for $0\le t<70$ (constant force $\mu=0.01$ over that range) and arbitrary after; $\delta=0.05$, $\bar A_{70}=0.51791$. Find the annual net premium rate.

Solution. "Net premium" ⇒ equivalence, no expenses: $P = 100000\,\bar A_{35}/\bar a_{35}$.
Split the whole life into a 35-year term plus a deferred piece. With constant force $\mu=0.01$ to age 70, $\bar A_{35:\overline{35}|}^{\,1} = \dfrac{\mu}{\mu+\delta}\big(1-e^{-(\mu+\delta)(35)}\big)$ and ${}_{35}E_{35}=e^{-(\mu+\delta)(35)}$:

$$\bar A_{35} = \frac{0.01}{0.06}\big(1-e^{-0.06\cdot 35}\big) + e^{-0.06\cdot 35}(0.51791) = 0.20968.$$

$\bar a_{35} = (1-\bar A_{35})/\delta = (1-0.20968)/0.05 = 15.8064$, so

$$P = 100000\cdot\frac{0.20968}{15.8064} = 1{,}326.55.$$
Answer
$P \approx \$1{,}326.55$ per year.
Example 4 Recover $\pi$ from two variances ▾

[SOA MLC 228] Fully continuous whole life of 1 on $(x)$: $\bar A_x = 1/3$, $\delta = 0.10$. With the equivalence premium, $\mathrm{Var}[L]=1/5$; with premium $\pi$, $\mathrm{Var}[L']=16/45$. Find $\pi$.

Solution. Equivalence premium $P = \dfrac{\delta\bar A_x}{1-\bar A_x} = \dfrac{0.10/3}{2/3} = 0.05$. Both variances share the factor $[{}^2\bar A_x-(\bar A_x)^2]$, so dividing kills it:

$$\frac{\mathrm{Var}[L']}{\mathrm{Var}[L]} = \left(\frac{1+\pi/0.10}{1+0.05/0.10}\right)^2 = \frac{16/45}{1/5} = \frac{16}{9} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{1+10\pi}{1.5} = \frac{4}{3} \;\Rightarrow\; \pi = 0.10.$$
Answer
$\pi = 0.10$. The ratio trick avoids ever computing ${}^2\bar A_x$.

Example 6 Return of premiums without interest ▾

[Refund feature] A special fully discrete 10-pay whole life on $(30)$ with level net premium $\pi$. The death benefit is \$1,000 plus a refund, without interest, of all net premiums paid. Given $A_{30}=0.102$, ${}_{10|}A_{30}=0.088$, $(IA)^{\,1}_{30:\overline{10}|}=0.078$, $\ddot a_{30:\overline{10}|}=7.747$. Find $\pi$.

EPV(premiums) $= \pi\,\ddot a_{30:\overline{10}|} = 7.747\pi$.
EPV(\$1,000 death benefit) $= 1000\,A_{30} = 102$.

Answer
$\pi \approx 15.02$. Treat any return-of-premium as an extra increasing-insurance benefit and add it to the benefit side.
Example 7 Gross premium, 5-pay whole life, mixed expenses ▾

[SOA Sample 6.28] Fully discrete 5-pay whole life of \$1,000 on $(40)$. Year 1: commission 20% of premium, per-policy \$10. Years 2–5: commission 5%, per-policy \$5. No expenses after year 5. SULT mortality, $i=0.05$. Gross premium $G$ by the equivalence principle.

Example 9 Variance of the gross loss — shortcut formula ▾

[SOA Sample 6.30] Fully discrete whole life of \$100 on $(x)$. First-year expense 10% of premium, later years 5%. Equivalence gross premium $G=2.338$, $i=0.04$, $\ddot a_x=16.50$, ${}^2A_x=0.17$. Find $\mathrm{Var}[L_0^g]$.

Solution. Here $b=100$, $E=0$, $e_r=0.05(2.338)=0.1169$, $d=0.04/1.04$. First get $A_x = 1 - d\,\ddot a_x = 1 - \tfrac{0.04}{1.04}(16.50) = 0.36538$. Then the gross variance shortcut:

$$\mathrm{Var}[L_0^g] = \Big(b + E + \frac{G - e_r}{d}\Big)^2\big({}^2A_x - (A_x)^2\big) = \Big(100 + \frac{2.338 - 0.1169}{0.04/1.04}\Big)^2\big(0.17 - 0.36538^2\big) = 908.14.$$
Answer
$\mathrm{Var}[L_0^g] \approx 908.14$. The first-year expense never enters the variance — it is a known constant.
Example 10 Portfolio percentile premium (Normal approximation) ▾

[S12:25 style] An insurer issues fully discrete whole life of \$100,000 to lives age 40, independent lifetimes. Issue expense 20% of first premium; renewal 6% of each premium from year 2. $A_{40}=0.161$, ${}^2A_{40}=0.048$, $\ddot a_{40}=14.822$, $i=0.06$. Find $G$ so that $\Pr[\text{portfolio loss}>0]=5\%$ over $N=40{,}000$ policies.

Per-policy mean. Renewal-level + excess on commissions ($e_r$ here is $0.06G$, first-year excess $0.14G$):

$$E[L] = 100000A_{40} + \big[0.06G\,\ddot a_{40} + 0.14G\big] - G\,\ddot a_{40} = 16100 - 13.79268G.$$

Per-policy variance (gross shortcut, $b=100000$, $E=0$, $e_r=0.06G$, $d=0.06/1.06$):

$$\mathrm{Var}[L] = \Big(100000 + \frac{G-0.06G}{0.06/1.06}\Big)^2(0.048 - 0.161^2) = (100000 + 16.61G)^2(0.022079).$$

Portfolio condition $\Pr[S>0]=0.05$ with $E[S]=40000\,E[L]$, $\mathrm{Var}[S]=40000\,\mathrm{Var}[L]$:

$$E[S] + 1.64485\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 40000(16100 - 13.79268G) + 1.64485\sqrt{40000}\,(100000+16.61G)\sqrt{0.022079} = 0.$$

Solving the (linear-in-$G$) equation gives $G = 1{,}177.88$.

Answer
$G \approx \$1{,}177.88$ per policy. The $\sqrt N$ makes the loading small relative to the equivalence premium.
Example 11 75th percentile of $L_0$ (graph + life table) ▾

[S-17:8] Fully continuous whole life of \$100,000 on $(35)$, premium rate \$926/yr, $i=0.06$, deaths UDD, with $l_{35}=9{,}420{,}657$, $l_{67}=7{,}201{,}635$, $l_{68}=7{,}018{,}432$. Find the 75th percentile of $L_0$.

$\pi_{0.75} \approx \$1{,}306$. A higher percentile of $L_0$ corresponds to an earlier boundary lifetime — draw the graph to keep the direction straight.
Example 12 $\Pr[L_0 > 0]$ for a single policy ▾

Fully discrete whole life of \$100,000 on $(40)$, SULT mortality, $i=0.05$, annual premium \$1,980. Find $\Pr[L_0 > 0]$. (Use $l_{40}=99{,}338.3$, $l_{65}=94{,}579.7$.)

Example 15 Substandard whole life — constant addition to the force $\mu+0.02$ ▾

A fully continuous whole life of \$100,000 is issued to $(x)$. Standard mortality has a constant force $\mu_x = 0.02$ at all ages; $\delta = 0.04$. Because $(x)$ is substandard, the insurer models the extra risk as a constant addition $c = 0.02$ to the force, i.e. $\mu^{\,*}=\mu+c$. Find (a) the standard net annual premium rate and (b) the substandard net annual premium rate.

Solution. Under a constant force, fully continuous whole life satisfies $\bar A_x = \dfrac{\mu}{\mu+\delta}$ and $\bar a_x = \dfrac{1}{\mu+\delta} = \dfrac{1-\bar A_x}{\delta}$. "Net premium" ⇒ equivalence, no expenses, so $P = 100000\,\bar A_x/\bar a_x$.

(a) Standard ($\mu=0.02$): $\;\bar A_x = \dfrac{0.02}{0.02+0.04} = 0.33333,\quad \bar a_x = \dfrac{1-0.33333}{0.04} = 16.6667,$

$$P_{\text{std}} = 100000\cdot\frac{0.33333}{16.6667} = 2{,}000.$$

(b) Substandard ($\mu^{\,*}=0.02+0.02=0.04$): $\;\bar A^{\,*}_x = \dfrac{0.04}{0.04+0.04} = 0.5,\quad \bar a^{\,*}_x = \dfrac{1-0.5}{0.04} = 12.5,$

$$P_{\text{sub}} = 100000\cdot\frac{0.5}{12.5} = 4{,}000.$$
Answer
(a) \$2,000/yr standard; (b) \$4,000/yr substandard. Same equivalence machinery, worse basis. Notice the constant-force shortcut on the rated basis: $P = b\cdot\dfrac{\bar A^{\,*}_x}{\bar a^{\,*}_x} = b\,(\mu^{\,*}+\delta)\cdot\dfrac{\mu^{\,*}}{\mu^{\,*}+\delta} = b\,\mu^{\,*}$, so the rated premium rate is just $100000(0.04)=4{,}000$ — the benefit times the rated force.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Using an immediate annuity in the denominator. Discrete premiums are paid in advance, so the denominator is an annuity-due $\ddot a$, never $a$. For continuous premiums use $\bar a$. (The SULT only tabulates $\ddot a$ — another reason to stay due.)
  • Mismatching the premium period and benefit period. A limited-pay, term-funded, or deferred-annuity policy uses the annuity over the actual premium-paying years ($\ddot a_{x:\overline{h}|}$ or $\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}$), not $\ddot a_x$.
  • Percent-of-premium expenses must stop when premiums stop. By SOA default, percent-of-premium expenses (commissions) are payable only while premiums are payable. On an $h$-pay policy their EPV uses the $h$-year annuity $\ddot a_{x:\overline h|}$, never the full $\ddot a_x$ — even though the benefit (and any per-policy/per-1000 expense) may run for life. Forgetting to cut the commissions off at year $h$ overstates expenses and the premium.
  • The variance shortcut requires a single $Z$. The clean forms ($\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = (b+P/d)^2[{}^2A - A^2]$ and its equivalence version) only hold for whole life and endowment, where the benefit PV is one random variable. For term (or any "other" product) the survival-to-$n$ outcome pays nothing, so $L_0$ is not affine in one $Z$ — build it outcome-by-outcome and use $\mathrm{Var}=E[L_0^2]-(E[L_0])^2$.
  • Equivalence-only shortcut used on a non-equivalence premium. $\mathrm{Var}=b^2\frac{{}^2A-A^2}{(1-A)^2}$ is valid only at the equivalence premium. With any other premium, fall back to the general $(b+P/d)^2[{}^2A-A^2]$.
  • Assuming "gross premium" means equivalence. "Net premium" implies equivalence; "gross premium" does not. Only use the equivalence equation for a gross premium if the question says so. If $E[L_0^g]\neq 0$, the equivalence principle was not used.
  • Using $d$ where $\delta$ belongs (or vice versa). Discrete: factor $1+P/d$. Continuous: $1+P/\delta$. The "double-force" ${}^2A$ uses $i^*=2i+i^2$ (discrete) or $\delta^*=2\delta$ (continuous) — never just double $A$.
  • Mixing per-policy and per-1000 expenses. Per-policy is a flat \$ amount regardless of face; per-1000 scales with $S/1000$. And the settlement expense $E$ rides on the benefit ($b+E$), not the annuity.
  • Putting per-premium expenses on the wrong side. Percentage-of-premium expenses are multiples of $G$, so subtract them from $G\ddot a$ (move to the premium side) before dividing — don't lump them with the benefit EPV.
  • First-year expense double counting. Charge the renewal rate for ALL years via the full annuity, then add the excess $(e_1-e)$ once at $t=0$.
  • Forgetting the $\times m$ on m-thly premiums. If $P$ is the m-thly premium, the annual EPV of premiums is $mP\,\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ — not $P\,\ddot a_x^{(m)}$.
  • Return-of-premium forgotten as a benefit. A refund of premiums on death is an extra benefit — model it as an increasing term insurance $P(IA)^{\,1}_{x:\overline n|}$ and add it to the benefit side before solving for $P$.
  • Portfolio percentile sign / $\sqrt N$ error. You need $\Pr[S>0]=\alpha$ small, forcing $E[L]<0$ (expected profit). Use $E[S]+\Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]}=0$ — mind the $\sqrt N$ inside $\mathrm{Var}[S]=N\,\mathrm{Var}[L]$. Also keep $\Pr[S<0]$ (profit, large) vs. $\Pr[S>0]$ (loss, small) straight.
  • Percentile-of-$L_0$ direction. $L_0$ decreases in $T_x$, so a high percentile of $L_0$ matches an early boundary lifetime and the survival probability ${}_hp_x$. Draw the graph every time.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

When a question says "net premium," what three things are you allowed to assume immediately?
(1) The equivalence principle is used; (2) expenses are ignored (even if stated); (3) hence $E[L_0]=0$, i.e. $\text{EPV(premiums)}=\text{EPV(benefits)}$. (Older exams called this the benefit premium.)
Why does the EPV of premiums use an annuity-due rather than an annuity-immediate?
Premiums are paid in advance (first one at issue), so a policyholder can't get coverage before paying. The advance pattern is exactly an annuity-due. (Bonus: the SULT tabulates $\ddot a$, not $a$.)
Write the net premium for (a) semi-continuous whole life and (b) an $n$-year deferred whole life annuity, and say over what period premiums are paid in (b).
(a) $P=\bar A_x/\ddot a_x$ — continuous death benefit, annual premiums. (b) $P={}_{n|}\ddot a_x/\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}$; premiums are payable only during the $n$-year deferral period (you don't pay while collecting).
Write the equivalence-principle net annual premium for an $n$-year endowment of 1 on $(x)$, in all three equivalent forms.
$P_{x:\overline{n}|} = \dfrac{A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}} = \dfrac{1}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}} - d = \dfrac{d\,A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{1-A_{x:\overline{n}|}}$, using $A_{x:\overline{n}|}=1-d\,\ddot a_{x:\overline{n}|}$.
For fully discrete whole life with annual net premium $P$, express $L_0$ as a linear function of $Z=v^{K_x+1}$ and state $\mathrm{Var}[L_0]$.
$L_0 = \big(b+\tfrac{P}{d}\big)v^{K_x+1} - \tfrac{P}{d}$, so $\mathrm{Var}[L_0] = \big(b+\tfrac{P}{d}\big)^2\big({}^2A_x - (A_x)^2\big)$. (Continuous: replace $d$ by $\delta$, $A$ by $\bar A$, $K_x+1$ by $T_x$.)
Which variance formula may you use with any premium, and which only with the equivalence premium?
General — works for any $P$: $\mathrm{Var}[L_0]=(b+\tfrac{P}{d})^2[{}^2A-A^2]$. Equivalence-only: $\mathrm{Var}[L_0]=b^2\dfrac{{}^2A-A^2}{(1-A)^2}$ (it bakes in the equivalence $P$). On the exam the general form is the safer default.
Why can you NOT use the shortcut $\mathrm{Var}[L_0]=(b+P/d)^2[{}^2A^{\,1}_{x:\overline n|}-(A^{\,1}_{x:\overline n|})^2]$ for a pure term insurance?
A term insurance's benefit PV is not a single random variable $Z$: survivors to time $n$ get nothing (PV 0) while deaths pay $v^{K+1}$, so $L_0$ is not affine in one $Z$ and the linear-in-$Z$ derivation collapses. Compute $\mathrm{Var}=E[L_0^2]-(E[L_0])^2$ outcome by outcome.
Does the word "gross premium" imply the equivalence principle? How can you tell from a computed $E[L_0^g]$?
No — unlike "net premium," "gross premium" carries no equivalence assumption (insurers often load for profit). Only assume it if the question says "gross premium calculated using the equivalence principle." If $E[L_0^g]\neq 0$, the equivalence principle was not used.
In the gross-loss variance $\mathrm{Var}[L_0^g]=(b+E+\tfrac{G-e_r}{d})^2[{}^2A_x-(A_x)^2]$, why does the initial expense $e_i$ not appear?
$L_0^g$ captures only future (renewal) expenses; $e_i$ is known and incurred at issue, i.e. a constant — and a constant has zero variance. (Settlement $E$ does appear, riding on the benefit as $b+E$.)
An insurer's renewal per-premium expense is 6% and first-year is 50%, with $\ddot a_x=12$. What is the EPV (as a multiple of $G$) of all per-premium expenses?
Renewal 6% every year: $0.06G\ddot a_x=0.72G$. First-year excess $(0.50-0.06)=0.44$ once: $0.44G$. Total $=1.16G$.
A death benefit refunds all net premiums paid, without interest. How do you incorporate this when solving for the net premium $P$?
The refund is an extra benefit equal to an arithmetically increasing term insurance: EPV $=P(IA)^{\,1}_{x:\overline n|}$ (plus, if applicable, level $n\cdot P\cdot {}_{n|}A_x$ after the pay period). Add it to the benefit side, then solve $P\,\ddot a = \text{death-benefit EPV} + \text{refund EPV}$ for $P$.
If quarterly premiums of $P$ are payable for life, what is the EPV of premiums, and what is the most common mistake?
EPV $= 4P\,\ddot a_x^{(4)}$. The classic error is writing $P\,\ddot a_x^{(4)}$ — forgetting that $P$ is the quarterly amount and must be multiplied by $m=4$ to annualize.
To find the 75th percentile of $L_0$ for fully continuous whole life, why do you set $0.75={}_hp_x$ (a survival probability)?
$L_0$ is decreasing in $T_x$, so $\{L_0\le \pi_{0.75}\}$ is the event $\{T_x > h\}$ for the boundary lifetime $h$. Thus $\Pr[L_0\le\pi_{0.75}]=\Pr[T_x>h]={}_hp_x=0.75$. Solve for $h$, then plug $T_x=h$ into $L_0$.
Distinguish $\Pr[S<0]$ from $\Pr[S>0]$ in the portfolio principle, and write the equation you actually solve.
$S$ is total loss: $S<0$ is profit (insurer wants this large, e.g. 95%); $S>0$ is loss (kept small, e.g. 5%); they are complements. With the Normal approximation you solve $E[S]+\Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[S]}=0$, where $E[S]=N\,E[L]$, $\mathrm{Var}[S]=N\,\mathrm{Var}[L]$.
In the portfolio percentile principle, what happens to the per-policy premium loading (above the equivalence premium) as $N\to\infty$, and why?
It shrinks to zero; the premium tends to the equivalence premium. The loading is $\propto \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\,\mathrm{SD}(L)/\sqrt N$, and the $\sqrt N$ drives it to 0 — diversification makes the relative risk of the average loss vanish.
Name the three standard ways to model substandard (extra-risk) mortality, and write the survival adjustment for each.
(a) Age rating: price at age $x+j$ — use $A_{x+j}$, $\ddot a_{x+j}$, ${}_tp_{x+j}$. (b) Constant addition to the force: $\mu^{\,*}=\mu+c$, giving ${}_tp^{\,*}_x = {}_tp_x\,e^{-ct}$ (acts like adding $c$ to $\delta$). (c) Constant multiple of the rate: $q^{\,*}=k\,q$, so ${}_tp^{\,*}_x=\prod(1-k\,q_{x+t})$. In every case the premium recipe $\text{EPV(benefit)}/\text{EPV(annuity)}$ is unchanged — only the basis differs.
Standard mortality is a constant force $\mu=0.03$; a substandard life adds $c=0.01$. For fully continuous whole life with $\delta=0.04$ and benefit $b$, what is the substandard net premium rate?
Rated force $\mu^{\,*}=0.04$. Then $\bar A^{\,*}_x=\dfrac{0.04}{0.04+0.04}=0.5$, $\bar a^{\,*}_x=\dfrac{1}{0.08}=12.5$, so $P=b\cdot\dfrac{0.5}{12.5}=0.04\,b = b\,\mu^{\,*}$. (Under a constant force the continuous whole-life net premium rate is just the benefit times the force.)
You are given that at a specific $T_x$ the gross loss equals a known dollar value, and asked for $E[L_0^g]$. Outline the two-step method and the check at the end.
Step 1 — back-solve $G$: write $L_0^g$ with $K_x+1=\lfloor T_x\rfloor+1$ a known integer, so every PV is a number; set it equal to the given loss and solve the (linear) equation for $G$. Step 2 — take the expectation: replace $v^{K_x+1}\to A_x$ and $\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|}\to\ddot a_x$ in $L_0^g$ and plug in $G$. Check: if $E[L_0^g]\neq 0$, the gross premium was not set by the equivalence principle (which you must not assume unless told). See Example 14.
State the two SOA default assumptions about expenses, and the consequence of the second for an $h$-pay policy.
(1) Expenses are payable at the start of each period (advance timing, on the annuity-due). (2) Percent-of-premium expenses are payable only while premiums are payable. Consequence: on an $h$-pay policy, commissions use $\ddot a_{x:\overline h|}$ and stop at year $h$, even though the benefit may run for life.
