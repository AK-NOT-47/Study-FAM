Life Tables and Fractional Ages

Every survival probability is a ratio of $l$ values — and between integer ages, one of two simple rules (UDD or constant force) fills in the gap. Add selection and you can read any select-and-ultimate table.

📖 AMLCR · Ch. 2–3 · SULT ⏱️ ~3 hrs 🔑 $l_x$ · $d_x$ · $K_x$ 🔑 UDD vs CFM 🔑 select & ultimate
Learning Objectives

After studying this note you should be able to:

  • Define and interpret the life table functions $l_x$, $d_x$, the radix $l_{x_0}$, and the limiting age $\omega$.
  • Compute survival and death probabilities — ${}_tp_x$, ${}_tq_x$, ${}_{u|t}q_x$ — directly from a life table, and in all three forms of the deferred-death probability ($p{-}p$, $q{-}q$, $p\times q$).
  • Build a life table from given $p_x$ / $q_x$ values, and recover missing $l_x$ entries — the workhorse technique for table problems.
  • Work with the curtate future lifetime $K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$: its pmf, distribution, and the curtate expectation $e_x$.
  • Use the Standard Ultimate Life Table (SULT) efficiently, including normal-approximation problems over non-homogeneous cohorts.
  • Apply the two standard fractional age assumptions to interpolate between integer ages — Uniform Distribution of Deaths (UDD) (linear interpolation) and Constant Force of Mortality (CFM) (exponential interpolation) — and know to split at integer boundaries when $s+t>1$.
  • Derive and apply formulas for ${}_sq_x$, ${}_sp_x$, $\mu_{x+s}$, $f_x(s)$, and the curtate/complete life expectancies (including the recursion $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5q_x + p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$) under each assumption.
  • Understand select and ultimate mortality, read a select life table in both layouts (issue-age rows and attained-age rows), use the $[x]+t$ notation, and identify when selection has worn off (the select period).

Key Concepts

1. The Life Table — Intuition First

A life table is just a bookkeeping device — a useful way to summarize a survival model, usually built from the mortality experience of a whole population (and often split into subgroups: male/female, smoker/non-smoker). Imagine a large cohort of $l_{x_0}$ newborns (or lives starting at some initial age $x_0$). The number $l_{x_0}$ is called the radix — an arbitrary positive scaling constant (commonly 100,000). As the cohort ages, people die. The table records:

  • $l_x$ = the expected number of survivors to exact age $x$.
  • ${}_nd_x$ = the expected number of deaths between exact ages $x$ and $x+n$. If $n=1$ the prefix is dropped, so $d_x$ means deaths in $[x,x+1)$.
  • $\omega$ = the limiting age — the age by which all lives are dead, i.e. the smallest age with $l_\omega = 0$.

Because everyone alive at age $x$ either survives to $x+1$ or dies in that year:

$$d_x = l_x - l_{x+1}.$$

The whole power of the life table is this: all survival probabilities are ratios of $l$ values. If you want the probability a life aged $x$ survives $t$ years, just count how many of the $l_x$ survivors are still alive at $x+t$:

$$ {}_tp_x = \frac{l_{x+t}}{l_x}.$$

This is a frequentist reading of probability — proportion of the cohort that survives. It connects to the continuous survival model via $l_{x} = l_{x_0}\,\cdot\,{}_{x-x_0}p_{x_0}$, i.e. $l_x \propto S_0(x)$, the survival function.

Reading the radix

The radix affects only the interpretation (how many lives we imagine), not the probabilities, which are always ratios. The $l_x$ values themselves need not be integers. On the FAM-L exam the symbol $\ell_x$ may also appear — it means the same thing as $l_x$.

Survival model basics — the continuous foundation

Before the table, there is the random variable. Let $(x)$ denote a life aged $x$ and $T_x$ its future lifetime — a continuous random variable, the time from now until $(x)$ dies. Two functions describe its distribution:

  • The survival function $S_x(t) = \Pr[T_x \gt t]$ — the probability $(x)$ survives at least $t$ years. (In actuarial notation this is ${}_tp_x$.)
  • The cumulative distribution function $F_x(t) = \Pr[T_x \le t]$ — the probability $(x)$ dies within $t$ years. (This is ${}_tq_x$.)

They are complements, and any $S_x$ is just a re-based version of the newborn's survival function $S_0$:

$$ S_x(t) = \frac{S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)}, \qquad F_x(t) = 1 - S_x(t). $$

The re-basing identity $S_x(t)=S_0(x+t)/S_0(x)$ comes straight from conditional probability: to be a life aged $x$ surviving $t$ more years, the newborn must reach $x+t$ given it reached $x$. This is the continuous twin of the life-table ratio ${}_tp_x = l_{x+t}/l_x$ — indeed $l_x \propto S_0(x)$.

Notation bridge — same idea, two languages

$S_x(t) = {}_tp_x$ and $F_x(t) = {}_tq_x$. Everything you do with $l$-ratios on a table you can also do in closed form whenever you are handed $S_0(x)$ — and the exam loves to hand you a closed-form $S_0(x)$ and ask for ${}_tp_x$, ${}_tq_x$, or a deferred probability (Example 9).

2. Probabilities From the Table

The three core symbols (with FAM/AMLCR meaning):

  • ${}_tp_x$ — probability $(x)$ survives at least $t$ years.
  • ${}_tq_x$ — probability $(x)$ dies within $t$ years. ${}_tq_x = 1 - {}_tp_x$.
  • ${}_{u|t}q_x$ — deferred mortality: probability $(x)$ survives $u$ years and then dies in the next $t$ years.

When $t=1$ the left subscript is dropped: $p_x = {}_1p_x$, $q_x = {}_1q_x$. The one-year rate $q_x$ is also called simply the mortality rate at age $x$.

Survival decomposes into one-year pieces

To survive $t$ years you must survive every year along the way, so ${}_tp_x$ is a product of one-year survival probabilities — the multiplicative law:

$$ {}_tp_x = p_x\cdot p_{x+1}\cdots p_{x+t-1}, \qquad {}_{t+u}p_x = {}_tp_x \cdot {}_up_{x+t}. $$

The second form can be split anywhere: ${}_5p_{20} = {}_2p_{20}\cdot{}_3p_{22} = {}_1p_{20}\cdot{}_4p_{21} = \dots$ — whichever cut matches the data you're given. This is the single most-used manipulation in FAM-L.

The deferred death probability — three forms

${}_{u|t}q_x = \Pr[u < T_x \le u+t]$ can be written three equivalent ways. Pick whichever matches the given quantities:

$p-p$ form $$ {}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x - {}_{u+t}p_x $$ $q-q$ form $$ {}_{u|t}q_x = {}_{u+t}q_x - {}_uq_x $$ $p\times q$ form $$ {}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x \cdot {}_tq_{x+u} $$

3. Building a Life Table From $p$ and $q$

Many exam problems hand you a few $p_x$ or $q_x$ values and ask for a probability. The clean technique: pick an arbitrary radix (say $l_x = 1{,}000$), then fill in survivors with $l_{x+1} = l_x\,p_x$. Once the $l$ column exists, every probability is just a ratio. Where a $q$ is given non-sequentially, back out the missing $l$ from $q_x = 1 - l_{x+1}/l_x$. This turns a tangle of $p$'s and $q$'s into a single tidy table (see Example 2).

4. Curtate Future Lifetime $K_x$

Alongside the continuous $T_x$ we use the curtate future lifetime $K_x$ — the number of complete future years lived: $K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$. If $(x)$ dies at age $21.4$ then $T_x = 1.4$ and $K_x = 1$. Its distribution:

$$ \Pr[K_x = k] = {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k} = {}_{k|}q_x, \qquad \Pr[K_x > k] = {}_{k+1}p_x, \qquad \Pr[K_x \le k] = {}_{k+1}q_x. $$

Summing the pmf gives the curtate expectation $e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{}_kp_x$. And the death-within-$n$-years probability is a sum of deferred one-year deaths:

$$ {}_nq_x = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} {}_{k|}q_x = q_x + {}_{1|}q_x + \cdots + {}_{n-1|}q_x. $$

5. The Standard Ultimate Life Table (SULT)

The SULT is a specific, exam-supplied table built from Makeham's law with $A = 0.00022$, $B = 2.7\times10^{-6}$, $c = 1.124$, with a radix of $l_{20} = 100{,}000$ and starting age 20. On the exam you are given the SULT; you do not derive it. It tabulates, at integer ages (typically 20–100):

  • $l_x$ and $q_x$ (sometimes $1000\,q_x$),
  • the complete and curtate expectations $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$, $e_x$,
  • annuity and insurance EPVs: $A_x$, ${}^2A_x$, $\ddot a_x$, and various term/endowment values at the standard interest rate $i = 0.05$.

Why it matters: the SULT lets you answer many problems by table lookup rather than computation. Master reading $l_x$ for ${}_tp_x = l_{x+t}/l_x$, and reading $\ddot a_x$, $A_x$ directly for insurance/annuity problems. Always confirm the interest rate (SULT uses 5%). Only the $l_x$ and $q_x$ columns are interest-free — the financial columns assume $i=0.05$.

6. Fractional Ages — The Problem

Life tables only give values at integer ages. But policies pay, and people die, at non-integer times. We need a rule to "fill in" the gap between age $x$ and age $x+1$. The two standard rules are UDD and constant force. In all that follows, take $0 \le s < 1$ and $0 \le s+t \le 1$, and let $q_x$ be the known integer-age value.

(a) Uniform Distribution of Deaths (UDD)

Intuition: spread the year's deaths evenly across the year. Equivalently, $l$ falls linearly between integer ages — so $l_{x+t}$ is a linear interpolation of $l_x$ and $l_{x+1}$:

$$ l_{x+t} = (1-t)\,l_x + t\,l_{x+1}, \qquad {}_tq_x = t\, q_x \quad(x\text{ integer}). $$

Why the weights tilt toward the nearer age: $l_{20.3} = 0.7\,l_{20} + 0.3\,l_{21}$ — more weight ($0.7$) on $l_{20}$ because $20.3$ is closer to $20$.

Consequences:

  • ${}_sp_x = 1 - s\,q_x$ (linear, decreasing).
  • The force of mortality increases through the year (denominator shrinks): $\mu_{x+s} = \dfrac{q_x}{1 - s\,q_x}$.
  • The future-lifetime density within the year is constant: $f_x(s) = q_x$ for $0 \le s < 1$.

(b) Constant Force of Mortality (CFM)

Intuition: assume the hazard rate $\mu$ is a constant $\mu^*$ throughout the year. Then survival is exponential within the year, and $l$ is exponentially interpolated:

$$ {}_sp_x = e^{-\mu^* s} = (p_x)^s, \qquad \mu^* = -\ln p_x, \qquad l_{x+t} = (l_x)^{1-t}\,(l_{x+1})^{t}. $$

Consequences:

  • ${}_sq_x = 1 - (p_x)^s$.
  • The force is flat within the year (by construction): $\mu_{x+s} = -\ln p_x = \mu^*$.
  • The within-year density is exponential: $f_x(s) = (p_x)^s\,\mu^*$, so slightly more deaths early in the year.
Mnemonic & the two-line strategy

UDD → "deaths uniform, force rising," linear interpolation, work with $q$'s.
CFM → "force flat, survival exponential ($p^s$)," exponential interpolation, work with $p$'s.

The universal method for any fractional-age probability

Express the probability in terms of $l$ values, then interpolate each $l$ (linearly for UDD, exponentially for CFM). This works regardless of whether $s+t\le 1$, so it never trips on integer boundaries. The shortcut formulas (next section) are faster but only valid inside one year of age.

7. Select and Ultimate Mortality

When someone buys a life insurance policy they are usually underwritten — screened for health (the selection process). Consider two people aged 50: Mike just bought a policy; Dave bought the same policy two years ago (selected at 48). Mike's health was just reviewed, so he meets today's criteria; Dave's health was last checked two years ago and may have drifted. So we expect $q$ for Dave to be higher: the beneficial effect of selection wears off with time.

  • $[x]$ denotes a life selected at age $x$ (just underwritten).
  • $[x]+t$ denotes a life selected at age $x$, now $t$ years later (current age $x+t$), still carrying the (fading) effect of selection.
  • The effect wears off after the select period of $n$ years. After that, mortality depends only on attained age — the ultimate portion.

All four examples $q_{[50]},\ q_{[49]+1},\ q_{[48]+2},\ q_{[41]+9}$ describe a life currently aged 50 — they differ only in the age at selection. Because selection wears off, the longer ago you were selected, the higher the rate: $q_{[x]} \le q_{[x-1]+1} \le q_{[x-2]+2} \le \cdots$. Once $t \ge n$, $q_{[x]+t} = q_{x+t}$ (ultimate).

A select life table is read across the row for the select period, then down the ultimate column once selection wears off. Survival probabilities still come from $l$ ratios, but you must track both the selection age and duration:

$$ {}_tp_{[x]+s} = \frac{l_{[x]+s+t}}{l_{[x]+s}}.$$

Once attained age $\ge x+n$, you use the ultimate $l_{x+t}$ values.

Two table layouts (the SOA notation note)

A select-and-ultimate table comes in one of two layouts, inferable from the headers:

  • Issue-age rows (headers $q_{[x]}, q_{[x]+1}, \dots, q_{x+n}$): each row is one age at selection. Read across then down.
  • Attained-age rows (headers $q_{[x]}, q_{[x-1]+1}, q_{[x-2]+2}, \dots, q_x$): every entry in a row is the same current age but a different selection age. Here you read down the diagonal until the ultimate column, then straight down.
You can never turn $[x]$ into $[x+1]$

The bracket is fixed at the age the life was selected — a life selected at $x$ is never "re-selected." So to move from the $[x]$ row to the $[x+1]$ row in an $l$ table you must route through the ultimate column: build forward from $[x]$ to an ultimate $l$, then divide backward by the $[x+1]$ select rates. See Example 4.

8. Moments / Expectations — how long does $(x)$ live?

The whole point of a survival model is to average over future lifetime. There are two future-lifetime variables, so two expectations:

  • Complete expectation $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \mathrm E[T_x]$ — average future lifetime measured to the exact instant of death (includes the fractional final year).
  • Curtate expectation $e_x = \mathrm E[K_x]$ — average number of complete future years (whole years only).

Each has a clean "survival-function" formula — no density needed, just sum/integrate the survival probability:

Complete (continuous) $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \mathrm E[T_x] = \int_0^\infty {}_tp_x\,dt, \qquad \mathrm E[T_x^2] = \int_0^\infty 2t\,{}_tp_x\,dt. $$ Curtate (discrete) $$ e_x = \mathrm E[K_x] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} {}_kp_x, \qquad \mathrm E[K_x^2] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (2k-1)\,{}_kp_x. $$

Both second-moment forms are the "$\int 2t\,S$" / "$\sum(2k-1)\,S$" tricks for a non-negative variable — they save you from ever touching the density $f_x(t)={}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}$. Variances then follow the usual way:

$$ \mathrm{Var}[T_x] = \mathrm E[T_x^2] - \big(\overset{\circ}{e}_x\big)^2, \qquad \mathrm{Var}[K_x] = \mathrm E[K_x^2] - \big(e_x\big)^2. $$
The two-line recipe for any moment problem

(i) Write ${}_tp_x$ (from a table, or from $S_0$). (ii) Plug into $\int_0^\infty{}_tp_x\,dt$ (mean) or $\int_0^\infty 2t\,{}_tp_x\,dt$ (second moment) — or the curtate sums. That's the entire machine. The $\overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + 0.5$ shortcut connects the two, but is exact only under UDD.

9. Temporary (limited) expectations

Often a contract only cares about the next $n$ years (a term annuity, an $n$-year benefit). The relevant variable is $\min(T_x,n)$ or $\min(K_x,n)$, capped at $n$. The expectations just truncate the integral / sum at $n$:

$n$-year temporary complete expectation $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \mathrm E[\min(T_x,n)] = \int_0^n {}_tp_x\,dt. $$ $n$-year temporary curtate expectation $$ e_{x:\overline{n}|} = \mathrm E[\min(K_x,n)] = \sum_{k=1}^{n} {}_kp_x. $$

The curtate sum literally adds up the first $n$ survival probabilities $p_x + {}_2p_x + \cdots + {}_np_x$ — on a life table this is just $\frac{1}{l_x}\big(l_{x+1}+l_{x+2}+\cdots+l_{x+n}\big)$ (Example 11).

10. Expectation recursions — stepping between ages

Both expectations split at any horizon $n$: live the first $n$ years, then (if you survive) carry on from age $x+n$:

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+n}, \qquad e_x = e_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,e_{x+n}. $$

Setting $n=1$ in the curtate version (using $e_{x:\overline{1}|}=p_x$) gives the single most-used recursion in this whole topic:

Curtate one-step recursion (memorize) $$ e_x = p_x\,(1 + e_{x+1}). $$

Read it: "survive the year (prob $p_x$); having done so you have lived 1 year plus whatever $(x+1)$ expects." It steps an $e$-column up or down one age at a time.

There is NO clean continuous analog $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$

The tidy $e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1})$ is a curtate-only result. The honest continuous recursion is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} + p_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$ with $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|}=\int_0^1{}_tp_x\,dt$. Only under UDD does $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|}=p_x+0.5\,q_x$ collapse it to $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5\,q_x + p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$.

Formulas

Life table relationships

$$ d_x = l_x - l_{x+1}, \qquad {}_tp_x = \frac{l_{x+t}}{l_x}, \qquad {}_tq_x = \frac{l_x - l_{x+t}}{l_x} = 1 - \frac{l_{x+t}}{l_x}. $$ $$ {}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x \cdot {}_tq_{x+u} = \frac{l_{x+u} - l_{x+u+t}}{l_x}, \qquad {}_{u|}q_x \equiv {}_{u|1}q_x = \frac{l_{x+u}-l_{x+u+1}}{l_x} = \frac{d_{x+u}}{l_x}. $$
Symbols

$l_x$ = expected survivors to age $x$; $d_x$ = expected deaths in $[x,x+1)$; $\omega$ = limiting age ($l_\omega=0$); ${}_tp_x$, ${}_tq_x$ = $t$-year survival / death probabilities for a life now aged $x$; ${}_{u|t}q_x$ = probability of surviving $u$ then dying within the next $t$; $K_x=\lfloor T_x\rfloor$ = curtate future lifetime; $\mu_{x}$ = force of mortality at exact age $x$.

Curtate lifetime

$$ \Pr[K_x = k] = {}_{k|}q_x = {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k}, \qquad \Pr[K_x > k] = {}_{k+1}p_x, \qquad e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{}_kp_x. $$ $$ \text{Caution: } {}_tq_x \ne q_x\,q_{x+1}\cdots q_{x+t-1}. \quad\text{Instead } {}_tq_x = 1-\prod_{k=0}^{t-1}(1-q_{x+k}). $$

Survival model (closed-form $S_0$)

$$ {}_tp_x = S_x(t) = \frac{S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)}, \qquad {}_tq_x = F_x(t) = 1 - \frac{S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)}, \qquad {}_{u|t}q_x = \frac{S_0(x+u) - S_0(x+u+t)}{S_0(x)}. $$

Moments and expectations

Complete (continuous) $T_x$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \int_0^\infty {}_tp_x\,dt, \qquad \mathrm E[T_x^2] = \int_0^\infty 2t\,{}_tp_x\,dt, \qquad \mathrm{Var}[T_x] = \mathrm E[T_x^2] - \big(\overset{\circ}{e}_x\big)^2. $$ Curtate (discrete) $K_x$ $$ e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} {}_kp_x, \qquad \mathrm E[K_x^2] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (2k-1)\,{}_kp_x, \qquad \mathrm{Var}[K_x] = \mathrm E[K_x^2] - \big(e_x\big)^2. $$ Temporary (limited to $n$ years) $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^n {}_tp_x\,dt, \qquad e_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} {}_kp_x. $$ Recursions $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+n}, \qquad e_x = e_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,e_{x+n}, \qquad e_x = p_x\,(1 + e_{x+1}). $$ Continuous ↔ curtate link (exact only under UDD) $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + \tfrac12, \qquad \overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5\,q_x + p_x\,(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})\ \text{(UDD)}. $$

Force of mortality and density (general)

$$ \mu_{x} = -\frac{1}{l_x}\frac{d\,l_x}{dx} = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln l_x, \qquad {}_tp_x = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^t \mu_{x+r}\,dr\right), \qquad f_x(t) = {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}. $$

Here $f_x(t)$ is the probability density of the future lifetime $T_x$ of $(x)$.

Symbol notes

$p_x = 1-q_x$; $s$, $u$ are fractional offsets within a single year of age. The shortcuts ${}_uq_{x+s}$ require the whole interval $[x+s,\,x+s+u]$ to lie within one year ($0\le s+u\le 1$); if $s+u>1$, split at the integer boundary (or just interpolate $l$ values). Under CFM the within-year survival depends only on the length $u$, not the start $s$ (memoryless), so ${}_uq_{x+s} = 1-(p_x)^u$.

Complete expectation of life under each assumption

Define the curtate expectation $e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} {}_kp_x$ (whole years only). The complete expectation $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \int_0^\infty {}_tp_x\,dt$ adds the average fractional year lived in the year of death. Writing $T_x = K_x + R_x$ (with $R_x$ the fraction lived in the death year), under UDD $R_x$ is uniform on $[0,1)$ and independent of $K_x$, so $\mathrm E[R_x]=\tfrac12$.

  • UDD (deaths uniform → on average die mid-year):
    $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = e_x + \tfrac{1}{2}, \qquad \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} = p_x + 0.5\,q_x, \qquad \overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5\,q_x + p_x\,(1 + \overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}). $$
    The "+½" is exact under UDD; the last identity is the UDD recursion — extremely handy for stepping between ages.
  • Constant force: there is no clean "+½"; integrate within each year:
    $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} {}_kp_x \int_0^1 (p_{x+k})^s\,ds = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} {}_kp_x\cdot \frac{1-p_{x+k}}{\mu^*_{x+k}}. $$
    For the contribution of a single year, the mean fraction lived is $\displaystyle \int_0^1 {}_sp_x\,ds = \frac{1-p_x}{-\ln p_x} = \frac{q_x}{\mu^*}$ under CFM, versus $\tfrac{1}{2}(1+p_x)$ under UDD.

Select and ultimate

$$ {}_tp_{[x]+s} = \frac{l_{[x]+s+t}}{l_{[x]+s}}, \qquad q_{[x]+t} = q_{x+t}\ \text{ for } t \ge n\ (\text{select period}), \qquad q_{[x]} \le q_{[x-1]+1}. $$
Symbols

$[x]+t$ = life selected at age $x$, now $t$ years past selection (attained age $x+t$); $n$ = length of select period; $l_{[x]+t}$ = select life table survivor count. Keep $[x]$ fixed: a life selected at $x$ is tracked as $[x]+t$ for all $t$, never $[x+1]$.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Probabilities straight from $l_x$ (with a deferred death) ▾

You are given the following extract:

$x$6061626364
$l_x$88,95087,40085,70083,82081,740

Compute (a) ${}_3p_{60}$, (b) ${}_2q_{61}$, (c) ${}_{2|1}q_{60}$ (survive 2 years, die in the 3rd).

Solution.

(a) $\displaystyle {}_3p_{60} = \frac{l_{63}}{l_{60}} = \frac{83{,}820}{88{,}950} = 0.94233.$

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  1. Inverting the $l$ ratio. ${}_tp_x = l_{x+t}/l_x$ — the older age is in the numerator. Writing $l_x/l_{x+t}$ gives a number $> 1$. Sanity check: a probability is $\le 1$.
  2. Confusing $d_x$ direction. $d_x = l_x - l_{x+1}$ (current minus next), never the reverse. The deferred-death formula ${}_{u|}q_x = d_{x+u}/l_x$ uses the deaths at the attained age $x+u$, divided by the original $l_x$ — not $l_{x+u}$.
  3. Multiplying $q$'s. ${}_tq_x \ne q_x\,q_{x+1}\cdots$. The right route is through the $p$'s: ${}_tq_x = 1 - \prod(1-q_{x+k})$, or sum the deferred deaths $\sum_{k=0}^{t-1}{}_{k|}q_x$.
  4. UDD force vs CFM force. Under UDD the force $\mu_{x+s} = q_x/(1-s q_x)$ increases with $s$; under CFM it is constant $=-\ln p_x$. Also: under UDD the density is constant ($f_x(s)=q_x$), while under CFM the force is constant — opposite functions are flat.
  5. Using $s q_x$ for a sub-interval not starting at the integer. ${}_uq_{x+s} = \dfrac{u\,q_x}{1-s q_x}$ under UDD (note the denominator), not $u\,q_x$.
  6. Crossing the integer boundary. The shortcut formulas need the whole interval inside one year of age ($s+u\le1$). When $s+u>1$, split at the integer or — safer — express in $l$'s and interpolate. (Example 4.)
  7. Wrong interpolation for the assumption. UDD uses linear interpolation of $l$; CFM uses exponential ($l_{x+t}=l_x^{1-t}l_{x+1}^t$). Mixing them up is a classic slip — and watch for problems that put UDD on some years and CFM on others.
  8. CFM is memoryless in length, UDD is not. Under CFM ${}_uq_{x+s}$ depends only on $u$; under UDD it depends on both $s$ and $u$.
  9. "+½" rule is UDD-only. $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = e_x + \tfrac12$ holds exactly only under UDD. Under constant force it is an approximation (within-year mean lived is $q_x/\mu^*$, not exactly ½).
  10. Select tables: read across, then down. Advance along the row for the whole select period, then switch to the ultimate column at attained age $x+n$. Don't jump straight to ultimate at duration 0. And $q_{[x]+t} \le q_{x+t}$ — selection lowers mortality.
  11. Mixing select age and attained age. $[60]+2$ and $[62]$ are both attained age 62 but have different mortality. Track the bracket — and remember $[x]$ never becomes $[x+1]$; to change selection age you must route through the ultimate column.
  12. Diagonal vs across. If the table's headers are $q_{[x]}, q_{[x-1]+1}, \dots$ (attained-age rows), read down the diagonal to the ultimate column, not across. Check the headers first.
  13. Wrong interest rate / wrong table. The SULT is at $i = 0.05$. With a different interest rate you cannot use the tabulated $A_x$, $\ddot a_x$ — only the $l_x$ and $q_x$ columns are interest-free.
  14. Curtate $e_x$ sums from $k=1$, not $k=0$. $e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{}_kp_x$. Starting at $k=0$ adds ${}_0p_x = 1$ and inflates your answer by exactly 1. (The pmf sum $\sum_{k=0}^\infty k\cdot{}_{k|}q_x$ does start at 0 — but its $k=0$ term is zero, so it agrees.)
  15. No clean continuous recursion. $e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1})$ is curtate-only. There is no $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$. The correct continuous step is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} + p_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$, which only simplifies to $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5q_x + p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$ under UDD.
  16. "$+0.5$" is UDD-only. $\overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + 0.5$ is exact only under UDD (and a trapezoidal approximation otherwise). Don't apply it as if it were always exact.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write ${}_tq_x$ in terms of $l$ values, and give all three forms of ${}_{u|t}q_x$.
$\displaystyle {}_tq_x = \frac{l_x - l_{x+t}}{l_x} = 1 - \frac{l_{x+t}}{l_x}$. Deferred death: $p{-}p$ form ${}_up_x - {}_{u+t}p_x$; $q{-}q$ form ${}_{u+t}q_x - {}_uq_x$; $p\times q$ form ${}_up_x\cdot{}_tq_{x+u}$; and in $l$'s $\dfrac{l_{x+u}-l_{x+u+t}}{l_x}$.
You are given only $p_{20}=0.98$, $p_{21}=0.975$, ${}_3p_{21}=0.94$, $q_{23}=0.03$. How would you find ${}_3p_{20}$ cleanly, and what is it?
Build a table off radix $l_{20}=1000$: $l_{21}=980$, $l_{22}=955.5$, $l_{24}=921.2$, then back out $l_{23}=921.2/0.97=949.69$. Then ${}_3p_{20}=l_{23}/l_{20}=0.9497$.
What is $\Pr[K_x = k]$, and what does $K_x$ equal in terms of $T_x$? If a life aged 20 dies at 21.4, what are $T_{20}$ and $K_{20}$?
$\Pr[K_x=k]={}_kp_x\,q_{x+k}={}_{k|}q_x$ (survive $k$ years, die in the next). $K_x=\lfloor T_x\rfloor$. If death is at $21.4$: $T_{20}=1.4$, $K_{20}=\lfloor1.4\rfloor=1$.
Under UDD with $q_x = 0.10$, what is $\mu_{x+0.5}$? Under constant force with the same $q_x$?
UDD: $\mu_{x+0.5} = \dfrac{0.10}{1 - 0.5(0.10)} = \dfrac{0.10}{0.95} = 0.10526$. CFM: $\mu_{x+0.5} = -\ln(0.90) = 0.10536$ (constant at any $s$).
You need ${}_{0.8}q_{20.3}$ under UDD. Why can't you just write $\tfrac{0.8\,q_{20}}{1-0.3\,q_{20}}$, and what should you do instead?
Because $s+t=0.3+0.8=1.1>1$ — the interval crosses age 21, leaving the single year of age the shortcut requires. Instead express in $l$'s and interpolate: ${}_{0.8}q_{20.3}=\dfrac{l_{20.3}-l_{21.1}}{l_{20.3}}$ with each $l$ linearly interpolated. (With $l_{20}=80,l_{21}=76,l_{22}=71$ this gives $0.04188$.)
True/False: Under constant force, ${}_{0.4}q_{x+0.3}$ equals ${}_{0.4}q_{x}$ within the same year of age. Explain.
True. CFM within-year survival is exponential, hence memoryless: ${}_uq_{x+s} = 1 - (p_x)^u$ depends only on length $u=0.4$, not the start $s$. So ${}_{0.4}q_{x+0.3}={}_{0.4}q_{x}=1-(p_x)^{0.4}$. (False under UDD, where the denominator $1 - s q_x$ matters.)
Under UDD, $q_x=0.6$ and $\overset{\circ}{e}_x=10$. Find $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$ in one step.
Use $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = 0.5q_x + p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$: $10 = 0.3 + 0.4(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}) \Rightarrow \overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}=23.25$.
A select table has a 3-year select period. A life is selected at age 55. Are $q_{[55]+1}$, $q_{[55]+3}$, $q_{56}$ equal or different? Express $q_{[55]+3}$ as an ultimate rate.
Selection wears off at duration 3 (attained 58). $q_{[55]+1}$ (attained 56, still select) is generally lower than $q_{56}$ (just selected at 56) — they differ. $q_{[55]+3}$ is at attained 58 with duration $=$ select period, so $q_{[55]+3}=q_{58}$ (ultimate).
In a select $l$ table, why can't you compute $l_{[51]}$ directly from $l_{[50]}$, and what's the route?
A life selected at 50 is never re-selected at 51, so $[50]$ and $[51]$ are separate cohorts — there's no direct $p$ linking them. Route through the ultimate column: build forward from $l_{[50]}$ to an ultimate $l$ (e.g. $l_{52},l_{53}$), then divide backward by the $[51]$ select survival rates to climb up the $[51]$ row.
A select table's headers are $q_{[x]},\,q_{[x-1]+1},\,q_{[x-2]+2},\,q_x$. How do you read it to get ${}_kp_{[x]+1}$?
This is the attained-age layout: each row is one current age. Read down a diagonal (matching fixed selection age $[x]$ as duration grows) until you hit the ultimate column, then continue straight down. (Not across, which would mix selection ages.)
Write the survival-function (no-density) formulas for $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$, $\mathrm E[T_x^2]$, $e_x$, and $\mathrm E[K_x^2]$. Watch the lower limits.
$\displaystyle \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \int_0^\infty {}_tp_x\,dt$,   $\displaystyle \mathrm E[T_x^2] = \int_0^\infty 2t\,{}_tp_x\,dt$,   $\displaystyle e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{}_kp_x$ (starts at $k=1$!),   $\displaystyle \mathrm E[K_x^2] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(2k-1)\,{}_kp_x$. Then $\mathrm{Var} = \mathrm E[\cdot^2] - (\text{mean})^2$.
Given $S_0(x)=\left(\tfrac{50}{50+x}\right)^3$, you found $\overset{\circ}{e}_{20}=35$ and $\mathrm E[T_{20}^2]=4900$. What is $\mathrm{Var}[T_{20}]$? And what is $\overset{\circ}{e}_{20:\overline{15}|}$?
$\mathrm{Var}[T_{20}] = 4900 - 35^2 = 3675$. The temporary expectation truncates the integral at 15: $\overset{\circ}{e}_{20:\overline{15}|} = \int_0^{15}\left(\tfrac{70}{70+t}\right)^3 dt = \tfrac{70^3}{2}\left(\tfrac{1}{70^2}-\tfrac{1}{85^2}\right) = 35 - 23.737 = 11.263$.
$e_x = 70$ and $p_x = 0.99$ — find $e_{x+1}$. Why can't you write $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$ the same way?
Curtate recursion: $e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1}) \Rightarrow 70 = 0.99(1+e_{x+1}) \Rightarrow e_{x+1}=69.71$. The continuous form has no clean $p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$; the honest version is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|}+p_x\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$, simplifying to $0.5q_x + p_x(1+\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1})$ only under UDD.
