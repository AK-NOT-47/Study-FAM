Policy Values (Reserves)

Level premiums overshoot the cost of insurance early and fall short late; the policy value $\,_tV$ is the money the insurer must hold to bridge the gap. Master the prospective definition, the one-year recursion, and the FPT shortcut and you own most of the reserve questions on the exam.

📖 AMLCR · Ch. 7 ⏱️ ~5–6 hrs 🔑 prospective · retrospective · recursion 🔑 gross · expense reserve · variance 🔑 interim · FPT · modified · Thiele
Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic you should be able to:

  • Define the time-$t$ net future loss random variable ${}_tL$ (conditional on the policy being in force) and the net premium policy value ${}_tV = \mathrm{E}[{}_tL]$, and explain why an insurer must hold a reserve.
  • Compute net premium policy values using the prospective formula (EPV future benefits − EPV future net premiums) and the retrospective formula (AV past premiums − AV past benefits), and state precisely when they are equal.
  • Apply the two special (shortcut) formulas for whole life and endowment policy values — the annuity-ratio form and the insurance-ratio form.
  • Compute the variance of the time-$t$ future loss $\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL]$ for whole life and endowment insurance, and from basic principles for special (non-level) plans.
  • Apply the recursive relationship between successive policy values (net, gross, and expense versions), adapt it for annuities (annuity benefit = negative premium), and use the Fackler accumulation form.
  • Calculate gross (expense-loaded) premium policy values and the expense reserve ${}_tV^e = {}_tV^g-{}_tV^n$, decompose the gross premium $P^g=P^n+P^e$, and explain why the expense reserve is normally negative.
  • Compute interim (mid-year) reserves ${}_{t+s}V$ by the exact recursion (both directions) and by linear interpolation, using UDD where needed.
  • State and apply Thiele's differential equation for the continuous policy value, including a numerical Euler step.
  • Compute modified reserves in general ($\alpha,\beta$ valuation premiums) and the Full Preliminary Term (FPT) method as the special case, and explain why insurers use them (new-business strain).
  • Evaluate the effect of changing assumptions (interest, mortality, expenses) on the reserve, and handle policy alterations using the current policy value as the available asset.

Key Concepts

Why reserves exist

For most life insurance and annuity products, the premium is level but the expected cost of providing the benefit rises with age (mortality increases). At issue, under the equivalence principle, future premiums exactly fund future benefits:

$$ \text{EPV}_0(\text{future benefits}) = \text{EPV}_0(\text{future premiums}). $$

But after some years the insured is older, mortality is higher, and that balance breaks. For a policy still in force at time $t$,

$$ \text{EPV}_t(\text{future benefits}) > \text{EPV}_t(\text{future premiums}). $$

Future premiums alone are no longer enough. The shortfall the insurer must already be holding is the reserve (policy value) ${}_tV$, so that reserve plus future premiums funds future benefits:

$$ \text{EPV}_t(\text{future benefits}) = \text{EPV}_t(\text{future premiums}) + {}_tV \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; {}_tV = \text{EPV}_t(\text{future benefits}) - \text{EPV}_t(\text{future premiums}). $$

It is a balance-sheet liability of the insurer — money owed, on average, to the block of policyholders still alive.

The future loss random variable ${}_tL$

The policy value is the expected value of a random variable. Recall ${}_0L$ is the present-value-at-issue of (future benefits − future premiums). Generalize it to time $t$, conditional on the policy being in force (the insured alive) at time $t$:

$$ {}_tL = \text{PV}_t(\text{future benefits}) - \text{PV}_t(\text{future premiums}), \qquad {}_tV = \mathrm{E}[{}_tL \mid T_x > t]. $$
Coach's Remark — ${}_tL$ only exists for in-force policies

The time-$t$ future loss is only meaningful while the policy is still active at time $t$. Once a policy has expired there are no further cash flows, so ${}_tV$ is effectively a conditional expectation ($\,{}_tL \mid T_x>t$); we usually drop the condition and write it implicitly. Standard FAM terminology is policy value (AMLCR); older LTAM material says reserve. The two are used interchangeably here.

Cash-flow timing conventions at time $t$

When the valuation date $t$ coincides with a premium/benefit date, you must decide which cash flows are "future." Unless told otherwise, the reserve is taken "at the end of year $t$" and:

  • Death benefits and claim/settlement expenses at time $t$ are treated as past (already paid to year-$t$ deaths) — excluded.
  • Premiums and premium-date expenses at time $t$ are treated as future (they start year $t{+}1$) — included.
  • Endowment / pure-endowment maturity payments at time $t$ are future — included.
  • Annuity payments at time $t$ are ambiguous: state explicitly whether they are past or future for the case at hand.

Net vs. gross

  • Net premium policy value ${}_tV^n$ (or just ${}_tV$): ignores expenses entirely. Uses the net premium $P$ (equivalence principle, no expense loading) in both the premium leg and the valuation.
  • Gross premium policy value ${}_tV^g$: uses the actual gross premium $G$ and explicitly includes future expenses in the EPV of outgo. The gross premium need not come from the equivalence principle.

A heavily tested subtlety: a net premium reserve is computed with the net premium, not the gross premium, even if the contract actually charges a gross premium. If the problem says "net premium policy value," back out / use $P$, not $G$.

Premium basis vs. reserve basis

The mortality/interest assumptions used for the premium (premium basis) need not match those used for the reserve (reserve basis). On the exam, assume they are the same unless told otherwise.

Coach's Remark — why bases differ in practice

Reserve assumptions are often set by regulation and are deliberately conservative (e.g. lower interest). For pricing, insurers use more realistic assumptions tuned to their own block of business. Hence the reserve basis is frequently more cautious than the premium basis — which is also why a regulator-prescribed net premium reserve can run high and create new-business strain (the motivation for modified reserves later).

Prospective vs. retrospective

Two equivalent ways to look forward or backward:

  • Prospective (looking forward): $$ {}_tV = \text{EPV future benefits} - \text{EPV future net premiums.} $$
  • Retrospective (looking backward): $$ {}_tV = \text{AV of past premiums} - \text{AV of past benefit outgo (cost of insurance)}, $$ where "AV" is the actuarial accumulated value — accumulated with interest and survivorship (divide a time-0 EPV by ${}_tE_x$).
Key theorem — when prospective = retrospective

The prospective and retrospective policy values are equal if and only if (1) the same basis (interest and mortality) is used for the premium and the reserve, and (2) the premium was set by the equivalence principle. When the bases differ, use the prospective method — it is the definition.

Coach's Remark — when retrospective is the faster tool

Although the retrospective method is technically off-syllabus, it is fair game on multiple-choice and is often much faster when there are no past benefits to accumulate. Classic cases: a deferred insurance or deferred annuity during the deferral period (zero past benefits), or a plan whose benefit changes only after time $t$ — the retrospective view only sees the simpler early benefits.

The recursion — the most important computational tool

Over one policy year, the reserve at the start, plus the premium, earns interest; out of that the insurer pays expected death benefits, and what survives is shared among survivors as the next reserve. Derived from a cohort of $\ell_{x+t}$ identical policies (divide through by $\ell_{x+t}$), for a fully discrete insurance with death benefit $b$ at end of year:

$$ ({}_tV + P)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\, b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V. $$

Read it: (reserve brought forward + premium) accumulated one year = expected death outgo + expected reserve carried forward for survivors. This single equation underlies Fackler, Thiele (its continuous limit), interim reserves, and nearly every reserve exam question.

Expense reserve

The gross premium policy value splits as

$$ {}_tV^{g} = {}_tV^{n} + {}_tV^{e}, \qquad {}_tV^{e} = \text{EPV}_t(\text{future expenses}) - \text{EPV}_t(\text{future expense loadings}). $$

The gross premium itself splits $P^g = P^n + P^e$, where the expense loading $P^e = P^g - P^n$ is the level amount funding expenses. Because acquisition expenses are front-loaded, the level loading falls short of expenses in year 1 and exceeds them later, so the expense reserve starts negative and climbs toward 0. Consequently ${}_tV^n \ge {}_tV^g$ in general.

Interim (mid-year) reserves

For a non-integer time $t+s$ ($t$ integer, $0\lt s<1$), value with the same one-year recursion logic but accumulate/discount over the fractional period, and convert mortality with UDD ($\,_s q_{x+t}=s\,q_{x+t}$). You can step forward from ${}_tV$ or backward from ${}_{t+1}V$; both give the same answer. The exact method is the modern exam default; linear interpolation is a quick approximation.

Modified reserves (FPT)

Holding a full net premium reserve from day 1 strains a new insurer's surplus because of acquisition costs ("new-business strain"). U.S. regulators allow modified reserves: replace the level net premium with non-level valuation premiums — a lower first-year premium $\alpha$ and a higher renewal premium $\beta$ — so the reserve at the end of year 1 is lower (often 0), freeing cash for acquisition expenses. Modified reserves still ignore expenses (the adjustment is implicit, through the premiums).

The universal modified-reserve constraint

For any modified method, the time-0 EPV of the valuation premiums must equal the EPV of benefits, which equals the EPV of level net premiums:

$$ \text{EPV}_0(\text{modified premiums}) = \text{EPV}_0(\text{net premiums}) = \text{EPV}_0(\text{benefits}). $$

So $\alpha + \beta\,a_x = P\,\ddot a_x$ — one equation, two unknowns; the method fixes one of $\alpha,\beta$. On the exam a modified net premium is often called the valuation premium.

The Full Preliminary Term (FPT) method is the special case that treats the first year as one-year term insurance:

  • First-year valuation premium $\alpha = $ net premium for 1-year term on $(x)$, i.e. $\alpha = v\,q_x = A^{1}_{x:\overline{1|}}$ (the first-year cost of insurance), making ${}_1V^{FPT}=0$.
  • Renewal valuation premium $\beta = $ net premium for the same contract issued to $(x+1)$.

So FPT reserves for the original policy equal the net premium reserves of an identical policy issued one year later, shifted by a year. (For an $n$-year endowment, the "contract on $(x+1)$" is the $(n{-}1)$-year endowment.)

Thiele's differential equation (continuous)

The continuous-time analogue of the recursion. For a policy with continuous premium rate $P_t$, force of interest $\delta$, death benefit $S_t$ paid at the moment of death:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\,{}_tV = \delta\,{}_tV + P_t - \mu_{x+t}\,(S_t - {}_tV). $$

Interpretation: the reserve grows by interest ($\delta\,{}_tV$) plus premium inflow ($P_t$), and is depleted by the net cost of dying — the net amount at risk $(S_t - {}_tV)$ paid out at rate $\mu_{x+t}$.

Policy alterations & changing assumptions

When a policyholder alters a contract (converts to paid-up, reduces benefit, changes term), the insurer uses the current policy value as the available asset and re-applies the equivalence principle to the new contract. Changing the valuation assumptions — e.g. lowering the interest rate, or strengthening mortality — generally increases the reserve, because lower interest means premiums accumulate less and benefits are discounted less.

Formulas

Notation

$(x)$ is the issue age, $t$ the duration, $i$ the effective annual rate, $v=1/(1+i)$, $d=iv=1-v$, $\delta=\ln(1+i)$, $b$ the death benefit. ${}_tE_x=v^t\,{}_tp_x$ is the $t$-year pure endowment (the interest-plus-survivorship factor). A superscript ${}^2$ on $A$ (written ${}^2A$) denotes evaluation at double the force of interest, for variances.

Net premium (equivalence principle)

For a fully discrete whole life on $(x)$ with unit benefit:

$$ P_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot a_x} = \frac{1}{\ddot a_x} - d, \qquad d = iv = 1 - v. $$

Prospective net premium policy value

General contract:

$$ {}_tV = \text{EPV}_t(\text{future benefits}) - \text{EPV}_t(\text{future net premiums}). $$

Fully discrete whole life, unit benefit, issued at age $x$, valued at duration $t$:

$$ {}_tV_x = A_{x+t} - P_x\,\ddot a_{x+t}. $$

Special (shortcut) formulas — whole life

For a fully discrete whole life of $b$ on $(x)$ with an ordinary level premium and level benefit:

Annuity-ratio form $$ {}_tV = b\left(1 - \frac{\ddot a_{x+t}}{\ddot a_x}\right), $$ Insurance-ratio form $$ {}_tV = b\left(\frac{A_{x+t} - A_x}{1 - A_x}\right). $$

(They follow from $A_{x+t}=1-d\,\ddot a_{x+t}$ and $P_x=\tfrac{1}{\ddot a_x}-d$.) An equivalent premium-difference form: ${}_tV_x = (P_{x+t}-P_x)\,\ddot a_{x+t}$.

Special (shortcut) formulas — endowment & continuous

For a fully discrete $n$-year endowment of $b$ — use $n{-}t$ on the remaining-term symbols:

$$ {}_tV = b\left(1 - \frac{\ddot a_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}}}{\ddot a_{x:\overline{n|}}}\right) = b\left(\frac{A_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}} - A_{x:\overline{n|}}}{1 - A_{x:\overline{n|}}}\right). $$

For fully continuous versions, replace $A\!\to\!\bar A$, $\ddot a\!\to\!\bar a$, $d\!\to\!\delta$ throughout. These shortcuts only work for ordinary level whole life and endowment plans — for special (non-level) plans, revert to basic principles.

Retrospective net premium policy value

$$ {}_tV_x = \frac{P_x\,\ddot a_{x:\overline{t|}} - A^{1}_{x:\overline{t|}}}{{}_tE_x} = P_x\,\ddot s_{x:\overline{t|}} - {}_tk_x, $$

where dividing by ${}_tE_x=v^t\,{}_tp_x$ converts a time-0 EPV into an actuarial accumulated value at time $t$:

$$ \ddot s_{x:\overline{t|}} = \frac{\ddot a_{x:\overline{t|}}}{{}_tE_x}, \qquad {}_tk_x = \frac{A^{1}_{x:\overline{t|}}}{{}_tE_x}. $$

Boundary values (use these to anchor recursions)

The three boundary conditions
$$ {}_0V = 0 \;\;(\text{equiv. principle}), \qquad {}_nV = 0 \;\;(n\text{-year term}), \qquad {}_nV = \text{endowment benefit} \;\;(n\text{-year endowment}). $$

Recursion (fully discrete net premium reserve)

Death benefit $b$ at end of year, premium $P$ at start of year:

The reserve recursion — memorize this one
$$ \boxed{\,({}_tV + P)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V\,} $$

Solved forward for the next reserve:

$$ {}_{t+1}V = \frac{({}_tV + P)(1+i) - q_{x+t}\,b}{p_{x+t}}. $$

NAR (net-amount-at-risk) form — handy for solving for $q_{x+t}$:

$$ ({}_tV + P)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,(b - {}_{t+1}V) + {}_{t+1}V, \qquad \text{NAR} = b - {}_{t+1}V. $$

Recursion adapted for annuities

An annuity payment $B_t$ at time $t$ is a negative premium; set $b=0$ if there is no death benefit:

$$ ({}_tV + P_t - B_t)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V. $$

Gross premium reserve, expense reserve & their recursions

Gross premium $G$, premium-date expense $e$, settlement expense $E$ (added to the death benefit):

$$ {}_tV^{g} = \text{EPV}_t(\text{benefits}) + \text{EPV}_t(\text{expenses}) - G\cdot\text{EPV}_t(\text{premiums}). $$
Gross — benefits AND expenses $$ ({}_tV^{g} + G - e)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,(b+E) + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V^{g}, $$ Net — benefits only $$ ({}_tV + P)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V, $$ Expense — expenses only (gross minus net) $$ ({}_tV^{e} + P^e - e)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,E + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V^{e}. $$

Decomposition and a useful special case:

$$ {}_tV^{e} = {}_tV^{g} - {}_tV^{n}; \qquad \text{if expenses are a level }100e\%\text{ of premium, } {}_tV^{e}=0 \text{ and } {}_tV^{g}={}_tV^{n}. $$

Variance of the future loss ${}_tL$

Just shift the at-issue variance formula's age from $x$ to $x+t$. Fully continuous whole life / endowment with premium $P$:

$$ \mathrm{Var}[{}_tL] = \left(b + \frac{P}{\delta}\right)^{2}\mathrm{Var}[Z], \quad \mathrm{Var}[Z]= {}^2\bar A_{x+t} - (\bar A_{x+t})^2 \;\;(\text{WL}),\;\; {}^2\bar A_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}} - (\bar A_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}})^2\;(\text{end.}). $$

If the equivalence principle holds, the premium term collapses (denominator uses age $x$):

$$ \mathrm{Var}[{}_tL] = b^2\,\frac{{}^2\bar A_{x+t} - (\bar A_{x+t})^2}{(1-\bar A_x)^2}\;(\text{WL}), \qquad \mathrm{Var}[{}_tL] = b^2\,\frac{{}^2\bar A_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}} - (\bar A_{x+t:\overline{n-t|}})^2}{(1-\bar A_{x:\overline{n|}})^2}\;(\text{end.}). $$

For fully discrete insurance, drop the bars and use $d$ in place of $\delta$. For a gross loss, replace $b$ with $\big(b+E+\tfrac{G-e_r}{d}\big)$ (renewal expense $e_r$; the initial expense, being a known constant, does not enter the variance).

Interim (mid-year) reserves — exact & interpolation

For $t$ integer, $0\lt s<1$, death benefit $b$ at year-end. Step forward from ${}_tV$ (premium $P$ included at time $t$):

$$ ({}_tV + P)(1+i)^{s} = {}_s q_{x+t}\,b\,v^{\,1-s} + {}_s p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+s}V. $$

Step backward from ${}_{t+1}V$ (no premium at the interim point):

$$ ({}_{t+s}V)(1+i)^{1-s} = {}_{1-s} q_{x+t+s}\,b + {}_{1-s} p_{x+t+s}\,{}_{t+1}V. $$

Under UDD, ${}_s q_{x+t}=s\,q_{x+t}$ and ${}_{1-s}q_{x+t+s}=\dfrac{(1-s)\,q_{x+t}}{1-s\,q_{x+t}}$. Linear-interpolation approximation:

$$ {}_{t+s}V \approx ({}_tV + P)(1-s) + s\cdot{}_{t+1}V. $$

Fackler / actuarial accumulated value form

Rearranged using $p_{x+t}=1-q_{x+t}$ (end-of-year benefit):

$$ {}_{t+1}V = ({}_tV + P)\,u_{x+t} - b\,k_{x+t}, \qquad u_{x+t} = \frac{1+i}{p_{x+t}}, \quad k_{x+t} = \frac{(1+i)\,q_{x+t}}{p_{x+t}}. $$

Thiele's differential equation (continuous)

Thiele's ODE
$$ \boxed{\,\frac{d}{dt}\,{}_tV = \delta_t\,{}_tV + P_t - \mu_{x+t}\,(S_t - {}_tV)\,} $$
  • Boundary: at maturity $n$, ${}_nV=$ survival benefit (e.g. $0$ for term, $1$ per unit for an $n$-year endowment).
  • Euler step: $ {}_{t+h}V \approx {}_tV + h\big[\delta_t\,{}_tV + P_t - \mu_{x+t}(S_t - {}_tV)\big].$

Modified reserves — general & Full Preliminary Term

General constraint and prospective valuation (whole life of $b$, valuation premiums $\alpha$ year 1, $\beta$ thereafter):

$$ \alpha + \beta\,a_x = P\,\ddot a_x, \qquad {}_tV^{\text{mod}} = \text{EPV}_t(\text{benefits}) - \text{EPV}_t(\text{modified premiums}). $$

FPT is the special case $\alpha = v\,q_x = A^{1}_{x:\overline{1|}}$ (so ${}_0V^{FPT}={}_1V^{FPT}=0$), with $\beta=A_{x+1}/\ddot a_{x+1}$ (the renewal valuation premium for the plan on $(x+1)$). Then for $t\ge 1$:

$$ {}_tV^{FPT}_{x} = {}_{t-1}V^{n}_{x+1} = b\left(1 - \frac{\ddot a_{x+t}}{\ddot a_{x+1}}\right) \;\;(\text{whole life}), \qquad {}_0V^{FPT}={}_1V^{FPT}=0. $$

Policy alteration (asset = current reserve)

$$ {}_tV^{\text{(old, as asset)}} + \text{EPV(future altered premiums)} = \text{EPV(future altered benefits + expenses)}. $$

For a paid-up alteration (no future premiums) to reduced sum insured $S'$:

$$ S' = \frac{{}_tV}{A_{x+t}} \quad(\text{paid-up whole life, ignoring expenses}). $$

Worked Examples

Example 1 Prospective net premium reserve (whole life) ▾

A fully discrete whole life insurance of \$1 is issued to $(40)$. You are given $A_{40}=0.20$, $A_{50}=0.30$, and $i=0.05$ so $d=0.05/1.05=0.047619$. Find ${}_{10}V_{40}$.

Step 1 — annuities from the $A$'s. $\ddot a_{40}=(1-A_{40})/d=0.80/0.047619=16.8$ and $\ddot a_{50}=(1-A_{50})/d=0.70/0.047619=14.7$.

Step 2 — net premium. $P_{40}=A_{40}/\ddot a_{40}=0.20/16.8=0.0119048.$

Step 3 — prospective reserve.

$$ {}_{10}V_{40} = A_{50} - P_{40}\,\ddot a_{50} = 0.30 - 0.0119048(14.7) = 0.30 - 0.175 = 0.125. $$

Check — insurance-ratio shortcut: ${}_{10}V=(A_{50}-A_{40})/(1-A_{40})=(0.30-0.20)/0.80=0.125.$ They agree, because the data are internally consistent and the equivalence-principle premium was used.

Answer
${}_{10}V_{40} = 0.125$ per \$1 of insurance.
Example 2 Recursion from both ends — endowment reserves ▾

(150-81-94:3 adapted.) A fully discrete 3-year endowment of \$1{,}000 on $(x)$, $i=0.06$, net premium $P=332.51$, with $\ell_x=1000,\ \ell_{x+1}=900,\ \ell_{x+2}=810$. Find ${}_1V$ and ${}_2V$.

${}_1V$ — recurse from ${}_0V=0$. Here $q_x=(1000-900)/1000=0.10$, $p_x=0.90$.

$$ (0+332.51)(1.06) = 0.10(1000) + 0.90\,{}_1V \;\Rightarrow\; 352.46 = 100 + 0.90\,{}_1V \;\Rightarrow\; {}_1V = 280.51. $$

${}_2V$ — recurse from the boundary ${}_3V=1000$ (endowment maturity). The death and survival outgo at year-end both total \$1{,}000:

$$ ({}_2V + 332.51)(1.06) = 1000 \;\Rightarrow\; {}_2V = \frac{1000}{1.06} - 332.51 = 610.89. $$
Answer
${}_1V = \$280.51$, ${}_2V = \$610.89$. Anchoring the recursion at both ends (${}_0V=0$ and ${}_nV=$ maturity) is the key move.
Example 3 Annuity reserve recursion (negative premium) ▾

A fully discrete 10-year deferred whole life annuity-due of \$1{,}000 on $(x)$. Net premiums $X$ are paid during the deferral period only; $i=0.06$; $q_{x+9}=0.012$, $q_{x+10}=0.014$; ${}_9V=8{,}683.07$, ${}_{11}V=9{,}675.46$. No death benefit ($b=0$). Find $X$.

Recursion: $({}_tV + P_t - B_t)(1.06) = p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V$, with $B_t=0$ for $t\le 9$ and $B_t=1000$ for $t\ge 10$, $P_t=X$ for $t\le 9$ else $0$.

Step 1 — connect ${}_{10}V$ and ${}_{11}V$ (payment at 10, no premium):

$$ ({}_{10}V + 0 - 1000)(1.06) = (1-0.014)(9{,}675.46) \;\Rightarrow\; {}_{10}V = 10{,}000. $$

Step 2 — connect ${}_9V$ and ${}_{10}V$ (premium $X$, no payment at 9):

$$ (8{,}683.07 + X)(1.06) = (1-0.012)(10{,}000) \;\Rightarrow\; X = 637.70. $$
Answer
$X = \$637.70$. Treat each \$1{,}000 annuity payout as a negative premium and the standard recursion still works.
Example 4 Gross premium & expense reserve (SOA Sample 7.9) ▾

Fully discrete whole life of \$2{,}000 on $(45)$, gross premium by equivalence principle, SULT, $i=0.05$ so $\ddot a_{45}=17.8162$, $\ddot a_{55}=16.0599$, $A_{45}=0.15161$, $A_{55}=0.23524$. Expenses (start of year): first year 25% of premium + 1.5 per 1{,}000 + 30 per policy; renewal 5% + 0.5 per 1{,}000 + 10 per policy.

Step 1 — gross premium. EPV premiums = EPV benefits + EPV expenses:

$$ G(17.8162) = 2000(0.15161) + [0.05G(17.8162)+0.20G] + [0.5(2)(17.8162)+1.0(2)] + [10(17.8162)+20]. $$ $$ \Rightarrow G = 31.16. $$

Step 2 — net premium & loading. $P^n = 2000(0.15161)/17.8162 = 17.02$, so $P^e = 31.16 - 17.02 = 14.14$.

Step 3 — gross reserve at $t=10$. Future renewal expense at age 55 is $0.05G + 0.5(2)+10 = 12.558$ per year:

$$ {}_{10}V^{g} = 2000(0.23524) + [0.05(31.16)+0.5(2)+10](16.0599) - 31.16(16.0599) = 470.48 + 201.68 - 500.43 = 171.73. $$

Step 4 — expense reserve. Either ${}_{10}V^e = \text{EPV(exp)}-\text{EPV(loading)} = 201.68 - 14.14(16.0599) = 201.68 - 227.09 = -25.4$, or ${}_{10}V^e = {}_{10}V^g - {}_{10}V^n = 171.73 - 197.14 = -25.4$.

Answer
$G=\$31.16$, ${}_{10}V^g=\$171.73$, ${}_{10}V^e=-\$25.4$. The negative expense reserve reflects the heavy first-year expense (\$40.79) vs. the level loading (\$14.14) — "new-business strain."
Example 5 Solving for the gross premium via recursion (F14:13) ▾

Fully discrete whole life of \$100{,}000 on $(45)$. ${}_5V^g=5{,}500$, ${}_6V^g=7{,}100$, $q_{50}=0.009$, $i=0.05$, renewal expense $50 + 4\%$ of $G$, claim expense $E=200$. Find $G$.

Gross recursion linking years 5 and 6:

$$ [5500 + G - (50 + 0.04G)](1.05) = 0.009(100{,}000 + 200) + (1-0.009)(7100). $$ $$ (5450 + 0.96G)(1.05) = 901.8 + 7035.9 \;\Rightarrow\; 5722.5 + 1.008G = 7937.7 \;\Rightarrow\; G = 2{,}197.82. $$
Answer
$G = \$2{,}197.82$. Settlement expense rides on the death-benefit term $(b+E)$; premium-date expense $e$ rides with $G$ on the left.
Example 6 Interim reserve — exact (both directions) vs. interpolation ▾

(4-S86:16 adapted.) Fully discrete whole life of \$1{,}000 on $(x)$. ${}_{10}V=311.00$, ${}_{11}V=340.86$, $P=60$, $q_{x+10}=0.08$, $i=0.0609$, UDD. Find ${}_{10.75}V$.

Exact, forward from ${}_{10}V$ ($s=0.75$): under UDD ${}_{0.75}q_{x+10}=0.75(0.08)=0.06$, ${}_{0.75}p=0.94$.

Example 9 Variance of the future loss via recursion (150-S87:9) ▾

$$ \frac{\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL]}{\mathrm{Var}[{}_{t+1}L]} = \frac{0.33 - 0.51962^2}{0.30 - 0.5^2} = \frac{0.06}{0.05} = 1.2. $$

Example 11 A realized ${}_tL$ — the loss as a random variable (M-S05:1) ▾

For a fully discrete 3-year endowment of \$1{,}000 on $(x)$ you are given $i=0.10$, $\ddot a_{x:\overline{3|}}=2.70182$, and equivalence-principle premiums. Calculate ${}_1L$ given that $(x)$ dies in the second policy year.

This is not asking for ${}_1V=\mathrm{E}[{}_1L]$ — it asks for the actual value the random variable ${}_1L$ takes on one specific death path. ${}_tL$ is a random variable; ${}_tV$ is only its mean.

Step 1 — net premium. With $d=\tfrac{0.10}{1.10}$ and $A_{x:\overline{3|}}=1-d\,\ddot a_{x:\overline{3|}}$:

${}_1L = 629.88$ on this death path. Different death years give different ${}_1L$ values; averaging them (weighted by their conditional probabilities) returns ${}_1V$. Always read whether the question wants a realized ${}_tL$ or its expectation ${}_tV$.
Example 12 Retrospective shortcut on a changing-benefit whole life (SOA Sample 7.1) ▾

A special fully discrete whole life on $(40)$: death benefit \$50{,}000 in the first 20 years and \$100{,}000 thereafter; level net premiums of \$875 payable for 20 years; SULT, $i=0.05$. Given $\ddot a_{40:\overline{10|}}=8.0863$, $A_{40:\overline{10|}}=0.61494$, ${}_{10}E_{40}=0.60920$. Find ${}_{10}V$.

Why retrospective wins here. Prospectively at time 10 you would face the benefit change (50k through year 20, then 100k) — two pieces. Retrospectively you look only at the past, where the benefit is a flat \$50{,}000, so the change never enters the calculation.

AV of past premiums (premiums were paid all 10 years):

AV of past benefits — only the flat \$50{,}000 term cost through time 10, using $A^{1}_{40:\overline{10|}}=A_{40:\overline{10|}}-{}_{10}E_{40}$:

Accumulate the difference with interest and survivorship (divide by ${}_{10}E_{40}$):

$$ {}_{10}V = \frac{875\,\ddot a_{40:\overline{10|}} - 50{,}000\,A^{1}_{40:\overline{10|}}}{{}_{10}E_{40}} = \frac{7{,}075.51 - 287}{0.60920} = 11{,}143. $$
Answer
${}_{10}V \approx \$11{,}143$. The prospective route gives \$11{,}152 (small rounding gap) but forces you to split the future benefit at the change point. When the benefit changes only after $t$, look backward — the retrospective view sees only the simpler early benefits.
Example 13 Fully continuous reserve via $\bar a_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ (150-S98:17) ▾

A fully continuous 25-year, 15-pay endowment on $(35)$ with face \$1{,}000: $\mu_{35+t}=0.03$ for $t\ge0$, $\delta=0.05$, $1000\,\bar A^{1}_{35:\overline{25|}}=324.25$, $\bar a_{35:\overline{15|}}=8.7351$. Find the net premium reserve ${}_5V$.

Key tool. Under a force of mortality that is constant during the term, the term symbols factor cleanly — and (per the source's first-principles proof) this holds even if $\mu$ changes after the term:

$$ \bar a_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar a_x\,(1-{}_nE_x), \qquad \bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar A_x\,(1-{}_nE_x), $$

Step 1 — net premium (15-pay; benefit is the full 25-year endowment $=\bar A^{1}_{35:\overline{25|}}+{}_{25}E_{35}$):

$$ P=\frac{1000\,\bar A_{35:\overline{25|}}}{\bar a_{35:\overline{15|}}}=\frac{324.25 + 1000\,e^{-(0.08)(25)}}{8.7351}=\frac{324.25+135.34}{8.7351}=52.61. $$

Step 2 — reserve at time 5 (remaining: 20-year endowment benefit, 10 years of premiums). Apply the factoring with $\mu+\delta=0.08$, $\bar A_{40}^{\!*}=\tfrac{0.03}{0.08}=0.375$, $\bar a_{40}^{\!*}=\tfrac1{0.08}=12.5$, ${}_{20}E_{40}=e^{-1.6}=0.20190$, ${}_{10}E_{40}=e^{-0.8}=0.44933$:

$$ {}_5V = 1000\big[\bar A_{40}^{\!*}(1-{}_{20}E_{40})+{}_{20}E_{40}\big] - P\big[\bar a_{40}^{\!*}(1-{}_{10}E_{40})\big]. $$ $$ {}_5V = 1000\big[0.375(0.79810)+0.20190\big] - 52.61\big[12.5(0.55067)\big] = 501.19 - 362.16 = 139.03. $$
Answer
${}_5V \approx \$139.03$. The identities $\bar a_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ and $\bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar A_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ turn every continuous term symbol into a one-line constant-force evaluation — and the post-term force is irrelevant to the term piece.

Example 15 FPT-endowment renewal equation & the gross NAR rearrangement ▾

Part A — FPT for an endowment. A fully discrete 10-year endowment of \$1{,}000 on $(40)$, $i=0.05$ ($d=\tfrac{0.05}{1.05}=0.047619$). After the first year (treated as 1-year term), the contract is the 9-year endowment on $(41)$. You are given $\ddot a_{41:\overline{9|}}=7.50$ and $\ddot a_{45:\overline{5|}}=4.50$. Find the renewal valuation premium $\beta$ and ${}_5V^{FPT}$.

The FPT renewal premium for an endowment is fixed by equating EPV of renewal premiums to EPV of benefits on the shifted plan:

FPT-endowment renewal equation$$ \beta\,\ddot a_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}} = A_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}}. $$

With $A_{41:\overline{9|}}=1-d\,\ddot a_{41:\overline{9|}}=1-0.047619(7.50)=0.642857$:

$$ \beta = 1000\cdot\frac{A_{41:\overline{9|}}}{\ddot a_{41:\overline{9|}}}=1000\cdot\frac{0.642857}{7.50}=85.71. $$

Because ${}_1V^{FPT}=0$, the policy "restarts" at time 1, so ${}_5V^{FPT}={}_4V^{n}_{41}$ (duration 4 of the 9-year endowment on $(41)$):

$$ {}_5V^{FPT}={}_4V^{n}_{41}=1000\left(1-\frac{\ddot a_{45:\overline{5|}}}{\ddot a_{41:\overline{9|}}}\right)=1000\left(1-\frac{4.50}{7.50}\right)=400.00. $$

Part B — gross recursion in net-amount-at-risk form. Start from the gross recursion (premium-date expense $e$, settlement expense $E$, death benefit $b$):

$$ ({}_tV^{g}+G-e)(1+i)=q_{x+t}(b+E)+p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V^{g}. $$

Substitute $p_{x+t}=1-q_{x+t}$ on the right and collect the $q_{x+t}$ terms to expose the gross net amount at risk:

Gross recursion, NAR form$$ ({}_tV^{g}+G-e)(1+i)=q_{x+t}\big(b+E-{}_{t+1}V^{g}\big)+{}_{t+1}V^{g}, \qquad \text{NAR}^{g}=b+E-{}_{t+1}V^{g}. $$

So the settlement expense $E$ rides with the death benefit inside the amount at risk, exactly as in Example 5. This rearrangement is the fast way to solve for $G$, $q_{x+t}$, or ${}_{t+1}V^g$ when the others are known.

Answer
$\beta=\$85.71$, ${}_5V^{FPT}=\$400.00$; gross NAR $=b+E-{}_{t+1}V^{g}$. For an $n$-year endowment, FPT shifts the plan to the $(n{-}1)$-year endowment on $(x+1)$ and pins $\beta$ via $\beta\,\ddot a_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}}=A_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}}$.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Using the gross premium in a "net premium reserve." If asked for the net premium policy value, compute the net premium $P$ (no expenses) and use it both in the premium leg and the valuation — even if the contract charges a gross premium $G$.
  • Wrong cash-flow timing at duration $t$. By convention the time-$t$ benefit/claim expense is past (excluded); the time-$t$ premium and its expense are future (included); endowment maturities are future. Annuity payments at $t$ are ambiguous — decide explicitly.
  • Mixing premium and valuation bases, then using retrospective. Prospective = retrospective only when the bases match and an equivalence-principle premium was used. When bases differ, use prospective.
  • Dividing by $p_{x+t}$ vs. $q_{x+t}$ in the recursion. The survivor term is $p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V$; to solve forward you divide by $p_{x+t}$.
  • Forgetting boundary conditions. ${}_0V=0$; for $n$-year term ${}_nV=0$; for $n$-year endowment ${}_nV=$ maturity benefit. Anchoring the recursion at the right end is half the battle.
  • Annuity sign error. An annuity payment is a negative premium in the recursion: $({}_tV + P_t - B_t)(1+i)=\dots$. Set $b=0$ if there is no death benefit.
  • Sign of the expense reserve. Early on it is negative (acquisition cost not yet recovered) — don't "fix" a negative answer. Hence ${}_tV^n \ge {}_tV^g$. (Exception: level-percent-of-premium expenses give ${}_tV^e=0$.)
  • Initial expense in the variance. The initial expense is a known constant and does not appear in $\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL^g]$ — only the renewal expense $e_r$ does.
  • Closed-form $\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL]$ fails for non-level plans — build a probability table. The $\big(b+\tfrac{P}{d}\big)^2\mathrm{Var}[Z]$ (and its equivalence-principle) shortcut applies only to ordinary level whole life and endowment. For a special non-level benefit (e.g. a 3-year endowment with changing death benefit), enumerate every in-force outcome's ${}_tL$ with its conditional probability, then compute $\mathrm{E}[{}_tL]$ and $\mathrm{E}[{}_tL^2]$ from the table — don't force the formula.
  • Interim reserve — UDD direction. Stepping forward uses ${}_s q_{x+t}=s\,q$; stepping backward uses ${}_{1-s}q_{x+t+s}=\dfrac{(1-s)q}{1-s\,q}$. Don't reuse $s\,q$ for the backward step.
  • Modified reserves ignore expenses. Even if the question lists expenses, FPT/modified reserves disregard them — the expense allowance is implicit in the lowered first-year valuation premium. Also enforce $\alpha+\beta a_x = P\ddot a_x$.
  • FPT off-by-one. ${}_tV^{FPT}_x = {}_{t-1}V_{x+1}$, not ${}_tV_{x+1}$; and ${}_1V^{FPT}=0$, not ${}_0V$. For an $n$-year endowment, the shifted plan on $(x+1)$ is the $(n{-}1)$-year endowment.
  • Net amount at risk uses ${}_{t+1}V$. $\text{NAR}=b-{}_{t+1}V$ (the end-of-year reserve), since the death benefit and the surviving reserve both occur at year-end.
  • Direction of the interest assumption. Lowering the valuation interest rate raises the reserve (benefits discounted less, premiums accumulate less). Several candidates get this backwards.
  • Thiele's sign. It is $-\mu_{x+t}(S_t - {}_tV)$, minus: the reserve is reduced by paying the net amount at risk.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Define the time-$t$ net future loss ${}_tL$ and the net premium policy value. What conditioning is implied?
${}_tL = \text{PV}_t(\text{future benefits}) - \text{PV}_t(\text{future premiums})$, defined only for a policy in force at $t$. The policy value is ${}_tV = \mathrm{E}[{}_tL \mid T_x>t]$ — a conditional expectation, since the random variable is meaningful only while the insured is alive at time $t$.
State the prospective formula for a fully discrete whole life of \$1 on $(x)$ at duration $t$, and name every symbol.
${}_tV_x = A_{x+t} - P_x\,\ddot a_{x+t}$, where $A_{x+t}$ is the EPV of the future unit death benefit at age $x+t$, $\ddot a_{x+t}$ the EPV of a unit whole-life annuity-due, and $P_x=A_x/\ddot a_x$ the level net annual premium set at issue.
Give the two special (shortcut) formulas for the whole life reserve, and one restriction.
Annuity-ratio: ${}_tV=b\big(1-\ddot a_{x+t}/\ddot a_x\big)$. Insurance-ratio: ${}_tV=b\big((A_{x+t}-A_x)/(1-A_x)\big)$. They hold only for ordinary level whole life / endowment (for endowment, use $n{-}t$ on the remaining-term symbols); for special non-level plans, revert to basic principles.
At what times are the boundary values of the net premium reserve known, and what are they?
${}_0V=0$ (equivalence principle); for $n$-year term ${}_nV=0$; for $n$-year endowment ${}_nV=$ the endowment benefit. These anchor recursions from either end.
Write the one-year net premium recursion (end-of-year benefit $b$, start-of-year premium $P$) and solve it for ${}_{t+1}V$. What is the NAR form?
$({}_tV + P)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V$, so ${}_{t+1}V = \dfrac{({}_tV+P)(1+i) - q_{x+t}\,b}{p_{x+t}}$. NAR form: $({}_tV+P)(1+i)=q_{x+t}(b-{}_{t+1}V)+{}_{t+1}V$, with net amount at risk $b-{}_{t+1}V$.
How do you adapt the recursion for an annuity that pays $B_t$ at time $t$?
Treat the annuity payment as a negative premium: $({}_tV + P_t - B_t)(1+i) = q_{x+t}\,b + p_{x+t}\,{}_{t+1}V$. If there is no death benefit, $b=0$; if no premium at $t$, $P_t=0$.
Under what condition are the prospective and retrospective policy values equal? When is retrospective faster?
Equal iff the same basis is used for premium and reserve and the premium came from the equivalence principle. Retrospective is faster when there are no past benefits to accumulate — e.g. deferred insurance/annuity in the deferral period, or plans whose benefit only changes after time $t$.
Give the net, gross, and expense reserve recursions side by side. Which expenses appear in each?
Net (benefits only): $({}_tV+P)(1+i)=q\,b+p\,{}_{t+1}V$. Gross (benefits + expenses): $({}_tV^g+G-e)(1+i)=q(b+E)+p\,{}_{t+1}V^g$. Expense (expenses only): $({}_tV^e+P^e-e)(1+i)=q\,E+p\,{}_{t+1}V^e$, where $P^e=G-P$.
Is the early-duration expense reserve positive or negative, and why? When is it identically zero?
Negative. The level expense loading is less than the front-loaded acquisition expenses early on; the deficit is recovered by later loadings, so ${}_tV^e$ rises from negative toward 0. It is identically 0 (so ${}_tV^g={}_tV^n$) when expenses are a level percentage of premium.
Give the formula for $\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL]$ for fully discrete whole life under the equivalence principle. Why does the initial expense not appear in the gross-loss variance?
$\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL]=b^2\,\dfrac{{}^2A_{x+t}-(A_{x+t})^2}{(1-A_x)^2}$ (numerator at age $x+t$, denominator at $x$). The initial expense is a known constant incurred at issue; a constant has zero variance, so only the future (renewal) expense enters $\mathrm{Var}[{}_tL^g]$.
Compute an interim reserve ${}_{t+s}V$ both ways. What UDD factor does each direction use?
Forward: $({}_tV+P)(1+i)^{s}={}_sq_{x+t}\,b\,v^{1-s}+{}_sp_{x+t}\,{}_{t+s}V$ with ${}_sq_{x+t}=s\,q$. Backward: ${}_{t+s}V(1+i)^{1-s}={}_{1-s}q_{x+t+s}\,b+{}_{1-s}p_{x+t+s}\,{}_{t+1}V$ with ${}_{1-s}q_{x+t+s}=\frac{(1-s)q}{1-s\,q}$. Both give the same exact value; interpolation $\approx ({}_tV+P)(1-s)+s\,{}_{t+1}V$.
State Thiele's differential equation and identify the net amount at risk.
$\dfrac{d}{dt}{}_tV = \delta_t\,{}_tV + P_t - \mu_{x+t}(S_t - {}_tV)$. The net amount at risk is $S_t - {}_tV$: the cash beyond the reserve already held that the insurer must find at a death.
What constraint must every modified reserve satisfy, and how is FPT a special case? Do modified reserves include expenses?
$\alpha + \beta a_x = P\ddot a_x$, i.e. EPV(modified premiums) = EPV(net premiums) = EPV(benefits) at issue. FPT fixes $\alpha=v\,q_x$ (1-year term cost), forcing ${}_1V^{FPT}=0$, and $\beta=A_{x+1}/\ddot a_{x+1}$. Modified reserves ignore expenses — the allowance is implicit in the lower first-year valuation premium.
Why does FPT give ${}_tV^{FPT}_x = {}_{t-1}V^n_{x+1}$, and what is the whole-life shortcut?
Because ${}_1V^{FPT}=0$ the policy "restarts" at time 1 as whole life on $(x+1)$; hence the duration-$t$ FPT reserve equals the duration-$(t{-}1)$ net reserve of that plan. Shortcut: ${}_tV^{FPT}_x = b\big(1-\ddot a_{x+t}/\ddot a_{x+1}\big)$.
A 3-year endowment of \$1{,}000 has $i=0.10$, equivalence-principle premium \$279.21. If $(x)$ dies in year 2, what is the realized ${}_1L$, and how does it relate to ${}_1V$?
Death benefit \$1{,}000 is paid at time 2; the only future premium is the one at time 1 (future by convention). So ${}_1L=1000v-P=\tfrac{1000}{1.10}-279.21=629.88$. This is one realized value of the random variable ${}_1L$; ${}_1V=\mathrm{E}[{}_1L\mid T_x>1]$ is the probability-weighted average over all death/survival paths, not this single number.
On a whole life whose death benefit jumps from \$50{,}000 to \$100{,}000 after year 20, why is the retrospective method easier for ${}_{10}V$?
Prospectively at time 10 you must split the future benefit at the year-20 change point. Retrospectively you only accumulate the past, where the benefit is a flat \$50{,}000 — the change never enters. ${}_{10}V=\big(875\,\ddot a_{40:\overline{10|}}-50{,}000\,A^{1}_{40:\overline{10|}}\big)/{}_{10}E_{40}\approx\$11{,}143$. Use retrospective whenever the benefit changes only after the valuation date.
Under a constant in-term force $\mu$ with force of interest $\delta$, how do you evaluate $\bar a_{x:\overline{n|}}$ and $\bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n|}}$? Does a post-term change in $\mu$ matter?
$\bar a_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ and $\bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n|}}=\bar A_x(1-{}_nE_x)$, with $\bar a_x=\tfrac1{\mu+\delta}$, $\bar A_x=\tfrac{\mu}{\mu+\delta}$, ${}_nE_x=e^{-(\mu+\delta)n}$ all using the in-term $\mu$. A change in $\mu$ after the term does not affect the term symbols — the integral only runs over $[0,n]$ — so the factoring still holds.
Why can't you use the $\big(b+\tfrac{P}{d}\big)^2\mathrm{Var}[Z]$ formula for a special endowment with non-level death benefits? What do you do instead?
That closed form assumes a level benefit (and level premium); with changing benefits the future loss is not an affine function of a single $Z$, so it fails. Instead enumerate every in-force outcome — each death year and survival — with its ${}_tL$ and conditional probability, then compute $\mathrm{E}[{}_tL]$ and $\mathrm{E}[{}_tL^2]$ from the table and take $\mathrm{Var}=\mathrm{E}[{}_tL^2]-\mathrm{E}[{}_tL]^2$.
State the FPT renewal equation for an $n$-year endowment, and rearrange the gross recursion into net-amount-at-risk form.
FPT-endowment renewal premium: $\beta\,\ddot a_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}}=A_{x+1:\overline{n-1|}}$ (the shifted plan is the $(n{-}1)$-year endowment on $(x+1)$, since the first year is 1-year term). Gross recursion NAR form: substituting $p=1-q$ into $({}_tV^g+G-e)(1+i)=q(b+E)+p\,{}_{t+1}V^g$ gives $({}_tV^g+G-e)(1+i)=q\big(b+E-{}_{t+1}V^g\big)+{}_{t+1}V^g$, so the gross net amount at risk is $b+E-{}_{t+1}V^g$ — the settlement expense $E$ rides with the death benefit.
