Life Annuities

An annuity is just a bundle of pure endowments paid while alive — write down which payments occur, when, and with what survival probability, and every formula falls out. Every life annuity is the mirror image of a life insurance, so master one relationship — $Y=(1-Z)/d$ — and the rest is bookkeeping.

📖 AMLCR · Ch. 5 ⏱️ ~4–5 hrs 🔑 EPV · $\ddot{a}_x=(1-A_x)/d$ · Woolhouse · UDD $\alpha,\beta$ · variance · percentiles
Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic, the SOA expects you to be able to:

  • Define the present-value random variable $Y$ for each annuity (continuous, due, immediate, $m$-thly) and relate it to the insurance variable $Z$ via $Y=(1-Z)/\,(\text{interest factor})$.
  • Compute the expected present value (EPV) of life annuities in every form: whole life, temporary (n-year), deferred, certain-and-life (guaranteed), and varying (increasing/decreasing).
  • Distinguish annuity-due ($\ddot{a}_x$), annuity-immediate ($a_x$), continuous ($\bar{a}_x$), and $m$-thly ($\ddot{a}_x^{(m)}$) annuities, and rank them.
  • Apply the fundamental annuity–insurance relationship $\ddot{a}_x = \dfrac{1 - A_x}{d}$ and its continuous / $m$-thly analogs — and know that no such direct relationship exists for the annuity-immediate.
  • Use recursion formulas (annual and $m$-thly) to step values by age, and decompose whole life = temporary + deferred.
  • Compute the variance of $Y$ via $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\dfrac{{}^2A-(A)^2}{(\text{interest factor})^2}$, and know why $E[Y^2]\neq {}^2\bar a_x$.
  • Apply UDD ($\alpha(m),\beta(m)$) and Woolhouse approximations for $m$-thly and continuous annuities, and use Woolhouse to recover insurance factors and $\mathring e_x$.
  • Use the constant-force and uniform shortcuts; compute accumulated values.
  • Find percentiles and probabilities of the present-value random variable $Y$.

Key Concepts

What is a life annuity? (annuity-certain vs. life annuity)

A life annuity is a series of payments made contingent on survival: the annuitant (buyer) receives each payment only if alive at the payment date; the insurer makes the payments. Because future payments depend on survival, the present value of the stream is a random variable (it depends on the random future lifetime $T_x$ or curtate lifetime $K_x$), and we are usually interested in its expected value — the EPV.

Coach's Remark — the one distinction that keeps everything straight
  • Annuities-certain (Exam FM) account for interest only. When you discount a cash flow you use $v^t$ alone. Symbols: $\ddot a_{\overline n|},\ a_{\overline n|},\ \bar a_{\overline n|}$.
  • Life annuities (FAM) account for interest and survivorship. When you discount a cash flow you use $v^t\,{}_tp_x$. Symbols: $\ddot a_x,\ a_x,\ \bar a_x$.
  • The actuarial $\bar a_x$ is an expected present value; the FM $\bar a_{\overline t|}$ is a present value. Don't confuse $\bar a_{T_x}$ (a random PV) with $\bar a_x$ (its expectation over survivorship).

Contrast with insurance: insurance pays a benefit on death; an annuity pays while alive. They are two sides of the same coin — which is exactly why $\ddot{a}_x = (1 - A_x)/d$ exists.

Annuity-certain refresher (from FM — you'll reuse these inside life formulas)
$$\ddot a_{\overline n|}=\frac{1-v^n}{d},\qquad a_{\overline n|}=\frac{1-v^n}{i},\qquad \bar a_{\overline n|}=\int_0^n v^t\,dt=\frac{1-v^n}{\delta},\qquad \ddot a_{\overline n|}^{(m)}=\frac{1-v^n}{d^{(m)}}.$$

FAM defaults to the annuity-due because premiums are paid in advance and because due annuities have the cleanest tie to insurance (the $Y=(1-Z)/d$ identity).

Building intuition: a life annuity is a bundle of pure endowments

A whole life annuity-due paying \$1/year makes a payment at time $k$ (for $k=0,1,2,\dots$) only if $(x)$ survives to age $x+k$. The EPV of that single payment is a pure endowment $v^k\,{}_kp_x$. Summing:

$$\ddot{a}_x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^k \, {}_k p_x.$$
The universal recipe for ANY life annuity EPV

Sum (or integrate) over all possible payment times the product of three things:

  • the amount paid at that time,
  • the discount factor $v^t$ (interest), and
  • the probability the payment is made $\,{}_tp_x$ (survivorship).

If you forget a formula — or face a varying / non-standard annuity — fall back to this. It never fails, and it's the only safe approach for varying annuities and higher moments.

The present-value random variable $Y$ (relate it to $Z$)

For a continuous whole life annuity, $Y=\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}=\dfrac{1-v^{T_x}}{\delta}=\dfrac{1-Z}{\delta}$ where $Z=v^{T_x}$ is the insurance variable. For a whole life annuity-due, $Y=\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|}=\dfrac{1-v^{K_x+1}}{d}=\dfrac{1-Z}{d}$ with $Z=v^{K_x+1}$. Taking expectations gives the EPV identities; taking variances gives the variance identities. This single linear relationship $Y=(1-Z)/(\text{factor})$ is the engine behind every mean and variance formula on this page.

Due vs. immediate

  • Annuity-due $\ddot{a}_x$: payments at $0,1,2,\dots$ (start of year). The first payment is certain (time 0).
  • Annuity-immediate $a_x$: payments at $1,2,3,\dots$ (end of year). No time-0 payment.

The only difference is the time-0 payment, so $\ddot{a}_x = 1 + a_x$. For a temporary annuity the difference is the time-0 payment and the last-payment timing — see Formulas and Traps.

Coach's Remark — there is NO insurance relationship for the immediate annuity

The clean identity $A_x = 1 - d\,\ddot a_x$ only holds for the annuity-due. There is no direct $A_x$↔$a_x$ shortcut. If you must bridge, go through the due form: $A_x = 1-d\,\ddot a_x = 1-d(a_x+1)$. On the exam, always compute the due value first, then convert.

Continuous, temporary, deferred, guaranteed

  • Continuous $\bar a_x$ pays at a rate of \$1/year while alive: $\bar a_x=\int_0^\infty v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt$.
  • Temporary ($n$-year): payments only for at most $n$ years (alive and within term).
  • Deferred (${}_{u|}\ddot a_x$): no payments for $u$ years; payments begin at $x+u$ if alive.
  • Guaranteed / certain-and-life ($\ddot a_{\overline{x:\overline n|}}$): pays the first $n$ years no matter what, then continues for life if alive. "Certain period" and "guarantee period" are interchangeable SOA terms.

Clean decompositions: whole life $=$ temporary $+$ deferred, and a temporary annuity splits into shorter pieces:

$$\ddot{a}_x = \ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_{n|}\ddot{a}_x,\qquad \ddot a_{x:\overline n|}=\ddot a_{x:\overline m|}+{}_mE_x\,\ddot a_{x+m:\overline{n-m}|}.$$

The insurance–annuity relationship (the most important identity)

The annuity–insurance identity (one line, three flavours)
$$\ddot{a}_x = \frac{1 - A_x}{d},\qquad \bar a_x=\frac{1-\bar A_x}{\delta},\qquad \ddot a_x^{(m)}=\frac{1-A_x^{(m)}}{d^{(m)}}.$$

Same pattern for temporary annuities with endowment insurance: $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}=\dfrac{1-A_{x:\overline n|}}{d}$ (note: endowment, not term insurance — there is no direct term-insurance ↔ temporary-annuity link). Rearranged: $A_x=1-d\,\ddot a_x$.

Intuition from $1=\delta\bar a_x+\bar A_x$: \$1 invested now $=$ PV of continuous interest paid on \$1 while $(x)$ lives $+$ PV of returning \$1 at death.

Constant force & uniform shortcuts

Under a constant force of mortality $\mu$ (exponential lifetime), age does not matter, so $\bar a_{x+n}=\bar a_x$ and:

$$\bar a_x=\frac{1}{\mu+\delta},\qquad \bar a_{x:\overline n|}=\bar a_x\bigl(1-{}_nE_x\bigr)=\frac{1}{\mu+\delta}\Bigl(1-e^{-(\mu+\delta)n}\Bigr).$$

Under a uniform future lifetime ($\omega$ limiting age), don't memorize new annuity formulas — get $\bar A_x$ (or $\bar A_{x:\overline n|}$) from the uniform insurance formulas, then convert with $\bar a_x=(1-\bar A_x)/\delta$.

$m$-thly annuities, ordering & approximations

An $m$-thly annuity pays $1/m$ at the start (due) or end (immediate) of each $\tfrac1m$-year period, \$1/year total. Crucially, $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ is an annual factor: to value \$250/quarter, multiply $\ddot a_x^{(4)}$ by the annual rate $250\times4=1000$, never by 250.

Ordering of annuity values (memorize the chain)
$$a_x \;<\; a_x^{(m)} \;<\; \bar a_x \;<\; \ddot a_x^{(m)} \;<\; \ddot a_x.$$

Earlier average payment dates ⇒ less discounting ⇒ larger value. The due forms also collect more in the year of death (the annual due grabs the full \$1 up front; the quarterly due only collects the quarters before death).

Two approximations connect $m$-thly/continuous annuities to the annual due: UDD (exact under uniform deaths, uses $\alpha(m),\beta(m)$) and Woolhouse (a series approximation, uses $\mu_x+\delta$). Both appear on the SULT.

What the SULT hands you for free (at $i=5\%$)
  • For each age, the table directly tabulates $\ddot a_x$, $\ddot a_{x:\overline{10}|}$, and $\ddot a_{x:\overline{20}|}$ — so common temporary and deferred annuities come straight off the page (e.g. $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}=\ddot a_x-{}_{n|}\ddot a_x=\ddot a_x-{}_nE_x\,\ddot a_{x+n}$ when $n\neq 10,20$).
  • The last page of the SULT lists $i^{(m)},d^{(m)},i/i^{(m)},d/d^{(m)},\alpha(m),\beta(m)$ and the Woolhouse 3-term coefficients — all at $i=5\%$. If a problem is set at $i=5\%$, do not recompute these; read them off. (At any other $i$ you must compute $i^{(m)},d^{(m)},\alpha(m),\beta(m)$ yourself.)

Formulas

Symbols used throughout
  • $x$ = current age; $T_x$ = future lifetime (continuous); $K_x=\lfloor T_x\rfloor$ = curtate lifetime.
  • ${}_kp_x$ = prob. $(x)$ survives $k$ years; ${}_{k|}q_x={}_kp_x\,q_{x+k}$ = prob. death in year $k{+}1$.
  • $v=1/(1+i)$; $d=iv=1-v$; $\delta=\ln(1+i)$; $v^t=e^{-\delta t}$.
  • ${}_nE_x=v^n\,{}_np_x$ = $n$-year pure endowment factor; ${}_0E_x=1$, ${}_\infty E_x=0$.
  • $A_x,\bar A_x$ = whole life insurance EPV; $A_{x:\overline n|}$ = endowment insurance EPV.
  • $d^{(m)}=m(1-v^{1/m})$, $i^{(m)}=m((1+i)^{1/m}-1)$ = nominal discount / interest rates.
  • The "${}^2$" prefix = the quantity evaluated at double the force of interest ($v\to v^2$): for discrete, $d\to 2d-d^2$; for continuous, $\delta\to2\delta$.

Present-value random variables (write these before computing variance)

Whole life $$Y_{\bar a}=\frac{1-v^{T_x}}{\delta}=\frac{1-Z}{\delta},\qquad Y_{\ddot a}=\frac{1-v^{K_x+1}}{d}=\frac{1-Z}{d},\qquad Y_{a}=\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|}-1.$$ Temporary ($n$-year) $$Y=\begin{cases}\dfrac{1-v^{T_x}}{\delta}=\dfrac{1-Z}{\delta},&T_x\lt n\\[4pt]\dfrac{1-v^{n}}{\delta},&T_x\ge n\end{cases}\quad\text{(continuous)},\qquad Y=\begin{cases}\ddot a_{\overline{K_x+1}|},&K_x\lt n\\ \ddot a_{\overline n|},&K_x\ge n\end{cases}\ \text{(due)}.$$ Deferred ($n$-year) $$Y=\begin{cases}0,&T_x\lt n\\ \bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}-\bar a_{\overline n|},&T_x\ge n.\end{cases}$$ Certain-and-life ($n$-year guarantee) $$Y=\begin{cases}\bar a_{\overline n|},&T_x\lt n\\ \bar a_{\overline{T_x}|},&T_x\ge n.\end{cases}$$

Whole life annuities (EPV — three equivalent forms)

Annuity-due $$\ddot{a}_x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^k\,{}_kp_x = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \ddot a_{\overline{k+1}|}\,{}_{k|}q_x= \frac{1 - A_x}{d}.$$ Annuity-immediate $$a_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} v^k\,{}_kp_x = \ddot{a}_x - 1.$$ Continuous $$\bar{a}_x = \int_0^\infty v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt = \int_0^\infty \bar a_{\overline t|}\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt= \frac{1 - \bar{A}_x}{\delta}.$$

Temporary ($n$-year) annuities

$$\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v^k\,{}_kp_x = \frac{1 - A_{x:\overline{n}|}}{d}\quad(\text{endowment insurance in numerator}),$$ $$a_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} v^k\,{}_kp_x,\qquad \ddot a_{x:\overline n|}-a_{x:\overline n|}=1-{}_nE_x,$$ $$\bar{a}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^n v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt = \frac{1 - \bar{A}_{x:\overline{n}|}}{\delta}.$$

Deferred annuities ($u$-year deferred)

$$ {}_{u|}\ddot{a}_x = \sum_{k=u}^{\infty} v^k\,{}_kp_x = {}_uE_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+u} = \ddot{a}_x - \ddot{a}_{x:\overline{u}|},\qquad {}_{u|}\bar{a}_x = {}_uE_x\,\bar{a}_{x+u}.$$ For the immediate deferred annuity, payments start at $u+1$: ${}_{u|}a_x=\sum_{k=u+1}^\infty v^k\,{}_kp_x={}_uE_x\,a_{x+u}=a_x-a_{x:\overline u|}.$

Guaranteed (certain-and-life)

$$\ddot{a}_{\overline{x:\overline{n}|}} = \ddot{a}_{\overline{n}|} + {}_{n|}\ddot{a}_x = \ddot{a}_{\overline{n}|} + {}_nE_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+n},\qquad \bar a_{\overline{x:\overline n|}}=\bar a_{\overline n|}+{}_{n|}\bar a_x,\qquad a_{\overline{x:\overline n|}}=a_{\overline n|}+{}_{n|}a_x.$$

Recursions

Step from age $x$ to $x+1$
Whole life — due / immediate $$\ddot{a}_x = 1 + v\,p_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+1},\qquad a_x = v\,p_x\,(1+a_{x+1})=v\,p_x+v\,p_x\,a_{x+1}.$$ Temporary — due / immediate $$\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|} = 1 + v\,p_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+1:\overline{n-1}|},\qquad a_{x:\overline n|}=v\,p_x+v\,p_x\,a_{x+1:\overline{n-1}|}.$$

Intuition: the due annuity pays \$1 now; survive the year (prob. $p_x$, discount $v$) and you own the same annuity one age older.

Variance — and the $E[Y^2]\neq{}^2\bar a_x$ trap

Because $Y=(1-Z)/(\text{factor})$ is linear in $Z$, $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\operatorname{Var}(Z)/(\text{factor})^2$:

$$\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\frac{{}^{2}\bar A_x-(\bar A_x)^2}{\delta^2}\ \text{(cont.)},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(Y)=\frac{{}^{2}A_x-(A_x)^2}{d^2}\ \text{(due)},$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\frac{{}^{2}A_{x:\overline n|}-(A_{x:\overline n|})^2}{d^2}\ \text{(temporary, endowment ins.)}.$$ The annuity-immediate has the same variance as the due ($Y_a=Y_{\ddot a}-1$ just shifts by a constant).
How to get ${}^2A$ from a doubled-interest annuity

If a problem hands you $\,{}^2\bar a_x$ (or $\,{}^2\ddot a_x$), it is meaningless on its own — convert it to the second insurance moment, then use it:

$$\text{continuous: }{}^2\bar A_x=1-(2\delta)\,{}^2\bar a_x,\qquad\text{discrete: }{}^2A_x=1-(2d-d^2)\,{}^2\ddot a_x.$$

Then $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\dfrac{{}^2A-(A)^2}{(\text{factor})^2}$. Never compute $\operatorname{Var}(Y)$ as $\,{}^2\bar a_x-(\bar a_x)^2$ — that is the planted trap, because $E[Y^2]\neq{}^2\bar a_x$ (the density of $Y$ is ${}_tp_x\mu_{x+t}$, not ${}_tp_x$). For a true second moment from first principles use $E[Y^2]=\int_0^\infty(\bar a_{\overline t|})^2\,{}_tp_x\mu_{x+t}\,dt$.

Varying annuities (use the "amount × discount × survival" recipe)

Continuously increasing / decreasing (rate $=t$ or $n-t$) $$(\bar I\bar a)_x=\int_0^\infty t\,v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt,\quad (\bar I\bar a)_{x:\overline n|}=\int_0^n t\,v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt,\quad (\bar D\bar a)_{x:\overline n|}=\int_0^n (n-t)\,v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt.$$ Annually increasing / decreasing (due) $$(I\ddot a)_x=\sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+1)\,v^k\,{}_kp_x,\quad (I\ddot a)_{x:\overline n|}=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(k+1)v^k\,{}_kp_x,\quad (D\ddot a)_{x:\overline n|}=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(n-k)v^k\,{}_kp_x.$$ Increasing-annuity recursions (annual) $$(I\ddot a)_x=\ddot a_x+v\,p_x\,(I\ddot a)_{x+1},\qquad (I\ddot a)_{x:\overline n|}=\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}+v\,p_x\,(I\ddot a)_{x+1:\overline{n-1}|}.$$

For varying annuities the SOA only tests the EPV; higher moments would need first principles and a computer, so don't memorize variance formulas for them.

$m$-thly annuities — basics, conversions, recursion

$$\ddot{a}_x^{(m)} = \sum_{r=0}^{\infty}\tfrac1m\,v^{r/m}\,{}_{r/m}p_x=\frac{1 - A_x^{(m)}}{d^{(m)}},\qquad a_x^{(m)} = \ddot{a}_x^{(m)} - \frac{1}{m}.$$ $$\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}-a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}=\frac1m\bigl(1-{}_nE_x\bigr),\qquad A_x^{(m)}=1-d^{(m)}\ddot a_x^{(m)},\quad A_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}=1-d^{(m)}\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}.$$ Recursion (split off the part before age $x+n$) $$\ddot a_x^{(m)}=\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}+{}_nE_x\,\ddot a_{x+n}^{(m)};\qquad\text{with } n=\tfrac1m:\ \ \ddot a_x^{(m)}=\tfrac1m+v^{1/m}\,{}_{1/m}p_x\,\ddot a_{x+1/m}^{(m)}.$$

UDD approximation ($\alpha(m),\beta(m)$ — exact under uniform deaths)

$$\ddot a_x^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\,\ddot a_x-\beta(m),\quad \ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\,\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}-\beta(m)\bigl(1-{}_nE_x\bigr),\quad {}_{n|}\ddot a_x^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\,{}_{n|}\ddot a_x-\beta(m)\,{}_nE_x,$$ $$\text{where}\quad \alpha(m)=\frac{i\,d}{i^{(m)}d^{(m)}},\qquad \beta(m)=\frac{i-i^{(m)}}{i^{(m)}d^{(m)}}.$$ Useful sanity checks: $\alpha(m)\approx1$ and $\beta(m)\approx\dfrac{m-1}{2m}$. (At $i=0.05$, read $i^{(m)},d^{(m)},\alpha(m),\beta(m)$ straight off the SULT.)

Mnemonic: annuity$^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\times$(annuity without $m$) $-\ \beta(m)\times$[EPV of the beginning cash flow $-$ EPV of the ending cash flow]. Whole life: $\beta(m)({}_0E_x-{}_\infty E_x)=\beta(m)(1-0)$. Temporary: $\beta(m)(1-{}_nE_x)$. Deferred: $\beta(m)({}_nE_x-0)$.

Woolhouse approximation (and extracting insurance / $\mathring e_x$)

3-term Woolhouse ($m$-thly due) $$\ddot{a}_x^{(m)} \approx \ddot{a}_x - \frac{m-1}{2m} - \frac{m^2 - 1}{12 m^2}\big(\mu_x+\delta\big).$$ Continuous (let $m\to\infty$) $$\bar{a}_x \approx \ddot{a}_x - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{12}\big(\mu_x+\delta\big).$$ Temporary $m$-thly $$\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|}^{(m)} \approx \ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|} - \frac{m-1}{2m}\big(1 - {}_nE_x\big) - \frac{m^2-1}{12 m^2}\big[\mu_x+\delta - {}_nE_x(\mu_{x+n}+\delta)\big].$$ 2-term Woolhouse (drop the $\mu$ term) $$\ddot{a}_x^{(m)} \approx \ddot{a}_x - \frac{m-1}{2m}.$$
Three Woolhouse spin-offs worth knowing
  • Force of mortality from a life table (when $\mu_x$ isn't given): $\ \mu_x\approx-\tfrac12\bigl(\ln p_{x-1}+\ln p_x\bigr).$
  • Insurance factors: approximate $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ by Woolhouse, then $A_x^{(m)}=1-d^{(m)}\ddot a_x^{(m)}$.
  • Complete expectation at $i=0$: letting $m\to\infty$ with $\delta=0$, $\ \mathring e_x\approx e_x+\tfrac12-\tfrac1{12}\mu_x.$

Accumulated values

$$\ddot{s}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|}}{ {}_nE_x } = \frac{\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|}}{v^n\,{}_np_x}.$$ This is the value per survivor at time $n$ — it spreads the accumulated fund only over those still alive (divide by the pure endowment factor: discount and survival).

Percentiles & probabilities of $Y$

The $100p$-th percentile $y_p$ satisfies $\Pr[Y\le y_p]=p$. Since $Y$ increases with $T_x$ (more years alive ⇒ more payments ⇒ larger PV), you translate the percentile of $Y$ into a percentile of $T_x$:

$$\Pr[Y\le y_p]=p\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \Pr[T_x\le h]={}_hq_x=p,\quad\text{then}\quad y_p=\bar a_{\overline h|}=\frac{1-e^{-\delta h}}{\delta}.$$

Steps: (1) sketch $Y$ vs. $T_x$; (2) find the $T_x$ value $h$ that cuts off probability $p$ from solving ${}_hq_x=p$; (3) plug $h$ into the PV. (Note the direction: the $75$th percentile of $Y$ corresponds to the $75$th percentile of $T_x$, because $Y$ is increasing in $T_x$.)

Worked Examples

Example 1 Whole life annuity-due from $A_x$ ▾

You are given $A_x = 0.25$ and $i = 0.05$. Find $\ddot{a}_x$, $a_x$, and the EPV of a whole life annuity-due paying \$2{,}000 per year.

Solution. $d = i/(1+i) = 0.05/1.05 = 0.047619$.

$$\ddot{a}_x = \frac{1 - A_x}{d} = \frac{0.75}{0.047619} = 15.75,\qquad a_x = \ddot{a}_x - 1 = 14.75.$$

EPV $= 2000 \times 15.75 = 31{,}500$.

Answer
$\ddot{a}_x = 15.75$, $a_x = 14.75$, EPV $= \$31{,}500$.
Example 2 Deferred annuity via pure endowment ▾

A life aged 65 will receive \$10,000/year in advance, starting at age 70 (a 5-year deferred whole life annuity-due). Given $i=0.06$, ${}_5p_{65}=0.92$, $\ddot a_{70}=11.20$. Find the EPV.

$$ {}_{5|}\ddot{a}_{65} = v^5\,{}_5p_{65}\,\ddot{a}_{70}=0.747258 \times 0.92 \times 11.20 = 7.6990.$$

EPV $= 10{,}000 \times 7.6990 = 76{,}990$.

Answer
EPV $\approx \$76{,}990$.
Example 3 Variance of a whole life annuity-due ▾

Given $A_x = 0.30$, ${}^{2}A_x = 0.12$, $i = 0.05$. Find $\operatorname{Var}(Y)$ for a \$1/year annuity-due.

Solution. $d^2 = 0.047619^2 = 0.00226757$.

$$\operatorname{Var}(Y) = \frac{{}^{2}A_x - (A_x)^2}{d^2} = \frac{0.12 - 0.09}{0.00226757} = 13.229.$$
Answer
$\operatorname{Var}(Y) \approx 13.23$.
Example 4 From a doubled-interest annuity to $E[\bar a_{\overline{T}|}^2]$ ▾ (CAS4A-S92:11)

Given a continuous whole life annuity on $(x)$: $\bar a_x = 15$, ${}^2\bar a_x = 9$, $\delta = 0.05$. Determine $E[\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}^2]$.

Solution. First convert each annuity to its insurance moment:

$$\bar A_x = 1-\delta\bar a_x = 1-0.05(15) = 0.25,\qquad {}^2\bar A_x = 1-(2\delta)\,{}^2\bar a_x = 1-0.10(9) = 0.10.$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(Y) = \frac{{}^2\bar A_x-(\bar A_x)^2}{\delta^2} = \frac{0.10-0.0625}{0.0025} = 15.$$

Now use $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=E[Y^2]-(E[Y])^2$ with $E[Y]=\bar a_x=15$:

$$15 = E[\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}^2]-15^2 \ \Rightarrow\ E[\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}^2] = 15+225 = 240.$$
Answer
$E[\bar a_{\overline{T_x}|}^2] = 240$. Note we did not use the trap $\operatorname{Var}(Y)={}^2\bar a_x-(\bar a_x)^2 = 9-225$ (which is negative — a giveaway that it's wrong).
Example 5 Constant-force whole life & temporary annuity ▾

Given $\mu_{20+t}=0.03$ for all $t$ and $\delta=0.04$. Find $\bar a_{20}$ and $\bar a_{20:\overline 5|}$.

$$\bar a_{20}=\frac{1}{\mu+\delta}=\frac{1}{0.07}=14.286.$$ $$\bar a_{20:\overline 5|}=\bar a_{20}\bigl(1-{}_5E_{20}\bigr)=\frac{1}{0.07}\Bigl(1-e^{-0.07(5)}\Bigr)=14.286(1-0.70469)=4.219.$$
Answer
$\bar a_{20}=14.286$, $\bar a_{20:\overline 5|}=4.219$. (The product form $\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ works only under constant force.)
Example 6 Certain-and-life equivalence (CAS4A-S97:5) ▾

A retiree chooses between (a) a continuous life annuity of \$30,000/year, and (b) a continuous life annuity with a 5-year certain period of benefit $b$/year, with equal EPV. Given $\delta=0.02$, $\mu=0.07$. Find $b$.

Solution. EPV of (a): $30{,}000\,\bar a_x = 30{,}000\cdot\dfrac{1}{0.02+0.07}=333{,}333.33.$

EPV of (b): $b\,\bigl(\bar a_{\overline 5|}+{}_{5|}\bar a_x\bigr)$ where

Example 17 Uniform lifetime: convert insurance to annuity ($\omega=80$) ▾

Example 18 Geometric survival: the annuity-immediate as a geometric series (CAS4A-S99:18) ▾

Mike, aged 30, will receive \$5{,}000 annually for life beginning one year from today (a whole life annuity-immediate). You are given $p_x=w$ for all $x$, $i=0.10$, and the EPV is \$22{,}500. Find $w$.

Solution. $5{,}000\,a_{30}=22{,}500\Rightarrow a_{30}=4.5$. Since $p_x=w$ for all ages, ${}_tp_{30}=w^t$, so the immediate-annuity sum is a geometric series with first term $vw$ and common ratio $vw$:

$$a_{30}=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} v^k\,{}_kp_{30}=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(vw)^k=\frac{vw}{1-vw}.$$

Set equal to $4.5$ and solve:

$$\frac{vw}{1-vw}=4.5\ \Rightarrow\ vw=\frac{4.5}{5.5}=\frac{9}{11}\ \Rightarrow\ w=\frac{9}{11}(1.1)=0.9.$$
Answer
$w=0.9$. Whenever survival is geometric (${}_tp_x=w^t$), the annuity-immediate collapses to $a_x=\dfrac{vw}{1-vw}$ and the annuity-due to $\ddot a_x=1+a_x=\dfrac{1}{1-vw}$ — a fast shortcut worth recognizing.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • $\operatorname{Var}(Y)\neq {}^2\bar a_x-(\bar a_x)^2$. The single most-planted trap. $E[Y^2]\neq{}^2\bar a_x$ because the density of $Y$ is ${}_tp_x\mu_{x+t}$, not ${}_tp_x$. Convert ${}^2\bar a_x\to{}^2\bar A_x=1-2\delta\,{}^2\bar a_x$ first, then $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\dfrac{{}^2\bar A_x-(\bar A_x)^2}{\delta^2}$. (If your "variance" comes out negative — as $9-15^2$ would — you fell for it.)
  • Matching the denominator to the frequency. Discrete annual uses $d$, continuous uses $\delta$, $m$-thly uses $d^{(m)}$. Mixing them is the classic error.
  • Doubling the force of interest is different for discrete vs. continuous. Continuous: $\delta\to2\delta$. Discrete: $d\to 2d-d^2$ (equivalently $i\to 2i+i^2$, $v\to v^2$). Using $2d$ instead of $2d-d^2$ is wrong.
  • ${}^{2}A_x\neq (A_x)^2$. The "2" prefix means evaluate at double the force of interest, not algebraic squaring.
  • No insurance relationship for the immediate annuity. $A_x=1-d\,\ddot a_x$ holds only for the due. There is no direct $A_x\leftrightarrow a_x$ formula — always solve the due first.
  • Temporary uses endowment, not term, insurance. $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}=\dfrac{1-A_{x:\overline n|}}{d}$ uses the endowment insurance $A_{x:\overline n|}$. There is no clean link to term insurance.
  • Immediate temporary conversion. $a_{x:\overline n|}=\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}-\bigl(1-{}_nE_x\bigr)$: you remove the time-0 payment but add back the time-$n$ payment ${}_nE_x$. Forgetting ${}_nE_x$ is extremely common.
  • Deferred annuity uses $\ddot{a}_{x+u}$, not $\ddot{a}_x$. ${}_{u|}\ddot{a}_x = {}_uE_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+u}$. The immediate deferred starts at $u+1$, not $u$.
  • Guaranteed annuity double-counting. In $\ddot{a}_{\overline{x:\overline n|}} = \ddot{a}_{\overline n|} + {}_{n|}\ddot{a}_x$, the first term is the annuity-certain (no survival), the second the deferred life part. Don't add a temporary life annuity for the first $n$ years — the certain part already covers them unconditionally.
  • $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ is an annual factor. To value \$250/quarter, multiply $\ddot a_x^{(4)}$ by the annual rate $1000$, never by the per-period $250$.
  • Constant-force product form is constant-force only. $\bar a_{x:\overline n|}=\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ requires constant force (so $\bar a_{x+n}=\bar a_x$). Otherwise use $\bar a_{x:\overline n|}=\bar a_x-{}_nE_x\bar a_{x+n}$.
  • Woolhouse sign and force terms. All correction terms are subtracted; the third term uses $(\mu_x+\delta)$ — forces, not $i$ and not $q_x$. Drop only the third term for "2-term."
  • UDD $\alpha,\beta$ vs. Woolhouse first two terms. The UDD simple approximations $\alpha(m)\approx1$, $\beta(m)\approx\frac{m-1}{2m}$ reproduce Woolhouse's first two terms — handy as a check, but use the exact $\alpha(m),\beta(m)$ when UDD is requested.
  • Accumulated value conditioning. $\ddot{s}_{x:\overline n|} = \ddot{a}_{x:\overline n|}/{}_nE_x$ divides by the pure endowment (discount and survival) — value per survivor. Dividing by $v^n$ alone is wrong.
  • $\ddot{a}_x = 1 + a_x$ only for whole life. Temporary: $\ddot{a}_{x:\overline n|} = 1 + a_{x:\overline{n-1}|}$ (note $n-1$).
  • Percentile direction. $Y$ is increasing in $T_x$, so the $p$-th percentile of $Y$ maps to the $p$-th percentile of $T_x$ (not $1-p$). Solve ${}_hq_x=p$, then $y_p=\bar a_{\overline h|}$.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write the EPV of a whole life annuity-due as a sum of pure endowments, and state its relationship to $A_x$.
$\ddot{a}_x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^k\,{}_kp_x$, and $\ddot{a}_x = \dfrac{1 - A_x}{d}$. Each term $v^k\,{}_kp_x$ is the EPV of a \$1 pure endowment at time $k$.
Given $A_x = 0.20$ and $i = 0.06$, compute $\ddot{a}_x$.
$d = 0.06/1.06 = 0.056604$; $\ddot{a}_x = 0.80/0.056604 = 14.133$.
Why can't $\operatorname{Var}(Y)$ for an annuity be computed as ${}^2\bar a_x-(\bar a_x)^2$? What should you do instead?
Because $E[Y^2]\neq{}^2\bar a_x$ — the integrand ${}_tp_x$ is not the density of $Y$ (the density is ${}_tp_x\mu_{x+t}$). Instead use $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\dfrac{{}^2\bar A_x-(\bar A_x)^2}{\delta^2}$, getting ${}^2\bar A_x$ from $1-2\delta\,{}^2\bar a_x$.
Explain in one sentence why ${}^{2}A_x \neq (A_x)^2$.
${}^{2}A_x$ is the insurance EPV evaluated at double the force of interest ($v^2$), a genuinely different value, not the algebraic square of $A_x$.
When you double the force of interest, what happens to $\delta$ (continuous) and to $d$ (discrete)?
Continuous: $\delta\to2\delta$. Discrete: $v\to v^2$, equivalently $i\to 2i+i^2$ and $d\to 2d-d^2$.
Is there a clean insurance–annuity relationship for the annuity-immediate? How would you find $a_x$ from $A_x$?
No direct one. Compute the due value $\ddot a_x=(1-A_x)/d$, then $a_x=\ddot a_x-1$.
Survival is geometric: ${}_tp_x=w^t$ for all $t$. Give a closed form for $a_x$ (whole life annuity-immediate).
$a_x=\sum_{k=1}^\infty v^k w^k=\dfrac{vw}{1-vw}$ (geometric series, first term $vw$, ratio $vw$). Then $\ddot a_x=1+a_x=\dfrac{1}{1-vw}$.
A 3-year deferred whole life annuity-due on $(x)$ pays \$1/year. Write its EPV in terms of $\ddot{a}_{x+3}$.
${}_{3|}\ddot{a}_x = v^3\,{}_3p_x\,\ddot{a}_{x+3}={}_3E_x\,\ddot a_{x+3}$.
Under a constant force $\mu$ with force of interest $\delta$, give $\bar a_x$ and $\bar a_{x:\overline n|}$.
$\bar a_x=\dfrac{1}{\mu+\delta}$ and $\bar a_{x:\overline n|}=\dfrac{1}{\mu+\delta}\bigl(1-e^{-(\mu+\delta)n}\bigr)=\bar a_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ (product form valid only under constant force).
Rank $a_x,\ a_x^{(m)},\ \bar a_x,\ \ddot a_x^{(m)},\ \ddot a_x$ from smallest to largest, and say why.
$a_x\lt a_x^{(m)}<\bar a_x<\ddot a_x^{(m)}<\ddot a_x$. Earlier average payment dates mean less discounting (and due forms collect more in the year of death), so values rise as payments move earlier.
Write the UDD formulas for $\ddot a_x^{(m)}$ and $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}$, including $\alpha(m)$ and $\beta(m)$.
$\ddot a_x^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\ddot a_x-\beta(m)$ and $\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}^{(m)}=\alpha(m)\ddot a_{x:\overline n|}-\beta(m)(1-{}_nE_x)$, with $\alpha(m)=\dfrac{i\,d}{i^{(m)}d^{(m)}}\approx1$, $\beta(m)=\dfrac{i-i^{(m)}}{i^{(m)}d^{(m)}}\approx\dfrac{m-1}{2m}$.
State the 2-term Woolhouse approximation for $\bar{a}_x$ and what the 3-term version adds.
$\bar{a}_x \approx \ddot{a}_x - \tfrac{1}{2}$. The 3-term version adds $-\tfrac{1}{12}(\mu_x+\delta)$, correcting for force of mortality and interest.
If $\mu_x$ is not given but a life table is, how do you approximate it for Woolhouse?
$\mu_x\approx-\tfrac12\bigl(\ln p_{x-1}+\ln p_x\bigr)$ (constant force assumed over $[x-1,x+1]$).
How do you find the $p$-th percentile of $Y$ for a continuous whole life annuity?
$Y$ increases with $T_x$, so solve ${}_hq_x=p$ for $h$, then $y_p=\bar a_{\overline h|}=\dfrac{1-e^{-\delta h}}{\delta}$.
Write the EPV of a continuously increasing whole life annuity (rate $=t$) and of an annually increasing whole life annuity-due.
$(\bar I\bar a)_x=\int_0^\infty t\,v^t\,{}_tp_x\,dt$; $(I\ddot a)_x=\sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+1)v^k\,{}_kp_x=1+2vp_x+3v^2{}_2p_x+\cdots$, with recursion $(I\ddot a)_x=\ddot a_x+vp_x(I\ddot a)_{x+1}$.
Using Woolhouse at $i=0$, approximate the complete expectation of life $\mathring e_x$.
$\mathring e_x\approx e_x+\tfrac12-\tfrac1{12}\mu_x$ (from $\bar a_x\approx\ddot a_x-\tfrac12-\tfrac1{12}\mu_x$ with $\delta=0$, using $\bar a_x=\mathring e_x$ and $\ddot a_x=1+e_x$).
