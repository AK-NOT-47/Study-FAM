Life Insurance Present Values

Every quantity in this chapter flows from one random variable: the present value of the benefit, Z. Master Z and the rest is mechanical — moments, variances, varying benefits, percentiles, and the continuous ↔ discrete ↔ $1/m$-thly conversions all fall out of writing $Z$ correctly.

Learning Objectives

After mastering this topic you should be able to:

  • Define the present-value-of-benefit random variable $Z$ for each standard insurance and write its functional form in terms of $T_x$ or $K_x$.
  • Compute the Expected Present Value (EPV) — also the Actuarial Present Value (APV) or net single premium — denoted with the standard $A$-symbols, and scale to any benefit $b$.
  • Distinguish continuous ($\bar{A}_x$, moment of death), discrete ($A_x$, end of year of death), and $1/m$-thly ($A_x^{(m)}$, end of $1/m$-year) models.
  • Handle the building blocks: whole life, term, endowment, deferred whole life, deferred term, pure endowment, plus combinations and their covariances.
  • Compute variance via the "second-moment-at-double-force" rule, use recursions (level and increasing), and relate continuous ↔ discrete ↔ $1/m$-thly under UDD and via the claims-acceleration approximation.
  • Handle varying benefits: continuously / annually increasing $(\bar I\bar A,\,IA)$ and decreasing $(\bar D\bar A,\,DA)$ insurance, and non-level benefits where no $A$-notation exists.
  • Apply the special-case shortcuts under constant force of mortality and under a uniform (de Moivre) distribution.
  • Compute percentiles $z_p$ and probabilities $\Pr[Z\le z]$ using the $Z$-vs-$T_x$ graph method.

Key Concepts

1. The random variable comes first

Everything here starts from a single random variable: the present value, at issue, of whatever benefit the contract pays. Call it $Z$. Because the time of payment depends on when the life dies, $Z$ is a function of $T_x$ (continuous models) or $K_x$ (discrete models).

The EPV is just $E[Z]$. The variance of the loss is $\text{Var}(Z)$. If you can write $Z$ correctly, every quantity follows mechanically. Candidates who memorize $A$-formulas without understanding $Z$ get stuck the moment a problem is phrased differently.

The universal recipe for any EPV

Whether continuous, discrete, or $1/m$-thly, the EPV is the sum/integral over all possible payment times of the product of three pieces:

(i) the benefit amount · (ii) the discount factor to time 0 · (iii) the probability the benefit is paid at that time.

If you ever forget a formula, rebuild it from these three pieces. This is the single most useful habit in the chapter.

2. The three timing conventions

A death benefit of 1 paid on a life $(x)$:

ConventionSymbolBenefit paid at…$Z$
Continuous$\bar{A}_x$the moment of death, $T_x$$v^{T_x} = e^{-\delta T_x}$
Discrete (annual)$A_x$the end of the year of death, $K_x+1$$v^{K_x+1}$
$1/m$-thly$A_x^{(m)}$the end of the $1/m$-year of death$v^{K_x^{(m)}+1/m}$

Continuous is the "purest," but life tables are integer-age, so in practice we compute $A_x$ from the table and convert. The benefit is discounted from a later point as we move continuous → $1/m$-thly → annual, so:

$$\bar{A}_x \;\ge\; A_x^{(m)} \;\ge\; A_x \quad (\text{larger } m \Rightarrow \text{closer to } \bar{A}_x).$$

3. The building blocks

  • Whole life $A_x$: benefit of 1 whenever death occurs.
  • $n$-year term $A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|}$: benefit of 1 only if death occurs within $n$ years. The "1" over the $x$ marks the death benefit.
  • $n$-year pure endowment $A^{\;\;1}_{x:\overline{n}|}$ (= $\,_{n}E_x$): benefit of 1 at time $n$ only if the life survives. The "1" over the $n$ marks the survival trigger.
  • $n$-year endowment insurance $A_{x:\overline{n}|}$: pays 1 at death if within $n$ years, or 1 at time $n$ on survival. It is the sum of term + pure endowment.
  • $u$-year deferred whole life $\,_{u|}A_x$: coverage doesn't start until time $u$.
  • $u$-year deferred $m$-year term $\,_{u|m}A_x = \,_{u|}A^1_{x:\overline{m}|}$: an $m$-year term whose coverage starts only after $u$ years.
  • Varying benefit: increasing $(IA)$ or decreasing $(DA)$, continuous or annual — see §6.
The structural identities to never forget
$$A_{x:\overline{n}|} = A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + A^{\;\;1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + \,_{n}E_x,$$ $$A_x = A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + \,_{n|}A_x \quad(\text{whole life}=\text{term}+\text{deferred whole life}).$$

4. Scaling and non-level benefits

For a level benefit $b$ instead of 1, just scale: $E[Z]=b\,A_x$, and because $Z$ is scaled by $b$, the second moment scales by $b^2$: $E[Z^2]=b^2\,{}^2A_x$, so $\text{Var}(Z)=b^2\big[{}^2A_x-(A_x)^2\big]$.

When the benefit is not level, the "2" notation breaks

If $b_t$ varies with time it cannot be pulled out of the integral/sum, so there is no clean shortcut — fall back to first principles:

$$E[Z]=\int_0^\infty b_t\,e^{-\delta t}\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt,\qquad E[Z^2]=\int_0^\infty \big(b_t\,e^{-\delta t}\big)^2\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$

There is generally no actuarial symbol for the EPV of a non-level benefit; write it as $E[Z]$. And the second moment is not ${}^2(\text{symbol})$ — the pre-superscript 2 only means "recompute at $2\delta$," which equals the true second moment only when the benefit is level.

5. Variance via "double force of interest"

Because $Z$ is a power of $v$, the square of $Z$ is the same power of $v^2 = e^{-2\delta}$:

$$E[Z^2] = E[(v^{T})^2] = E[e^{-2\delta T}] = (\text{the same EPV but at force } 2\delta).$$

We write $^2A_x$ for "the whole-life EPV computed at force $2\delta$." Doubling the force is not doubling the rate:

$$\delta \to 2\delta, \qquad v \to v^2, \qquad i \to j = (1+i)^2 - 1 = 2i + i^2.$$

Then $\text{Var}(Z) = {}^{2}A_x - (A_x)^2$. The pre-superscript "2" always means "recompute at double force" — the workhorse of the whole chapter.

6. Effect of interest

Higher $i$ ⇒ stronger discounting ⇒ lower EPV for death benefits. As $i \to 0$, $v \to 1$ and $A_x \to 1$. As $i \to \infty$, $A_x \to 0$. The EPV is a decreasing function of $i$.

7. Pure endowment is the "life-contingent discount factor"

On Exam FM you discounted certain cash flows by $v^n$. Here cash flows are contingent on survival, so the discount factor also carries survivorship:

Life-contingent discount & accumulation $$_{n}E_x = v^n\,{}_np_x \quad(\text{discount}), \qquad \frac{1}{{}_nE_x} = \frac{1}{v^n\,{}_np_x}\quad(\text{accumulate}).$$ Splitting (chain) rule — extremely common with the SULT $$_{t+h}E_x = {}_tE_x \cdot {}_hE_{x+t}, \qquad {}_t^2E_x = v^t\cdot{}_tE_x = (v^t)^2\,{}_tp_x.$$

This is why $\,_{n|}A_x = {}_nE_x\,A_{x+n}$: discount the future coverage back through both interest and survival.

Formulas

Symbols

$\,_tp_x$ = prob. $(x)$ survives $t$ years; $\,_tq_x$ = prob. dies within $t$; $\mu_{x+t}$ = force of mortality; $f_{T_x}(t)=\,_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}$ = density of $T_x$; $\,_{k|}q_x = \,_kp_x\,q_{x+k}$ = prob. of dying in year $k+1$.

Continuous insurances (benefit at moment of death)

Whole life $$\bar{A}_x = E[v^{T_x}] = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$ $n$-year term $$\bar{A}^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^{n} e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$ $n$-year pure endowment (fixed-time payment) $$_{n}E_x = A^{\;\;1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = e^{-\delta n}\, {}_np_x = v^{n}\, {}_np_x.$$ $n$-year endowment insurance $$\bar{A}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \bar{A}^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_{n}E_x.$$ $u$-year deferred whole life $$_{u|}\bar{A}_x = \int_u^{\infty} e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt = {}_uE_x \cdot \bar{A}_{x+u} = \bar{A}_x - \bar{A}^{1}_{x:\overline{u}|}.$$

Discrete insurances (benefit at end of year of death)

Whole life $$A_x = E[v^{K_x+1}] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^{k+1}\, {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x.$$ $n$-year term $$A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x.$$ $n$-year endowment insurance $$A_{x:\overline{n}|} = A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_{n}E_x = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x \;+\; v^{n}\,{}_np_x.$$ $u$-year deferred whole life $$_{u|}A_x = {}_uE_x\cdot A_{x+u} = A_x - A^{1}_{x:\overline{u}|}.$$ $u$-year deferred $m$-year term ($\,_{u|m}A_x = \,_{u|}A^1_{x:\overline{m}|}$) $$_{u|m}A_x = \sum_{k=u}^{u+m-1} v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x = {}_uE_x \cdot A^{1}_{x+u:\overline{m}|}.$$
Two slick discrete shortcuts (great on the SULT)

1-year endowment is just $v$: a benefit of 1 is paid whether you live or die, so

$$A_{x:\overline{1}|} = v\,q_x + v\,p_x = v.$$

(If a problem hands you $A_{x:\overline{1}|}$, that is your $v$.) $n$-year term two ways from the SULT:

$$A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = A_x - {}_nE_x\,A_{x+n} = A_{x:\overline{n}|} - {}_nE_x.$$

3-year endowment with only two years of mortality: once you survive to the start of the last year, $1$ is paid regardless, so $q$ and $p$ in that last year collapse to 1:

$$A_{x:\overline{3}|} = v\,q_x + v^2\,{}_{1|}q_x + v^3\,{}_2p_x.$$

Varying benefit insurance

Continuously increasing whole life ($Z = T_x\,v^{T_x}$) $$(\bar I\bar A)_x = \int_0^{\infty} t\,e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$ Continuously increasing $n$-year term ($Z = T_x v^{T_x}$, $T_x<n$) $$(\bar I\bar A)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^{n} t\,e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$ Continuously decreasing $n$-year term ($Z = (n-T_x)v^{T_x}$, $T_x<n$) $$(\bar D\bar A)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^{n} (n-t)\,e^{-\delta t}\, {}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt.$$ Annually increasing whole life ($Z = (K_x+1)v^{K_x+1}$) $$(IA)_x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (k+1)\,v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x.$$ Annually increasing / decreasing $n$-year term $$(IA)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (k+1)\,v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x, \qquad (DA)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (n-k)\,v^{k+1}\, {}_{k|}q_x.$$
Increasing + Decreasing = level (note the $n$ vs $n+1$!)
$$(\bar I\bar A)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + (\bar D\bar A)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = n\,\bar{A}^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|}, \qquad (IA)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + (DA)^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = (n+1)\,A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|}.$$

The continuous benefits run $0\!\to\! n$ and $n\!\to\!0$ (sum $=n$); the annual benefits run $1\!\to\! n$ and $n\!\to\!1$ (sum $=n+1$). There is no decreasing whole life — a decreasing benefit must hit 0 and cannot decrease past it.

Variances (square the benefit ⇒ double the force, $1+j=(1+i)^2$)

$$\text{Var}(v^{T_x}) = {}^{2}\bar{A}_x - \left(\bar{A}_x\right)^2, \qquad \text{Var}(v^{K_x+1}) = {}^{2}A_x - \left(A_x\right)^2.$$ Endowment insurance (most-tested variance) $$\text{Var}\!\left(Z_{x:\overline{n}|}\right) = {}^{2}A_{x:\overline{n}|} - \left(A_{x:\overline{n}|}\right)^2,$$ where ${}^{2}A_{x:\overline{n}|} = {}^{2}A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}^{2}\!\left({}_nE_x\right)$ and ${}^{2}\!\left({}_nE_x\right) = v^{2n}\,{}_np_x$. Pure endowment — Bernoulli shortcut (Z takes only 2 values) $$\text{Var}\!\left({}_nE_x\right) = v^{2n}\,{}_np_x\,{}_nq_x = (v^n)^2\,{}_np_x\,(1-{}_np_x).$$
Combining insurances: watch the covariance

If $Z = Z_1 + Z_2$ then $\text{Var}(Z)=\text{Var}(Z_1)+\text{Var}(Z_2)+2\,\text{Cov}(Z_1,Z_2)$. When the two benefits never pay at the same time (e.g. term vs. its pure endowment, or coverage in disjoint time intervals), $Z_1Z_2=0$, so $E[Z_1Z_2]=0$ and

$$\text{Cov}(Z_1,Z_2) = -\,E[Z_1]\,E[Z_2].$$

That negative covariance is why endowment-insurance variance is smaller than the naïve sum of the two pieces' variances.

Recursions (move one age at a time)

The backward recursions — memorize these
$$A_x = v\,q_x + v\,p_x\,A_{x+1}, \qquad {}^2A_x = v^2 q_x + v^2 p_x\,{}^2A_{x+1}.$$

Read it as: "next year you either die (prob $q_x$, pay 1 discounted one year) or survive (prob $p_x$, now holding $A_{x+1}$, discounted one year)." At double force, every $v$ becomes $v^2$.

Term $$A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = v\,q_x + v\,p_x\,A^{1}_{x+1:\overline{n-1}|}.$$ Endowment $$A_{x:\overline{n}|} = v\,q_x + v\,p_x\,A_{x+1:\overline{n-1}|}, \qquad A_{x:\overline{1}|} = v.$$ Increasing whole life (two equivalent forms) $$(IA)_x = v\,q_x + v\,p_x\big(A_{x+1} + (IA)_{x+1}\big) = A_x + v\,p_x\,(IA)_{x+1}.$$ $1/m$-thly whole life $$A_x^{(m)} = A^{1\;(m)}_{x:\overline{1/m}|} + {}_{1/m}E_x\,A_{x+1/m}^{(m)} = v^{1/m}\,{}_{1/m}q_x + v^{1/m}\,{}_{1/m}p_x\,A_{x+1/m}^{(m)}.$$

$1/m$-thly insurance from first principles

Define the $1/m$-thly curtate lifetime $K_x^{(m)} = \tfrac1m\lfloor m\,T_x\rfloor$ (future lifetime rounded down to the nearest $1/m$). Its mass function is $\Pr[K_x^{(m)}=r] = {}_rp_x\,{}_{1/m}q_{x+r}$. Then the benefit is paid at $K_x^{(m)}+\tfrac1m$:

Whole life, $1/m$-thly ($Z = v^{K_x^{(m)}+1/m}$) $$A_x^{(m)} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} v^{(k+1)/m}\, {}_{\frac{k}{m}|}{}_{\frac1m}q_x.$$ $n$-year term, $1/m$-thly ($mn$ periods) $$A^{1\,(m)}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{mn-1} v^{(k+1)/m}\, {}_{\frac{k}{m}|}{}_{\frac1m}q_x.$$

Converting continuous / $1/m$-thly to discrete (under UDD)

Under UDD the death-benefit pieces scale by a constant factor:

$$\bar{A}_x = \frac{i}{\delta}\,A_x, \qquad A_x^{(m)} = \frac{i}{i^{(m)}}\,A_x, \qquad \,_{u|}\bar A_x = \frac{i}{\delta}\,{}_{u|}A_x,$$ and likewise for term: $\;\bar{A}^1_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{i}{\delta}A^1_{x:\overline{n}|}$, $\;A^{1(m)}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{i}{i^{(m)}}A^1_{x:\overline{n}|}$. At double force: $\;{}^2\bar A_x = \frac{i^2+2i}{2\delta}\,{}^2A_x$ (replace $i\to i^2+2i$, $\delta\to2\delta$).
Endowment insurance: convert only the death part

The pure endowment is already paid at a fixed time and is unchanged:

$$\bar{A}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{i}{\delta}\,A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_{n}E_x, \qquad A^{(m)}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{i}{i^{(m)}}\,A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_{n}E_x.$$

Here $i^{(m)} = m\left[(1+i)^{1/m}-1\right]$ and $\delta=\ln(1+i)$. Note $\frac{i}{\delta} > \frac{i}{i^{(m)}} > 1$, consistent with $\bar{A}_x \ge A_x^{(m)} \ge A_x$. And crucially $\;\bar A_{x:\overline n|}\ne\frac{i}{\delta}A_{x:\overline n|}$ — that is the trap.

The factor $i/i^{(m)}$ is just the general version of $i/\delta$: as $m\to\infty$, $i^{(m)}\to\delta$ and $A_x^{(m)}\to\bar A_x$. At $i=0.05$ the FAM-L table gives $i^{(m)},\,d^{(m)},\,i/i^{(m)},\,d/d^{(m)}$ directly; for any other $i$ use the all-in-one relation $(1+i)^t = \big(1+\tfrac{i^{(m)}}{m}\big)^{mt} = e^{\delta t} = v^{-t}$.

Claims-acceleration approximation (alternative to UDD)

A heuristic: a $1/m$-thly benefit is paid, on average, $\frac{m-1}{2m}$ of a year earlier than the annual benefit, so accumulate $A_x$ forward for that fraction of a year:

$$A_x^{(m)} \approx (1+i)^{\frac{m-1}{2m}}\,A_x, \qquad \bar A_x \approx (1+i)^{1/2}\,A_x \;\;(m\to\infty).$$

As with UDD, apply the factor to the death/term part only for endowment insurance: $A^{(m)}_{x:\overline n|}\approx (1+i)^{\frac{m-1}{2m}}A^1_{x:\overline n|} + {}_nE_x$.

Special cases — constant force of mortality

Master set ($\,_np_x=e^{-\mu n}$, age-independent) $$_{n}E_x = e^{-(\mu+\delta)n}, \qquad \bar A_x = \frac{\mu}{\mu+\delta}, \qquad {}^2\bar A_x = \frac{\mu}{\mu+2\delta},$$ $$\bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \bar A_x\big(1-{}_nE_x\big) = \frac{\mu}{\mu+\delta}\Big(1-e^{-(\mu+\delta)n}\Big), \qquad \bar A_{x:\overline{n}|} = \bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_nE_x.$$
$\bar A^1_{x:\overline n|}=\bar A_x(1-{}_nE_x)$ needs constant force

This shortcut works only because age is irrelevant under constant force, so $\bar A_{x+n}=\bar A_x$. For any other mortality, use the always-true $\bar A^1_{x:\overline n|} = \bar A_x - {}_nE_x\,\bar A_{x+n}$.

Special cases — uniform (de Moivre) distribution, $\omega$ limiting age

$$_{n}E_x = v^n\cdot\frac{\omega-x-n}{\omega-x}, \qquad \bar A_x = \frac{\bar a_{\overline{\omega-x}|}}{\omega-x} = \frac{1-v^{\omega-x}}{\delta(\omega-x)}, \qquad \bar A^{1}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{\bar a_{\overline{n}|}}{\omega-x} = \frac{1-v^{n}}{\delta(\omega-x)},$$ where $\bar a_{\overline{n}|} = \dfrac{1-v^n}{\delta}$ is the continuous certain annuity.

Link to annuities (cross-chapter, very common)

$$A_{x:\overline{n}|} = 1 - d\,\ddot{a}_{x:\overline{n}|}, \qquad A_x = 1 - d\,\ddot{a}_x, \qquad \bar{A}_x = 1 - \delta\,\bar{a}_x,$$ where $d = iv = 1 - v$ is the annual discount rate.

Percentiles & probabilities of $Z$ (the graph method)

The $100p$-th percentile of $Z$ is $z_p$ with $\Pr[Z\le z_p]=p$. Because $Z$ is a decreasing function of $T_x$ for a death benefit, "small $Z$" means "large $T_x$." The reliable procedure:

Three steps that never fail
  1. Graph $Z$ (vertical) against $T_x$ (horizontal) — note any jumps where the benefit changes.
  2. Identify the region of $T_x$ giving the target $Z$ values, and translate the percentile/probability condition into a statement about $T_x$.
  3. Solve for the threshold time(s), then plug back to get $z_p$ or evaluate the survival/death probability.

For a level whole life of $b$: $z_p = b\,v^{h}$ where $h$ solves $\,_hp_x = p$ (since $\Pr[Z\le z_p]=\Pr[T_x>h]=\,_hp_x$).

Worked Examples

Example 1 Whole life EPV & variance (constant force) ▾

A life aged $x$ has constant force $\mu = 0.02$ for all ages; $\delta = 0.05$; benefit 1 at the moment of death. Find $\bar{A}_x$, $\text{Var}(v^{T_x})$, and the SD.

Solution. With constant force, $\,_tp_x = e^{-\mu t}$ and density $\mu e^{-\mu t}$:

$$\bar{A}_x = \int_0^\infty e^{-\delta t}\,\mu e^{-\mu t}\,dt = \frac{\mu}{\mu+\delta} = \frac{0.02}{0.07} = 0.285714.$$

Second moment — replace $\delta$ by $2\delta = 0.10$:

$$^{2}\bar{A}_x = \frac{\mu}{\mu+2\delta} = \frac{0.02}{0.12} = 0.166667.$$ $$\text{Var}(v^{T_x}) = 0.166667 - (0.285714)^2 = 0.085034, \quad \text{SD} = 0.291606.$$
Answer
$\bar{A}_x = 0.28571$, $\text{Var} = 0.08503$, $\text{SD} = 0.29161$.
Example 2 Endowment via its two pieces + UDD conversion ▾

For a life aged 50: $A^{1}_{50:\overline{10}|} = 0.04$, $\,_{10}p_{50} = 0.92$, $i = 0.05$, deaths UDD. (a) Find $A_{50:\overline{10}|}$. (b) Find $\bar{A}_{50:\overline{10}|}$.

Solution. Pure endowment: $v^{10} = 1.05^{-10} = 0.613913$, so $\,_{10}E_{50} = 0.613913 \times 0.92 = 0.564800.$

(a) $A_{50:\overline{10}|} = 0.04 + 0.564800 = 0.604800.$

(b) Convert only the term part with $\frac{i}{\delta}$, $\delta=\ln1.05=0.0487902$:

$$\tfrac{i}{\delta} = \tfrac{0.05}{0.0487902} = 1.024797, \quad \bar{A}^{1}_{50:\overline{10}|} = 1.024797 \times 0.04 = 0.040992.$$ $$\bar{A}_{50:\overline{10}|} = 0.040992 + 0.564800 = 0.605792.$$
Answer
(a) $0.60480$  ·  (b) $0.60579$.

Example 4 Non-level continuous benefit — no $A$-shortcut ▾

[CAS4A-S92] Whole life, benefit $b_t=e^{0.04t}$ at the moment of death, $\mu_{x+t}=0.03$, $\delta=0.05$. Find $\text{Var}[Z]$.

Solution. Use first principles — the benefit varies, so no $\bar A$ symbol applies. With $\,_tp_x\mu_{x+t}=0.03\,e^{-0.03t}$:

$$E[Z]=\int_0^\infty e^{0.04t}\,e^{-0.05t}\,(0.03)e^{-0.03t}\,dt = 0.03\!\int_0^\infty e^{-0.04t}\,dt = \frac{0.03}{0.04}=0.75.$$ $$E[Z^2]=\int_0^\infty \big(e^{0.04t}e^{-0.05t}\big)^2(0.03)e^{-0.03t}\,dt = 0.03\!\int_0^\infty e^{-0.05t}\,dt = \frac{0.03}{0.05}=0.60.$$ $$\text{Var}[Z]=0.60-(0.75)^2 = 0.0375.$$
Answer
$\text{Var}[Z]=0.0375$. Note $E[Z^2]$ was built by squaring benefit × discount, not by a "$^2A$" lookup.
Example 5 Term variance from an endowment via covariance ▾

[CAS3-F03] $Z_1$ = $n$-year term of 1, $Z_2$ = $n$-year endowment insurance of 1 (claims at moment of death). Given $v^n=0.250$, $\,_np_x=0.400$, $E[Z_2]=0.400$, $\text{Var}[Z_2]=0.055$. Find $\text{Var}[Z_1]$.

Solution (decomposition + covariance). Let $Z_3$ be the pure endowment, so $Z_2=Z_1+Z_3$ and $Z_1Z_3=0$ (they never both pay).

$$_nE_x = v^n\,{}_np_x = 0.250(0.400)=0.100, \quad E[Z_1]=E[Z_2]-{}_nE_x = 0.400-0.100=0.300.$$ $$\text{Var}[Z_3]=v^{2n}\,{}_np_x\,{}_nq_x = 0.25^2(0.4)(0.6)=0.015, \quad \text{Cov}[Z_1,Z_3]=-E[Z_1]E[Z_3]=-0.300(0.100)=-0.03.$$ $$\text{Var}[Z_2]=\text{Var}[Z_1]+\text{Var}[Z_3]+2\,\text{Cov} \;\Rightarrow\; 0.055 = \text{Var}[Z_1]+0.015+2(-0.03).$$
Answer
$\text{Var}[Z_1]=0.055-0.015+0.06 = 0.100.$

Example 13 Discrete probability — round the integer $K$ up off the table ▾

Whole life of 100,000 on $(80)$, benefit at the end of the year of death, $i=0.06$. Using the table below, find $\Pr[Z\le 80{,}000]$.

$x$8081828384
$\ell_x$10092745838

Solution. Here $Z = 100{,}000\,v^{K_{80}+1}$, a step function of the integer $K_{80}$. Translate the dollar condition into a condition on $K_{80}$:

$$100{,}000\,v^{K_{80}+1}\le 80{,}000 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; v^{K_{80}+1}\le 0.8 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; (1.06)^{-(K_{80}+1)}\le 0.8.$$

Take logs. Because $\ln v \lt 0$, dividing flips the inequality to a $\ge$:

$$-(K_{80}+1)\ln 1.06 \le \ln 0.8 \;\Longrightarrow\; K_{80}+1 \ge \frac{-\ln 0.8}{\ln 1.06}=\frac{0.223144}{0.058269}=3.8295 \;\Longrightarrow\; K_{80}\ge 2.8295.$$

Now the crucial step: $K_{80}$ takes only integer values, so $K_{80}\ge 2.8295$ means $K_{80}\ge 3$ — the boundary rounds UP to the next integer (any $K_{80}=2$ gives $Z$ above 80,000). Read the survival probability straight off the table:

$$\Pr[Z\le 80{,}000]=\Pr[K_{80}\ge 3]={}_3p_{80}=\frac{\ell_{83}}{\ell_{80}}=\frac{58}{100}=0.58.$$
Answer
$\Pr[Z\le 80{,}000]=0.58.$
Round UP, then read $\ell_x$ — don't truncate

For a discrete death benefit, "$Z$ small" $\Leftrightarrow$ "death late" $\Leftrightarrow$ "$K$ large," so the threshold becomes $K_x\ge(\text{a number})$. Always round that number up to the next integer ($2.83\to3$): rounding down would wrongly include $K_{80}=2$, whose $Z=100{,}000v^3=83{,}962\gt80{,}000$. Then the answer is a single ${}_kp_x=\ell_{x+k}/\ell_x$ off the life table — no summation needed. Contrast this with the continuous percentile in Example 11, where $T_x$ is not rounded.

Example 14 Master decomposition — whole life $=$ term $+$ deferred term $+$ deferred whole life, with chained $_tE_x$ ▾

This example ties the building blocks together. A whole life of 1 on $(50)$ is split across three disjoint time windows: deaths in years $0\!-\!5$, deaths in years $5\!-\!12$, and deaths after year $12$. You are given:

QuantityValueQuantityValue
$A^{1}_{50:\overline{5}|}$ (5-yr term)$0.012$${}_5E_{50}$$0.78$
$A^{1}_{55:\overline{7}|}$ (7-yr term at 55)$0.030$${}_7E_{55}$$0.70$
$A_{62}$ (whole life at 62)$0.30$

Find $A_{50}$ by decomposing it into a 5-year term, a 5-deferred 7-year term, and a 12-deferred whole life:

$$A_{50} = \underbrace{A^{1}_{50:\overline{5}|}}_{\text{die yrs }0\text{–}5} \;+\; \underbrace{{}_{5|7}A_{50}}_{\text{die yrs }5\text{–}12} \;+\; \underbrace{{}_{12|}A_{50}}_{\text{die after yr }12}.$$

Step 1 — chain the pure endowments. Coverage in the second window starts at time 5, so discount with ${}_5E_{50}$; coverage in the third window starts at time 12, and we build ${}_{12}E_{50}$ by chaining (survive 5 years to 55, then 7 more to 62):

$$_{12}E_{50} = {}_5E_{50}\cdot{}_7E_{55} = (0.78)(0.70) = 0.546.$$

Step 2 — value each window by sliding the coverage out to where it begins, then discounting back with the matching ${}_tE_x$:

$$_{5|7}A_{50} = {}_5E_{50}\cdot A^{1}_{55:\overline{7}|} = (0.78)(0.030)=0.0234,$$ $$_{12|}A_{50} = {}_{12}E_{50}\cdot A_{62} = (0.546)(0.30)=0.1638.$$

Step 3 — add the three disjoint pieces:

$$A_{50} = 0.012 + 0.0234 + 0.1638 = 0.1992.$$

Consistency check. The first two windows together are exactly a 12-year term, $A^{1}_{50:\overline{12}|}=0.012+0.0234=0.0354$, so the familiar two-piece split must also hold:

$$A_{50} = A^{1}_{50:\overline{12}|} + {}_{12|}A_{50} = 0.0354 + 0.1638 = 0.1992. \;\checkmark$$
Answer
$A_{50}=0.1992$. Any whole life can be sliced into as many disjoint time windows as you like; each window is valued at its own age and pulled back with a chained ${}_tE_x={}_aE_x\cdot{}_bE_{x+a}$.
The two moves that drive every multi-period split

(i) Slide-and-discount: a deferred term equals the same term started at the later age, discounted back by the pure endowment — $\,_{u|m}A_x={}_uE_x\,A^{1}_{x+u:\overline{m}|}$ and $\,_{u|}A_x={}_uE_x\,A_{x+u}$. (ii) Chain the survival discount: ${}_{t+h}E_x={}_tE_x\cdot{}_hE_{x+t}$ (e.g. ${}_{12}E_x={}_5E_x\cdot{}_7E_{x+5}$), so you never need a 12-year survival probability directly — only the SULT pieces you already have.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Converting the wrong part of an endowment. Under UDD (or $1/m$-thly, or claims acceleration) multiply only the term/death piece by $i/\delta$ (or $i/i^{(m)}$). The pure endowment $\,_nE_x$ is never rescaled, and $\bar A_{x:\overline n|}\ne\frac{i}{\delta}A_{x:\overline n|}$. This is the #1 error.
  • Off-by-one in the discrete benefit. $A_x = \sum v^{k+1}\,{}_{k|}q_x$ — exponent $k+1$, so the first term is $v^1 q_x$, not $v^0 q_x$. Term sums to $n-1$.
  • "Double force" means $1+j=(1+i)^2$, not $2i$. Use $\delta\to2\delta$ or $j=2i+i^2$. For pure endowment, $^2(_nE_x)=v^{2n}\,{}_np_x$ — survival prob. is not squared, only the discount.
  • Using "$^2$" on a non-level benefit. The pre-superscript 2 equals the true second moment only for level benefits. For varying or increasing benefits, $E[Z^2]\ne{}^2(IA)$ etc. — go to first principles and square benefit × discount.
  • Squaring the EPV before subtracting. $\text{Var}={}^2A - A^2$, not $^2A - {}^2A^2$.
  • Misplacing the "1". $A^1_{x:\overline{n}|}$ (1 over $x$) = term/death; $A^{\;1}_{x:\overline{n}|}$ (1 over $n$) = pure endowment/survival. Swapping them swaps the problem.
  • Deferred vs. term sign error. $_{u|}A_x = A_x - A^1_{x:\overline{u}|}$ and $_{u|}A_x = {}_uE_x\,A_{x+u}$ — subtract the skipped term, or discount with both interest and survival.
  • Forgetting the covariance. When combining overlapping benefits, $\text{Var}(Z_1+Z_2)$ needs $2\,\text{Cov}$. For disjoint payments $Z_1Z_2=0$ so $\text{Cov}=-E[Z_1]E[Z_2]$ (negative!).
  • Increasing+decreasing sum. Continuous gives $n\,\bar A^1$; annual gives $(n+1)A^1$. The off-by-one is deliberate exam bait.
  • SULT at the wrong $i$. SULT $A$-columns assume $i=0.05$. If $i\ne0.05$, only $\ell_x$ and $q_x$ are usable — recompute everything else.
  • Percentile direction. For a death benefit $Z$ decreases in $T_x$, so $\Pr[Z\le z_p]=\Pr[T_x>h]=\,_hp_x$ — flip survival vs. death carefully, and watch for benefit jumps that make $Z$ non-monotone.
  • Treating $A_x$ as a probability. It is an EPV. As $i\to0$, $A_x\to1$ regardless of mortality.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Write the present-value RV $Z$ for an $n$-year endowment insurance of 1 on $(x)$, in terms of $K_x$.
$Z = v^{K_x+1}$ if $K_x < n$ (death in first $n$ years), and $Z = v^{n}$ if $K_x \ge n$ (survival). Compactly, $Z = v^{\min(K_x+1,\,n)}$.
You have $A_x = 0.30$ at $i=0.05$ under UDD. What is $\bar{A}_x$?
$\bar{A}_x = \frac{i}{\delta}A_x$ with $\delta=\ln1.05=0.0487902$, so $\frac{i}{\delta}=1.024797$ and $\bar{A}_x = 1.024797\times0.30 = 0.30744.$
Given $^2A_{40}=0.085$ and $A_{40}=0.20$, find $\text{Var}(v^{K_{40}+1})$.
$\text{Var} = {}^2A_{40} - (A_{40})^2 = 0.085 - 0.040 = 0.045.$
For a 5-year pure endowment with $\,_5p_{60}=0.95$ and $i=0.04$, compute $\,_5E_{60}$ and $\text{Var}({}_5E_{60})$.
$_5E_{60} = v^5\,{}_5p_{60} = 1.04^{-5}\times0.95 = 0.821927\times0.95 = 0.78083.$ Bernoulli variance: $v^{10}\,{}_5p_{60}\,{}_5q_{60}=0.821927^2(0.95)(0.05)=0.03209.$
True/False: to convert a discrete endowment to continuous under UDD, multiply the entire $A_{x:\overline{n}|}$ by $i/\delta$.
False. Only the term component gets $i/\delta$. The pure endowment is unchanged: $\bar{A}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{i}{\delta}A^1_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_nE_x.$
A benefit varies as $b_t=e^{0.04t}$. Why can't you write its second moment as $^2(\bar A)$?
The pre-superscript 2 only means "recompute the EPV at force $2\delta$," which equals the true $E[Z^2]$ only for level benefits. Here $b_t$ varies, so $E[Z^2]=\int (b_t v^t)^2\,{}_tp_x\mu_{x+t}\,dt$ has no actuarial symbol — use first principles.
State both recursions for $(IA)_x$ and explain the second intuitively.
$(IA)_x = vq_x + vp_x\big(A_{x+1}+(IA)_{x+1}\big) = A_x + vp_x(IA)_{x+1}.$ The second form: an increasing whole life $=$ a level whole life $A_x$ (the "+1 each year" base) plus, on survival, another increasing whole life starting one year later, discounted with interest and survival.
Sum of $(IA)^1_{x:\overline n|}$ and $(DA)^1_{x:\overline n|}$? And the continuous version?
Annual: $(IA)^1+(DA)^1=(n+1)A^1_{x:\overline n|}$. Continuous: $(\bar I\bar A)^1+(\bar D\bar A)^1=n\,\bar A^1_{x:\overline n|}$. The $n$ vs $n+1$ is the classic gotcha.
If a problem gives you $A_{x:\overline 1|}=0.9434$, what have you actually been told?
$A_{x:\overline 1|}=vq_x+vp_x=v$, so $v=0.9434$ (and $i=1/v-1\approx 0.06$). A 1-year endowment always pays 1, so it equals the bare discount factor.
Express $\,_{3|2}A_{30}$ (3-year deferred 2-year term) using a discount and a term insurance.
$_{3|2}A_{30} = {}_3E_{30}\cdot A^1_{33:\overline 2|}$ — value the 2-year term starting at age 33, then discount back 3 years with interest and survival.
$Z=1000v^{T_x}$ whole life under $\mu=0.04$, $\delta=0.07$. Find the 70th percentile of $Z$.
$Z$ decreases in $T_x$, so $\Pr[Z\le z_{0.70}]=\Pr[T_x>h]={}_hp_x=e^{-0.04h}=0.70\Rightarrow h=8.917$. Then $z_{0.70}=1000e^{-0.07(8.917)}=535.70.$
Under the claims-acceleration approach, how do you get $\bar A_x$ from $A_x$? Compare to UDD.
Accumulate half a year: $\bar A_x\approx(1+i)^{1/2}A_x$ (the $m\to\infty$ limit of $(1+i)^{(m-1)/2m}$). UDD instead uses the exact-under-UDD factor $\bar A_x=\frac{i}{\delta}A_x$. Both apply only to the death part of an endowment.
