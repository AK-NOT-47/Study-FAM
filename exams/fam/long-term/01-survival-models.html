Long-Term Coverages and Survival Models

Every long-term product prices the same uncertainty — when a life dies — so the survival model is the foundation the whole exam stands on.

📖 AMLCR · Ch. 1–3 ⏱️ ~3–4 hrs 🔑 $S_0$ · $\mu_x$ · $\,_tp_x$ · $K_x$ · $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$ 🧮 4 mortality laws ⭐ High-frequency
Learning Objectives

After studying this note, you should be able to:

  • Describe the main long-term insurance and retirement products: term insurance, whole life insurance, endowment insurance, universal life, life annuities, and pension plans, and explain how they differ in benefit structure and risk.
  • Define the future lifetime random variable $T_x$ (including the conditional form $T_x = (T_0 - x \mid T_0 > x)$) and the curtate future lifetime $K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$, and state their relationship and probability functions.
  • Define the survival function $S_0(x)$, the cumulative distribution function $F_0(x)$, the probability density function $f_0(x)$, and the force of mortality $\mu_x$, and derive the relationships among them.
  • Compute and interpret the standard actuarial probabilities ${}_tp_x$, ${}_tq_x$, and the deferred mortality probability ${}_{u|t}q_x$, including the product (chain) form ${}_tp_x = p_x\,p_{x+1}\cdots p_{x+t-1}$, and apply them to percentile and multiple-life / binomial problems.
  • Adjust a survival model for extra risk — a multiplicative force ($\mu^*=k\mu$) or an additive constant ($\mu^*=\mu+c$) — and recompute survival probabilities.
  • Calculate the complete expectation $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$, the curtate expectation $e_x$, their second moments and variances, and the $n$-year temporary expectations $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|}$ and $e_{x:\overline{n}|}$, using both definitions and recursions.
  • Apply the common analytic survival laws — constant force (exponential), uniform / De Moivre, the generalized (beta) law, Gompertz, and Makeham — to compute survival probabilities, forces of mortality, and life expectancies.

(Notation and framework follow Dickson, Hardy & Waters, Actuarial Mathematics for Life Contingent Risks, Chapters 1–3.)

Key Concepts

1. The menu of long-term coverages

Long-term products are contracts whose payoff depends on when (or whether) a life survives or dies over a multi-year horizon. The candidate needs a qualitative grip on each:

  • Term insurance. Pays a death benefit only if death occurs within a fixed term of $n$ years. No benefit if the insured survives to the end. Pure protection; cheapest; no maturity/savings value.
  • Whole life insurance. Pays a death benefit whenever death occurs — coverage never expires. Premiums may be paid for life or for a limited period.
  • Endowment insurance. Pays the benefit on death if death occurs within $n$ years, OR pays a (usually equal) pure endowment if the life survives to time $n$. It is the combination: term insurance + pure endowment. So a benefit is paid either way — this is the most "savings-like" of the traditional contracts.
  • Universal life (UL). A flexible-premium product with a transparent "account value." The policyholder pays premiums into an account that earns interest (credited rate); the insurer deducts mortality charges (cost of insurance) and expense charges. Death benefit and premiums are adjustable. It bundles protection and a cash-accumulation account.
  • Life annuities. A series of payments made while the annuitant survives. Can be paid annually, monthly, etc.; can be immediate (payments start now) or deferred (start later, e.g., at retirement); can be whole life (until death) or temporary (for at most $n$ years). The mirror image of life insurance — the insurer's risk is longevity (people living too long) rather than mortality.
  • Pensions. Employer-sponsored retirement arrangements. Defined benefit (DB) plans promise a retirement benefit (often a life annuity) based on salary and service; the sponsor bears investment/longevity risk. Defined contribution (DC) plans accumulate contributions in an individual account; the member bears the risk.
Big-picture intuition

Insurance benefits are large when death is early; annuity benefits are large when death is late. Pricing all of them requires a probability model for the timing of death — that is the survival model.

2. The future lifetime random variable

Notation: $(x)$ means "a life aged $x$" — so write "$(20)$" or "a life aged 20", never "a life aged $(20)$". Fix a life currently aged exactly $x$. The central random variable is:

  • $T_x$ = complete future lifetime = the additional time, in years (continuous, $\ge 0$), that $(x)$ survives. Death occurs at age $x + T_x$.

For a newborn, $T_0$ is the age at death. The whole model is built from $T_0$ and then "conditioned" to age $x$. Formally, $T_x$ is the remaining lifetime of $(0)$ given survival to $x$:

$$T_x = \big(T_0 - x \mid T_0 > x\big).$$

The vertical bar is the conditioning "given that," exactly as in $\Pr[A \mid B]$. We always implicitly assume the life is alive at age $x$ — there is no reason to study its future beyond $x$ otherwise.

The curtate future lifetime is the integer part:

$$K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$$

i.e. the number of complete future years lived. If a life aged 20 dies at age 21.4, then $T_{20}=1.4$ and $K_{20}=\lfloor 1.4 \rfloor = 1$. $K_x$ is a discrete random variable taking values $0, 1, 2, \dots$ — useful when benefits are paid at year-ends.

3. Survival function, density, CDF, and force of mortality

Work from the newborn lifetime $T_0$ with age-at-death distribution:

  • Survival function $S_0(x) = \Pr[T_0 > x]$: probability a newborn survives past age $x$. It satisfies $S_0(0)=1$, is non-increasing, and $S_0(x)\to 0$ as $x\to\infty$ (or at the limiting age $\omega$).
  • CDF $F_0(x) = \Pr[T_0 \le x] = 1 - S_0(x)$.
  • Density $f_0(x) = F_0'(x) = -S_0'(x)$.

Conditioned to age $x$, the survival function of $T_x$ is written $S_x(t)=\Pr[T_x>t]$, and it has three defining properties: $S_x(0)=1$ (alive now), $S_x(\infty)=0$ (no one lives forever), and $S_x(t)$ is non-increasing in $t$. The CDF $F_x(t)=\Pr[T_x\le t]=1-S_x(t)$ is the mirror — $0$ at $t=0$, rising to $1$, non-decreasing. Because $T_x$ is continuous, strict vs. non-strict inequalities make no difference: $\Pr[T_x = c]=0$.

The force of mortality $\mu_x$ is the instantaneous death rate at age $x$ — the analogue of a hazard rate. Intuitively, for small $dt$,

$$\Pr[\text{life aged } x \text{ dies before } x+dt] \approx \mu_x\, dt.$$

It is the density of death at age $x$ relative to the survivors to age $x$. The force of mortality completely determines the survival model: knowing $\mu_x$ for all $x$ lets you reconstruct $S_0$.

Key relationship (memorize)
$$\mu_x = -\frac{S_0'(x)}{S_0(x)} = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln S_0(x) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad S_0(x) = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^x \mu_s\, ds\right).$$

4. Conditioning to age x: the actuarial probabilities

Because we usually care about a life already aged $x$, we condition on survival to $x$. Define $S_x(t) = \Pr[T_x > t]$ = probability $(x)$ survives a further $t$ years. Then:

  • ${}_tp_x = \Pr[T_x > t] = \dfrac{S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)}$ — probability $(x)$ survives $t$ years.
  • ${}_tq_x = \Pr[T_x \le t] = 1 - {}_tp_x$ — probability $(x)$ dies within $t$ years. The one-year rate $q_x$ is called the mortality rate at age $x$.
  • ${}_{u|t}q_x = \Pr[u < T_x \le u+t]$ — deferred probability: $(x)$ survives $u$ years and then dies in the following $t$ years.

When $t=1$ the left subscript is dropped: $p_x = {}_1p_x$, $q_x = {}_1q_x$.

A crucial structural fact is the multiplicative (consistency) rule for survival, which comes straight from the conditional probability definition:

$${}_{t+u}p_x = {}_tp_x \cdot {}_up_{x+t}.$$

Survival over $t+u$ years = survive the first $t$ years, then (given you're now $x+t$) survive the next $u$. Repeatedly applied, it gives the chain (product) form in one-year steps:

$${}_tp_x = p_x \cdot p_{x+1} \cdots p_{x+t-1} = \prod_{k=0}^{t-1} p_{x+k}.$$

This is the workhorse when a table of yearly $p$'s or $q$'s is supplied. There are many valid splits, e.g. ${}_5p_{20} = {}_2p_{20}\cdot{}_3p_{22} = {}_1p_{20}\cdot{}_4p_{21}$ — break the interval wherever the data lets you.

Death probabilities do NOT multiply

${}_tq_x \neq q_x\, q_{x+1}\cdots q_{x+t-1}$. Instead, go through survival: ${}_tq_x = 1 - {}_tp_x = 1-\prod_{k=0}^{t-1}(1-q_{x+k})$.

5. The deferred death probability — three faces

${}_{u|t}q_x$ shows up constantly, and the exam expects you to switch fluidly between its three equivalent forms depending on what the problem hands you:

  • $p-p$ form: survive $u$ but not $u+t$: ${}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x - {}_{u+t}p_x$.
  • $q-q$ form: die within $u+t$ but not within $u$: ${}_{u|t}q_x = {}_{u+t}q_x - {}_uq_x$.
  • $p\times q$ form: survive $u$, then die in the next $t$: ${}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x \cdot {}_tq_{x+u}$.

6. The curtate lifetime $K_x$ — its distribution

$K_x$ counts complete future years. Its probability mass function is "survive $k$ years, then die in the next one":

$$\Pr[K_x = k] = {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k} = {}_{k|}q_x, \qquad k = 0,1,2,\dots$$

and the masses sum to 1. The survival function of $K_x$ is clean: surviving more than $k$ complete years means surviving at least $k+1$ full years, so

$$\Pr[K_x > k] = \Pr[K_x \ge k+1] = {}_{k+1}p_x, \qquad \Pr[K_x \le k] = {}_{k+1}q_x.$$

Likewise ${}_nq_x = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}{}_{k|}q_x$ — dying within $n$ years is the sum of "die in year 1" + "die in year 2" + … + "die in year $n$".

6½. The life table — survival in tabular form

A life table summarizes a survival model as a column of expected survivors. Let $\ell_x$ (also written $l_x$) be the expected number of survivors at exact age $x$, starting from a chosen radix $\ell_0$ — an arbitrary positive starting count (often $100{,}000$). The radix only scales the table; all probabilities are ratios and are unaffected by it. Two more pieces of notation:

  • ${}_nd_x = \ell_x - \ell_{x+n}$ — the expected number of deaths between ages $x$ and $x+n$. (Drop the left prefix when $n=1$: $d_x = \ell_x - \ell_{x+1}$.)
  • $\omega$ — the limiting age: the first age at which $\ell_\omega = 0$, i.e. everyone is dead. (Not every model has a finite $\omega$ — constant force and Gompertz/Makeham do not.)

The three actuarial probabilities read straight off the $\ell$-column as simple ratios:

$${}_tp_x = \frac{\ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x}, \qquad {}_tq_x = \frac{\ell_x - \ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x} = \frac{{}_td_x}{\ell_x}, \qquad {}_{u|t}q_x = \frac{\ell_{x+u} - \ell_{x+u+t}}{\ell_x}.$$

Each is just "(relevant count) ÷ (alive at age $x$)": survivors to $x+t$ over survivors to $x$; deaths in the window over survivors to $x$. The numerator of ${}_{u|t}q_x$ is the deaths between $x+u$ and $x+u+t$ — survive to $x+u$, then die in the next $t$ years.

The table is just the ratio engine

Every probability on this page can be computed from an $\ell$-column. When some $\ell_x$ are missing, fill the gaps with the given $p_x$ ($\ell_{x+1}=\ell_x\,p_x$) or $q_x$ ($\ell_{x+1}=\ell_x(1-q_x)$), then read off any ratio you need. The exam supplies the Standard Ultimate Life Table (SULT) in exactly this form.

7. Adjusting for extra risk (substandard lives)

Underwriting often loads the baseline mortality for a higher-risk life. Two standard adjustments — both frequently tested — start from $\mu_{x+s}$ and produce an adjusted ${}_tp_x^{*}$:

  • Multiplicative (proportional) extra risk: $\mu^{*}_{x+s} = k\,\mu_{x+s}$. Because survival is $\exp(-\int\mu)$, multiplying the force by $k$ raises survival to the power $k$: $${}_tp_x^{*} = \big({}_tp_x\big)^{k}.$$ This is exactly why the generalized (beta) law, with $\mu_x = \tfrac{\alpha}{\omega-x}$, gives ${}_tp_x = \big({}_tp_x^{\text{unif}}\big)^{\alpha}$.
  • Additive (constant) extra risk: $\mu^{*}_{x+s} = \mu_{x+s} + c$. A constant add-on factors out as an exponential discount: $${}_tp_x^{*} = {}_tp_x \cdot e^{-ct}.$$
Power vs. product

A multiplied force gives a survival probability raised to a power; an added constant force gives a survival probability times $e^{-ct}$. Mixing these up is a classic error. And ${}_tq^{*} \neq ({}_tq)^k$ — always adjust $p$, then take the complement.

8. Expected future lifetime

  • Complete expectation of life $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \mathrm{E}[T_x]$ — average additional years lived, measured continuously.
  • Curtate expectation of life $e_x = \mathrm{E}[K_x]$ — average complete years lived.

Because $T_x$ is non-negative, both can be written as integrals/sums of survival probabilities (the "tail-sum" formula for expectation). The same survival functions also give the second moments, hence the variances. When the horizon is capped at $n$ years we use the limited lifetime $\min(T_x,n)$, with expectation $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|}$ (and its discrete twin $e_{x:\overline{n}|}$) — the right tool for term/temporary contracts.

Under the uniform distribution of deaths (UDD) approximation within each year of age, $\overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + \tfrac12$. The complete one is the larger (you add the partial year), consistent with $T_x \ge K_x$.

Regularity assumptions (so the moments exist)

For every survival model in this course we assume $S_x(t)$ is differentiable for $t>0$, and $\lim_{t\to\infty} t\,S_x(t)=0$ and $\lim_{t\to\infty} t^2 S_x(t)=0$. The last two guarantee the mean and variance of $T_x$ are finite — they hold for any realistic human-lifetime model.

9. Analytic survival laws — the ones you must know

These are closed-form choices for $\mu_x$ or $S_0(x)$ that make integrals tractable. The first three are "unrealistic but easy" (older exams); Gompertz/Makeham are "realistic but harder" (more common on recent exams). Know each one's $\mu_x$, ${}_tp_x$, and life-expectancy shortcut.

  • Constant force / exponential: $\mu_x = \mu$ for all $x$. "Memoryless" — future lifetime distribution is identical at every age, so $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x+n} = 1/\mu$ and ${}_tp_{20}={}_tp_{50}=e^{-\mu t}$.
  • De Moivre / uniform: $T_0\sim\text{Uniform}(0,\omega)$ so $T_x\sim\text{Uniform}(0,\omega-x)$; one person dies per year ($l_x=k(\omega-x)$); force rises to infinity at the limiting age $\omega$.
  • Beta / generalized De Moivre: $\mu_x = \tfrac{\alpha}{\omega-x}$, with $l_x=k(\omega-x)^\alpha$ and ${}_tp_x=\big(\tfrac{\omega-x-t}{\omega-x}\big)^\alpha$. Setting $\alpha=1$ recovers plain uniform.
  • Gompertz: force grows exponentially with age, $\mu_x = B c^x$ ($B>0,\,c>1$) — captures the empirical doubling of adult mortality roughly every 8 years.
  • Makeham: Gompertz plus a constant "accident" term, $\mu_x = A + Bc^x$ ($A\ge -B$). The $A$ term represents age-independent causes.
What's on the exam table

The Standard Ultimate Life Table (SULT) provided at the exam is generated by Makeham's law with $A = 0.00022$, $B = 2.7\times10^{-6}$, $c = 1.124$, at $5\%$ interest. The Makeham $\mu_x$ and ${}_tp_x$ formulas are printed on the table's last page — so you do not need to memorize Gompertz/Makeham; just set $A=0$ to drop from Makeham to Gompertz.

Formulas

Core distribution relationships

$$F_0(x) = 1 - S_0(x), \qquad f_0(x) = -S_0'(x) = F_0'(x)$$ $$\mu_x = \frac{f_0(x)}{S_0(x)} = -\frac{S_0'(x)}{S_0(x)} = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln S_0(x)$$ $$S_0(x) = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^x \mu_s\, ds\right), \qquad f_0(x) = S_0(x)\,\mu_x$$
Notation
  • $S_0(x)$: probability a newborn survives to age $x$.
  • $F_0(x), f_0(x)$: CDF and pdf of the age at death $T_0$.
  • $\mu_x$: force of mortality at exact age $x$ ($\mu_x \ge 0$).

Survival/death probabilities for a life aged $x$

$$ {}_tp_x = \frac{S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)} = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^t \mu_{x+s}\, ds\right) = \prod_{k=0}^{t-1} p_{x+k} $$ $$ {}_tq_x = 1 - {}_tp_x = \frac{S_0(x) - S_0(x+t)}{S_0(x)} = 1 - \prod_{k=0}^{t-1}(1-q_{x+k}) $$ $$ {}_{u|t}q_x = {}_up_x - {}_{u+t}p_x = {}_{u+t}q_x - {}_uq_x = {}_up_x \cdot {}_tq_{x+u} $$ $$ {}_{t+u}p_x = {}_tp_x \cdot {}_up_{x+t} \qquad\text{(consistency rule)} $$ $$ {}_tp_x \,\mu_{x+t} = -\frac{d}{dt}\,{}_tp_x = f_{T_x}(t) \qquad\text{(density of } T_x \text{)} $$
Notation
  • ${}_tp_x$: $(x)$ survives $t$ more years; ${}_tq_x$: $(x)$ dies within $t$ years.
  • ${}_{u|t}q_x$: $(x)$ survives $u$ years then dies within the next $t$ years. ($u|q_x$ means $t=1$.)
  • $\mu_{x+t}$: force of mortality at age $x+t$.

Life-table probabilities

$${}_tp_x = \frac{\ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x}, \qquad {}_tq_x = \frac{\ell_x - \ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x} = \frac{{}_td_x}{\ell_x}, \qquad {}_{u|t}q_x = \frac{\ell_{x+u} - \ell_{x+u+t}}{\ell_x}$$ $${}_nd_x = \ell_x - \ell_{x+n}, \qquad \ell_{x+1} = \ell_x\,p_x = \ell_x\,(1 - q_x)$$
Notation
  • $\ell_x$ (or $l_x$): expected number of survivors to exact age $x$; radix $\ell_0$ is an arbitrary positive starting count.
  • ${}_nd_x$: expected deaths between ages $x$ and $x+n$ (prefix dropped when $n=1$).
  • $\omega$: limiting age, the smallest age with $\ell_\omega = 0$.

The curtate lifetime $K_x$

$$\Pr[K_x = k] = {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k} = {}_{k|}q_x \qquad (k=0,1,2,\dots)$$ $$\Pr[K_x > k] = {}_{k+1}p_x, \qquad \Pr[K_x \le k] = {}_{k+1}q_x$$ $${}_nq_x = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}{}_{k|}q_x, \qquad K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$$

Extra-risk (substandard) adjustments

Multiplicative: $\mu^{*}_{x+s}=k\,\mu_{x+s}$ $$ {}_tp_x^{*} = \big({}_tp_x\big)^{k} $$ Additive: $\mu^{*}_{x+s}=\mu_{x+s}+c$ $$ {}_tp_x^{*} = {}_tp_x\cdot e^{-ct} $$

Expectations of future lifetime

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \mathrm{E}[T_x] = \int_0^\infty t\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt = \int_0^\infty {}_tp_x\, dt, \qquad \mathrm{E}[T_x^2] = \int_0^\infty t^2\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt = 2\int_0^\infty t\,{}_tp_x\, dt $$ $$ e_x = \mathrm{E}[K_x] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k\,{}_{k|}q_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} {}_kp_x, \qquad \mathrm{E}[K_x^2] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (2k-1)\,{}_kp_x $$ $$ \mathrm{Var}(T_x) = \mathrm{E}[T_x^2] - (\overset{\circ}{e}_x)^2, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(K_x) = \mathrm{E}[K_x^2] - e_x^2, \qquad \overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + \tfrac{1}{2}\ \ (\text{UDD}) $$

$n$-year temporary (limited) expectations

Continuous, $\min(T_x,n)$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \int_0^n t\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt + n\cdot{}_np_x = \int_0^n {}_tp_x\, dt $$ $$ \mathrm{E}\!\left[\min(T_x,n)^2\right] = \int_0^n t^2\,{}_tp_x\,\mu_{x+t}\,dt + n^2\cdot{}_np_x = \int_0^n 2t\,{}_tp_x\, dt $$ Discrete, $\min(K_x,n)$ $$ e_{x:\overline{n}|} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} k\,{}_{k|}q_x + n\cdot{}_np_x = \sum_{k=1}^{n} {}_kp_x $$ $$ \mathrm{E}\!\left[\big(\min(K_x,n)\big)^2\right] = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (2k-1)\,{}_kp_x $$ $$ \mathrm{Var}\!\left[\min(T_x,n)\right] = \mathrm{E}\!\left[\min(T_x,n)^2\right] - \big(\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|}\big)^2 $$

Recursions for life expectancy

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+n}, \qquad e_x = e_{x:\overline{n}|} + {}_np_x\,e_{x+n} $$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{m+n}|} = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{m}|} + {}_mp_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+m:\overline{n}|}, \qquad e_{x:\overline{m+n}|} = e_{x:\overline{m}|} + {}_mp_x\,e_{x+m:\overline{n}|} $$ $$ e_x = p_x\,(1 + e_{x+1}) \qquad (n=1\text{ curtate recursion}) $$
Read the recursion in words

"Your life expectancy = your expectancy over the next $n$ years, plus (if you survive those $n$ years) your expectancy starting from age $x+n$." The temporary version just caps the second piece at $n$ more years. There is no continuous analogue of $e_x=p_x(1+e_{x+1})$; the correct continuous one-step form is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} + p_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$ with $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|}=\int_0^1{}_tp_x\,dt$.

The analytic laws at a glance

Constant force (exponential), $\mu_x = \mu$ $$ S_0(x) = e^{-\mu x}, \quad {}_tp_x = e^{-\mu t}, \quad {}_tq_x = 1 - e^{-\mu t}, \quad f_x(t)=\mu e^{-\mu t} $$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \frac{1}{\mu}, \quad \mathrm{Var}(T_x)=\frac{1}{\mu^2}, \quad \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} = \frac{1}{\mu}\big(1 - e^{-\mu n}\big) $$ De Moivre / uniform on $[0,\omega]$ $$ S_0(x) = 1 - \frac{x}{\omega}, \quad \mu_x = \frac{1}{\omega - x}, \quad {}_tp_x = \frac{\omega-x-t}{\omega-x}, \quad {}_tq_x = {}_{u|t}q_x\big|_{\text{any }u}= \frac{t}{\omega - x} $$ $$ T_x \sim \text{Uniform}(0,\ \omega - x), \quad \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \frac{\omega - x}{2}, \quad \mathrm{Var}(T_x) = \frac{(\omega-x)^2}{12} $$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|} = {}_np_x\,(n) + {}_nq_x\!\left(\tfrac{n}{2}\right) $$ Beta / generalized De Moivre, parameter $\alpha$ $$ \mu_x = \frac{\alpha}{\omega - x}, \quad l_x = k(\omega-x)^\alpha, \quad {}_tp_x = \left(\frac{\omega - x - t}{\omega - x}\right)^{\!\alpha}, \quad \overset{\circ}{e}_x = \frac{\omega - x}{\alpha + 1} $$ Gompertz, $\mu_x = B c^x$ ($B>0,\ c>1$) $$ {}_tp_x = \exp\!\left(-\frac{B c^x (c^t - 1)}{\ln c}\right) $$ Makeham, $\mu_x = A + B c^x$ ($A \ge -B,\ B>0,\ c>1$) $$ {}_tp_x = \exp\!\left(-A t - \frac{B c^x (c^t - 1)}{\ln c}\right) $$

Setting $\alpha=1$ in the beta law recovers plain De Moivre; setting $A=0$ in Makeham recovers Gompertz; setting $B=0$ recovers constant force. Caution: under the beta law ${}_tp_x = \big({}_tp_x^{\text{unif}}\big)^\alpha$, but ${}_tq_x \neq \big({}_tq_x^{\text{unif}}\big)^\alpha$ — always raise the survival probability to $\alpha$, then take the complement for $q$.

Worked Examples

Example 1 Force of mortality to survival probability ▾

You are given $\mu_x = \dfrac{1}{120 - x}$ for $0 \le x < 120$. Find ${}_{10}p_{40}$, ${}_{10}q_{40}$, ${}_{5|10}q_{40}$, and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{40}$.

Solution. This is De Moivre with $\omega = 120$ (since $\mu_x = 1/(\omega - x)$).

Survival: ${}_tp_x = \dfrac{\omega-x-t}{\omega-x}$, so

$$ {}_{10}p_{40} = \frac{120 - 40 - 10}{120 - 40} = \frac{70}{80} = 0.875. $$

Death: ${}_{10}q_{40} = 1 - 0.875 = 0.125.$

Deferred: ${}_{5|10}q_{40} = {}_5p_{40} - {}_{15}p_{40}$.

$$ {}_5p_{40} = \frac{75}{80} = 0.9375, \qquad {}_{15}p_{40} = \frac{65}{80} = 0.8125. $$ $$ {}_{5|10}q_{40} = 0.9375 - 0.8125 = 0.125. $$

Complete expectation: $\overset{\circ}{e}_{40} = \dfrac{\omega - x}{2} = \dfrac{120 - 40}{2} = 40.$

Answer
${}_{10}p_{40} = 0.875$, ${}_{10}q_{40} = 0.125$, ${}_{5|10}q_{40} = 0.125$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{40} = 40$ years.
Example 2 Building a mini life table from yearly $p$'s and $q$'s ▾

You are given $p_{20}=0.98$, $p_{21}=0.975$, ${}_3p_{21}=0.94$, $q_{23}=0.03$. Find ${}_2p_{20}$, ${}_2p_{21}$, ${}_3p_{20}$, and ${}_{1|}q_{20}$.

Solution. Use the chain form ${}_tp_x = \prod p_{x+k}$ and the consistency rule.

${}_2p_{20} = p_{20}\,p_{21} = 0.98 \times 0.975 = 0.9555.$

For ${}_2p_{21}$: since ${}_3p_{21} = {}_2p_{21}\cdot p_{23}$ and $p_{23}=1-q_{23}=0.97$,

$$ {}_2p_{21} = \frac{{}_3p_{21}}{p_{23}} = \frac{0.94}{0.97} = 0.9691. $$

${}_3p_{20} = p_{20}\cdot {}_2p_{21} = 0.98 \times 0.9691 = 0.9497.$

Deferred death (two equivalent routes):

$$ {}_{1|}q_{20} = p_{20}\,q_{21} = 0.98\,(1-0.975) = 0.0245, \quad\text{or}\quad {}_{1|}q_{20} = p_{20} - {}_2p_{20} = 0.98 - 0.9555 = 0.0245. $$
Answer
${}_2p_{20}=0.9555$, ${}_2p_{21}=0.9691$, ${}_3p_{20}=0.9497$, ${}_{1|}q_{20}=0.0245$.
Example 3 Median (percentile) future lifetime ▾

[CAS4A-F93] You are given $S_0(x) = 1 - 0.01x$ for $0 < x \le 100$. Find the median future lifetime of a life aged 10.

Solution. The median is the value $\pi_{0.5}$ with $\Pr[T_{10} > \pi_{0.5}] = 0.5$, i.e. $S_{10}(\pi_{0.5}) = 0.5$. Using $S_{10}(t) = \dfrac{S_0(10+t)}{S_0(10)}$:

$$ \frac{1 - 0.01(10+\pi_{0.5})}{1 - 0.01(10)} = 0.5 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{0.9 - 0.01\pi_{0.5}}{0.9} = 0.5. $$

So $0.9 - 0.01\pi_{0.5} = 0.45 \Rightarrow 0.01\pi_{0.5} = 0.45 \Rightarrow \pi_{0.5} = 45.$

Answer
Median future lifetime $= 45$ years. (For the $100p$-th percentile, solve $S_x(\pi_p)=1-p$.)
Example 4 Multiple independent lives → binomial ▾

You are given ${}_{10}p_{40} = 0.7$ and ${}_{20}q_{40} = 0.5$. Three independent lives are all aged 40. Find the probability that at least two of them die between ages 50 and 60.

Solution. "Die between 50 and 60" means $10 < T_{40} < 20$, with probability

$$ \Pr[10 < T_{40} < 20] = {}_{10}p_{40} - {}_{20}p_{40} = {}_{10}p_{40} - (1 - {}_{20}q_{40}) = 0.7 - (1 - 0.5) = 0.2. $$

Let $N\sim\text{Binomial}(3, 0.2)$ count such deaths. Then

$$ \Pr[N\ge 2] = \binom{3}{2}(0.2)^2(0.8) + \binom{3}{3}(0.2)^3 = 0.096 + 0.008 = 0.104. $$
Answer
$\Pr[N \ge 2] = 0.104$. (Same template scales up to large groups via the normal approximation — compute each subgroup's $np$ and $np(1-p)$, sum under independence, then use $\Phi^{-1}$.)
Example 5 Constant-force master problem ▾

You are given $\mu_{30+t} = 0.3$ for $t\ge 0$. Find ${}_3p_{30}$, ${}_5q_{30}$, ${}_{5|2}q_{30}$, $f_{30}(t)$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30}$, $\mathrm{Var}(T_{30})$, and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{4}|}$.

Solution. Under constant force, ${}_tp_x = e^{-\mu t}$ at every age.

  • ${}_3p_{30} = e^{-0.3(3)} = e^{-0.9} = 0.4066.$
  • ${}_5q_{30} = 1 - e^{-0.3(5)} = 1 - e^{-1.5} = 0.7769.$
  • ${}_{5|2}q_{30} = {}_5p_{30} - {}_7p_{30} = e^{-1.5} - e^{-2.1} = 0.1007.$
  • $f_{30}(t) = {}_tp_{30}\,\mu = 0.3\,e^{-0.3t}.$
  • $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30} = 1/\mu = 1/0.3 = 3.\overline{3}.$
  • $\mathrm{Var}(T_{30}) = 1/\mu^2 = 1/0.09 = 11.11.$
  • $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{4}|} = \dfrac{1}{\mu}\big(1 - e^{-\mu(4)}\big) = \dfrac{1}{0.3}\big(1 - e^{-1.2}\big) = 2.33.$
Answer
$0.4066$, $0.7769$, $0.1007$, $0.3e^{-0.3t}$, $3.33$, $11.11$, $2.33$. Memorize this whole bundle as the "constant force checklist."
Example 6 Variance of $T_x$ from a power-law survival function ▾

[SOA3-F04:24] The future lifetime of $(0)$ follows $S_0(x) = \left(\dfrac{50}{50+x}\right)^3$, $x\ge 0$. Calculate $\mathrm{Var}(T_{20})$.

Solution. First condition to age 20:

$$ {}_tp_{20} = \frac{S_0(20+t)}{S_0(20)} = \frac{(50/(70+t))^3}{(50/70)^3} = \left(\frac{70}{70+t}\right)^3. $$

First moment (tail-integral):

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{20} = \int_0^\infty \left(\frac{70}{70+t}\right)^3 dt = 70^3\left[\frac{(70+t)^{-2}}{-2}\right]_0^\infty = \frac{70^3}{2\cdot 70^2} = 35. $$

Second moment:

$$ \mathrm{E}[T_{20}^2] = \int_0^\infty 2t\left(\frac{70}{70+t}\right)^3 dt = 4{,}900. $$

Therefore

$$ \mathrm{Var}(T_{20}) = 4{,}900 - 35^2 = 4{,}900 - 1{,}225 = 3{,}675. $$
Answer
$\overset{\circ}{e}_{20} = 35$, $\mathrm{Var}(T_{20}) = 3{,}675$.
Example 7 Curtate & temporary expectation from a life table ▾

You are given a life table with $l_{80},\dots,l_{87} = 100, 90, 76, 58, 40, 25, 12, 0$. Find $e_{80}$, approximate $\overset{\circ}{e}_{80}$ under UDD, and find the 4-year temporary curtate expectation $e_{80:\overline{4}|}$.

Solution. Use $e_x = \sum_{k\ge 1}{}_kp_x = \frac{1}{l_x}\sum_{k\ge 1} l_{x+k}$.

$$ e_{80} = \frac{90+76+58+40+25+12+0}{100} = \frac{301}{100} = 3.01. $$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{80} \approx e_{80} + \tfrac12 = 3.01 + 0.5 = 3.51. $$

The temporary version caps the sum at 4 terms:

$$ e_{80:\overline{4}|} = \sum_{k=1}^{4}{}_kp_{80} = \frac{90+76+58+40}{100} = 2.64. $$
Answer
$e_{80}=3.01$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{80}\approx 3.51$, $e_{80:\overline{4}|}=2.64$. Interpretation: an 80-year-old expects roughly 3 more full years (about 3½ counting the partial year).
Example 8 Recursion with a piecewise-constant force ▾

[3-F01:1] You are given $\mu_x = 0.04$ for $0\le x\le 40$ and $\mu_x = 0.05$ for $x>40$. Calculate $\overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{25}|}$.

Solution. The 25 years from age 25 straddle the break at 40: 15 years at force $0.04$, then 10 years at force $0.05$. Split with the temporary recursion:

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{25}|} = \overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{15}|} + {}_{15}p_{25}\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{40:\overline{10}|}. $$

Each piece is a constant-force temporary expectation $\frac{1}{\mu}(1-e^{-\mu n})$:

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{15}|} = \frac{1}{0.04}\big(1 - e^{-0.04(15)}\big) = 25\big(1 - e^{-0.6}\big) = 11.281, $$ $$ {}_{15}p_{25} = e^{-0.04(15)} = e^{-0.6} = 0.5488, \qquad \overset{\circ}{e}_{40:\overline{10}|} = \frac{1}{0.05}\big(1 - e^{-0.05(10)}\big) = 20\big(1 - e^{-0.5}\big) = 7.869. $$ $$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{25}|} = 11.281 + 0.5488\times 7.869 \approx 15.6. $$
Answer
$\overset{\circ}{e}_{25:\overline{25}|} \approx 15.6$ years. The recursion is the clean way to handle a force that changes at a known age.
Example 9 Beta (generalized De Moivre) law ▾

You are given $\mu_x = \dfrac{1}{200 - 2x}$. Calculate ${}_{10|}q_{25}$ and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{25}$.

Solution. Rewrite the force to read off the parameters:

$$ \mu_x = \frac{1}{200-2x} = \frac{1/2}{100 - x} = \frac{\alpha}{\omega - x} \;\Rightarrow\; \alpha = \tfrac12,\ \omega = 100. $$

So ${}_tp_x = \big(\tfrac{\omega-x-t}{\omega-x}\big)^{1/2}$. Use the $p-p$ form for the deferred death:

$$ {}_{10|}q_{25} = {}_{10}p_{25} - {}_{11}p_{25} = \left(\frac{75-10}{75}\right)^{1/2} - \left(\frac{75-11}{75}\right)^{1/2} = \sqrt{\tfrac{65}{75}} - \sqrt{\tfrac{64}{75}} = 0.0072. $$

And the expectation shortcut:

$$ \overset{\circ}{e}_{25} = \frac{\omega - x}{\alpha + 1} = \frac{100 - 25}{\tfrac12 + 1} = \frac{75}{1.5} = 50. $$
Answer
${}_{10|}q_{25} \approx 0.0072$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{25} = 50$. Note we raised the survival probability to $\alpha$, not the death probability.
Example 10 Gompertz / Makeham survival ▾

A life follows Makeham's law with $A = 0.0005$, $B = 0.0000075$, $c = 1.09$. Find ${}_{10}p_{50}$.

Solution. Use

$$ {}_tp_x = \exp\!\left(-A t - \frac{B c^x (c^t - 1)}{\ln c}\right). $$

Compute the pieces with $x=50,\ t=10$:

  • $A t = 0.0005 \times 10 = 0.005.$
  • $c^x = 1.09^{50}$. $\ln(1.09)=0.0861777$; $50 \times 0.0861777 = 4.308886$; $c^{50}=e^{4.308886}=74.3575.$
  • $c^t = 1.09^{10} = e^{0.861777} = 2.367364$, so $c^t - 1 = 1.367364.$
  • $\ln c = 0.0861777.$

Gompertz term:

$$ \frac{B c^x (c^t-1)}{\ln c} = \frac{0.0000075 \times 74.3575 \times 1.367364}{0.0861777} = \frac{0.00076258}{0.0861777} = 0.0088489. $$

Total exponent: $-(0.005 + 0.0088489) = -0.0138489.$

$$ {}_{10}p_{50} = e^{-0.0138489} = 0.98625. $$
Answer
${}_{10}p_{50} \approx 0.9863$. Setting $A=0$ here would give the pure Gompertz survival probability.
Example 11 Uniform / De Moivre master problem ▾

Assume future lifetime is uniform (De Moivre) with $\omega = 80$. Find the full bundle: ${}_3p_{30}$, ${}_5q_{30}$, ${}_{5|2}q_{30}$, $f_{30}(t)$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30}$, $\mathrm{Var}(T_{30})$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{4}|}$, and $\mu_{30+t}$.

Solution. Here $\omega - x = 80 - 30 = 50$, so $T_{30}\sim\text{Uniform}(0,50)$ — read every quantity off the uniform formulas. (Recall under De Moivre "one person dies per year", so ${}_tq_x = {}_{u|t}q_x = \tfrac{t}{\omega-x}$ for any deferral $u$.)

  • ${}_3p_{30} = \dfrac{80-30-3}{80-30} = \dfrac{47}{50} = 0.94.$
  • ${}_5q_{30} = \dfrac{5}{80-30} = \dfrac{5}{50} = 0.10.$
  • ${}_{5|2}q_{30} = \dfrac{2}{80-30} = \dfrac{2}{50} = 0.04.$ (The deferral doesn't matter — it is always $\tfrac{t}{\omega-x}$.)
  • $f_{30}(t) = \dfrac{1}{\omega-x} = \dfrac{1}{50} = 0.02$ for $0\lt t\lt 50.$
  • $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30} = \dfrac{\omega - x}{2} = \dfrac{50}{2} = 25.$
  • $\mathrm{Var}(T_{30}) = \dfrac{(\omega-x)^2}{12} = \dfrac{50^2}{12} = 208.33.$
  • $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{4}|} = {}_4p_{30}(4) + {}_4q_{30}\!\left(\tfrac{4}{2}\right) = \tfrac{46}{50}(4) + \tfrac{4}{50}(2) = 3.68 + 0.16 = 3.84.$
  • $\mu_{30+t} = \dfrac{1}{\omega-(x+t)} = \dfrac{1}{50 - t}.$
Answer
$0.94$, $0.10$, $0.04$, $0.02$, $25$, $208.33$, $3.84$, $\tfrac{1}{50-t}$. Memorize this as the "uniform checklist" — the exact mirror of the constant-force one in Example 5.

Example 13 Normal-approximation percentile, non-homogeneous group ▾

A father-son club has $4{,}000$ members: $2{,}000$ aged 20 and $2{,}000$ aged 45. In 25 years they hold a reunion. All lives are independent and follow the SULT, with $\ell_{20}=100{,}000$, $\ell_{45}=99{,}033.9$, $\ell_{70}=91{,}082.4$. Using the normal approximation (no continuity correction), find the 99th percentile of the number of surviving members at the reunion.

Solution. Split by age — the two subgroups have different survival probabilities, so handle each as its own binomial, then add under independence.

Fathers (aged 45 → 70): $p = {}_{25}p_{45} = \dfrac{\ell_{70}}{\ell_{45}} = \dfrac{91{,}082.4}{99{,}033.9} = 0.91971.$

$$n p = 2{,}000(0.91971) = 1{,}839.42, \qquad n p(1-p) = 2{,}000(0.91971)(0.08029) = 147.69.$$

Sons (aged 20 → 45): $p = {}_{25}p_{20} = \dfrac{\ell_{45}}{\ell_{20}} = \dfrac{99{,}033.9}{100{,}000} = 0.99034.$

$$n p = 2{,}000(0.99034) = 1{,}980.68, \qquad n p(1-p) = 2{,}000(0.99034)(0.00966) = 19.14.$$

Let $N = N_{\text{father}} + N_{\text{son}}$. Sum the means, and sum the variances — because the groups are independent, $\mathrm{Cov}=0$:

$$\mathrm{E}[N] = 1{,}839.42 + 1{,}980.68 = 3{,}820.10,$$ $$\mathrm{Var}[N] = 147.69 + 19.14 + 2(0) = 166.83.$$

The 99th percentile uses $\Phi^{-1}(0.99) = 2.32635$:

$$\pi_{0.99} = \mathrm{E}[N] + 2.32635\,\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[N]} = 3{,}820.10 + 2.32635\sqrt{166.83} = 3{,}820.10 + 30.05 \approx 3{,}850.$$
Answer
$\pi_{0.99} \approx 3{,}850$ survivors. The template: split into homogeneous subgroups, get each subgroup's $np$ and $np(1-p)$, add means and add variances (Cov $=0$ by independence), then apply the normal $\Phi^{-1}$.

Common Exam Traps

The traps that cost real points
  • Confusing $T_x$ and $K_x$. $K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$. Use $e_x$ (sum starting at $k=1$) for the curtate; use $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$ (integral) for the complete. Mixing the two costs easy points.
  • Death probabilities don't multiply. ${}_tp_x = \prod p_{x+k}$ chains, but ${}_tq_x \neq \prod q_{x+k}$. Compute ${}_tp_x$ first, then ${}_tq_x = 1 - {}_tp_x$.
  • Curtate sum index. $e_x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}{}_kp_x$ starts at $k=1$, not $k=0$. Including $k=0$ adds ${}_0p_x = 1$ and overstates by exactly 1.
  • Forgetting to condition on age $x$. ${}_tp_x = S_0(x+t)/S_0(x)$, not $S_0(t)$. Constant force is the only law where the age $x$ drops out (memoryless).
  • De Moivre denominator. ${}_tq_x = \tfrac{t}{\omega - x}$, with $\omega - x$ in the denominator, not $\omega$. Remaining lifetime is uniform on $(0,\omega-x)$, so $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = (\omega-x)/2$ and variance $(\omega-x)^2/12$.
  • Beta law: raise $p$, not $q$. ${}_tp_x = \big({}_tp_x^{\text{unif}}\big)^\alpha$ but ${}_tq_x \neq \big({}_tq_x^{\text{unif}}\big)^\alpha$. Always work through survival.
  • Extra-risk: power vs. product. Multiplied force $\mu^*=k\mu \Rightarrow {}_tp_x^*=({}_tp_x)^k$; added force $\mu^*=\mu+c \Rightarrow {}_tp_x^* = {}_tp_x\,e^{-ct}$. Don't swap them.
  • UDD adjustment direction. $\overset{\circ}{e}_x \approx e_x + \tfrac12$. The complete expectation is the larger one (you add the half-year). A common error is subtracting.
  • No continuous version of $e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1})$. The curtate one-step recursion has no clean complete analogue; use $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} + p_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$ instead.
  • Sign of the force in the integral. ${}_tp_x = \exp\!\big(-\int_0^t \mu_{x+s}\,ds\big)$. The minus sign and the shifted argument $\mu_{x+s}$ are both easy to drop.
  • $\ln c$ vs $c$ in Gompertz/Makeham. Integrating $Bc^{x+s}$ over $s$ divides by $\ln c$, not by $c$. Always include $\ln c$.
  • ${}_{u|t}q_x$ structure. It equals ${}_up_x - {}_{u+t}p_x = {}_up_x\cdot{}_tq_{x+u}$. Do not write ${}_up_x \cdot {}_tq_x$ — the death must be evaluated at age $x+u$.
  • Limited expectation, two-term form. $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{n}|}$ as a density integral has the extra $n\cdot{}_np_x$ "survivors capped at $n$" term; the tail-integral form $\int_0^n {}_tp_x\,dt$ already includes it — don't double-count.
  • Insurance vs annuity risk direction. Insurer loses on early death (insurance) but on late death / longevity (annuity). Conceptual questions test this.
  • Endowment = term + pure endowment. It pays on death-within-$n$ or survival-to-$n$, never both.
  • Shortcuts are a supplement, not a substitute for basics. The analytic-law formulas (De Moivre's $\overset{\circ}{e}_x=(\omega-x)/2$, constant-force $1/\mu$, etc.) save time, but a problem phrased slightly differently can break a memorized shortcut. Always be able to fall back to first principles — ${}_tp_x = \exp(-\int_0^t\mu_{x+s}ds)$ and the tail-sum/integral for expectations. Students who only memorize shortcuts get stuck.
  • Non-homogeneous group: split by age, then add variances. When a cohort mixes ages (or smokers/non-smokers, etc.), do not use one $p$ for everyone. Treat each homogeneous subgroup as its own binomial, compute each $np$ and $np(1-p)$, then sum the means and sum the variances. Independence means $\mathrm{Cov}=0$, so $\mathrm{Var}[N]=\sum \mathrm{Var}[N_i]$ — never average the $p$'s.

Self-Check Questions

Try each before revealing the answer.

Express $T_x$ in terms of $T_0$ using conditional notation, and state the relationship between $T_x$ and $K_x$.
$T_x = (T_0 - x \mid T_0 > x)$, the remaining lifetime of a newborn given survival to age $x$. And $K_x = \lfloor T_x \rfloor$, the number of complete future years.
Write the two-way relationship between $\mu_x$ and $S_0(x)$ (derivative form and integral form).
$\displaystyle \mu_x = -\frac{d}{dx}\ln S_0(x)$ and $\displaystyle S_0(x) = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^x \mu_s\,ds\right)$.
Give the chain (product) form of ${}_tp_x$ in one-year steps, and explain why ${}_tq_x$ cannot be written the same way with $q$'s.
${}_tp_x = p_x\,p_{x+1}\cdots p_{x+t-1}$ — survival requires surviving every year, so probabilities multiply. Death within $t$ years is the complement of this product, ${}_tq_x = 1 - \prod(1-q_{x+k})$; it is not $\prod q_{x+k}$ (which would be the probability of dying in every year, impossible).
Write the three equivalent forms of ${}_{3|2}q_x$.
${}_{3|2}q_x = {}_3p_x - {}_5p_x = {}_5q_x - {}_3q_x = {}_3p_x \cdot {}_2q_{x+3}$.
State the pmf and the survival function of $K_x$. What is $\Pr[K_x \ge 3]$?
$\Pr[K_x = k] = {}_kp_x\,q_{x+k} = {}_{k|}q_x$; $\Pr[K_x > k] = {}_{k+1}p_x$ and $\Pr[K_x \le k] = {}_{k+1}q_x$. Hence $\Pr[K_x \ge 3] = \Pr[K_x > 2] = {}_3p_x$.
A life's force of mortality is doubled ($\mu^* = 2\mu$). How does ${}_tp_x$ change? What if instead a constant $c$ is added to the force?
Multiplied force: ${}_tp_x^* = ({}_tp_x)^2$ (raise to the power). Added constant: ${}_tp_x^* = {}_tp_x\,e^{-ct}$ (multiply by an exponential discount). Never apply the power/discount to $q$ directly.
$S_0(x) = 1 - 0.01x$ on $(0,100]$. Find the median future lifetime of $(20)$.
Solve $S_{20}(\pi)=0.5$: $\frac{1-0.01(20+\pi)}{1-0.01(20)} = \frac{0.8-0.01\pi}{0.8}=0.5 \Rightarrow 0.8-0.01\pi = 0.4 \Rightarrow \pi = 40$ years. (This is De Moivre with $\omega=100$, so the median equals $(\omega-x)/2 = 40$.)
Under a constant force $\mu = 0.03$, what is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x$, $\mathrm{Var}(T_x)$, and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{10}|}$? Does any of it depend on $x$?
$\overset{\circ}{e}_x = 1/\mu = 33.\overline{3}$, $\mathrm{Var}(T_x) = 1/\mu^2 = 1111.1$, and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{10}|} = \frac{1}{0.03}(1-e^{-0.3}) = 8.64$. None depends on $x$ — constant force is memoryless.
State the curtate one-step recursion and the temporary-expectation recursion. Why is there no continuous analogue of $e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1})$?
$e_x = p_x(1+e_{x+1})$ and $e_{x:\overline{m+n}|} = e_{x:\overline{m}|} + {}_mp_x\,e_{x+m:\overline{n}|}$. The continuous version fails because the "+1" is a discrete artifact; the correct continuous one-step form is $\overset{\circ}{e}_x = \overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|} + p_x\,\overset{\circ}{e}_{x+1}$ with $\overset{\circ}{e}_{x:\overline{1}|}=\int_0^1 {}_tp_x\,dt$.
For De Moivre with $\omega = 100$, compute ${}_{20}q_{30}$, $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30}$, and $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{10}|}$.
${}_{20}q_{30} = \tfrac{20}{70} = 0.2857$; $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30} = \tfrac{70}{2} = 35$. For the temporary: ${}_{10}p_{30} = \tfrac{60}{70}=0.8571$, ${}_{10}q_{30}=0.1429$, so $\overset{\circ}{e}_{30:\overline{10}|} = {}_{10}p_{30}(10) + {}_{10}q_{30}(5) = 8.571 + 0.714 = 9.286$.
Write the three life-table probabilities in terms of $\ell$'s, and define the radix and limiting age $\omega$.
${}_tp_x = \dfrac{\ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x}$, ${}_tq_x = \dfrac{\ell_x-\ell_{x+t}}{\ell_x} = \dfrac{{}_td_x}{\ell_x}$, ${}_{u|t}q_x = \dfrac{\ell_{x+u}-\ell_{x+u+t}}{\ell_x}$. The radix $\ell_0$ is the arbitrary positive starting count (it cancels in every ratio); $\omega$ is the limiting age, the first age with $\ell_\omega=0$.
A table has $\ell_{20}=1{,}000$, $\ell_{21}=980$, and you are told $q_{21}=0.025$. Find $\ell_{22}$ and ${}_{1|}q_{20}$.
$\ell_{22}=\ell_{21}(1-q_{21})=980(0.975)=955.5$. Then ${}_{1|}q_{20}=\dfrac{\ell_{21}-\ell_{22}}{\ell_{20}}=\dfrac{980-955.5}{1{,}000}=0.0245$.
State the second moment of the limited curtate lifetime $\min(K_x,n)$, and how variance follows.
$\mathrm{E}\!\left[(\min(K_x,n))^2\right] = \sum_{k=1}^{n}(2k-1)\,{}_kp_x$ (the analogue of $\mathrm{E}[K_x^2]=\sum_{k\ge1}(2k-1)\,{}_kp_x$ truncated at $n$). Then $\mathrm{Var}[\min(K_x,n)] = \mathrm{E}[(\min(K_x,n))^2] - (e_{x:\overline{n}|})^2$.
A club has 1,000 lives aged 30 and 1,000 aged 60. Using the normal approximation for the number of survivors after 20 years, how do you combine the two groups?
Treat each age as its own binomial: $N_{30}\sim\text{Bin}(1000,\,{}_{20}p_{30})$, $N_{60}\sim\text{Bin}(1000,\,{}_{20}p_{60})$. Compute each $np$ and $np(1-p)$. Then $\mathrm{E}[N]=\mathrm{E}[N_{30}]+\mathrm{E}[N_{60}]$ and, because the groups are independent ($\mathrm{Cov}=0$), $\mathrm{Var}[N]=\mathrm{Var}[N_{30}]+\mathrm{Var}[N_{60}]$. A percentile is then $\mathrm{E}[N]+\Phi^{-1}(p)\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[N]}$. Never average the two survival probabilities.
Which product pays a benefit whether the life dies within the term or survives to the end of the term: term, whole life, or endowment? Briefly say why.
Endowment insurance. It is term insurance (death within $n$) plus a pure endowment (survival to $n$), so a benefit is paid in either case.
